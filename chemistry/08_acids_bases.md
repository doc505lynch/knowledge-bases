# Acids and Bases

## Definitions

### Arrhenius (1884)
- **Acid**: produces H‚Āļ in aqueous solution (e.g., HCl ‚Üí H‚Āļ + Cl‚ĀĽ)
- **Base**: produces OH‚ĀĽ in aqueous solution (e.g., NaOH ‚Üí Na‚Āļ + OH‚ĀĽ)
- Limited to aqueous solutions

### Br√łnsted-Lowry (1923)
- **Acid**: proton (H‚Āļ) donor
- **Base**: proton (H‚Āļ) acceptor
- Applies to non-aqueous systems; emphasizes conjugate pairs

**Conjugate acid-base pair**: differ by one H‚Āļ
- Example: HCl (acid) / Cl‚ĀĽ (conjugate base)
- Example: NH‚āÉ (base) / NH‚āĄ‚Āļ (conjugate acid)
- Stronger acid ‚Üí weaker conjugate base

**Amphoteric species**: can act as acid or base (e.g., H‚āāO, HCO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ, HSO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ)

### Lewis (1923)
- **Acid**: electron pair acceptor (Lewis acid)
- **Base**: electron pair donor (Lewis base)
- Broadest definition; includes reactions with no H‚Āļ transfer
- Examples: BF‚āÉ (Lewis acid, empty orbital), NH‚āÉ (Lewis base, lone pair)
- Metal cations are Lewis acids; ligands are Lewis bases

## The pH Scale

```
pH = ‚ąílog[H‚Āļ]    [H‚Āļ] = 10^(‚ąípH)
pOH = ‚ąílog[OH‚ĀĽ]  [OH‚ĀĽ] = 10^(‚ąípOH)
pH + pOH = 14.00  (at 25¬įC)
Kw = [H‚Āļ][OH‚ĀĽ] = 1.0 √ó 10‚ĀĽ¬Ļ‚Āī
```

| pH | [H‚Āļ] | Solution type |
|----|------|---------------|
| 0 | 1 M | Very strongly acidic |
| 3 | 10‚ĀĽ¬≥ M | Strongly acidic (vinegar) |
| 7 | 10‚ĀĽ‚Ā∑ M | Neutral (pure water) |
| 9 | 10‚ĀĽ‚ĀĻ M | Mildly basic (baking soda) |
| 14 | 10‚ĀĽ¬Ļ‚Āī M | Very strongly basic (conc. NaOH) |

## Strong Acids and Bases

**Strong acids** (essentially 100% dissociation):
HCl, HBr, HI, HNO‚āÉ, H‚āāSO‚āĄ (first H), HClO‚āĄ, HClO‚āÉ

**Strong bases** (essentially 100% dissociation):
Group 1 hydroxides: LiOH, NaOH, KOH, RbOH, CsOH
Group 2 hydroxides (heavy): Ca(OH)‚āā, Sr(OH)‚āā, Ba(OH)‚āā

**pH of strong acid**: pH = ‚ąílog[H‚Āļ] = ‚ąílog[HA]
**pH of strong base**: pOH = ‚ąílog[OH‚ĀĽ], then pH = 14 ‚ąí pOH

## Weak Acids

Weak acids partially dissociate:
HA ‚áĆ H‚Āļ + A‚ĀĽ
```
Ka = [H‚Āļ][A‚ĀĽ] / [HA]
pKa = ‚ąílog Ka
```

Lower pKa (or higher Ka) ‚Üí stronger acid

### Calculating pH of Weak Acid
**Example**: 0.100 M acetic acid, Ka = 1.8 √ó 10‚ĀĽ‚ĀĶ

ICE:
|  | CH‚āÉCOOH | H‚Āļ | CH‚āÉCOO‚ĀĽ |
|--|---------|-----|---------|
| I | 0.100 | 0 | 0 |
| C | ‚ąíx | +x | +x |
| E | 0.100‚ąíx | x | x |

Ka = x¬≤/(0.100‚ąíx) ‚Čą x¬≤/0.100 (assuming x << 0.100)
x = ‚ąö(1.8√ó10‚ĀĽ‚ĀĶ √ó 0.100) = ‚ąö(1.8√ó10‚ĀĽ‚Ā∂) = 1.34 √ó 10‚ĀĽ¬≥ M
Check: 1.34√ó10‚ĀĽ¬≥/0.100 = 1.34% < 5% ‚úď
pH = ‚ąílog(1.34√ó10‚ĀĽ¬≥) = **2.87**
% dissociation = 1.34%

### Percent Dissociation
```
% dissociation = [H‚Āļ]eq / [HA]‚āÄ √ó 100%
```
- Increases as concentration decreases (dilution law)

## Weak Bases

B + H‚āāO ‚áĆ BH‚Āļ + OH‚ĀĽ
```
Kb = [BH‚Āļ][OH‚ĀĽ] / [B]
```

**Common weak bases**: NH‚āÉ (Kb = 1.8√ó10‚ĀĽ‚ĀĶ), amines (RNH‚āā, R‚āāNH, R‚āÉN), pyridine

**Calculating pH of weak base**: same ICE approach, find [OH‚ĀĽ], then pOH, then pH

## Polyprotic Acids

Acids with more than one ionizable proton; Ka‚āĀ > Ka‚āā > Ka‚āÉ (each subsequent ionization harder)

| Acid | Ka‚āĀ | Ka‚āā | Ka‚āÉ |
|------|-----|-----|-----|
| H‚āāSO‚āĄ | Large (strong) | 1.2√ó10‚ĀĽ¬≤ | ‚ÄĒ |
| H‚āāCO‚āÉ | 4.3√ó10‚ĀĽ‚Ā∑ | 4.7√ó10‚ĀĽ¬Ļ¬Ļ | ‚ÄĒ |
| H‚āÉPO‚āĄ | 7.5√ó10‚ĀĽ¬≥ | 6.2√ó10‚ĀĽ‚Āł | 4.8√ó10‚ĀĽ¬Ļ¬≥ |
| H‚āāS | 1.0√ó10‚ĀĽ‚Ā∑ | ~10‚ĀĽ¬Ļ‚Ā∑ | ‚ÄĒ |

For most calculations, contribution of Ka‚āā to [H‚Āļ] is negligible.

## Acid-Base Properties of Salts (Hydrolysis)

Salt from strong acid + strong base ‚Üí **neutral solution** (e.g., NaCl ‚Üí pH 7)
Salt from weak acid + strong base ‚Üí **basic solution** (A‚ĀĽ hydrolyzes: A‚ĀĽ + H‚āāO ‚áĆ HA + OH‚ĀĽ)
Salt from strong acid + weak base ‚Üí **acidic solution** (BH‚Āļ hydrolyzes: BH‚Āļ ‚áĆ B + H‚Āļ)
Salt from weak acid + weak base ‚Üí depends on Ka vs. Kb

### Hydrolysis of Metal Ions
Metal ions act as Lewis acids; coordinate water molecules donate H‚Āļ:
[Fe(H‚āāO)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ ‚áĆ [Fe(H‚āāO)‚āÖOH]¬≤‚Āļ + H‚Āļ
‚Üí High charge density cations (Al¬≥‚Āļ, Fe¬≥‚Āļ) produce acidic solutions

## Buffer Solutions

### Definition
A buffer resists pH changes when small amounts of acid or base are added. Contains:
- Weak acid + its conjugate base (acidic buffer)
- Weak base + its conjugate acid (basic buffer)

### Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation
```
pH = pKa + log([A‚ĀĽ]/[HA])
```

- pH = pKa when [A‚ĀĽ] = [HA] (equal concentrations)
- Buffer range: pH = pKa ¬Ī 1

### Buffer Capacity
- Maximum when [A‚ĀĽ]/[HA] = 1 (ratio closest to 1)
- Increases with higher concentrations of buffer components
- Choose weak acid with pKa closest to desired pH

### Common Buffers
| Buffer system | pH range | Use |
|--------------|----------|-----|
| Acetic acid/acetate | 3.7‚Äď5.8 | Lab, food preservation |
| Phosphate (H‚āāPO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ/HPO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ) | 6.2‚Äď8.2 | Biological systems, PBS |
| Carbonate/bicarbonate | 6.4‚Äď10.3 | Blood, geological systems |
| TRIS | 7.0‚Äď9.0 | Biochemistry |
| Ammonia/ammonium | 8.2‚Äď10.2 | Lab |

### Buffer Calculation Example
Prepare pH 5.00 buffer using acetic acid (pKa = 4.74):
pH = pKa + log([A‚ĀĽ]/[HA])
5.00 = 4.74 + log(ratio)
log(ratio) = 0.26 ‚Üí ratio = 10^0.26 = 1.82
Use [CH‚āÉCOO‚ĀĽ]/[CH‚āÉCOOH] = 1.82 (e.g., 0.182 M acetate + 0.100 M acetic acid)

## Acid-Base Titrations

### Strong Acid/Strong Base
- Equivalence point: pH = 7.00
- Sharp endpoint; any indicator near pH 7 works
- Before equivalence: pH set by excess acid
- After equivalence: pH set by excess base

### Weak Acid/Strong Base
- Equivalence point: pH > 7 (conjugate base hydrolyzes)
- At half-equivalence point: pH = pKa (buffer region maximum)
- Gradual pH rise; need indicator with color change at correct pH

### Weak Base/Strong Acid
- Equivalence point: pH < 7
- At half-equivalence: pH = pKa (of conjugate acid) = 14 ‚ąí pKb

### Indicators
Weak acids where HIn (one color) ‚áĆ H‚Āļ + In‚ĀĽ (different color)
Useful range: pKa(indicator) ¬Ī 1

| Indicator | pH range | Acid color | Base color |
|-----------|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Methyl orange | 3.1‚Äď4.4 | Red | Yellow |
| Methyl red | 4.4‚Äď6.2 | Red | Yellow |
| Bromothymol blue | 6.0‚Äď7.6 | Yellow | Blue |
| Phenolphthalein | 8.2‚Äď10.0 | Colorless | Pink |
| Thymolphthalein | 9.4‚Äď10.6 | Colorless | Blue |

## Relative Acid Strength

**Oxyacids (same central atom)**: more oxygens ‚Üí stronger acid (pulls electron density from O‚ÄďH bond)
HClO < HClO‚āā < HClO‚āÉ < HClO‚āĄ

**Oxyacids (same structure)**: higher EN central atom ‚Üí stronger acid
H‚āāSO‚āĄ > H‚āāSeO‚āĄ > H‚āāTeO‚āĄ

**Binary acids (H-X)**: larger/less electronegative X ‚Üí stronger acid (bond strength dominates in non-aqueous)
HI > HBr > HCl > HF (in water: bond strength effect dominates)

**Organic acids**: electron-withdrawing groups increase acidity; electron-donating groups decrease it
- Cl‚āÉCCOOH (Ka = 0.23) > Cl‚āāCHCOOH > ClCH‚āāCOOH > CH‚āÉCOOH (Ka = 1.8√ó10‚ĀĽ‚ĀĶ)

## Superacids and Superbases

**Superacids**: stronger than 100% sulfuric acid
- Magic acid: FSO‚āÉH + SbF‚āÖ
- Fluorosulfuric acid: FSO‚āÉH
- Protonates hydrocarbons, noble gas compounds

**Superbases**: stronger than NaOH
- Butyllithium (nBuLi), lithium diisopropylamide (LDA)
- Deprotonates C‚ÄďH bonds in organic chemistry
