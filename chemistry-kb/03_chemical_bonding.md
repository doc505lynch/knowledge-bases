# Chemical Bonding

## Types of Chemical Bonds

| Bond Type | Between | Mechanism | Typical EN Difference |
|-----------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| Ionic | Metal + nonmetal | Electron transfer; electrostatic attraction | > 1.7 |
| Covalent (polar) | Two nonmetals | Shared electrons (unequal) | 0.4–1.7 |
| Covalent (nonpolar) | Same or similar nonmetals | Shared electrons (equal) | < 0.4 |
| Metallic | Metal atoms | Delocalized "sea of electrons" | — |

## Ionic Bonding

- **Formation**: metal loses e⁻ → cation; nonmetal gains e⁻ → anion; opposites attract
- **Lattice energy**: energy released when gaseous ions combine to form ionic solid; increases with charge, decreases with ion size
- **Born-Haber cycle**: thermodynamic cycle relating lattice energy to formation enthalpy, ionization energy, electron affinity, sublimation energy
- **Properties**: high MP/BP, brittle, conduct electricity when dissolved or molten, soluble in polar solvents

## Covalent Bonding

### Lewis Structures

**Drawing Lewis structures**:
1. Count total valence electrons
2. Connect atoms with single bonds (2e⁻ each)
3. Complete octets of outer atoms with lone pairs
4. Give remaining electrons to central atom
5. If central atom lacks octet, convert lone pairs to double/triple bonds

**Formal charge** = (valence e⁻) − (lone pair e⁻) − ½(bonding e⁻)
- Best structure minimizes formal charges; negative FC on more electronegative atom

### Resonance
- When multiple valid Lewis structures exist, actual structure is a hybrid (average)
- Example: O₃ has two resonance structures; actual O–O bond is intermediate (bond order 1.5)
- Resonance structures share same connectivity; only electron positions differ
- Resonance stabilizes molecules (lowers energy)

### Bond Properties

| Bond Order | Bond Length | Bond Energy | Example |
|------------|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Single (1) | Longest | Lowest | C–C (154 pm, 347 kJ/mol) |
| Double (2) | Medium | Medium | C=C (134 pm, 614 kJ/mol) |
| Triple (3) | Shortest | Highest | C≡C (120 pm, 839 kJ/mol) |

**Bond polarity**: δ+ on less electronegative atom, δ− on more electronegative atom

## VSEPR Theory (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion)

Electron pairs (bonding + lone pairs) arrange to minimize repulsion.

**Repulsion order**: lone pair–lone pair > lone pair–bonding > bonding–bonding

### Bond Angles
- Tetrahedral ideal: 109.5°; lone pairs compress angles (NH₃ 107°, H₂O 104.5°)
- Trigonal planar: 120°
- Linear: 180°
- Trigonal bipyramidal: 120° (equatorial), 90° (axial-equatorial)

## Hybridization

| Hybridization | Geometry | Bond angle | Example |
|--------------|----------|-----------|---------|
| sp | Linear | 180° | BeCl₂, C in CO₂, alkynes |
| sp² | Trigonal planar | 120° | BF₃, alkenes, carbonyl C |
| sp³ | Tetrahedral | 109.5° | CH₄, NH₃, H₂O |
| sp³d | Trigonal bipyramidal | 90°/120° | PCl₅ |
| sp³d² | Octahedral | 90° | SF₆ |

- **σ (sigma) bonds**: head-on orbital overlap; rotation allowed; all single bonds are σ
- **π (pi) bonds**: sideways p orbital overlap; no rotation; second bond in double, second+third in triple

## Molecular Orbital Theory

- Atomic orbitals combine to form molecular orbitals (MOs)
- **Bonding MO**: lower energy; electrons stabilize molecule
- **Antibonding MO (*)**: higher energy; electrons destabilize molecule
- **Bond order** = ½(bonding electrons − antibonding electrons)

### MO Diagram for O₂ (16 electrons)
σ1s², σ*1s², σ2s², σ*2s², σ2p², π2p⁴, π*2p² → bond order = 2; paramagnetic (2 unpaired e⁻ in π*)

## Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)

Weaker than intramolecular bonds; determine physical properties.

| Force | Origin | Strength | Example |
|-------|--------|----------|---------|
| London dispersion (LDF) | Temporary dipoles in nonpolar molecules | Weakest; increases with molecular mass | noble gases, nonpolar organics |
| Dipole-dipole | Permanent dipoles | Moderate | HCl, SO₂ |
| Hydrogen bonding | H bonded to N, O, or F; interacts with lone pair | Strong for size | H₂O, HF, NH₃, DNA base pairs |
| Ion-dipole | Ion + polar molecule | Strongest IMF | NaCl in water |
| Ion-induced dipole | Ion + polarizable nonpolar | Moderate | O₂ in blood |

### Consequences of IMFs
- **Boiling point**: stronger IMF → higher BP (more energy to overcome)
- **Viscosity**: stronger IMF → higher viscosity
- **Surface tension**: stronger IMF → higher surface tension
- **Solubility**: "like dissolves like" — polar solutes in polar solvents; nonpolar in nonpolar

### Hydrogen Bonding — Special Properties of Water
- Each H₂O can form 4 H-bonds (2 donors, 2 acceptors)
- High BP (100°C) and MP (0°C) for such a small molecule
- Ice less dense than liquid water (open hexagonal lattice in solid) → ice floats
- High specific heat capacity (4.18 J/g·K) → thermal buffer

## Metallic Bonding

- Positive metal ions (cations) in a "sea" of delocalized valence electrons
- Explains: electrical conductivity, thermal conductivity, malleability, ductility, metallic luster
- **Band theory**: atomic orbitals → molecular orbitals → continuous bands
  - Conductors: overlapping valence/conduction bands
  - Semiconductors: small band gap (~1 eV); conductivity increases with temperature
  - Insulators: large band gap (>5 eV)

## Polarity of Molecules

A molecule is polar if:
1. It has polar bonds (EN difference > 0.4), AND
2. The bond dipoles do NOT cancel due to geometry

| Molecule | Polar bonds? | Geometry | Net dipole? |
|----------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| CO₂ | Yes (C=O) | Linear | No (cancel) |
| H₂O | Yes (O–H) | Bent | Yes |
| CH₄ | Yes (C–H) | Tetrahedral | No (cancel) |
| NH₃ | Yes (N–H) | Pyramidal | Yes |
| CHCl₃ | Yes | Tetrahedral (asymmetric) | Yes |

## Expanded Octets and Exceptions

- **Electron deficient** (fewer than 8): BH₃, BF₃, AlCl₃ (group 13); act as Lewis acids
- **Odd-electron molecules** (radicals): NO, NO₂, ClO₂
- **Expanded octets** (period 3+): PCl₅, SF₆, XeF₄ — use of d orbitals allows >8 electrons
