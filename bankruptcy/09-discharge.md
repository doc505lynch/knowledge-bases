# Bankruptcy Discharge in California

## What Is a Bankruptcy Discharge?

A **discharge** is the ultimate goal of most bankruptcy filings. It is a federal court order under **11 U.S.C. § 524** that permanently eliminates a debtor's personal liability for specified debts. Once a debt is discharged:

- The creditor is permanently **prohibited** from taking any action to collect the discharged debt
- The creditor cannot file lawsuits, send demand letters, call, or report new adverse information on that debt
- The debtor is no longer legally obligated to pay

**Important:** Discharge eliminates personal liability but does **not** automatically remove liens. A mortgage lien survives discharge — the creditor cannot sue you personally but can still foreclose on the collateral.

---

## When Is Discharge Granted?

### Chapter 7
- Discharge enters approximately **60–90 days after the 341 Meeting**, assuming no objections and the debtor completed the debtor education course
- Total timeline: **3–4 months** from filing

### Chapter 13
- Discharge enters **after successful completion of the entire repayment plan** (3–5 years)
- Debtor must be current on all domestic support obligations and have completed debtor education
- A "hardship discharge" may be available earlier under narrow circumstances

---

## Debts That CAN Be Discharged

- Credit card debt
- Medical bills
- Personal loans (unsecured)
- Payday loans
- Utility bills (past-due balances)
- Lease obligations (for rejected leases)
- Most civil judgment debts
- Deficiency balances after repossession or short sale
- Business debts (if the individual is personally liable)
- HOA dues (pre-petition only)

---

## Debts That CANNOT Be Discharged — 11 U.S.C. § 523

### 1. Student Loans
Presumptively non-dischargeable unless the debtor proves **"undue hardship"** under the **Brunner Test** (applied in the Ninth Circuit):

1. The debtor cannot maintain a minimal standard of living if required to repay
2. This state of affairs is likely to persist for a significant portion of the repayment period
3. The debtor has made good faith efforts to repay

**Note (2022–2024):** DOJ guidance and some courts in the Ninth Circuit have shown greater willingness to discharge student loans under streamlined processes — consult an attorney for current developments.

### 2. Child Support and Alimony (Domestic Support Obligations)
- **100% non-dischargeable** in both Chapter 7 and Chapter 13
- Collection agencies acting on DSO creditors' behalf are not stopped by the automatic stay

### 3. Most Tax Debts
Tax debts are non-dischargeable unless ALL of the following apply:

| Rule | Requirement |
|------|-------------|
| **3-Year Rule** | Tax return was due more than 3 years before filing (including extensions) |
| **2-Year Rule** | Tax return was actually filed more than 2 years before filing |
| **240-Day Rule** | Tax was assessed more than 240 days before filing |
| **No fraud** | Return was not fraudulent |
| **No willful evasion** | Debtor did not willfully attempt to evade |

California FTB income taxes follow similar rules. Property taxes are generally not dischargeable.

### 4. Debts from Fraud
Debts obtained through **fraud, false representations, or false pretenses** are non-dischargeable if the creditor files a timely adversary proceeding. Examples:
- Credit applications with materially false statements
- Luxury goods purchases ($725+ within 90 days of filing) — presumed fraudulent
- Cash advances ($1,000+ within 70 days of filing) — presumed fraudulent

### 5. Debts from Willful and Malicious Injury
Debts from **intentional, deliberate harm** to another person or their property.

**Note:** In Chapter 13, willful and malicious injury to **property** (not persons) can be discharged — a key advantage over Chapter 7.

### 6. Criminal Fines, Restitution, and Government Penalties
- Criminal fines and penalties
- Restitution ordered as part of a criminal sentence
- Civil penalties payable to a governmental unit

### 7. DUI-Related Debts
Debts for death or personal injury caused by the debtor's operation of a motor vehicle while intoxicated.

### 8. Debts Not Listed in the Bankruptcy Schedules
If a creditor is not notified of the bankruptcy (not listed in schedules), the debt **may** survive discharge in a no-asset Chapter 7 case.

### 9. Marital Property Settlement Obligations
In **Chapter 7**, non-support property settlement obligations from divorce are non-dischargeable.
In **Chapter 13**, such debts can be discharged upon plan completion — a key advantage.

### 10. Pension Fund Contributions
Debts owed to employee benefit plans are generally non-dischargeable.

## Objections to Discharge

Creditors or the trustee may file an **adversary proceeding** within **60 days** of the first date set for the 341 Meeting:

- **§ 523 objections** — Specific debt is non-dischargeable (fraud, willful injury, etc.)
- **§ 727 objections** — The entire discharge should be denied (debtor concealed assets, made false oaths, etc.)

A § 727 objection, if granted, denies the debtor any discharge — all debts survive.

---

## Reaffirmation Agreements

A **reaffirmation agreement** is a voluntary contract in which the debtor agrees to remain personally liable on a debt after discharge — opting out of discharge for that specific debt.

### When Used
- To keep a financed vehicle
- To maintain a mortgage in good standing with the lender

### Requirements (11 U.S.C. § 524(c))
1. Agreement must be made before the discharge is entered
2. Debtor must receive disclosures including the right to rescind
3. Attorney (if represented) must declare the agreement is voluntary and does not impose undue hardship
4. If unrepresented, the court must hold a hearing and approve the agreement

### Right to Rescind
A debtor may rescind a reaffirmation agreement at any time before the discharge or within **60 days** after it is filed with the court, whichever is later.

---

## Effect of Discharge — Creditor Violations

After discharge, any attempt by a creditor to collect a discharged debt violates the **discharge injunction** under 11 U.S.C. § 524. Violations may result in:
- Contempt of court sanctions
- Actual and punitive damages
- Attorney fees awarded to the debtor

California debtors receiving collection contacts on discharged debts should contact an attorney immediately.
