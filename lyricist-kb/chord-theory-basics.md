# Music Theory for Lyricists

Understanding basic music theory helps lyricists write words that work WITH the music, not against it. You don't need to be a musician — you need to understand how chords and keys create emotional context for your lyrics.

---

## 1. Major and Minor Keys

### Major Keys
- Sound: Happy, bright, uplifting, triumphant, resolved
- Used in: Pop, country, dance music, anthems, children's songs
- Example: C major (C-D-E-F-G-A-B)
- Songs in major keys: "Happy" (Pharrell), "Shake It Off" (Taylor Swift), "Hey Jude" (Beatles)

### Minor Keys
- Sound: Sad, dark, moody, tense, mysterious, emotional
- Used in: Ballads, R&B, metal, trap, film scores, blues
- Example: A minor (A-B-C-D-E-F-G)
- Songs in minor keys: "Billie Jean" (MJ), "Rolling in the Deep" (Adele), "Lose Yourself" (Eminem)

### How This Affects Lyrics
- **Major key + sad lyrics** = bittersweet, nostalgic, wistful (e.g., "Hey Ya!" by OutKast)
- **Minor key + happy lyrics** = ironic, unsettling, complex
- **Minor key + sad lyrics** = deeply emotional, heavy
- **Major key + happy lyrics** = straightforward joy, celebration

The contrast between lyrical content and musical key creates emotional complexity that makes songs interesting.

## 2. Common Chord Progressions

### The Nashville Number System

Instead of letter names (C, Am, F, G), Nashville uses numbers (I, ii, iii, IV, V, vi, vii). This makes progressions key-independent.

| Number | Scale Degree | Quality | Feel |
|--------|-------------|---------|------|
| I | Root/Tonic | Major | Home, resolved, stable |
| ii | Second | Minor | Gentle tension, moving |
| iii | Third | Minor | Dreamy, floating |
| IV | Fourth | Major | Uplifting, hopeful, open |
| V | Fifth | Major | Tension, wants to resolve home |
| vi | Sixth | Minor | Emotional, melancholic |
| vii° | Seventh | Diminished | Very tense, rarely used alone |

### The Big Four Progressions

#### I - V - vi - IV (The "Pop Progression")
**Most used progression in modern music**
- In C: C - G - Am - F
- Feel: Uplifting, anthemic, slightly emotional
- Songs: "Let It Be," "No Woman No Cry," "With or Without You," "Someone Like You," "Despacito," "Take Me Home Country Roads"
- Lyric match: Hopeful, anthemic, universal themes. Perfect for choruses.

#### vi - IV - I - V (The "Sad/Emotional Progression")
Same chords as above, but starting on the vi (minor chord)
- In C: Am - F - C - G
- Feel: Melancholic, emotional, yearning
- Songs: "Save Tonight," "Africa," "Zombie," "Numb"
- Lyric match: Heartbreak, longing, emotional confession

#### I - IV - V - IV (Classic Rock)
- In C: C - F - G - F
- Feel: Driving, powerful, straightforward
- Songs: "La Bamba," "Twist and Shout," "Wild Thing"
- Lyric match: High energy, rebellious, simple and direct

#### I - vi - IV - V (The 50s Progression / Doo-Wop)
- In C: C - Am - F - G
- Feel: Nostalgic, sweet, romantic
- Songs: "Stand By Me," "Every Breath You Take," "Earth Angel"
- Lyric match: Romantic, innocent, timeless love

### Genre-Specific Progressions

#### 12-Bar Blues (I - I - I - I - IV - IV - I - I - V - IV - I - V)
```
| I  | I  | I  | I  |     ← 4 bars of home
| IV | IV | I  | I  |     ← tension then return
| V  | IV | I  | V  |     ← resolution and turnaround
```
- Feel: Foundational blues/rock feel, cyclical, storytelling
- Lyric match: The AAB blues verse structure maps perfectly onto this. Tell your trouble, repeat it, then deliver the punchline.

#### ii - V - I (Jazz Standard)
- In C: Dm7 - G7 - Cmaj7
- Feel: Sophisticated, smooth, resolving
- Most common progression in jazz. Often extended and modified.
- Lyric match: Clever, sophisticated, urbane

#### i - VII - VI - V (Andalusian Cadence / Flamenco)
- In Am: Am - G - F - E
- Feel: Spanish, dramatic, passionate, dark
- Songs: "Hit the Road Jack," "Sultans of Swing" (intro)
- Lyric match: Dramatic, passionate, storytelling

#### I - III - IV - iv (Major to Minor IV / "Creep" Progression)
- In G: G - B - C - Cm
- Feel: Hopeful turning melancholic, beautiful sadness
- Songs: "Creep" (Radiohead), "Space Oddity" (Bowie)
- Lyric match: Self-doubt, beauty in imperfection, longing

#### i - iv - v (Minor Blues/Rock)
- In Am: Am - Dm - Em
- Feel: Dark, brooding, tense
- Lyric match: Dark themes, inner turmoil, conflict

#### I - V - vi - iii - IV - I - IV - V (Pachelbel's Canon)
- The "graduation ceremony" progression
- Feel: Stately, building, ceremonial, emotional
- Songs: Countless pop songs draw from this

#### vi - V - IV - V (Axis of Awesome variant)
- Feel: Building hope from sadness
- Lyric match: Overcoming, rising from struggle

## 3. How Chord Changes Relate to Lyrical Sections

### Verse Chords
- Typically more movement, more chords
- Can be more complex/unexpected
- Support storytelling — the harmony "walks" with the narrative
- Often lower energy than the chorus

### Chorus Chords
- Simpler, more powerful, more resolved
- Often start on the I or IV chord for maximum impact
- The hook lands on the strongest chord (usually I)
- Higher energy, more "open" sound

### Pre-Chorus Chords
- Create TENSION that resolves into the chorus
- Often use the V chord or build chromatically
- The lyrical content should mirror this building tension
- "Something is about to happen" feeling

### Bridge Chords
- Should go somewhere the song hasn't been yet
- Often starts on a chord not used in verse/chorus
- The IV, ii, or iii chord are common starting points
- Creates a "new perspective" feeling
- Lyric content should also offer a new angle

### Practical Example

```
Verse (reflective): Am - F - C - G
  "Walking through the empty house..."

Pre-Chorus (building): F - G - Am - G
  "But something's telling me to stay..."

Chorus (release): C - G - Am - F
  "Because you're still here in everything..."

Bridge (new territory): Dm - Em - F - G
  "Maybe it's not about letting go..."
```

Notice how the verse feels introspective (starting minor), the pre-chorus builds tension, the chorus resolves with power (starting major), and the bridge explores new harmonic ground.

## 4. Tension and Resolution

### Musical Tension
- **V chord** creates the strongest pull back to I (called a "dominant resolution")
- **vii° chord** is extremely tense, almost always resolves to I
- **Suspended chords** (Csus4, Csus2) create a "waiting" feeling
- **7th chords** add color and mild tension
- **Diminished chords** create maximum tension
- **Chromatic movement** (moving by half-steps) builds anticipation

### Emotional Mapping

| Musical Moment | Lyrical Equivalent |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Tension (V, vii°) | Uncertainty, question, conflict, longing |
| Resolution (I) | Answer, relief, arrival, home, truth |
| Suspension (sus chords) | Waiting, hovering, unresolved emotions |
| Minor chord | Sadness, reflection, complexity |
| Major chord | Clarity, happiness, strength |
| Unexpected chord | Surprise, twist, revelation |

### Using Tension in Lyrics

When the music is tense (V chord, buildup):
- Ask questions
- Express doubt
- Build the conflict
- Use incomplete thoughts

When the music resolves (I chord, downbeat):
- Deliver the answer
- State the truth
- Complete the thought
- Land the hook

## 5. Key Changes / Modulation

### Types of Key Changes

**Truck Driver Modulation (Step Up)**
- Moving up a half or whole step, usually before the final chorus
- Creates a surge of energy and emotion
- Classic in power ballads and gospel
- Example: "I Will Always Love You" (Whitney Houston) — the key lifts for the final chorus

**Relative Major/Minor Switch**
- Moving between a major key and its relative minor (e.g., C major to A minor)
- Same notes, different emotional center
- Verse in minor → Chorus in relative major is very common
- Creates natural contrast without jarring the listener

**Parallel Major/Minor Switch**
- Same root note, different mode (C major to C minor)
- Dramatic emotional shift
- "There Goes My Hero" — alternates between major and minor

**Pivot Chord Modulation**
- A chord shared by both keys serves as a "bridge" between them
- Smooth, subtle key change
- Lyric tip: The pivot moment in the lyrics should align with the harmonic pivot

### Lyric Tips for Key Changes

- A key change up = escalation. Lyrics should intensify.
- A key change down = introspection or de-escalation (rare but powerful)
- Time the key change with a lyrical revelation or emotional peak
- The final chorus after a key change should feel like the DEFINITIVE statement of the song

## 6. Nashville Number System (Quick Reference)

The Nashville Number System lets you describe chord progressions without specifying a key. Musicians in Nashville use this on the fly.

### How It Works

Each number represents a chord built on that scale degree:

**In the key of C:**
| Number | Chord | Notes |
|--------|-------|-------|
| 1 | C | C-E-G |
| 2m | Dm | D-F-A |
| 3m | Em | E-G-B |
| 4 | F | F-A-C |
| 5 | G | G-B-D |
| 6m | Am | A-C-E |

**In the key of G:**
| Number | Chord | Notes |
|--------|-------|-------|
| 1 | G | G-B-D |
| 2m | Am | A-C-E |
| 3m | Bm | B-D-F# |
| 4 | C | C-E-G |
| 5 | D | D-F#-A |
| 6m | Em | E-G-B |

### Why Lyricists Should Know This

- You can communicate with musicians using numbers regardless of key
- "The chorus is 1-5-6m-4" works in any key
- Helps you understand song analysis and chord charts
- Many Suno guides and songwriting resources reference chord functions by number

### Common Nashville Shorthand

```
1  = major chord on root
1m = minor chord on root (parallel minor)
4  = major chord on fourth degree
5  = major chord on fifth degree
6m = minor chord on sixth degree
/  = bar line (2 chords per bar: "1 4 / 5 1")
```

## 7. Practical Chord-Lyric Pairing Guide

### For Happy/Uplifting Songs
- Progressions: I-IV-V, I-V-vi-IV, I-IV-I-V
- Key: Major
- Tempo: 110-130 BPM
- Lyrics: Celebration, love, empowerment, hope

### For Sad/Emotional Songs
- Progressions: vi-IV-I-V, i-VI-III-VII, i-iv-v
- Key: Minor (or major with sad lyrics for bittersweet)
- Tempo: 60-90 BPM
- Lyrics: Loss, regret, longing, heartbreak

### For Dark/Intense Songs
- Progressions: i-VII-VI-V, i-iv-VII-III, i-v-iv-VII
- Key: Minor
- Tempo: Variable (slow for doom, fast for aggression)
- Lyrics: Conflict, darkness, power, struggle

### For Nostalgic Songs
- Progressions: I-vi-IV-V, I-iii-IV-V, vi-IV-I-V
- Key: Major (with minor touches)
- Tempo: 85-110 BPM
- Lyrics: Memory, youth, looking back, bittersweet

### For Dance/Party Songs
- Progressions: I-V-vi-IV, vi-I-V-IV, I-IV (simple two-chord vamp)
- Key: Major (minor for dark dance)
- Tempo: 120-130 BPM
- Lyrics: Fun, freedom, night out, euphoria

### For Blues Songs
- Progressions: 12-bar blues (I-IV-V), i-iv-v
- Key: Major or minor (blues scale)
- Tempo: 70-120 BPM
- Lyrics: Hardship, love gone wrong, resilience, storytelling

## 8. Quick Theory Cheat Sheet

| Term | Meaning | Why It Matters |
|------|---------|----------------|
| Key | The "home" note/chord of the song | Determines the overall tonal center |
| Major | Happy/bright scale | Uplifting, resolved feel |
| Minor | Sad/dark scale | Emotional, tense feel |
| Chord | 3+ notes played together | Creates the harmonic backdrop for lyrics |
| Progression | Sequence of chords | The harmonic "story" under the lyrics |
| Resolution | Tension releasing to stability | Where the payoff line should land |
| Modulation | Changing key mid-song | Creates dramatic lift or shift |
| BPM | Beats per minute (tempo) | Controls energy level |
| Time signature | Beats per measure (4/4, 3/4, 6/8) | Determines rhythmic feel |
| Syncopation | Accent on unexpected beats | Creates groove and rhythmic interest |
| Dynamics | Loud vs. soft | Emotional contrast and drama |
