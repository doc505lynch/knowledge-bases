# Songwriting Fundamentals

A comprehensive reference for writing great lyrics across all genres.

---

## 1. Song Structures

### Verse-Chorus (Most Common in Pop/Rock)

```
Verse 1 → Chorus → Verse 2 → Chorus → Bridge → Chorus
```
The verse tells the story; the chorus delivers the emotional payoff. The bridge provides contrast before the final chorus.

### AABA (Tin Pan Alley / Standards)

```
A (verse) → A (verse, same melody) → B (bridge, contrasting) → A (verse)
```
Classic structure used in jazz standards and early pop. "Over the Rainbow," "Yesterday" by The Beatles.

### Verse-Chorus-Bridge

```
Verse 1 → Chorus → Verse 2 → Chorus → Bridge → Chorus → (Outro)
```
The workhorse of modern songwriting. Bridge typically appears only once, 2/3 through the song.

### Verse-Pre-Chorus-Chorus

```
Verse 1 → Pre-Chorus → Chorus → Verse 2 → Pre-Chorus → Chorus → Bridge → Chorus
```
The pre-chorus builds anticipation. Common in contemporary pop and rock.

### AAA (Strophic / Through-Composed Feel)

```
Verse 1 → Verse 2 → Verse 3 → Verse 4...
```
Same melody, different lyrics each time. Used in folk, blues, and storytelling songs. "Blowin' in the Wind."

### ABAB

```
Verse → Chorus → Verse → Chorus
```
Simple alternating structure without a bridge. Common in shorter songs and radio singles.

### Through-Composed

No repeating sections. Each part is new. Used in art songs, progressive rock, and some musical theater.

### Verse-Refrain

```
Verse 1 (ending with refrain line) → Verse 2 (ending with refrain line) → ...
```
The refrain is a single repeated line or phrase at the end of each verse, not a full chorus. "The Times They Are a-Changin'."

### Rondo (ABACABA)

```
A → B → A → C → A → B → A
```
The A section keeps returning. Common in classical and progressive arrangements.

### EDM / Drop Structure

```
Intro → Buildup → Drop → Breakdown → Buildup → Drop → Outro
```
Lyrics are sparse. The "drop" is the climactic instrumental section.

## 2. Rhyme Schemes

### Perfect Rhyme (Exact)
Words that match exactly in their ending sounds:
- love / above, fire / desire, night / light

### Slant Rhyme (Near Rhyme / Half Rhyme)
Words that almost rhyme — similar but not identical sounds:
- love / move, home / gone, time / mine
- Extremely useful for avoiding forced or cliche rhymes

### Internal Rhyme
Rhyme occurring within a single line:
- "I'm **pacing** and **racing** through the halls"

### Multi-Syllable Rhyme
Rhyming multiple syllables (valued highly in rap):
- "penny-less" / "nemesis", "eloquent" / "elegant"

### Common Rhyme Scheme Patterns

**AABB (Couplets)**
```
I walked along the morning shore (A)
And found a shell I'd seen before (A)
The waves came crashing, wild and free (B)
And whispered ancient songs to me (B)
```
Simple, punchy, good for hip-hop and children's songs.

**ABAB (Alternating)**
```
The city sleeps beneath the snow (A)
While streetlights flicker, pale and thin (B)
And somewhere in the dark below (A)
A broken heart is caving in (B)
```
Creates tension by making you wait for the rhyme resolution. Very common.

**ABCB (Common Meter / Ballad Rhyme)**
```
I drove all night to reach your door (A)
The rain was coming down (B)
I left behind the life I knew (C)
In a quiet little town (B)
```
Only lines 2 and 4 rhyme. Feels natural and conversational. Widely used in country, folk, and pop.

**AABA**
```
I held your hand beneath the stars (A)
And promised you the world was ours (A)
But promises are fragile things (B)
And dawn revealed a sky of scars (A)
```

**ABBA (Enclosed Rhyme)**
```
The fire burns inside my chest (A)
While shadows dance across the wall (B)
I hear your voice echo the hall (B)
And find no comfort, find no rest (A)
```
Wrapping structure creates a sense of enclosure.

**Free Verse (No Rhyme Scheme)**
No consistent rhyme pattern. Relies on rhythm, imagery, and line breaks for effect. Common in modern indie and art pop.

### Rhyme Tips for Songwriters

- **Avoid forced rhymes** — If a rhyme sounds unnatural, use a slant rhyme or rewrite the line
- **Rhyme density varies by genre** — Rap: very high; folk: moderate; indie: low to none
- **End-of-line rhymes aren't mandatory** — Internal rhymes and assonance can be equally powerful
- **Predictable rhymes kill songs** — If the listener can guess the rhyme, it's too obvious (fire/desire, heart/apart, love/above are heavily overused)
- **Use a rhyming dictionary** — But only as a starting point. RhymeZone.com is excellent
- **Assonance** (matching vowel sounds) and **consonance** (matching consonant sounds) are sophisticated alternatives to perfect rhyme

## 3. Meter and Rhythm in Lyrics

### Syllable Stress

English words have natural stress patterns:
- **a-BOVE** (iambic: da-DUM)
- **NE-ver** (trochaic: DUM-da)
- **un-der-STAND** (anapestic: da-da-DUM)
- **BEAU-ti-ful** (dactylic: DUM-da-da)

### Common Meters

**Iambic (da-DUM)** — Most natural in English speech
```
a-MAZED / by ALL / the THINGS / you DO
```

**Trochaic (DUM-da)** — More driving, insistent
```
NOTH-ing / ELSE could / MAKE me / STAY here
```

**Anapestic (da-da-DUM)** — Galloping, energetic
```
in the DARK / of the NIGHT / when the STARS / are a-LIGHT
```

### Matching Rhythm to Music

- Write lyrics that match the rhythmic feel of the melody
- Stressed syllables should fall on strong beats
- Unstressed syllables should fall on weak beats
- Mismatched stress creates an awkward, unsingable lyric
- Read lyrics aloud and tap the rhythm before finalizing

### Line Length

- Keep line lengths relatively consistent within sections
- Longer lines = more conversational
- Shorter lines = more punchy and dramatic
- Vary between sections for contrast (short verse lines, longer chorus)

## 4. Hook Writing

The hook is the most memorable part of your song. It's the phrase that sticks in the listener's head.

### Types of Hooks

1. **Melodic hook** — A catchy melody (often the chorus melody)
2. **Lyrical hook** — A memorable phrase or title line
3. **Rhythmic hook** — A distinctive rhythm pattern
4. **Instrumental hook** — A riff or lick (guitar riff in "Smoke on the Water")
5. **Production hook** — A sound or effect (the vocoder in "Around the World")

### Hook Placement

- **Title in the chorus** — The song title should usually appear in or as the chorus hook
- **First line of chorus** — Most impactful placement
- **Last line of chorus** — Leaves the hook ringing in the ear
- **Both first and last** — Maximum memorability

### Writing Better Hooks

- **Keep it short** — 3-7 words ideal for a hook phrase
- **Make it singable** — Easy vowels, natural melody
- **Use contrast** — The hook should feel different from the verse
- **Repeat it** — The more it repeats, the more it sticks
- **Use universal language** — Hooks should connect with everyone
- **Rhythmic interest** — Syncopation, unexpected rhythms
- **Melodic leap** — A jump in pitch grabs attention

### Great Hook Examples

- "I Will Always Love You" — Title IS the hook
- "Shake It Off" — Rhythmic repetition
- "Hey Jude" — Simple, singable, building
- "Billie Jean" — Rhythmic bass hook + lyrical hook
- "Bad Guy" — Minimal, quirky, rhythmically distinctive

## 5. Bridge Writing

The bridge provides contrast and keeps the song from feeling repetitive.

### Bridge Functions

1. **New perspective** — Look at the topic from a different angle
2. **Emotional shift** — Go deeper or pull back
3. **Musical contrast** — Different chord progression, melody, rhythm
4. **Resolution or revelation** — The "aha" moment
5. **Build to final chorus** — Escalate energy

### Bridge Techniques

- **Change the rhyme scheme** — If verses are ABAB, make the bridge AABB or free
- **Change the rhythm** — If verses are driving, make the bridge floating
- **Shift perspective** — From "I" to "you" or "we"
- **Ask a question** — Rhetorical questions create engagement
- **Use contrast** — If the song is loud, make the bridge quiet (and vice versa)
- **Keep it short** — 4-8 lines is typical. Don't overstay.

## 6. Storytelling in Songs

### Narrative Approaches

**Linear Narrative** — Story unfolds chronologically
- Verse 1: Setup
- Verse 2: Development
- Bridge: Turning point
- Final Chorus: Resolution

**Vignette/Snapshot** — Captures a single moment in vivid detail
- Each verse paints a different scene
- Chorus ties them together thematically

**Retrospective** — Looking back on past events
- Present tense chorus, past tense verses
- Nostalgia, regret, or gratitude

**Conversational** — Direct address to another person
- "You said...", "I told you...", "Remember when..."

**Montage** — Quick cuts between images/scenes
- Works well in up-tempo songs
- Each line is a snapshot

### Storytelling Tips

- **Specific beats general** — "A rusted '72 Ford" beats "an old car"
- **Sensory details** — What do you see, hear, smell, taste, feel?
- **Start in media res** — Begin in the middle of the action
- **Leave gaps** — Let the listener fill in details
- **One story per song** — Don't try to cover too much ground
- **The chorus is the thesis** — It should capture the song's core truth

## 7. Emotional Arc

Every great song takes the listener on an emotional journey.

### Basic Emotional Arcs

**Rising** — Starts quiet/uncertain, builds to triumphant
```
V1 (doubt) → V2 (determination) → Bridge (breakthrough) → Final Chorus (triumph)
```

**Falling** — Starts hopeful, descends into loss
```
V1 (happiness) → V2 (cracks show) → Bridge (realization) → Final Chorus (loss)
```

**V-Shape** — Down then up
```
V1 (pain) → V2 (deeper pain) → Bridge (turning point) → Final Chorus (hope)
```

**Inverted V** — Up then down
```
V1 (excitement) → Chorus (peak joy) → V2 (doubt creeping in) → Final Chorus (bittersweet)
```

**Circular** — Ends where it began
```
V1 (state A) → journey through change → Final Chorus (back to state A, but wiser)
```

### Building Emotional Intensity

- Each verse should escalate from the previous one
- The bridge is the emotional pivot point
- The final chorus should feel different from earlier choruses (even with same lyrics)
- Dynamic contrast (quiet vs. loud) amplifies emotion
- Specificity increases emotional impact

## 8. Point of View

### First Person ("I")
Most common in songwriting. Personal, intimate, relatable.
- "I can't help falling in love with you"
- Risk: can feel self-centered if overdone

### Second Person ("You")
Direct address. Creates intimacy or confrontation.
- "You're the one that I want"
- Great for love songs and accusations

### Third Person ("He/She/They")
Storytelling perspective. Creates distance, allows for narrative.
- "She's leaving home" (Beatles)
- "Eleanor Rigby" — observational

### First Person Plural ("We")
Communal, inclusive. Great for anthems.
- "We are the champions"
- "We will rock you"

### Shifting POV
Changing perspective between sections can be powerful:
- Verse: "I" (personal)
- Chorus: "We" (universal)
- Bridge: "You" (direct address)

## 9. Show Don't Tell

The single most important lyric writing principle.

### Telling (Weak)
```
I'm sad because you left me
I feel heartbroken and alone
```

### Showing (Strong)
```
Your coffee cup still on the counter
The bed is cold on your side
I'm talking to the walls again
And they've got nothing to reply
```

### Techniques for Showing

1. **Concrete images** — Replace abstract emotions with physical details
   - "Lonely" → "The house echoes when I walk"
   - "Happy" → "Dancing barefoot in the kitchen"
   - "Angry" → "Threw your picture at the wall"

2. **Actions over adjectives** — What does the character DO?
   - "I'm heartbroken" → "I drive past your house at 2 AM"

3. **Sensory language** — Engage the five senses
   - Sight: "amber streetlight glow"
   - Sound: "the click of the lock"
   - Touch: "cold sheets"
   - Smell: "your perfume on my pillow"
   - Taste: "whiskey burns going down"

4. **Metaphor and simile** — Compare emotions to tangible things
   - "My heart is a locked room"
   - "Love hit like a freight train"
   - "She's the sun I orbit around"

5. **Synecdoche** — A part representing the whole
   - "Your boots by the door" (representing the whole person)
   - "These four walls" (representing the whole house/life)

## 10. Imagery and Figurative Language

### Metaphor
Direct comparison: "Life is a highway"
- Extended metaphor: sustain one metaphor through the whole song
- Mixed metaphor: AVOID ("drowning in a sea of fire while the clock ticks")

### Simile
Comparison using "like" or "as": "My love is like a red, red rose"
- More conversational than metaphor
- Works well in country and folk

### Personification
Giving human qualities to non-human things:
- "The wind whispered your name"
- "The city never sleeps"
- "These walls remember everything"

### Hyperbole
Exaggeration for effect:
- "I'd walk a thousand miles"
- "You mean the world to me"
- "I've told you a million times"

### Symbolism
Objects representing abstract ideas:
- Rain = sadness, renewal, or cleansing
- Fire = passion, destruction, or transformation
- Roads = journey, choice, or freedom
- Doors = opportunity, closure, or transition
- Seasons = stages of life or relationship

### Juxtaposition
Placing contrasting ideas together:
- "Beautiful disaster"
- "Bitter sweet symphony"
- "The sound of silence"

### Alliteration
Repeated consonant sounds:
- "She sells seashells"
- Creates rhythm and memorability
- Use sparingly; overuse sounds forced

### Assonance
Repeated vowel sounds:
- "I feel the breeze beneath the trees"
- Creates internal musicality
- More subtle than rhyme

## 11. Practical Lyric Writing Process

### Step 1: Start with the Core Idea
- What is the song about? One sentence.
- What emotion do you want to evoke?
- Who is the audience?

### Step 2: Find the Title/Hook
- Write 20 potential titles
- Pick the most evocative one
- This becomes your chorus anchor

### Step 3: Build the Chorus First
- The chorus should be the strongest part
- It contains the hook and the emotional core
- Write the chorus before the verses

### Step 4: Write the Verses
- Each verse should advance the story or deepen the theme
- Verse 1: Set the scene
- Verse 2: Develop/complicate
- Keep verses fresh while maintaining consistent feel

### Step 5: Write the Bridge
- What hasn't been said yet?
- What's a different angle?
- Make it the emotional pivot

### Step 6: Edit Ruthlessly
- Cut anything that doesn't serve the song
- Replace generic words with specific ones
- Read it aloud — does it flow naturally?
- Sing it — do the stressed syllables land right?
- Kill your darlings — if a clever line doesn't serve the whole, cut it

## 12. Common Lyric Writing Pitfalls

1. **Cliches** — "Heart on fire," "tears like rain," "broken wings." Find fresh ways to express old feelings.
2. **Forced rhymes** — Never sacrifice meaning for a rhyme. Slant rhymes are your friend.
3. **Too abstract** — "Love is everything and nothing" says nothing. Get specific.
4. **Too many ideas** — One song, one theme. Save other ideas for other songs.
5. **Explaining the metaphor** — "Like a bird, I mean I feel free." Trust the listener.
6. **Filler words** — "Oh," "yeah," "baby" should be intentional, not space-fillers.
7. **Inconsistent tense** — Pick past, present, or future and stick with it (unless shifting intentionally).
8. **Overcrowded lines** — Leave room for the melody. Not every beat needs a syllable.
9. **No contrast** — If every section sounds the same, the song is monotonous.
10. **Writing for yourself, not the listener** — Inside references and private meanings don't translate.
