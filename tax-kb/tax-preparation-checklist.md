## 1. Documents Needed Checklist

Gather everything **before** you start. Missing a single form can mean an amended return later.

### Income Documents

- [ ] **W-2** -- Wages from each employer (due to you by Jan 31)
- [ ] **1099-NEC** -- Nonemployee compensation (freelance, contract work, $600+)
- [ ] **1099-MISC** -- Rents, royalties, prizes, other miscellaneous income
- [ ] **1099-INT** -- Interest income from banks, bonds ($10+)
- [ ] **1099-DIV** -- Dividend income from investments ($10+)
- [ ] **1099-B** -- Proceeds from broker/barter exchange (stock sales, crypto)
- [ ] **1099-R** -- Distributions from retirement accounts (IRA, 401k, pension)
- [ ] **1099-G** -- Government payments (unemployment compensation, state tax refunds)
- [ ] **1099-K** -- Payment card and third-party network transactions (thresholds below)
- [ ] **Schedule K-1** -- Income from partnerships, S-corps, estates, trusts
- [ ] **SSA-1099** -- Social Security benefits received
- [ ] **1099-SA** -- HSA or MSA distributions
- [ ] **1099-Q** -- Distributions from 529 plans or Coverdell ESAs
- [ ] **W-2G** -- Gambling winnings

#### 1099-K Reporting Thresholds (Important Changes)

| Tax Year | Threshold | Transaction Minimum |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| 2023 | $20,000 | 200 transactions |
| 2024 | $5,000 | No minimum |
| 2025 | $20,000 (restored by OBBBA) | 200 transactions |

> **Note:** Even if you do NOT receive a 1099-K, you must still report all taxable income. The IRS gets copies of every 1099 sent to you and matches them against your return.

### Deduction Documents

- [ ] **1098** -- Mortgage interest paid (from your lender)
- [ ] **1098-T** -- Tuition payments (from educational institution)
- [ ] **1098-E** -- Student loan interest paid ($600+)
- [ ] **Charitable contribution receipts** -- Cash and non-cash donations; written acknowledgment required for donations $250+
- [ ] **Medical and dental expense receipts** -- Only deductible above 7.5% of AGI
- [ ] **Property tax statements** -- From county/city assessor
- [ ] **State and local income tax records** -- Or general sales tax records if no state income tax (NM has state income tax)
- [ ] **Casualty and theft loss records** -- Only deductible if in a federally declared disaster area (2018-2025)

### Health Insurance Documents

- [ ] **1095-A** -- Health Insurance Marketplace Statement (REQUIRED if you had marketplace coverage -- reconciles Premium Tax Credit)
- [ ] **1095-B** -- Health Coverage (from insurer, verifies minimum essential coverage)
- [ ] **1095-C** -- Employer-Provided Health Insurance (from employer with 50+ employees)

> **Action item:** If you had marketplace insurance, you MUST file Form 8962 to reconcile your Premium Tax Credit, even if you would not otherwise need to file.

### Identity & Prior Year Information

- [ ] **SSN or ITIN** for every person on the return (you, spouse, all dependents)
- [ ] **Date of birth** for all household members
- [ ] **Prior year AGI** -- Needed for e-file identity verification (on Line 11 of your prior year 1040)
- [ ] **IP PIN** -- Identity Protection PIN if assigned by the IRS (6-digit number, new one issued each year)
- [ ] **Bank account and routing numbers** -- For direct deposit of refund
- [ ] **Copy of prior year return** -- Useful reference for consistency

### Business / Self-Employment Records

- [ ] **Income records** -- Invoices, payment records, 1099-NEC forms received
- [ ] **Expense records** -- Categorized by type (supplies, advertising, insurance, etc.)
- [ ] **Mileage log** -- Date, destination, business purpose, miles driven for each trip
  - 2023 rate: 65.5 cents/mile
  - 2024 rate: 67 cents/mile
  - 2025 rate: 70 cents/mile
- [ ] **Home office measurements** -- Square footage of dedicated office space AND total home square footage (for simplified method: $5/sq ft up to 300 sq ft = max $1,500)
- [ ] **Asset purchase records** -- Date, cost, description of equipment/property bought for business (for depreciation or Section 179)
- [ ] **Estimated tax payment records** -- Dates and amounts of all quarterly payments made
- [ ] **Health insurance premiums** -- If self-employed and paying own premiums
- [ ] **Retirement plan contributions** -- SEP-IRA, SIMPLE IRA, Solo 401(k)

## 2. Filing Status Decision Tree

Your filing status determines your standard deduction, tax bracket thresholds, and credit eligibility. Choose carefully -- it can mean thousands of dollars.

### Step-by-Step Decision Process

```
Were you married on December 31 of the tax year?
|
+-- YES --> Did your spouse die during the tax year?
|           |
|           +-- YES --> You can file Married Filing Jointly for that year
|           +-- NO  --> Choose: Married Filing Jointly (MFJ) or Married Filing Separately (MFS)
|
+-- NO  --> Did your spouse die in the prior 1-2 years AND do you have a dependent child?
            |
            +-- YES --> You may qualify as Qualifying Surviving Spouse
            +-- NO  --> Do you have a qualifying dependent AND pay more than half the
                        cost of keeping up your home?
                        |
                        +-- YES --> Head of Household (HoH)
                        +-- NO  --> Single
```

### Single vs Head of Household

**Head of Household** gives you a larger standard deduction and wider tax brackets. You qualify if ALL of the following are true:

1. You are unmarried (or "considered unmarried") on Dec 31
2. You paid more than half the cost of keeping up your home for the year
3. A qualifying person lived with you for more than half the year (exceptions for parents)

**"Considered unmarried" rule:** You can file HoH even if legally married if:
- You lived apart from your spouse for the last 6 months of the year
- You paid more than half the cost of your home
- Your home was the main home for your child (whom you can claim as a dependent) for more than half the year
- You file a separate return

### Married Filing Jointly vs Separately

**Default choice: MFJ is almost always better.** MFS costs more in taxes in most cases.

**When MFS might make sense:**

- **Income-Driven Repayment (IDR) for student loans** -- MFS can lower the payment calculation by using only one spouse's income (but check: some IDR plans now use household income regardless)
- **Spouse tax liability concerns** -- If you suspect your spouse is underreporting income or you do not want to be jointly liable for their tax debt
- **Medical expense deduction** -- Lower AGI for one spouse can make it easier to exceed the 7.5% floor
- **State tax reasons** -- Some community property states create complications

**What you LOSE with MFS:**
- Earned Income Tax Credit (completely disallowed)
- Child and Dependent Care Credit (completely disallowed)
- Education credits (completely disallowed)
- Student loan interest deduction (completely disallowed)
- Lower IRA contribution phaseout ranges
- Higher tax rates at every bracket

### Qualifying Surviving Spouse

You can use this status for **two years** after the year your spouse died if:
- You could have filed jointly the year your spouse died
- You did not remarry before the end of the tax year
- You have a dependent child who lived with you all year
- You paid more than half the cost of keeping up your home

This gives you the same standard deduction and brackets as MFJ.

## 3. Itemize vs Standard Deduction Decision

### Standard Deduction Amounts

| Filing Status | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|--------------|------|------|------|
| Single | $13,850 | $14,600 | $15,000 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $27,700 | $29,200 | $30,000 |
| Married Filing Separately | $13,850 | $14,600 | $15,000 |
| Head of Household | $20,800 | $21,900 | $22,500 |

**Additional standard deduction (age 65+ or blind):**

| Situation | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|-----------|------|------|------|
| Single or HoH, 65+ or blind | +$1,850 | +$1,950 | +$2,000 |
| Married (either status), 65+ or blind | +$1,500 | +$1,550 | +$1,600 |

> If you are 65+ AND blind, the additional amount doubles.

### The Decision: When to Itemize

**Itemize when your total Schedule A deductions exceed the standard deduction.** The main itemized deductions are:

1. **State and local taxes (SALT)** -- Income tax (or sales tax) + property tax, capped at **$10,000** ($5,000 if MFS)
2. **Mortgage interest** -- On up to $750,000 of acquisition debt ($375,000 MFS)
3. **Charitable contributions** -- Cash up to 60% of AGI; appreciated stock up to 30% of AGI
4. **Medical expenses** -- Only the amount exceeding 7.5% of AGI

**Quick test worksheet:**

```
SALT (state income tax + property tax, max $10,000): $________
Mortgage interest (from 1098):                       $________
Charitable donations:                                $________
Medical expenses above 7.5% of AGI:                  $________
                                              TOTAL: $________

Is TOTAL > your standard deduction?
  YES --> Itemize
  NO  --> Take the standard deduction
```

### Bunching Strategy

If your itemized deductions are close to but below the standard deduction, consider **bunching** -- concentrating two years of charitable giving into one year.

**Example (Single filer, 2025):**
- Normal annual giving: $5,000
- SALT: $10,000
- Mortgage interest: $3,000
- Normal total: $18,000 (above $15,000 standard deduction -- itemize)

But if mortgage interest drops and total is $14,500:
- Year 1: Give $10,000 (two years' worth), itemize at $23,000
- Year 2: Give $0, take standard deduction of $15,000
- Two-year benefit: $38,000 in deductions vs. $30,000 if standard both years

**Donor-Advised Fund (DAF):** Fund the DAF in a bunching year for the full deduction, then distribute grants to charities over time.

### SALT Cap Impact

The $10,000 SALT cap (introduced by TCJA in 2018) remains in effect through 2025. This cap is the single biggest reason many former itemizers now take the standard deduction. If your state/local taxes alone exceed $10,000, you are leaving money on the table that cannot be deducted.

## 4. Common Filing Mistakes

These are the errors that delay refunds, trigger IRS notices, or cost you money.

### Identity and Signature Errors
- [ ] **Wrong or missing SSN/ITIN** -- Triple-check every SSN on the return. Transposed digits are the #1 cause of rejected e-files.
- [ ] **Not signing the return** -- Both spouses must sign an MFJ return. Electronic returns use a PIN or prior year AGI.
- [ ] **Wrong prior year AGI for e-file** -- If the IRS rejects your e-file for AGI mismatch, use $0 if you did not file the prior year, or check your IRS transcript.

### Income Errors
- [ ] **Missing income** -- The IRS receives copies of every W-2, 1099, and K-1 sent to you. If you leave one off, you WILL get a CP2000 notice (usually 12-18 months later). Common culprits: small bank interest, forgotten side gigs, state tax refunds.
- [ ] **Reporting gross 1099-K instead of net profit** -- A 1099-K reports gross payment volume. If you had expenses, report the gross on Schedule C and deduct expenses to arrive at net profit.
- [ ] **Forgetting to report cryptocurrency** -- All crypto sales, exchanges, and payments for goods/services are taxable events. The IRS specifically asks about this on page 1 of Form 1040.

### Filing Status and Dependent Errors
- [ ] **Wrong filing status** -- Especially missing Head of Household when you qualify (worth $2,000+ in tax savings over Single)
- [ ] **Claiming wrong dependents** -- Cannot claim a child who provided more than half their own support, or a child claimed by the other parent
- [ ] **Both parents claiming same child** -- IRS tie-breaker rules apply (see Section 5)

### Math and Banking Errors
- [ ] **Math errors** -- E-filing eliminates most of these, but manual overrides can introduce them
- [ ] **Wrong bank routing/account number** -- If the IRS cannot deposit your refund, they mail a check (adding weeks). Double-check the numbers; some banks have different routing numbers for direct deposit vs. checks.
- [ ] **Wrong tax amount from tax table** -- If filing on paper, use the correct column for your filing status

### Deduction and Credit Errors
- [ ] **Not reconciling Form 1095-A** -- If you had marketplace insurance, failing to file Form 8962 will hold up your refund indefinitely
- [ ] **Missing the Earned Income Credit** -- Many eligible filers do not claim it, leaving $500-$7,000+ on the table
- [ ] **Forgetting estimated tax payments** -- If you made quarterly payments, make sure every one is listed on Form 1040

## 5. Dependent Rules

### Qualifying Child (All Five Tests Must Be Met)

| Test | Requirement |
|------|------------|
| **Relationship** | Your child, stepchild, foster child, sibling, step-sibling, or descendant of any of these |
| **Age** | Under 19 at end of year, OR under 24 if a full-time student, OR any age if permanently and totally disabled |
| **Residency** | Lived with you for more than half the year (temporary absences like school count as living with you) |
| **Support** | The child did NOT provide more than half of their own support for the year |
| **Joint Return** | The child did not file a joint return for the year (exception: filed only to claim a refund) |

### Qualifying Relative (All Four Tests Must Be Met)

| Test | Requirement |
|------|------------|
| **Not a Qualifying Child** | Cannot be the qualifying child of you or any other taxpayer |
| **Relationship or Member of Household** | Related to you (parent, sibling, aunt/uncle, in-law, etc.) OR lived with you all year as a member of your household |
| **Gross Income** | Gross income less than $4,700 (2023), $5,050 (2024), $5,200 (2025) |
| **Support** | You provided more than half of the person's total support for the year |

### Tie-Breaker Rules (When Multiple People Can Claim the Same Child)

Applied in this order:
1. **Parent vs. non-parent** -- Parent wins
2. **Two parents who do not file jointly** -- The parent the child lived with longer wins
3. **Equal time with both parents** -- The parent with the higher AGI wins
4. **Non-parent vs. non-parent** -- The person with the higher AGI wins

### Special Rules for Divorced/Separated Parents

By default, the **custodial parent** (the one the child lives with more nights) claims the child. However:

- The custodial parent can release the claim using **Form 8332** (Release/Revocation of Release of Claim to Exemption for Child by Custodial Parent)
- Form 8332 transfers ONLY the Child Tax Credit and dependency exemption -- NOT Head of Household status, EITC, or Child and Dependent Care Credit (those always stay with the custodial parent)
- Can be released for a single year or multiple years
- A divorce decree alone does NOT transfer the claim -- Form 8332 (or equivalent written declaration) is required

## 6. Above-the-Line Deductions (Schedule 1, Part II)

These reduce your AGI directly, which in turn can increase eligibility for other deductions and credits. You get these even if you take the standard deduction.

### Educator Expenses (Line 11)
- Up to **$300** per qualifying educator ($600 if both spouses are educators filing jointly)
- Must be a K-12 teacher, instructor, counselor, principal, or aide who works at least 900 hours per school year
- Covers classroom supplies, books, computer equipment, supplementary materials, COVID protective items

### HSA Contributions (Line 13)

| | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|--|------|------|------|
| Self-only | $3,850 | $4,150 | $4,300 |
| Family | $7,750 | $8,300 | $8,550 |
| Catch-up (55+) | +$1,000 | +$1,000 | +$1,000 |

- Must have a qualifying High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP)
- Cannot be enrolled in Medicare
- Cannot be claimed as a dependent on someone else's return
- Contributions are deductible, growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free (triple tax advantage)

### Self-Employment Tax Deduction (Line 15)
- Deduct **50%** of your self-employment tax (the "employer half")
- Calculated on Schedule SE
- This is automatic -- not a choice; if you owe SE tax, you get this deduction

### Self-Employed Health Insurance (Line 17)
- Deduct 100% of health, dental, and long-term care insurance premiums for yourself, your spouse, and dependents
- You must have net self-employment income (the deduction cannot exceed your net SE income)
- Cannot claim for any month you were eligible for an employer-subsidized plan (yours or your spouse's)

### Student Loan Interest (Line 21)
- Deduct up to **$2,500** of student loan interest paid
- Phaseout (2025): MAGI $80,000-$95,000 (Single), $165,000-$195,000 (MFJ)
- Not available if MFS
- The loan must be for you, your spouse, or a dependent

### IRA Contributions (Line 20)

| | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|--|------|------|------|
| Under 50 | $6,500 | $7,000 | $7,000 |
| 50 and over | $7,500 | $8,000 | $8,000 |

- **Traditional IRA deduction** depends on whether you (or your spouse) are covered by a workplace retirement plan
- If covered by a workplace plan, deduction phases out at certain MAGI levels
- **Roth IRA** contributions are never deductible (but qualified withdrawals are tax-free)

### Moving Expenses (Line 14)
- **Military only** (2018-2025): Active duty members of the Armed Forces moving due to a military order for a permanent change of station
- Civilians cannot deduct moving expenses through 2025

### Alimony Paid (Line 18a)
- Deductible ONLY for divorce/separation agreements executed **before January 1, 2019**
- Post-2018 agreements: alimony is not deductible by the payer and not taxable to the recipient
- Must include the recipient's SSN on your return

## 7. Key Tax Credits

Credits reduce your tax dollar-for-dollar (much more valuable than deductions).

### Child Tax Credit

| | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|--|------|------|------|
| Credit per child | $2,000 | $2,000 | $2,000 |
| Refundable portion (ACTC) | up to $1,600 | up to $1,700 | up to $1,700 |
| Child age limit | Under 17 | Under 17 | Under 17 |
| Income phaseout start (MFJ) | $400,000 | $400,000 | $400,000 |
| Income phaseout start (other) | $200,000 | $200,000 | $200,000 |

- Requires a valid SSN for the child (not ITIN)
- Earned income must be at least $2,500 for the refundable portion

### Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

**Maximum credit amounts (2025):**

| Qualifying Children | Max Credit | Max AGI (Single/HoH) | Max AGI (MFJ) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | ~$632 | ~$19,104 | ~$26,214 |
| 1 | ~$4,213 | ~$50,434 | ~$57,554 |
| 2 | ~$6,960 | ~$57,310 | ~$64,430 |
| 3+ | ~$7,830 | ~$61,555 | ~$68,675 |

- **Not available if MFS**
- Investment income must be $11,600 or less (2025)
- Must have earned income (wages, self-employment)
- One of the most commonly missed credits

### Education Credits

**American Opportunity Credit (AOTC):**
- Up to $2,500 per eligible student (first 4 years of postsecondary education)
- 40% refundable (up to $1,000 back even if you owe no tax)
- MAGI phaseout: $80,000-$90,000 (Single), $160,000-$180,000 (MFJ)

**Lifetime Learning Credit:**
- Up to $2,000 per return (not per student)
- Not refundable
- No limit on years you can claim
- MAGI phaseout: $80,000-$90,000 (Single), $160,000-$180,000 (MFJ) -- for 2023+

### Energy Credits

**Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit (2023-2025):**
- 30% of costs for qualifying improvements
- Annual limits: $1,200 total (doors, windows, insulation, etc.) + separate $2,000 for heat pumps/water heaters
- Resets each year -- you can claim it every year you make improvements

**Residential Clean Energy Credit (2023-2025):**
- 30% of costs for solar panels, solar water heaters, battery storage, wind turbines, geothermal
- No annual cap
- **Important:** Under the OBBBA, most IRA clean energy credits phase out after 2025. If you are planning solar or EV purchases, act before year-end 2025.

**Clean Vehicle Credits:**
- New EV: Up to $7,500 (must meet assembly and battery sourcing requirements)
- Used EV: 30% of sale price, up to $4,000 (price must be $25,000 or less; buyer MAGI limit applies)
- **Note:** Purchase agreements must be signed before September 30, 2025 to qualify under current rules.

## 8. Tax Planning Strategies

### Maximize Retirement Contributions

| Account | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|---------|------|------|------|
| 401(k)/403(b) under 50 | $22,500 | $23,000 | $23,500 |
| 401(k)/403(b) 50+ catch-up | +$7,500 | +$7,500 | +$7,500 |
| 401(k)/403(b) ages 60-63 catch-up | N/A | N/A | +$11,250 |
| Traditional/Roth IRA under 50 | $6,500 | $7,000 | $7,000 |
| Traditional/Roth IRA 50+ | $7,500 | $8,000 | $8,000 |
| SEP-IRA | 25% of comp, up to $66,000 | 25% of comp, up to $69,000 | 25% of comp, up to $70,000 |
| SIMPLE IRA under 50 | $15,500 | $16,000 | $16,500 |
| Solo 401(k) total | $66,000 | $69,000 | $70,000 |

**Action:** If you have not maxed out your 401(k) or IRA for the year, you have until:
- 401(k): December 31 of the tax year
- IRA: Tax filing deadline (typically April 15 of the following year)
- SEP-IRA: Tax filing deadline including extensions

### Tax-Loss Harvesting

- Sell investments at a loss to offset capital gains
- Up to $3,000 of net capital losses can offset ordinary income per year ($1,500 if MFS)
- Excess losses carry forward to future years indefinitely
- **Wash sale rule:** Cannot buy substantially identical securities within 30 days before or after the sale
- Best done in November/December to lock in losses for the current tax year

### Roth Conversion Planning

- Convert traditional IRA/401(k) funds to Roth in low-income years (job change, sabbatical, early retirement)
- Pay tax now at a lower rate; future growth and withdrawals are tax-free
- No income limit on conversions (unlike Roth contributions)
- **Strategy:** Fill up lower tax brackets with conversions. Example: If your taxable income is $40,000 (Single, 2025), you are in the 12% bracket up to $48,475. Convert $8,475 at the 12% rate.
- Cannot undo (recharacterize) Roth conversions after 2017

### Timing Income and Deductions

- **Defer income:** If you expect to be in a lower bracket next year, delay invoicing (self-employed) or defer bonuses
- **Accelerate deductions:** Prepay state estimated taxes, make January's mortgage payment in December, bunch charitable giving
- **Accelerate income:** If you expect to be in a higher bracket next year, take income now

### Estimated Tax Payment Planning

Avoid underpayment penalties by paying the lesser of:
- 90% of current year's tax liability, OR
- 100% of prior year's tax liability (110% if prior year AGI exceeded $150,000)

### Charitable Giving Strategies

- **Donor-Advised Fund (DAF):** Make a large contribution in one year (get full deduction), distribute to charities over multiple years
- **Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD):** If you are 70 1/2 or older, donate up to $105,000 (2024; indexed for inflation) directly from your IRA to charity. Counts toward RMD, excluded from income entirely. Better than deducting because it reduces AGI.
- **Appreciated stock donations:** Donate long-term appreciated stock directly to charity. Deduct the full market value, avoid capital gains tax entirely.
- **Bunching:** Combine multiple years of giving into one year to exceed the standard deduction (see Section 3)

### Education Funding Strategies

- **529 Plan:** Contributions grow tax-free; withdrawals for qualified education expenses are tax-free. Some states offer a state tax deduction. Can now use up to $10,000/year for K-12 tuition.
- **Coverdell ESA:** Up to $2,000/year, broader qualifying expenses than 529
- **AOTC/LLC:** Coordinate with 529 withdrawals -- cannot double-dip on the same expenses

### Energy Credit Planning

- **Before year-end 2025:** Install solar panels, battery storage, or geothermal for the 30% Residential Clean Energy Credit (no cap). This credit is being phased out.
- **Heat pumps and efficient HVAC:** Up to $2,000 credit per year through 2025
- **EV purchase:** Must meet assembly and sourcing requirements; check fueleconomy.gov for eligible vehicles. Consider point-of-sale transfer to dealer for immediate savings.

## 9. Self-Employment Tax Planning

### Self-Employment Tax Rates

| Component | Rate | Wage Base |
|-----------|------|-----------|
| Social Security | 12.4% | $160,200 (2023), $168,600 (2024), $176,100 (2025) |
| Medicare | 2.9% | No limit |
| Additional Medicare (high earners) | 0.9% | On SE income above $200,000 Single / $250,000 MFJ |
| **Total SE Tax** | **15.3%** | On first $160,200-$176,100 (year dependent) |

> You only pay SE tax on 92.35% of net self-employment earnings (the IRS multiplies your net income by 0.9235 before applying the 15.3% rate). Then you deduct 50% of the SE tax from your AGI.

### Estimated Payment Worksheet (Simplified)

```
1. Expected net self-employment income:           $__________
2. Multiply by 0.9235:                            $__________
3. Multiply by 0.153 (SE tax):                    $__________
4. Expected income tax on all income:              $__________
5. Total expected tax (line 3 + line 4):           $__________
6. Subtract withholding from W-2 jobs (if any):   -$__________
7. Subtract credits:                              -$__________
8. Amount due through estimated payments:          $__________
9. Divide by 4 = quarterly payment:                $__________
```

### Quarterly Payment Schedule

**Tax Year 2025:**

| Period | Due Date |
|--------|----------|
| Q1: Jan 1 - Mar 31 | April 15, 2025 |
| Q2: Apr 1 - May 31 | June 16, 2025 |
| Q3: Jun 1 - Aug 31 | September 15, 2025 |
| Q4: Sep 1 - Dec 31 | January 15, 2026 |

**Tax Year 2024:**

| Period | Due Date |
|--------|----------|
| Q1: Jan 1 - Mar 31 | April 15, 2024 |
| Q2: Apr 1 - May 31 | June 17, 2024 |
| Q3: Jun 1 - Aug 31 | September 16, 2024 |
| Q4: Sep 1 - Dec 31 | January 15, 2025 |

**Payment methods:** IRS Direct Pay (irs.gov/directpay), EFTPS (eftps.gov), credit/debit card (fees apply), check with Form 1040-ES voucher.

> **Tip:** If you file your annual return and pay the full balance by February 1 of the following year, you can skip the Q4 payment.

### S-Corp Election Considerations

If your net self-employment income consistently exceeds ~$50,000-$60,000, an S-Corp election may save on self-employment tax:

**How it works:**
1. Form an LLC (or already have one)
2. Elect S-Corp tax treatment (Form 2553, due by March 15 for current year)
3. Pay yourself a "reasonable salary" (subject to payroll taxes)
4. Take remaining profits as distributions (NOT subject to SE tax)

**Example:**
- Net income: $120,000
- Reasonable salary: $70,000 (payroll tax: ~$10,710)
- Distribution: $50,000 (no SE tax)
- **Savings:** ~$7,650 in SE tax vs. paying 15.3% on the full $120,000

**Costs and requirements:**
- Must run payroll (cost: $500-$2,000/year for a payroll service)
- Must file Form 1120-S (S-Corp tax return) + Schedule K-1
- Salary must be "reasonable" for your industry and role -- IRS scrutinizes salaries that are too low
- Does not make sense if your net income is low (payroll costs eat the savings)

### Record-Keeping Requirements

- **Keep records for at least 3 years** from the date you filed the return (or 2 years from the date you paid the tax, whichever is later)
- **Keep records for 6 years** if you underreported income by more than 25%
- **Keep records forever** for: real estate purchase/sale documents, retirement account contributions (basis tracking), and any year you did not file a return

**What to keep:**
- All 1099s received and issued
- Bank and credit card statements
- Receipts for all deductible expenses
- Mileage log (contemporaneous -- not reconstructed at year-end)
- Home office documentation
- Asset purchase records and depreciation schedules
- Contracts with clients
- Health insurance premium statements

## Filing Checklist -- Final Steps

Before you hit "submit" or mail your return:

- [ ] All income documents accounted for (compare to prior year -- did you miss any sources?)
- [ ] Filing status is optimal (not just default)
- [ ] Claimed all eligible dependents
- [ ] Chose the better of standard deduction vs. itemizing
- [ ] Claimed all eligible credits (EITC, CTC, education, energy)
- [ ] Entered estimated tax payments made during the year
- [ ] Reconciled Form 1095-A if applicable
- [ ] SSNs are correct for everyone on the return
- [ ] Bank routing and account numbers verified for direct deposit
- [ ] Return is signed (both spouses for MFJ)
- [ ] Made a copy of the complete return for your records
- [ ] If you owe: payment is included or scheduled
- [ ] If extending: payment of estimated amount due is still required by April 15
