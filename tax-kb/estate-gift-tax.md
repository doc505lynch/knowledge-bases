# Estate and Gift Tax (2023, 2024, 2025)

> **Sources:** IRS Rev. Proc. 2022-38, 2023-34, 2024-40; IRC Sections 2001, 2010, 2503, 2631; IRS Publication 559

---

## Estate Tax Exemption (Basic Exclusion Amount)

| Year | Per Individual | Per Married Couple (with portability) |
|------|---------------|--------------------------------------|
| 2023 | $12,920,000 | $25,840,000 |
| 2024 | $13,610,000 | $27,220,000 |
| 2025 | $13,990,000 | $27,980,000 |

### Estate Tax Rate
- A flat **40%** tax rate applies to the taxable estate amount exceeding the exemption
- Technically, the estate tax has graduated rates from 18% to 40%, but the unified credit effectively exempts amounts up to the basic exclusion

### Portability
- A surviving spouse can use the deceased spouse's unused exclusion amount (DSUE)
- Must elect portability on a timely filed estate tax return (Form 706), even if no estate tax is owed
- IRS has granted a simplified method for late portability elections (Rev. Proc. 2022-32): estate can file Form 706 within 5 years of the decedent's death solely for portability

## Gift Tax Annual Exclusion

| Year | Per Donee (Per Donor) |
|------|-----------------------|
| 2023 | $17,000 |
| 2024 | $18,000 |
| 2025 | $19,000 |

### Key Rules
- Each donor can give each recipient up to the annual exclusion with NO gift tax or reporting
- **Gift-splitting:** Married couples can elect to split gifts, effectively doubling the exclusion to $34,000 (2023), $36,000 (2024), $38,000 (2025) per recipient
- Must file Form 709 if gift-splitting is elected, even if total gifts are under the exclusion
- Annual exclusion applies to "present interest" gifts only (not future interests)
- Gifts to non-citizen spouses: special annual exclusion of $175,000 (2023), $185,000 (2024), $190,000 (2025)

### Gifts Not Subject to Gift Tax (Unlimited)
- Payments made directly to educational institutions for tuition (not room/board)
- Payments made directly to medical care providers
- Gifts to political organizations
- Gifts to qualifying charities (unlimited charitable deduction)
- Gifts to U.S. citizen spouse (unlimited marital deduction)

## Gift Tax Lifetime Exemption

The lifetime gift tax exemption is **unified** with the estate tax exemption (same basic exclusion amount):

| Year | Lifetime Gift Tax Exemption |
|------|-----------------------------|
| 2023 | $12,920,000 |
| 2024 | $13,610,000 |
| 2025 | $13,990,000 |

### How It Works
- Gifts exceeding the annual exclusion reduce the lifetime exemption dollar-for-dollar
- No gift tax is owed until the cumulative lifetime taxable gifts exceed the exemption
- At death, the remaining exemption shelters the estate from estate tax
- Gift tax rate: 40% on amounts exceeding the lifetime exemption

### Gift Tax Return (Form 709)
- Due: April 15 of the year following the gift (extensions available via Form 4868 or Form 8892)
- Required when: gifts to any one person exceed the annual exclusion, gift-splitting is elected, gifts of future interests, gifts to certain trusts

## Generation-Skipping Transfer (GST) Tax

| Year | GST Exemption |
|------|---------------|
| 2023 | $12,920,000 |
| 2024 | $13,610,000 |
| 2025 | $13,990,000 |

### Key Rules
- GST tax rate: **40%** (flat rate equal to the maximum estate tax rate)
- Applies to transfers to "skip persons" (generally grandchildren or persons 37.5+ years younger than transferor)
- Three types of GST transfers:
  1. **Direct skip:** Gift or bequest directly to a skip person
  2. **Taxable distribution:** Distribution from a trust to a skip person
  3. **Taxable termination:** When a trust interest terminates and only skip persons remain
- GST exemption is separate from the estate/gift tax exemption but equals the same amount
- Can be allocated to transfers during life or at death
- Portability does NOT apply to the GST exemption (unlike the estate tax exemption)

## TCJA Sunset and 2026 Implications

### Original TCJA Sunset Scenario
Under the original TCJA (2017), the doubled exemption amounts were scheduled to sunset after December 31, 2025, reverting to approximately $7 million (adjusted for inflation) in 2026.

### "One, Big, Beautiful Bill" (Signed July 4, 2025)
- **Estate tax exemption for 2026:** Increased to $15,000,000 per individual ($30,000,000 per married couple)
- This effectively made the higher exemption amounts permanent and increased them further
- The $15 million amount will be indexed for inflation in future years

### Anti-Clawback Rule (Important)
- IRS finalized regulations (T.D. 9884) confirming that gifts made during the high-exemption period will NOT be "clawed back" if the exemption later decreases
- This was relevant during the TCJA sunset uncertainty and remains protective

### Planning Considerations
- Even with the increased exemption, wealthy individuals should consider:
  - Irrevocable trusts for asset protection and estate tax reduction
  - Annual gifting programs to reduce the taxable estate
  - Life insurance trusts (ILITs) for estate liquidity
  - Grantor retained annuity trusts (GRATs)
  - Qualified personal residence trusts (QPRTs)
  - Family limited partnerships (FLPs)
  - Charitable remainder trusts (CRTs) and charitable lead trusts (CLTs)

## Basis Step-Up at Death

| Detail | 2023-2025 |
|--------|-----------|
| General rule | Inherited assets receive a "stepped-up" basis to fair market value at date of death |
| Alternative valuation | Executor may elect to value estate 6 months after death (Section 2032) |
| Community property | Both halves receive step-up in community property states (including NM) |
| Effect | Eliminates capital gains tax on appreciation during decedent's lifetime |

**Important:** There have been proposals to eliminate or limit the step-up in basis, but none have been enacted for 2023-2025.

## State Estate Taxes

While this knowledge base focuses on federal taxes, note that several states impose their own estate or inheritance taxes with much lower exemptions than the federal level. States with estate taxes include: Connecticut, Hawaii, Illinois, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Minnesota, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Vermont, Washington, and the District of Columbia.

**New Mexico does NOT have a state estate tax or inheritance tax.**
