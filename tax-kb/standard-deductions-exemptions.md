# Standard Deductions and Exemptions (2023, 2024, 2025)

> **Sources:** IRS Rev. Proc. 2022-38, Rev. Proc. 2023-34, Rev. Proc. 2024-40, IRS Publication 501

---

## Standard Deduction Amounts

| Filing Status | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|---------------|------|------|------|
| Single | $13,850 | $14,600 | $15,000 |
| Married Filing Jointly (MFJ) | $27,700 | $29,200 | $30,000 |
| Married Filing Separately (MFS) | $13,850 | $14,600 | $15,000 |
| Head of Household (HoH) | $20,800 | $21,900 | $22,500 |
| Qualifying Surviving Spouse | $27,700 | $29,200 | $30,000 |

---

## Additional Standard Deduction (Age 65+ or Blind)

Taxpayers who are age 65 or older, or who are blind, receive an additional standard deduction amount. If both 65+ AND blind, the additional amount is doubled.

### Married Filers (MFJ, MFS, Qualifying Surviving Spouse)

| Year | Additional Amount (per qualifying condition) |
|------|---------------------------------------------|
| 2023 | $1,500 |
| 2024 | $1,550 |
| 2025 | $1,600 |

### Unmarried Filers (Single, HoH)

| Year | Additional Amount (per qualifying condition) |
|------|---------------------------------------------|
| 2023 | $1,850 |
| 2024 | $1,950 |
| 2025 | $2,000 |

**Example:** A single filer who is 65+ and blind in 2025 gets $15,000 + $2,000 + $2,000 = $19,000 standard deduction.

## Personal Exemptions

Personal exemptions remain at **$0** for tax years 2018-2025 under the TCJA. The exemption amount was $4,050 in 2017, the last year it was available.

**Note:** While the exemption amount is technically set at $0, the TCJA effectively suspended personal exemptions by setting the phaseout to $0. The "One, Big, Beautiful Bill" (signed July 2025) extended this suspension.

---

## Dependent Standard Deduction

For an individual who can be claimed as a dependent on another taxpayer's return, the standard deduction is limited to the greater of:
- $1,250 (2023), $1,300 (2024), $1,350 (2025), OR
- Earned income plus $400 (2023), $450 (2024), $450 (2025)

...but cannot exceed the regular standard deduction amount for the filing status.

## Itemized Deductions

Taxpayers can choose to itemize deductions on Schedule A instead of taking the standard deduction if their itemized total exceeds the standard deduction.

### State and Local Tax (SALT) Deduction Cap

| Year | SALT Cap |
|------|----------|
| 2023 | $10,000 ($5,000 MFS) |
| 2024 | $10,000 ($5,000 MFS) |
| 2025 | $10,000 ($5,000 MFS) |

- Includes state and local income taxes (or general sales taxes), plus property taxes
- The $10,000 cap was established by the TCJA and applies through 2025
- The "One, Big, Beautiful Bill" raised this cap to $40,000 for 2026 (with phaseouts for high earners)

### Mortgage Interest Deduction

| Rule | 2023-2025 |
|------|-----------|
| Acquisition debt limit (loans originated after 12/15/2017) | $750,000 |
| Acquisition debt limit (loans originated on or before 12/15/2017) | $1,000,000 |
| Home equity loan interest | Deductible only if used to buy, build, or substantially improve the home securing the loan |
| Qualifying properties | Primary residence + one additional home |

**Note:** MFS filers have half the above limits ($375,000 / $500,000).

### Charitable Contribution Deductions

| Rule | 2023-2025 |
|------|-----------|
| Cash contributions to public charities | Up to 60% of AGI |
| Capital gains property to public charities | Up to 30% of AGI |
| Cash to private foundations | Up to 30% of AGI |
| Capital gains property to private foundations | Up to 20% of AGI |
| Carryforward period for excess contributions | 5 years |

**Documentation requirements:**
- Cash donations of any amount: Written record (bank record, receipt, or written communication from charity)
- Cash donations $250+: Written acknowledgment from the charity
- Non-cash donations over $500: Form 8283
- Non-cash donations over $5,000: Qualified appraisal required (except publicly traded securities)

### Medical and Dental Expenses

| Year | AGI Threshold |
|------|---------------|
| 2023 | 7.5% of AGI |
| 2024 | 7.5% of AGI |
| 2025 | 7.5% of AGI |

Only the amount of unreimbursed medical expenses that exceeds 7.5% of AGI is deductible.

**Qualifying expenses include:** Doctor/dentist fees, prescriptions, insurance premiums (not pre-tax), long-term care insurance (subject to age-based limits), medical equipment, travel for medical care.

**Non-qualifying expenses:** Cosmetic surgery (unless medically necessary), health club dues, over-the-counter medications (unless prescribed), general health improvement programs.

### Casualty and Theft Losses

- For 2023-2025: Deductible ONLY if attributable to a **federally declared disaster**
- Each loss must exceed $100, and total losses must exceed 10% of AGI
- The TCJA eliminated the deduction for personal casualty/theft losses not from federally declared disasters

### Other Itemized Deductions

**Still deductible (2023-2025):**
- Investment interest expense (limited to net investment income)
- Gambling losses (limited to gambling winnings)

**NOT deductible under TCJA (2023-2025):**
- Unreimbursed employee business expenses
- Tax preparation fees
- Investment advisory fees
- Safe deposit box fees
- Other miscellaneous deductions formerly subject to 2% AGI floor

---

## Pease Limitation (Itemized Deduction Phaseout)

The Pease limitation on itemized deductions is **suspended** for 2018-2025 under the TCJA. There is no phaseout of itemized deductions based on income for these years.

---

## Standard Deduction vs. Itemizing Decision Factors

Itemizing typically makes sense when:
- Mortgage interest is substantial (early years of a large mortgage)
- State/local taxes are high (though capped at $10,000)
- Significant charitable contributions
- Large unreimbursed medical expenses
- Significant casualty losses from a federally declared disaster
- The combined total exceeds the standard deduction

Most taxpayers (approximately 87-90%) take the standard deduction due to the TCJA's nearly doubled standard deduction and $10,000 SALT cap.
