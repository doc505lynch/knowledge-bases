## 1. American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC)

**IRC Section 25A(b); IRS Form 8863**

The AOTC is a partially refundable credit for qualified education expenses paid during the first four years of postsecondary education.

### Credit Calculation

| Detail | 2023-2025 |
|--------|-----------|
| Maximum credit per student | $2,500 |
| Credit calculation | 100% of first $2,000 + 25% of next $2,000 |
| Refundable portion | 40% (up to $1,000) |
| Available for | First 4 years of postsecondary education |
| Half-time enrollment required | Yes |
| Felony drug conviction | Disqualifies |
| Per student or per return | Per student |

### MAGI Phaseout (All Years 2023-2025 -- NOT Inflation-Indexed)

| Filing Status | Phaseout Range |
|---------------|---------------|
| Single / HoH | $80,000 - $90,000 |
| MFJ | $160,000 - $180,000 |
| MFS | Not eligible |

> Note: The AOTC phaseout thresholds are statutory (set by IRC 25A) and are **not** indexed for inflation. They have remained unchanged since the credit was made permanent by the PATH Act of 2015.

### Qualified Expenses for AOTC

- Tuition and required enrollment fees
- Course-related books, supplies, and equipment (even if not purchased from the school)
- **NOT eligible:** Room and board, insurance, medical expenses, transportation, personal/living expenses, same expenses paid with tax-free funds

### Key Eligibility Rules

- Student must be enrolled at least half-time in a degree or certificate program
- Student must not have completed the first 4 years of postsecondary education
- Student must not have a felony drug conviction
- Cannot be claimed in the same year as LLC for the same student
- Married Filing Separately status is **ineligible**

## 2. Lifetime Learning Credit (LLC)

**IRC Section 25A(c); IRS Form 8863**

The LLC is a nonrefundable credit for qualified tuition and related expenses. Unlike the AOTC, there is no limit on the number of years you can claim it, and it applies to undergraduate, graduate, and professional degree courses -- including courses to acquire or improve job skills.

### Credit Calculation

| Detail | 2023-2025 |
|--------|-----------|
| Maximum credit per return | $2,000 |
| Credit calculation | 20% of up to $10,000 in qualified expenses |
| Refundable | No |
| Available for | Any year of postsecondary education + job skill courses |
| Half-time enrollment required | No |
| Per student or per return | Per return (not per student) |
| Felony drug conviction | Does NOT disqualify |

### MAGI Phaseout (All Years 2023-2025 -- NOT Inflation-Indexed)

| Filing Status | Phaseout Range |
|---------------|---------------|
| Single / HoH | $80,000 - $90,000 |
| MFJ | $160,000 - $180,000 |
| MFS | Not eligible |

> Note: The Taxpayer Certainty and Disaster Tax Relief Act of 2020 increased the LLC income limits (previously $59,000-$69,000 single / $118,000-$138,000 MFJ) to match the AOTC phaseout ranges beginning in tax year 2021. These thresholds are statutory and not inflation-adjusted.

### Qualified Expenses for LLC

- Tuition and required enrollment fees
- Does NOT include books/supplies unless required to be purchased from the school
- Does NOT include room and board

## 3. AOTC vs. LLC Comparison

| Feature | AOTC | LLC |
|---------|------|-----|
| Max credit | $2,500/student | $2,000/return |
| Refundable | Partially (40%) | No |
| Years available | First 4 years | Unlimited |
| Enrollment | At least half-time | Any amount |
| Number of claims | Per student | Per return |
| Graduate school | No | Yes |
| Job skills courses | No | Yes |
| Drug conviction bar | Yes | No |
| Phaseout (Single) | $80K-$90K | $80K-$90K |
| Phaseout (MFJ) | $160K-$180K | $160K-$180K |
| Form | 8863 | 8863 |

**Note:** Cannot claim both AOTC and LLC for the same student in the same year. If eligible for both, AOTC is generally more beneficial.

## 4. Student Loan Interest Deduction

**IRC Section 221; IRS Form 1040 Schedule 1, Line 21**

An above-the-line deduction (no itemizing required) for interest paid on qualified education loans.

### Deduction and Phaseout by Year

| Detail | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|--------|------|------|------|
| Maximum deduction | $2,500 | $2,500 | $2,500 |
| Deduction type | Above-the-line (Schedule 1) | Same | Same |
| Filing status restriction | Cannot be MFS; cannot be claimed as dependent | Same | Same |

### MAGI Phaseout Ranges (Inflation-Indexed)

| Filing Status | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|---------------|------|------|------|
| Single / HoH | $75,000 - $90,000 | $80,000 - $95,000 | $85,000 - $100,000 |
| MFJ | $155,000 - $185,000 | $165,000 - $195,000 | $170,000 - $200,000 |

> These thresholds **are** adjusted annually for inflation.

### Qualifying Loans

- Loans taken solely to pay qualified education expenses
- Includes federal and private student loans
- Does NOT include loans from related parties, employer plans, or qualified plan distributions
- Interest must be legally obligated to pay
- Voluntary interest payments during deferment or forbearance qualify

## 5. 529 Qualified Tuition Programs

**IRC Section 529; IRS Publication 970 Chapter 8**

### Federal Tax Treatment

| Detail | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|--------|------|------|------|
| Federal tax deduction for contributions | None | None | None |
| Tax-free growth | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Tax-free withdrawals for qualified expenses | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Contribution limit | No federal limit; subject to gift tax rules | Same | Same |
| Annual gift tax exclusion per beneficiary | $17,000 | $18,000 | $19,000 |
| 5-year gift tax averaging | Up to $85,000 | Up to $90,000 | Up to $95,000 |
| K-12 tuition annual limit | $10,000 | $10,000 | $10,000 |
| Student loan repayment (lifetime per beneficiary) | $10,000 | $10,000 | $10,000 |
| Penalty for nonqualified withdrawals (earnings) | 10% + income tax | 10% + income tax | 10% + income tax |

### Qualified Expenses

- Tuition and fees at eligible postsecondary institutions
- Room and board (if enrolled at least half-time; limited to the greater of school's allowance or actual cost)
- Books, supplies, and equipment required for enrollment
- Computer equipment, software, and internet access
- Special needs services
- **K-12 tuition: up to $10,000 per year per beneficiary** (elementary and secondary schools, public/private/religious)
- **Apprenticeship programs:** Fees, books, supplies, and equipment
- **Student loan repayment:** Up to $10,000 lifetime per beneficiary (also $10,000 for each beneficiary's siblings)

### 529-to-Roth IRA Rollover (SECURE Act 2.0)

Effective for distributions made after December 31, 2023:

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Effective date** | January 1, 2024 |
| **Lifetime limit** | $35,000 per beneficiary (aggregate, across all 529 accounts) |
| **Account age requirement** | 529 account must have been open for at least **15 years** |
| **Recent contribution restriction** | Contributions (and their earnings) made within the last **5 years** are ineligible |
| **Annual cap** | Subject to the annual Roth IRA contribution limit for the year |
| **2024 annual Roth IRA limit** | $7,000 (under age 50) / $8,000 (age 50+) |
| **2025 annual Roth IRA limit** | $7,000 (under age 50) / $8,000 (age 50+) |
| **Coordination with other contributions** | Rollover amount is reduced by any other IRA contributions made that year |
| **Beneficiary match** | Roth IRA must be in the name of the 529 beneficiary |
| **Income limits** | The beneficiary must have earned income; however, the Roth IRA income limits do **not** apply to 529 rollovers |

> The IRS has not yet issued formal regulations or detailed guidance on 529-to-Roth rollovers. The rules above are based on the statutory text of SECURE Act 2.0, Section 126.

**Example:** A parent opens a 529 for their child. After 15+ years, up to $35,000 of unused funds can be rolled into the child's Roth IRA (up to the annual Roth IRA contribution limit each year). If the Roth IRA limit is $7,000, it would take at least 5 years to roll over $35,000.

### New Mexico 529 Plan -- State Tax Benefits

New Mexico offers one of the most generous 529 state tax deductions in the nation:

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **State income tax deduction** | **Unlimited** -- 100% of contributions are deductible |
| **Which plan qualifies** | Only "The Education Plan" (NM's official 529) |
| **Other states' 529 plans** | NOT deductible on NM state returns |
| **Tax-free growth at state level** | Yes, for qualified expenses |
| **Holding period** | None -- contribute and immediately withdraw for qualified expenses and still get the deduction |
| **Clawback on nonqualified withdrawals** | Earnings are subject to NM income tax + 10% federal penalty |

> New Mexico is one of only four states offering an unlimited state tax deduction for 529 contributions. NM residents with high incomes should strongly consider maximizing 529 contributions as a state tax planning strategy.

### Coverdell vs. 529 Comparison

| Feature | Coverdell ESA | 529 Plan |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Annual contribution limit | $2,000 | No federal limit |
| Income limits for contributors | Yes | No |
| K-12 expenses | Yes (broad) | Yes ($10,000/year tuition only) |
| Investment options | Self-directed | Plan-selected |
| Age limits | Contributions before 18, use by 30 | None |
| State tax deduction | No | Often yes (unlimited in NM) |

## 6. Coverdell Education Savings Accounts (ESA)

**IRC Section 530; IRS Publication 970 Chapter 7**

### Contribution and Income Limits (NOT Inflation-Indexed)

| Detail | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|--------|------|------|------|
| **Max contribution per beneficiary** | $2,000 | $2,000 | $2,000 |
| **Tax-free growth** | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| **Tax-free withdrawals (qualified expenses)** | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| **Beneficiary age limit for contributions** | Before age 18 | Before age 18 | Before age 18 |
| **Distribution deadline** | By age 30 | By age 30 | By age 30 |
| **Can be used for K-12** | Yes | Yes | Yes |

### MAGI Phaseout for Contributors (All Years -- NOT Inflation-Indexed)

| Filing Status | Phaseout Range (all years) |
|---------------|---------------------------|
| Single / HoH | $95,000 - $110,000 |
| MFJ | $190,000 - $220,000 |

> The Coverdell ESA contribution limits and phaseouts are set by statute and have **not** changed.

### Key Rules

- Contributions are **not** tax-deductible
- Earnings grow tax-free; distributions for qualified expenses are tax-free
- Qualified expenses include elementary, secondary, AND postsecondary education
- Broader expense coverage than 529 plans (includes uniforms, tutoring, room and board for K-12 in some cases)
- Excess contributions are subject to a 6% excise tax
- Special needs beneficiaries are exempt from the age-18 contribution and age-30 distribution limits

## 7. Employer Educational Assistance (Section 127)

**IRC Section 127; IRS Publication 15-B**

### Annual Exclusion

| Detail | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|--------|------|------|------|
| **Annual exclusion** | $5,250 | $5,250 | $5,250 |
| **Qualifying expenses** | Tuition, fees, books, supplies, equipment | Same | Same |
| **Includes student loan repayment** | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| **Graduate education** | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| **Tax form reporting** | Not reported on W-2 if within exclusion | Same | Same |

### Student Loan Repayment Provision

- Originally enacted by the **CARES Act** (March 2020), extended by the **Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2021**, and further extended by **SECURE Act 2.0**.
- Allows employers to make tax-free student loan payments on behalf of employees under a Section 127 plan.
- **Applies to payments made after March 27, 2020, and before January 1, 2026.**
- The $5,250 limit is shared between traditional educational assistance and student loan repayments.
- **After December 31, 2025:** Unless Congress extends the provision, employer student loan repayments will revert to being taxable income. The base $5,250 exclusion for educational assistance (tuition, books, etc.) remains **permanent**.

### Employer Requirements

- Must maintain a written educational assistance plan (EAP)
- Cannot discriminate in favor of highly compensated employees
- No more than 5% of benefits can go to shareholders/owners (or their spouses/dependents)
- Employees do not need to be in a degree program
- Excess over $5,250 is taxable as wages

### Qualified Expenses Under Section 127

- Tuition, fees, and similar payments
- Books, supplies, and equipment
- Principal or interest on qualified student loans (through 2025)
- **NOT covered:** Meals, lodging, transportation, tools/supplies retained after the course

## 8. Tuition and Fees Deduction (Expired)

**Former IRC Section 222**

| Tax Year | Status |
|----------|--------|
| 2020 and earlier | Available (up to $4,000 above-the-line deduction) |
| **2021 and later** | **EXPIRED -- No longer available** |

The **Taxpayer Certainty and Disaster Tax Relief Act of 2020** repealed the tuition and fees deduction for tax years beginning after 2020. To offset this, the same act increased the LLC income phaseout thresholds to match the AOTC levels ($80,000-$90,000 single / $160,000-$180,000 MFJ), making the LLC accessible to a broader range of taxpayers.

**Bottom line:** For tax years 2023, 2024, and 2025, the tuition and fees deduction does **not** exist. Use the AOTC or LLC instead.

## 9. Scholarship and Fellowship Taxability

**IRC Section 117; IRS Publication 970 Chapter 1; Topic No. 421**

### General Rules

| Scholarship/Grant Used For | Tax Treatment |
|---------------------------|---------------|
| Tuition and required enrollment fees | **Tax-free** |
| Course-related books, supplies, and equipment (required) | **Tax-free** |
| Room and board | **Taxable** |
| Travel | **Taxable** |
| Research or clerical help | **Taxable** |
| Optional equipment | **Taxable** |
| Payment for teaching, research, or other services | **Taxable** (with exceptions below) |

### Key Rules

1. **Degree candidate requirement:** To exclude scholarship income, you must be a candidate for a degree at an eligible educational institution.
2. **Services requirement:** Amounts received as payment for teaching, research, or other required services are **taxable** -- even if all students must perform the service.
   - **Exceptions:** National Health Service Corps Scholarship Program, Armed Forces Health Professions Scholarship Program, and comprehensive student work-learning-service programs (as defined under 42 USC 2756).
3. **Reporting:** Taxable scholarship income is reported on Form 1040, Line 1a (or 1r for tax year 2023+). It is **not** subject to self-employment tax.
4. **Coordination with credits:** You can choose to include otherwise tax-free scholarship amounts in income to increase your AOTC or LLC. This is a valid planning strategy when the credit benefit exceeds the tax on the scholarship income.
5. **Pell Grants and need-based grants:** Tax-free to the extent used for qualified education expenses (same rules as scholarships).
6. **Employer-provided scholarships:** If your employer provides a scholarship, it may be taxable as compensation unless it meets the Section 117 exclusion requirements.

### Fellowship Grants

- Follow the same rules as scholarships
- Amounts for travel, research, or living expenses are generally **taxable**
- Amounts designated for tuition and required fees are **tax-free** (if the recipient is a degree candidate)

## 10. Education Savings Bond Program (Series EE/I)

**IRC Section 135; IRS Form 8815**

You may be able to exclude from gross income all or part of the interest earned on Series EE or Series I U.S. savings bonds redeemed to pay qualified higher education expenses.

### MAGI Phaseout Ranges (Inflation-Indexed)

| Tax Year | Single / HoH | Married Filing Jointly |
|----------|--------------|----------------------|
| **2023** | $91,850 - $106,850 | $137,800 - $167,800 |
| **2024** | $96,800 - $111,800 | $145,200 - $175,200 |
| **2025** | $99,500 - $114,500 | $149,250 - $179,250 |

> Phaseout ranges are adjusted annually for inflation. The phaseout range is always $15,000 for single filers and $30,000 for joint filers.

### Eligibility Requirements

- Bonds must be **Series EE or Series I** issued **after 1989**
- Bond owner must have been **age 24 or older** before the bond's issue date
- Bonds must be registered in your name (or jointly with your spouse)
- **Married Filing Separately is ineligible**
- If bond interest exceeds qualified expenses, only a proportional amount is excludable

### Qualified Higher Education Expenses (for this program)

- Tuition and required fees at an eligible institution
- Contributions to a 529 qualified tuition program
- Contributions to a Coverdell ESA
- **Room and board do NOT qualify**
- Expenses must be reduced by tax-free scholarships, employer assistance, and other tax-free education benefits

## 11. Educator Expense Deduction

**IRC Section 62(a)(2)(D); IRS Form 1040 Schedule 1, Line 11**

### Deduction Amounts

| Tax Year | Maximum per Educator | Maximum per MFJ Return (both educators) |
|----------|---------------------|----------------------------------------|
| **2023** | $300 | $600 |
| **2024** | $300 | $600 |
| **2025** | $300 | $600 |

> The $300 limit was increased from $250 beginning in tax year 2022 and is subject to future inflation adjustments in $50 increments (rounded down).

### Key Features

- **Above-the-line deduction** -- claimed whether you take the standard deduction or itemize
- Eligible educators: K-12 teachers, instructors, counselors, principals, or aides
- Must work at least **900 hours** during the school year
- School must provide elementary or secondary education under state law

### Qualified Expenses

- Books, supplies, and other materials used in the classroom
- Computer equipment, software, and services
- Professional development courses
- Personal protective equipment (PPE), disinfectant, and other COVID-related supplies (for applicable years)
- **Must be unreimbursed** (not paid by the school or other source)
- **NOT deductible:** Home schooling expenses, nonathletic supplies for health/PE courses (unless for courses in health or physical education)

## 12. Taxability of Forgiven Student Loans

**IRC Section 108(f); ARPA Section 9675**

### ARPA Provision: Tax-Free Treatment of Student Loan Forgiveness

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Applicable period** | Discharges after December 31, 2020, and **before January 1, 2026** |
| **Covered loans** | Federal student loans, private student loans, and institutional loans for postsecondary education |
| **Tax treatment during ARPA period** | **Excluded from gross income** (tax-free) |
| **Tax treatment AFTER December 31, 2025** | **Taxable as cancellation of debt income** (unless another exclusion applies) |

### Year-by-Year Summary

| Tax Year | Student Loan Forgiveness Taxable? |
|----------|----------------------------------|
| **2023** | **No** -- excluded under ARPA Section 9675 |
| **2024** | **No** -- excluded under ARPA Section 9675 |
| **2025** | **No** -- excluded under ARPA Section 9675 (for discharges on or before 12/31/2025) |
| **2026+** | **Yes** -- reverts to general rule: taxable as ordinary income (cancellation of debt) |

### Permanent Exclusions (Unaffected by ARPA Expiration)

The following types of student loan forgiveness remain **permanently tax-free** under IRC 108(f)(1):

- **Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF)** -- loans forgiven after 120 qualifying payments
- **Teacher Loan Forgiveness**
- Forgiveness for working in certain public service positions as required by the loan terms
- **Closed school discharges**
- **Borrower defense to repayment discharges**
- **Total and permanent disability discharges** (permanently tax-free under the TCJA through 2025; status after 2025 may depend on legislative action)

### Planning Note

If income-driven repayment (IDR) plan forgiveness occurs **after** December 31, 2025, the forgiven amount will generally be treated as taxable ordinary income. This could result in a significant one-time tax liability. Taxpayers anticipating IDR forgiveness in 2026 or later should:

- Increase withholdings or make estimated tax payments
- Set aside savings for the anticipated tax bill
- Consider whether insolvency or other IRC 108 exclusions might apply

## 13. Education Tax Benefits Coordination Rules

You cannot "double-dip" -- the same expenses cannot be used for multiple benefits:

1. Cannot claim AOTC/LLC on expenses paid with tax-free 529 distributions
2. Cannot claim AOTC/LLC on expenses paid with tax-free scholarship/fellowship
3. Cannot claim AOTC/LLC on expenses paid with employer educational assistance
4. Cannot deduct student loan interest for expenses that were paid with tax-free funds
5. Can strategically allocate scholarship to room/board (making it taxable) to free up tuition expenses for AOTC/LLC
6. Cannot exclude savings bond interest if the same expenses are used for education credits
7. 529-to-Roth IRA rollover does not count as a qualified education expense distribution

## 15. Key Expiration Dates to Watch

| Provision | Expiration |
|-----------|-----------|
| ARPA student loan forgiveness tax exclusion | December 31, 2025 |
| Section 127 student loan repayment by employer | December 31, 2025 |
| Total/permanent disability discharge tax exclusion | December 31, 2025 (unless extended) |
| AOTC, LLC, student loan interest deduction | **Permanent** (no expiration) |
| Educator expense deduction | **Permanent** (no expiration) |

| Section 127 base educational assistance ($5,250) | **Permanent** (student loan piece expires) |
