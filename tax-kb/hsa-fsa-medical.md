# HSA, FSA, and Medical Tax Benefits (2023, 2024, 2025)

> **Sources:** IRS Rev. Proc. 2022-24 (TY2023), Rev. Proc. 2023-23 (TY2024), Rev. Proc. 2024-25 (TY2025); IRS Publication 969

---

## Health Savings Account (HSA)

### Contribution Limits

| Detail | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|--------|------|------|------|
| Self-only coverage | $3,850 | $4,150 | $4,300 |
| Family coverage | $7,750 | $8,300 | $8,550 |
| Catch-up (age 55+) | $1,000 | $1,000 | $1,000 |
| Total: Self-only + catch-up | $4,850 | $5,150 | $5,300 |
| Total: Family + catch-up | $8,750 | $9,300 | $9,550 |

### High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP) Requirements

To contribute to an HSA, you must be enrolled in a qualifying HDHP:

| Detail | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|--------|------|------|------|
| **Minimum annual deductible** | | | |
| Self-only | $1,500 | $1,600 | $1,650 |
| Family | $3,000 | $3,200 | $3,300 |
| **Maximum out-of-pocket** | | | |
| Self-only | $7,500 | $8,050 | $8,300 |
| Family | $15,000 | $16,100 | $16,600 |

### HSA Triple Tax Advantage
1. **Contributions are tax-deductible** (or pre-tax through payroll)
2. **Earnings grow tax-free** (interest, dividends, capital gains)
3. **Withdrawals are tax-free** when used for qualified medical expenses

### Key HSA Rules
- **Eligibility:** Must be enrolled in HDHP, cannot be enrolled in Medicare, cannot be claimed as a dependent, cannot have other non-HDHP coverage (dental/vision OK)
- **No use-it-or-lose-it:** Balances roll over indefinitely year to year
- **Portability:** Account belongs to the individual, not the employer
- **Contribution deadline:** April 15 of the following year (tax filing deadline, not including extensions)
- **Investment:** Most HSA custodians allow investing in mutual funds once balance reaches a threshold
- **Non-qualified withdrawals:** Taxable as income + 20% penalty (no penalty after age 65, but still taxable)
- **After age 65:** Non-medical withdrawals taxed as ordinary income but no penalty (functions like a Traditional IRA)
- **Death:** If beneficiary is spouse, becomes their HSA. Otherwise, HSA ceases; fair market value is taxable to the beneficiary.

### Last-Month Rule
If you become HSA-eligible on December 1, you can contribute the full annual amount. However, you must remain eligible through December 31 of the following year (testing period) or include the excess contribution in income plus 10% penalty.

### Qualified Medical Expenses (IRC Section 213(d))
- Doctor visits, hospital stays, surgery
- Prescription medications
- Dental and vision care
- Mental health services
- Insulin (always qualified)
- Over-the-counter medications and menstrual care products (since 2020, CARES Act)
- Long-term care insurance premiums (subject to age-based limits)
- COBRA premiums
- Medicare premiums (Parts A, B, D, Medicare Advantage) -- but NOT Medigap premiums

## Flexible Spending Account (FSA) -- Health Care

### Contribution Limits

| Detail | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|--------|------|------|------|
| Employee contribution limit | $3,050 | $3,200 | $3,300 |
| Carryover maximum (if plan allows) | $610 | $640 | $660 |

### Key FSA Rules
- **Use-it-or-lose-it:** Unused funds are forfeited at plan year end (unless employer offers carryover or grace period)
- **Carryover option:** Employer may allow up to the carryover maximum to roll to next year
- **Grace period option:** Employer may offer up to 2.5 months after plan year end to use remaining funds
- **Cannot have both** carryover and grace period
- **Pre-tax contributions** through salary reduction (reduces gross income, FICA, and income tax)
- **Cannot contribute to both** a general-purpose Health FSA and an HSA simultaneously
  - Exception: **Limited-purpose FSA** (dental/vision only) is compatible with an HSA
  - Exception: **Post-deductible FSA** that only reimburses after HDHP deductible is met

### FSA vs. HSA Comparison

| Feature | Health FSA | HSA |
|---------|-----------|-----|
| Employer plan required | Yes | No (just need HDHP) |
| Contribution limit (2025) | $3,300 | $4,300/$8,550 |
| Use-it-or-lose-it | Yes (with limited carryover) | No -- rolls over |
| Portability | No (tied to employer) | Yes (yours forever) |
| Investment option | No | Yes |
| Available Day 1 | Full amount available immediately | Only what's been contributed |
| Tax savings | Income + FICA | Income (+ FICA if through payroll) |

## Dependent Care FSA (DCFSA)

| Detail | 2023-2025 |
|--------|-----------|
| Maximum contribution | $5,000 ($2,500 if MFS) |
| Eligible dependents | Children under 13, disabled spouse/dependent |
| Both spouses must work | Yes (or one must be a full-time student/disabled) |

### Key DCFSA Rules
- Pre-tax salary reduction
- Use-it-or-lose-it (with possible grace period, no carryover option)
- Cannot claim Child and Dependent Care Credit on the same expenses
- Coordinated with the dependent care credit -- generally more beneficial for higher-income taxpayers
- Qualifying expenses: Day care, preschool, before/after school care, summer day camp, nanny/au pair wages

## Medical Expense Deduction (Schedule A)

### AGI Threshold

| Year | Threshold |
|------|-----------|
| 2023 | 7.5% of AGI |
| 2024 | 7.5% of AGI |
| 2025 | 7.5% of AGI |

Only the amount of unreimbursed qualifying medical expenses that exceeds 7.5% of AGI is deductible.

**Note:** The 7.5% threshold is permanent. It was temporarily scheduled to increase to 10% but the Taxpayer Certainty and Disaster Tax Relief Act of 2019 made 7.5% permanent.

### Who Benefits from the Medical Expense Deduction
- Must itemize (cannot take standard deduction)
- Most beneficial for taxpayers with very high medical costs relative to income
- Common qualifying large expenses: nursing home care, assisted living, major surgery, chronic disease management, health insurance premiums (if not paid pre-tax)

### Qualifying Medical Expenses
- Same general list as HSA qualified expenses, plus:
  - Long-term care services and facilities
  - Home modifications for medical reasons (wheelchair ramps, widened doorways)
  - Special schools for learning disabilities
  - Service animal costs
  - Transportation for medical care (actual costs or standard mileage: 22 cents 2023, 21 cents 2024-2025)
  - Lodging while away from home for medical care ($50/night per person)

### Non-Qualifying Expenses
- Cosmetic surgery (unless for congenital abnormality, injury, or disfiguring disease)
- General health expenses (gym memberships, vitamins, cosmetics)
- Funeral expenses
- Nonprescription drugs (except insulin and OTC medications per CARES Act)
- Premiums paid with pre-tax dollars (already excluded from income)

## Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction

| Detail | 2023-2025 |
|--------|-----------|
| Deduction type | Above-the-line (Schedule 1) |
| Who qualifies | Self-employed individuals (including >2% S-corp shareholders) |
| What's deductible | Health, dental, vision, and long-term care premiums for self, spouse, and dependents |
| Limitation | Cannot exceed net SE income |
| Cannot be claimed | For months when eligible for employer-subsidized health plan |

This deduction reduces AGI (not SE income). It is NOT deductible for SE tax purposes but IS deductible for income tax purposes.

## Long-Term Care Insurance Premium Deduction Limits (Age-Based)

If qualifying premiums are included in itemized medical expenses:

| Age at End of Year | 2023 Limit | 2024 Limit | 2025 Limit |
|--------------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| 40 or under | $480 | $470 | $480 |
| 41-50 | $890 | $880 | $900 |
| 51-60 | $1,790 | $1,760 | $1,800 |
| 61-70 | $4,770 | $4,710 | $4,810 |
| Over 70 | $5,960 | $5,880 | $6,010 |

These limits apply per person. They are the maximum amounts that can be treated as qualified medical expenses for long-term care insurance premiums.
