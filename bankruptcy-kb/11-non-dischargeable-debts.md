# Non-Dischargeable Debts in Bankruptcy

## Overview

Not all debts can be eliminated in bankruptcy. Federal law (11 U.S.C. § 523) lists specific categories of debts that survive both Chapter 7 and Chapter 13 discharge. Understanding which debts cannot be discharged is critical for deciding whether bankruptcy is the right strategy.

---

## Debts Non-Dischargeable in ALL Chapters (7, 11, 13)

### 1. Domestic Support Obligations (DSOs)

**11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(5)**

- Child support (current and past-due)
- Spousal support / alimony
- Applies whether owed to a spouse, former spouse, child, or government agency collecting on their behalf
- Includes amounts assigned to or owed to a state/local government
- DSOs receive highest priority in bankruptcy (ahead of all other creditors)

**California specifics:**
- California Family Code governs support orders
- Wage garnishment for support can continue during bankruptcy
- Automatic stay does NOT stop collection of DSOs (§ 362(b)(2))

### 2. Most Tax Debts

**11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(1)**

Generally non-dischargeable:
- Income taxes for which a return was due within 3 years before filing
- Assessed within 240 days before filing
- Tax returns filed late (after 2 years before filing)
- Fraudulent returns or willful tax evasion (never dischargeable)
- Trust fund taxes (employee withholding — FICA, income tax)

**The "3-2-240" rule for potentially dischargeable taxes:**
1. **3-year rule:** Tax return was due more than 3 years before filing
2. **2-year rule:** Tax return was actually filed more than 2 years before filing
3. **240-day rule:** Tax was assessed more than 240 days before filing

All three conditions must be met AND the return must not be fraudulent or a substitute-for-return filed by the IRS.

**California state taxes:** Follow the same general framework. Franchise Tax Board (FTB) debts and Employment Development Department (EDD) debts may be dischargeable if they meet the timing tests.

### 3. Debts Not Listed on Schedules

**11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(3)**

- Debts the debtor fails to list on bankruptcy schedules
- Creditor must not have had actual knowledge of the case in time to file a proof of claim
- This is why accurate, complete schedules are essential

### 4. Debts from Fraud, False Pretenses, or False Financial Statements

**11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(2)**

**(a)(2)(A) — Fraud / false pretenses:**
- Money, property, or services obtained through fraud, false pretenses, or false representation
- Creditor must prove: (1) debtor made a false representation, (2) knowing it was false, (3) intending to deceive, (4) creditor reasonably relied, (5) creditor suffered a loss

**(a)(2)(B) — Written financial statements:**
- Use of a materially false written statement about financial condition
- Must be in writing, made with intent to deceive, and creditor must have reasonably relied on it
- Common example: lying on a credit application about income or assets

**(a)(2)(C) — Recent luxury purchases / cash advances:**
- Consumer debts >$800 for luxury goods/services to a single creditor within 90 days of filing are presumed non-dischargeable
- Cash advances >$1,100 within 70 days of filing are presumed non-dischargeable
- Debtor can rebut the presumption with evidence of honest intent

### 5. Debts from Embezzlement, Larceny, or Fiduciary Fraud

**11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(4)**

- Fraud or defalcation while acting as a fiduciary
- Embezzlement (fraudulent appropriation of property entrusted to you)
- Larceny (theft)
- Requires proof of intent (defalcation requires at least recklessness)

### 6. Willful and Malicious Injury

**11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(6)**

- Debts for willful and malicious injury to another person or their property
- "Willful" = deliberate and intentional act
- "Malicious" = wrongful act done without just cause or excuse
- Includes intentional torts: assault, battery, conversion, intentional infliction of emotional distress
- Does NOT include negligence (e.g., ordinary car accident)

### 7. Government Fines, Penalties, and Forfeitures

**11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(7)**

- Fines, penalties, or forfeitures payable to and for the benefit of a governmental unit
- Not compensation for actual pecuniary loss
- Includes criminal fines, traffic tickets, regulatory penalties
- Excludes tax penalties (governed by § 523(a)(1))

### 8. Student Loans

**11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(8)**

- Educational loans made, insured, or guaranteed by a governmental unit
- Qualified education loans (as defined by the IRS)
- Includes both federal and most private student loans

**Undue hardship exception (Brunner test):**
To discharge student loans, debtor must prove ALL three prongs:
1. Cannot maintain a minimal standard of living for self and dependents while repaying
2. The hardship will persist for a significant portion of the repayment period
3. Made good-faith efforts to repay (e.g., applied for income-driven repayment, deferment)

**California (9th Circuit) approach:**
- Uses a "totality of circumstances" test rather than strict Brunner
- More flexible than other circuits but still difficult to meet
- Factors: income, expenses, age, health, dependents, employment prospects
- Partial discharge possible (some courts will discharge a portion)

**Recent developments:**
- DOJ guidance (2022+) instructs US Attorneys not to oppose discharge in cases meeting certain criteria
- Some courts have become more willing to grant discharge
- Income-driven repayment plan exhaustion strengthens the case

### 9. DUI / DWI Debts

**11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(9)**

- Death or personal injury caused by operating a motor vehicle, vessel, or aircraft while intoxicated
- Applies to alcohol, drugs, or other substances
- Non-dischargeable in both Chapter 7 and Chapter 13
- Includes both criminal restitution and civil judgments

### 10. Prior Bankruptcy Debts

**11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(10)**

- Debts that were or could have been listed in a prior bankruptcy where discharge was denied or waived

### 11. Criminal Restitution

**11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(13)**

- Court-ordered restitution under federal criminal statutes
- Also non-dischargeable under § 523(a)(7) as government penalties

### 12. Debts Owed to Tax-Advantaged Retirement Plans

**11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(18)**

- Debts owed under federal tax provisions related to pension/retirement plans
- Includes loans from 401(k) plans

## Debts Non-Dischargeable in Chapter 7 Only (Dischargeable in Chapter 13)

These debts can be included in a Chapter 13 repayment plan and discharged upon completion:

### Property Settlement Debts

**11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(15)**

- Debts to a spouse, former spouse, or child incurred during divorce or separation
- NOT support obligations (those are never dischargeable)
- Includes property division obligations, hold-harmless agreements
- Chapter 13 advantage: these CAN be discharged through the plan

### Willful and Malicious Injury to Property

While § 523(a)(6) debts are non-dischargeable in Chapter 7, the Chapter 13 "superdischarge" historically included some property damage debts. However, BAPCPA (2005) significantly narrowed the Chapter 13 superdischarge.

## Adversary Proceedings for Dischargeability

Most § 523 exceptions require the creditor to file an **adversary proceeding** (a lawsuit within the bankruptcy case) to have the debt declared non-dischargeable:

| Exception | Who must act | Deadline |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| § 523(a)(2) — Fraud | Creditor must file complaint | 60 days after first meeting of creditors |
| § 523(a)(4) — Fiduciary fraud | Creditor must file complaint | 60 days after first meeting of creditors |
| § 523(a)(6) — Willful injury | Creditor must file complaint | 60 days after first meeting of creditors |
| All others | Automatic — no complaint needed | N/A |

**If a creditor misses the 60-day deadline for (a)(2), (a)(4), or (a)(6), the debt IS discharged** even if it would otherwise qualify as non-dischargeable.

## Strategic Considerations

### When Non-Dischargeable Debt Is the Primary Burden

If most of your debt is non-dischargeable:
- Bankruptcy may still help by eliminating dischargeable debts, freeing cash flow for non-dischargeable obligations
- Chapter 13 can restructure non-dischargeable tax debts into a manageable payment plan
- Chapter 13 can cure mortgage arrears even though the mortgage itself isn't discharged

### Tax Debts Strategy

- File all unfiled returns BEFORE filing bankruptcy
- Wait for the 3-2-240 timing windows if possible
- Offers in Compromise (OIC) may be better than bankruptcy for tax-only debt
- California FTB has its own OIC program

### Student Loan Strategy

- Apply for income-driven repayment (IDR) first
- Document all hardship thoroughly
- Consider filing an adversary proceeding if circumstances are compelling
- Partial discharge is a possibility in the 9th Circuit

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Domestic support obligations and most tax debts** are the most common non-dischargeable debts
2. **Fraud-based debts** require the creditor to take action within 60 days — if they don't, the debt is discharged
3. **Student loans** are technically dischargeable but require proving undue hardship
4. **Chapter 13** can discharge some debts that Chapter 7 cannot (property settlement obligations)
5. **Accurate schedules** are essential — unlisted debts won't be discharged
6. **Criminal fines and restitution** survive bankruptcy without exception
7. **DUI/DWI injury debts** are never dischargeable regardless of chapter
