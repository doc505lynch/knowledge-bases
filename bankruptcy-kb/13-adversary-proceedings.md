# Adversary Proceedings in Bankruptcy

## Overview

An adversary proceeding is a lawsuit filed within a bankruptcy case. It is a separate action with its own case number, governed by Part VII of the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure (Rules 7001-7087), which largely incorporate the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.

Adversary proceedings handle disputes that require formal litigation rather than simple motions — issues like fraud allegations, lien avoidance, objections to discharge, and recovery of fraudulent transfers.

---

## When an Adversary Proceeding Is Required

**Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7001** lists the matters that require an adversary proceeding:

| Type | Rule 7001 subsection | Description |
|------|---------------------|-------------|
| Recovery of money or property | (1) | Trustee suing to recover assets |
| Determine validity of lien | (2) | Disputes about whether a lien is valid |
| Obtain approval of sale of interest | (3) | When co-owner's interest is involved |
| Object to or revoke discharge | (4) | Deny debtor's entire discharge |
| Determine dischargeability of debt | (6) | Is a specific debt dischargeable? |
| Obtain injunction | (7) | Restraining orders, injunctions |
| Subordinate a claim | (8) | Push a claim behind others in priority |
| Fraudulent transfer recovery | (1) | Trustee avoiding pre-bankruptcy transfers |
| Preference recovery | (1) | Trustee recovering preferential payments |

## Common Types of Adversary Proceedings

### 1. Objection to Discharge (§ 727)

**Who files:** Trustee, U.S. Trustee, or any creditor
**Against:** The debtor
**Effect if successful:** Debtor receives NO discharge at all — all debts survive

Grounds for denying discharge entirely:

| Ground | § 727 subsection | Description |
|--------|-----------------|-------------|
| Fraudulent transfer | (a)(2) | Transferred/concealed property within 1 year before filing with intent to defraud |
| Destruction of records | (a)(3) | Concealed, destroyed, or failed to keep adequate financial records |
| False oath | (a)(4) | Made a false oath or account in connection with the case |
| Failure to explain loss | (a)(5) | Failed to satisfactorily explain any loss or deficiency of assets |
| Refusal to obey court order | (a)(6) | Refused to obey a lawful court order or answer questions |
| Same act in insider case | (a)(7) | Committed any of the above in connection with an insider's bankruptcy |
| Prior discharge | (a)(8)-(9) | Received Ch. 7 discharge within 8 years or Ch. 13 within 6 years |
| Failure to complete education | (a)(11) | Did not complete debtor education course |

**Deadline:** 60 days after the first date set for the § 341 meeting of creditors. Court may extend for cause if motion filed before deadline.

### 2. Determination of Dischargeability (§ 523)

**Who files:** A creditor claiming their specific debt is non-dischargeable
**Against:** The debtor
**Effect if successful:** That specific debt survives — other debts may still be discharged

Grounds requiring adversary proceeding (60-day deadline):
- § 523(a)(2) — Fraud, false pretenses, false financial statements
- § 523(a)(4) — Fraud/defalcation as fiduciary, embezzlement, larceny
- § 523(a)(6) — Willful and malicious injury

Other § 523 exceptions (taxes, support, student loans, DUI) are automatically non-dischargeable without an adversary proceeding.

### 3. Preference Actions (§ 547)

**Who files:** The bankruptcy trustee
**Against:** Creditors who received preferential payments
**Purpose:** Recover payments made to creditors shortly before bankruptcy to ensure equal treatment

**Elements of a preference:**
1. Transfer of debtor's property
2. To or for the benefit of a creditor
3. On account of an antecedent (pre-existing) debt
4. Made while the debtor was insolvent
5. Within **90 days before filing** (or **1 year** if the creditor was an insider)
6. That enabled the creditor to receive more than it would in a Chapter 7 liquidation

**Defenses to preference claims:**

| Defense | § 547 subsection | Description |
|---------|-----------------|-------------|
| Contemporaneous exchange | (c)(1) | Payment was intended as and was a substantially contemporaneous exchange (e.g., COD purchase) |
| Ordinary course of business | (c)(2) | Payment was in the ordinary course (e.g., regular monthly utility bill) |
| Purchase money security interest | (c)(3) | Creditor gave new value enabling debtor to acquire the property |
| New value | (c)(4) | Creditor extended new unsecured credit after the transfer |
| Floating lien on inventory/receivables | (c)(5) | Security interest in inventory or accounts receivable |
| Small transfers | (c)(9) | Aggregate transfers <$7,575 (consumer); <$7,575 (business) |

### 4. Fraudulent Transfer Actions (§ 548)

**Who files:** The bankruptcy trustee
**Against:** Recipients of fraudulent transfers
**Purpose:** Recover assets the debtor transferred to put them beyond creditors' reach

**Two types:**

**Actual fraud (§ 548(a)(1)(A)):**
- Debtor transferred property with actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud creditors
- Look-back period: **2 years before filing**
- "Badges of fraud" (indicators of intent):
  - Transfer to insider (family, business partner)
  - Debtor retained possession/control after transfer
  - Transfer concealed
  - Debtor was insolvent at time of transfer
  - Transfer was of substantially all assets
  - Debtor absconded
  - Transfer occurred shortly before or after a large debt was incurred

**Constructive fraud (§ 548(a)(1)(B)):**
- Debtor received less than reasonably equivalent value AND was insolvent at the time (or became insolvent as a result)
- No intent required
- Look-back period: **2 years before filing**

**California fraudulent transfer law (CUVTA — Civ. Code §§ 3439-3439.12):**
- The trustee can also use state fraudulent transfer law under § 544(b)
- California's look-back period: **4 years** (actual fraud) or **4 years** (constructive fraud)
- This gives the trustee a longer reach than federal § 548

### 5. Lien Avoidance

Several types of liens can be avoided in bankruptcy:

**Judicial lien avoidance (§ 522(f)):**
- Can avoid a judicial lien that impairs an exemption
- Filed as a **motion**, not an adversary proceeding
- Common example: judgment lien on homestead

**Lien stripping in Chapter 13:**
- Wholly unsecured junior liens on residence can be stripped (removed)
- Requires an adversary proceeding or motion (varies by district)
- California courts generally allow by motion

**Unperfected security interests (§ 544(a)):**
- Trustee can avoid security interests that were not properly perfected (e.g., UCC filing not made)
- The trustee has the powers of a hypothetical bona fide purchaser or judicial lien creditor

## Procedure for Adversary Proceedings

### Filing

1. **Complaint** filed with the bankruptcy court (Official Form B1040)
2. **Filing fee:** Currently $350 (may be waived for debtors)
3. **Summons** issued by the clerk
4. **Service** of complaint and summons on defendant

### Timeline

| Event | Deadline |
|-------|----------|
| Answer or motion to dismiss | 30 days after service |
| Discovery | Court sets schedule (typically 90-180 days) |
| Pre-trial conference | Court discretion |
| Trial | Court sets date |
| Decision | After trial or summary judgment |

### Discovery

Full discovery is available, similar to civil litigation:
- Interrogatories
- Requests for production of documents
- Requests for admission
- Depositions
- Subpoenas

### Settlement

Most adversary proceedings settle before trial. Common resolutions:
- Stipulated judgment for reduced amount
- Payment plan agreement
- Consent to discharge (or non-discharge) of specific debt
- Return of portion of transferred property

### Appeal

Appeals from adversary proceedings go to:
- **Bankruptcy Appellate Panel (BAP)** for the 9th Circuit, OR
- **U.S. District Court** (at the election of any party)
- Then to the **9th Circuit Court of Appeals**

## Trustee's Strong-Arm Powers (§ 544)

The trustee has special powers to avoid certain transfers:

| Power | Section | Description |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Judicial lien creditor | § 544(a)(1) | Trustee has the rights of a hypothetical creditor with a judicial lien on all debtor's property |
| Bona fide purchaser | § 544(a)(3) | Trustee has the rights of a bona fide purchaser of real property |
| Successor to unsecured creditor | § 544(b) | Trustee can use the avoidance rights of any actual unsecured creditor (including state law) |

**Practical impact:**
- Unrecorded deeds → trustee can avoid the transfer
- Unperfected security interests → trustee takes priority
- Transfers avoidable under California's CUVTA → trustee can reach back 4 years

---

## Damages and Remedies

Courts can award:
- Return of transferred property (or its value)
- Money judgment for the amount of the preference or fraudulent transfer
- Attorneys' fees (in some circumstances)
- Punitive damages (rare, for egregious fraud)
- Injunctive relief

## Strategic Considerations

### For Debtors

- **Respond to complaints promptly** — default judgments are enforceable
- **Consider settlement** — litigation is expensive and uncertain
- **Review pre-bankruptcy transfers carefully** — trustees routinely scrutinize transfers to family
- **Complete all requirements** — missing debtor education or schedules invites § 727 objections

### For Creditors

- **Watch deadlines** — the 60-day bar date for § 523(a)(2), (4), (6) is strict
- **File proof of claim** even while pursuing adversary proceeding
- **Gather evidence early** — discovery in bankruptcy is faster than state court
- **Cost-benefit analysis** — litigation costs may exceed the debt

### For Trustees

- **Review bank statements** — look for unusual transfers in the 90-day (and 1-year insider) window
- **Use California's 4-year CUVTA reach** via § 544(b) when federal § 548's 2-year period is too short
- **Small preference threshold** — don't pursue transfers below $7,575 (cost-prohibitive)
- **Demand letters first** — many preference defendants return funds voluntarily

---

## Key California-Specific Notes

1. **CUVTA (Uniform Voidable Transactions Act)** replaced the older UFTA in 2016 — same substance, updated terminology
2. **4-year statute of limitations** for fraudulent transfers under state law (vs. 2 years federal)
3. **Lien stripping** in Chapter 13 is well-established in the 9th Circuit (In re Zimmer, In re Lam)
4. **BAP elections** — either party can elect district court over BAP for appeals
5. **Local rules** vary significantly between California's four bankruptcy districts — always check
