# Chapter 7 Bankruptcy in California

## What Is Chapter 7?

Chapter 7 bankruptcy — known as "liquidation bankruptcy" — is the fastest and most common form of personal bankruptcy. It allows individuals to discharge most unsecured debts in exchange for surrendering non-exempt assets to a court-appointed trustee. In practice, the vast majority of Chapter 7 cases in California are "no-asset" cases, meaning the filer's assets are fully protected by California's exemptions and creditors receive nothing.

The entire process from filing to discharge typically takes **3 to 6 months**.

## Who Is Eligible for Chapter 7?

### 1. The Means Test
The primary eligibility hurdle is the **Means Test** (Official Form 122A). You automatically pass if your average monthly income over the past 6 months is below California's median income for your household size. If you exceed that threshold, you must complete a more detailed calculation of allowable expenses.

### 2. Prior Bankruptcy Restrictions
- Cannot have received a Chapter 7 discharge within the **past 8 years**
- Cannot have received a Chapter 13 discharge within the **past 6 years** (with some exceptions)
- Cannot have had a bankruptcy case dismissed within the **past 180 days** for willful failure to comply with court orders

### 3. Credit Counseling Requirement
You must complete an approved **credit counseling course** within 180 days before filing. The certificate must be filed with the court.

## The Chapter 7 Process Step by Step

1. Complete credit counseling from an approved agency
2. Gather financial documents (pay stubs 60 days, tax returns 2 years, bank statements, debt statements)
3. Complete and file the petition — pay the $338 filing fee (fee waivers available)
4. Automatic stay activates immediately
5. Trustee is assigned
6. 341 Meeting of Creditors — held 20–40 days after filing
7. Trustee reviews assets
8. Creditor objection deadline — 60 days after the 341 meeting
9. Complete debtor education course
10. Discharge order — typically 60–90 days after the 341 meeting

## What Happens to Your Assets?

### Non-Exempt Assets
The trustee can sell non-exempt assets to pay creditors. Common non-exempt assets include:
- Second vehicles above the exemption limit
- Vacation homes or investment properties
- Valuable collections above exemption limits
- Cash or bank account balances above the wildcard exemption

### Exempt Assets (Protected)
California allows filers to choose between two exemption systems. Key protections:
- Primary residence (homestead): up to $600,000 depending on county
- One motor vehicle: up to $3,625 (System 1 with wildcard can be much more)
- Retirement accounts: generally 100% exempt

## What Debts Are Discharged?

**Typically discharged:**
- Credit card debt
- Medical bills
- Personal loans (unsecured)
- Utility arrears
- Most civil judgments
- Deficiency balances after repossession

**Not dischargeable in Chapter 7:**
- Student loans (except rare hardship cases)
- Child support and alimony
- Most tax debts less than 3 years old
- Debts from fraud, willful misconduct, or false financial statements
- Criminal fines and restitution
- Debts from DUI-related injuries

## Secured Debts in Chapter 7

Chapter 7 discharges personal liability but does not eliminate liens. For secured debts:

| Option | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Surrender** | Give property back to lender; discharged of any deficiency |
| **Reaffirm** | Sign reaffirmation agreement to remain personally liable and keep the property |
| **Redeem** | Pay lender the current value of the collateral in a lump sum |
