## Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus

### Overview

Type 1 diabetes mellitus (T1DM) is a chronic autoimmune disease characterized by immune-mediated destruction of pancreatic beta cells, resulting in absolute insulin deficiency. It accounts for approximately 5-10% of all diabetes cases. T1DM can occur at any age, though peak incidence occurs in childhood and adolescence (bimodal peaks: ages 4-6 and 10-14). Without exogenous insulin, T1DM is rapidly life-threatening.

---

### Pathophysiology and Diagnosis

#### Autoimmune Mechanism

The pathogenesis of T1DM involves a combination of genetic predisposition and environmental triggers that initiate T-cell-mediated destruction of pancreatic beta cells. The process often begins years before clinical hyperglycemia manifests. HLA class II alleles (DR3-DQ2 and DR4-DQ8 haplotypes) confer the highest genetic risk. The presence of islet autoantibodies identifies individuals at high risk for progression to clinical T1DM.

**Islet Autoantibody Markers**

| Antibody | Target | Sensitivity | Notes |
|----------|--------|-------------|-------|
| Anti-GAD65 (GADA) | Glutamic acid decarboxylase 65 | 70-80% | Most sensitive single marker; also elevated in LADA |
| Anti-IA-2 (IA-2A) | Islet antigen 2 (tyrosine phosphatase) | 55-75% | Predicts faster progression to T1DM |
| Anti-ZnT8 (ZnT8A) | Zinc transporter 8 | 60-80% | Present in ~25% negative for other antibodies |
| Anti-insulin (IAA) | Insulin | 50-70% in children | Most prevalent in young children; disappears after insulin therapy |
| Anti-islet cell (ICA) | Multiple beta-cell antigens | 70-80% | Older assay; largely replaced by specific antibody testing |

- Presence of 2 or more autoantibodies: near 100% lifetime risk of clinical T1DM
- Presence of 1 autoantibody: significant risk (~15% over 5 years, variable)
- Teplizumab (Tzield): FDA-approved anti-CD3 monoclonal antibody that delays onset of Stage 3 T1DM by ~2 years when given to high-risk individuals (Stage 2: dysglycemia + 2 antibodies)

#### LADA — Latent Autoimmune Diabetes in Adults

LADA is adult-onset autoimmune diabetes with slower beta-cell destruction than classic childhood T1DM. Often initially misdiagnosed as T2DM.

**Characteristics:**
- Age of onset typically >30 years
- Lean or normal BMI at presentation
- Anti-GAD65 antibody positive (most sensitive marker for LADA)
- Preserved C-peptide initially, declining over months to years
- Does not require insulin at diagnosis but progresses to insulin dependence (typically within 5-10 years, often sooner)
- Should not be treated with sulfonylureas (accelerate beta-cell loss)
- SPIDDM (Slowly Progressive Insulin-Dependent Diabetes Mellitus) — overlapping term used in Japan

**LADA vs T1DM vs T2DM Differentiation:**

| Feature | T1DM | LADA | T2DM |
|---------|------|------|------|
| Age of onset | Usually <30 | Usually >30 | Usually >40 |
| BMI | Normal/low | Normal/slightly elevated | Overweight/obese |
| GAD65 antibody | Positive | Positive | Negative |
| C-peptide at diagnosis | Low/absent | Normal initially | Normal/elevated |
| Rate of progression to insulin | Rapid (weeks-months) | Slow (months-years) | Slow (years) |
| Ketosis at presentation | Common | Rare initially | Rare |
| Family history of T2DM | No | Sometimes | Common |

#### Diagnostic Criteria for Diabetes Mellitus

The ADA diagnostic criteria apply to all types of diabetes. Any ONE of the following, confirmed on repeat testing (unless clinically unambiguous):

| Test | Diabetes Threshold | Prediabetes Range | Normal |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|--------|
| Fasting plasma glucose (FPG) | ≥126 mg/dL | 100-125 mg/dL (IFG) | <100 mg/dL |
| 2-hour plasma glucose during 75g OGTT | ≥200 mg/dL | 140-199 mg/dL (IGT) | <140 mg/dL |
| Hemoglobin A1c | ≥6.5% | 5.7-6.4% | <5.7% |
| Random plasma glucose with symptoms | ≥200 mg/dL + classic symptoms | N/A | N/A |

- Classic symptoms of hyperglycemia: polyuria, polydipsia, unexplained weight loss, blurred vision, fatigue
- In the absence of unambiguous hyperglycemia, diagnosis requires two abnormal results from the same sample OR one from each of two separate samples
- A1c testing is not reliable in: hemolytic anemia, sickle cell disease, recent transfusion, G6PD deficiency, iron deficiency anemia (falsely elevated A1c), pregnancy (use glucose-based testing)

#### DKA as Presenting Feature

Approximately 30% of new T1DM diagnoses present in diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA). Higher rates in younger children and in areas with less access to healthcare. DKA as presentation is more common in:
- Delayed diagnosis (symptoms for several weeks before presentation)
- Lower socioeconomic status
- Children under 5 years old
- Absence of first-degree relative with T1DM (no family recognition of symptoms)

#### C-Peptide in T1DM

C-peptide is produced in equimolar amounts with endogenous insulin during proinsulin cleavage. It is therefore a marker of residual beta-cell function.

- T1DM: low or undetectable C-peptide (<0.6 ng/mL fasting, <1.0 ng/mL stimulated)
- T2DM/LADA: normal or elevated C-peptide (may be low in long-standing T2DM)
- Clinical use: differentiates T1DM from T2DM, guides insulin initiation in LADA, measures honeymoon phase preservation
- Honeymoon phase: transient partial remission in new T1DM after insulin initiation; C-peptide may briefly normalize; insulin requirements decrease; may last weeks to 2 years

### Insulin Therapy

#### Insulin Pharmacokinetics

| Type | Onset | Peak | Duration | Examples |
|------|-------|------|----------|---------|
| Rapid-acting analogs | 10-15 min | 1-2h | 3-5h | Lispro (Humalog), Aspart (NovoLog), Glulisine (Apidra) |
| Faster rapid-acting | 2.5-5 min | 1.5-2h | 3-5h | Faster aspart (Fiasp), Lispro-aabc (Lyumjev) |
| Short-acting (Regular) | 30-60 min | 2-4h | 6-8h | Regular human insulin (Humulin R, Novolin R) |
| Intermediate (NPH) | 2-4h | 4-10h | 12-18h | NPH/isophane (Humulin N, Novolin N) |
| Long-acting analogs | 1-2h | Peakless | 20-24h | Glargine U-100 (Lantus, Basaglar), Detemir (Levemir) |
| Long-acting concentrated | 6h | Peakless | 24h | Glargine U-300 (Toujeo) — 3x concentration |
| Ultra-long-acting | 6h | Peakless | 36-42h | Degludec U-100/U-200 (Tresiba) |
| Premixed (rapid/intermediate) | Variable | Biphasic | 10-16h | 70/30 (NPH/Regular), 75/25 and 50/50 (lispro mixtures) |
| Inhaled insulin | 12-15 min | 30 min | 3h | Technosphere insulin (Afrezza) — pre-meal only |

**Key pharmacokinetic notes:**
- Glargine U-300 has a longer, flatter profile than U-100 with less peak effect; dose is NOT interchangeable 1:1 (typically 10-18% dose increase when switching from U-100 to U-300)
- Degludec accumulates over 3-4 days to steady state; very long half-life allows once-daily dosing with flexible timing
- Detemir has more pronounced peak than glargine; may require twice-daily dosing in some patients
- Inhaled insulin (Afrezza) is contraindicated in chronic lung disease (COPD, asthma); requires spirometry before initiation
- Concentrated regular insulin U-500 (Humulin R U-500): reserved for severe insulin resistance; has intermediate-acting profile

#### Basal-Bolus Regimen (Physiologic Replacement)

**Total Daily Dose (TDD) Calculation:**
- Initial dose (newly diagnosed T1DM, not in DKA): 0.4-0.5 units/kg/day
- Well-controlled T1DM: typical range 0.5-1.0 units/kg/day
- Higher doses needed in: puberty, obesity, illness, glucocorticoid therapy
- Lower doses needed in: honeymoon phase, renal failure, adrenal insufficiency

**Dose Distribution:**
- Basal insulin: 40-50% of TDD given as once-daily long-acting (or twice-daily detemir/NPH)
- Bolus insulin: 50-60% of TDD split among meals (roughly 1/3 at each meal, adjusted for carb content)

**Insulin-to-Carbohydrate Ratio (ICR) — 500 Rule:**
- ICR = 500 / TDD
- Example: TDD = 50 units → ICR = 500/50 = 10 → 1 unit covers 10g carbohydrates
- Adjust empirically: if postprandial glucose rises >50 mg/dL above premeal, ICR too low (increase insulin); if postprandial <50 mg/dL or hypoglycemia occurs, ICR too high

**Insulin Sensitivity Factor (ISF) — 1800 Rule:**
- ISF = 1800 / TDD (for rapid-acting analogs)
- ISF = 1500 / TDD (for regular insulin)
- Example: TDD = 50 units → ISF = 1800/50 = 36 → 1 unit drops glucose by ~36 mg/dL
- Use to calculate correction doses

**Correction Dose Formula:**
```
Correction dose = (Current glucose - Target glucose) / ISF
Target glucose: typically 100-120 mg/dL
```

Example: Current glucose 280, target 100, ISF 36 = (280-100)/36 = 5 units correction

**Stacking insulin:** Correction doses given while previous correction is still active (within 2-3 hours) can cause hypoglycemia. Most CGM systems calculate "insulin on board" (IOB) to prevent stacking.

#### Continuous Subcutaneous Insulin Infusion (CSII) — Insulin Pump

**Indications:**
- Suboptimal glycemic control on multiple daily injections (MDI)
- Frequent or severe hypoglycemia
- Hypoglycemia unawareness
- Wide glycemic variability
- Dawn phenomenon (early morning glucose rise)
- Pregnancy (tight control needed)
- Patient preference/lifestyle

**Pump Programming:**
- Basal rates: can program multiple rates throughout 24 hours (e.g., lower overnight, higher pre-dawn)
- Bolus types:
  - Normal (standard): all insulin delivered immediately — for fast-absorbing foods
  - Extended (square wave): delivers insulin over 1-8 hours — for fat/protein-rich meals, gastroparesis
  - Multiwave (combination): portion immediately + remainder extended — for complex meals

**Pump Initiation:**
- Starting basal rate: TDD x 0.5 (basal portion) / 24 hours = hourly rate
- ICR and ISF carried over from MDI settings

**Pump Troubleshooting:**
- Unexplained hyperglycemia: check infusion site (lipohypertrophy, kinked cannula, air bubbles), check reservoir, replace site and tubing, give MDI correction with separate pen as backup
- DKA risk: pump failure, site issues, or battery failure can lead to rapid ketosis within 4-6 hours (no subcutaneous depot)

#### Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM)

**Key Metrics:**

| Metric | Description | Target (ADA) |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| Time in Range (TIR) | % time 70-180 mg/dL | >70% |
| Time Below Range Level 1 (TBR1) | % time <70 mg/dL | <4% |
| Time Below Range Level 2 (TBR2) | % time <54 mg/dL | <1% |
| Time Above Range Level 1 (TAR1) | % time 180-250 mg/dL | <25% |
| Time Above Range Level 2 (TAR2) | % time >250 mg/dL | <5% |
| GMI (Glucose Management Indicator) | A1c estimate from CGM data | Varies by target |
| Coefficient of Variation (CV) | Glycemic variability measure | <36% (stable) |
| Mean glucose | Average sensor glucose | Varies |

**CGM Devices:**
- Real-time CGM (rtCGM): Dexcom G7 (10-day wear, no fingerstick calibration), Libre 3 (14-day, real-time transmission to phone), Medtronic Guardian 4 (integrated with 780G pump)
- Intermittently scanned CGM (isCGM): Libre 2 (scan to see value, alarms available)
- Professional CGM: blinded/unblinded retrospective use for assessment; 14-day wear

**CGM Integration with Insulin:**
- Predictive low glucose suspend (PLGS): pauses pump when predicted to drop below threshold
- Closed-loop systems use CGM as input for automated insulin delivery

#### Hybrid Closed-Loop Systems (Artificial Pancreas)

Closed-loop (CL) systems combine CGM + insulin pump + algorithm to automate basal insulin delivery. Bolus dosing still requires user input for meals.

| System | Algorithm Type | Special Features |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| Tandem Control-IQ | Hybrid closed-loop (Dexcom G7) | Automatic bolus for high glucose; sleep mode |
| MiniMed 780G (Medtronic) | Advanced hybrid closed-loop | Auto bolus every 5 min; SmartGuard; AHCL mode |
| Omnipod 5 (Insulet) | Hybrid closed-loop, tubeless (Dexcom G6/G7) | Podders' app; no tubing |
| iAPS / OpenAPS | DIY open-source algorithm | Variable; community-developed |
| Loop / AAPS | DIY open-source (iPhone / Android) | Requires unlocked pump hardware |

**Clinical Outcomes with AID Systems:**
- MiniMed 780G: TIR increased to ~78-80% in pivotal trials
- Control-IQ: TIR increased to ~72% (pivotal trial)
- All AID systems reduce A1c and TBR compared to sensor-augmented pump (SAP) therapy
- Particularly effective overnight (patient not actively managing)

### Sick Day Management (T1DM)

Intercurrent illness causes stress-hormone release (cortisol, epinephrine, glucagon) leading to insulin resistance and elevated glucose. Risk of DKA is significantly increased.

**Core Principles:**
- NEVER stop insulin during illness, even if not eating — insulin requirements increase, not decrease
- Check blood glucose every 2-4 hours around the clock
- Check ketones every 2-4 hours (urine or blood; blood beta-hydroxybutyrate preferred if available)
- Maintain hydration aggressively

**Supplemental Insulin for Hyperglycemia with Ketones:**
- Blood glucose >250 mg/dL with ketones: add 10-20% of TDD as supplemental rapid-acting insulin every 2-4 hours
- Blood ketones >0.6 mmol/L: treat aggressively with supplemental insulin and fluids
- Blood ketones >1.5 mmol/L with glucose >250: high risk for DKA progression — contact provider

**Fluid Management During Illness:**
- If unable to eat solid food: consume sugar-containing clear fluids (juice, regular soda, sports drinks, broth)
- Target: 8 oz (240 mL) fluid per hour
- If vomiting and unable to keep fluids down: emergency room evaluation needed

**Ketone-Positive Vomiting = Emergency**

**DKA Warning Signs Requiring Emergency Care:**
- Persistent vomiting preventing fluid intake
- Blood ketones >3.0 mmol/L or large urine ketones
- Blood glucose >400 mg/dL not responding to supplemental insulin
- Symptoms of DKA: abdominal pain, Kussmaul breathing (rapid deep breathing), fruity breath (acetone), altered mental status
- Rapidly worsening condition despite home management

## Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus

### Pathophysiology

Type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) is a complex metabolic disorder characterized by progressive insulin resistance in peripheral tissues combined with relative insulin deficiency from beta-cell dysfunction. It accounts for approximately 90-95% of all diabetes cases.

#### The Ominous Octet (Ralph DeFronzo)

Eight simultaneous pathophysiologic defects drive T2DM:

1. **Decreased insulin secretion** — progressive beta-cell failure (loss of first-phase insulin response early; eventual 50-80% beta-cell mass loss at diagnosis)
2. **Decreased incretin effect** — reduced GLP-1 and GIP response to meals; normally responsible for 50-70% of postprandial insulin secretion
3. **Increased lipolysis** — elevated free fatty acids from adipocytes worsen insulin resistance in muscle and liver
4. **Increased glucagon secretion** — alpha-cell dysfunction leads to fasting and postprandial hyperglucagonemia; suppression by GLP-1 is impaired
5. **Increased hepatic glucose production** — increased gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis; normally suppressed by insulin
6. **Impaired glucose transport** — skeletal muscle insulin resistance (reduced GLUT4 translocation and glucose uptake); accounts for majority of postprandial glucose disposal
7. **Increased renal glucose reabsorption** — SGLT2 upregulation in diabetes increases renal glucose threshold from ~180 to ~240 mg/dL, reabsorbing more glucose
8. **Neurotransmitter dysfunction** — CNS insulin resistance impairs satiety signaling; hypothalamic glucose sensing disrupted

#### NASH/Obesity Pathway

- Visceral adiposity → increased portal FFA flux → hepatic steatosis → NASH
- Hepatic fat directly impairs insulin signaling → increased hepatic glucose production
- Adipokine dysregulation: decreased adiponectin (insulin-sensitizing), increased resistin and TNF-alpha (insulin-desensitizing)
- Ectopic fat in skeletal muscle (intramyocellular lipid) impairs glucose uptake
- Weight loss of 5-10% improves insulin sensitivity; >10-15% can induce T2DM remission
- GLP-1 agonist/tirzepatide-induced remission: 20-40% of T2DM patients achieve A1c <6.5% off medications with sustained weight loss

### Diagnostic Criteria

**Same ADA thresholds as for T1DM (see table above).**

**Prediabetes:**

| Category | FPG (mg/dL) | 2h OGTT (mg/dL) | A1c (%) |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|---------|
| Impaired Fasting Glucose (IFG) | 100-125 | — | — |
| Impaired Glucose Tolerance (IGT) | — | 140-199 | — |
| Prediabetes by A1c | — | — | 5.7-6.4% |

- Risk of progression from prediabetes to T2DM: ~5-10% per year; 15-30% over 5 years without intervention
- Diabetes Prevention Program (DPP): intensive lifestyle intervention (7% weight loss + 150 min/week exercise) reduced T2DM risk by 58%; metformin reduced by 31%
- USPSTF recommends prediabetes counseling and referral to intensive lifestyle program

**T2DM Screening Recommendations (ADA 2024):**
- All adults age 35-70 who are overweight or obese: screen every 3 years
- Any age with BMI ≥25 (or ≥23 in Asian Americans) plus one or more risk factors:
  - First-degree relative with diabetes
  - High-risk race/ethnicity (Black, Hispanic, Native American, Asian, Pacific Islander)
  - Hypertension (≥140/90 mmHg or on treatment)
  - HDL <35 mg/dL or triglycerides >250 mg/dL
  - Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
  - Physical inactivity
  - History of gestational diabetes
  - Previous A1c 5.7-6.4% or IFG/IGT
  - Acanthosis nigricans (insulin resistance marker)

### Glycemic Targets

#### A1c Targets by Patient Profile

| Population | A1c Target | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Most non-pregnant adults | <7.0% | Balance efficacy vs hypoglycemia risk |
| Younger patients, short duration, no CVD | <6.5% | Tighter control if achievable safely |
| Elderly, complex/intermediate health | <8.0% | Hypoglycemia risk; limited benefit from tight control |
| Very elderly, multiple comorbidities, limited life expectancy | <8.5% or individualized | Avoid hypoglycemia; quality of life focus |
| Pregnant (T1DM or T2DM) | 6.0-7.0% | Fetal outcomes; minimize DKA risk |
| Gestational diabetes | See GDM section | — |

#### Self-Monitoring of Blood Glucose (SMBG) Targets

- Fasting / pre-meal: 80-130 mg/dL
- 2-hour postprandial: <180 mg/dL
- Bedtime: 100-140 mg/dL

#### CGM Time in Range Targets (T2DM)

- TIR (70-180 mg/dL): >70%
- TBR (<70 mg/dL): <4%
- Elderly/high-risk: TIR >50%, TBR (<70) <1%

### T2DM Pharmacotherapy — Step Therapy

#### Lifestyle Modification (Foundational)

- Medical Nutrition Therapy (MNT): reduce refined carbohydrates and added sugars; increase fiber; Mediterranean or low-carb diet preferred
- Physical activity: 150 min/week moderate aerobic + resistance training 2-3x/week; reduce prolonged sitting
- Weight loss: 5% weight loss improves glycemia; bariatric surgery for BMI ≥35 with T2DM (Roux-en-Y gastric bypass, sleeve gastrectomy)

---

#### Metformin — First-Line Pharmacotherapy

**Mechanism:** Primarily inhibits hepatic gluconeogenesis via AMPK activation and Complex I of the mitochondrial respiratory chain. Secondary effects: improve peripheral glucose uptake, reduce intestinal glucose absorption.

**Dosing:**
- Initial: 500 mg once daily with evening meal (minimize GI side effects)
- Titration: increase by 500 mg weekly as tolerated
- Target dose: 1000 mg twice daily with meals (most effective dose range)
- Maximum approved dose: 2550 mg/day (2000 mg/day is typically maximum effective dose)
- Extended-release (XR) formulation: take once daily with evening meal; less GI side effects

**Contraindications and Precautions:**

| Condition | Recommendation |
|-----------|----------------|
| eGFR <45 mL/min/1.73m2 | Use with caution; reassess frequently |
| eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m2 | Contraindicated (lactic acidosis risk) |
| Iodinated contrast dye (IV) | Hold on day of procedure; restart 48h later if renal function stable |
| Acute dehydration, illness, surgery | Hold temporarily |
| Hepatic impairment | Avoid (impaired lactate clearance) |
| Alcohol use disorder | Caution (increased lactic acidosis risk) |
| Upcoming general anesthesia | Hold day before and day of procedure |

**A1c Lowering:** ~1-1.5% (dose-dependent)

**Benefits Beyond Glucose:**
- Weight: neutral to modest loss (~1-2 kg)
- Cardiovascular: neutral to modest benefit (UKPDS showed CV benefit vs sulfonylurea)
- Cost: very low (generic available)
- Cancer: possible reduced incidence/mortality (observational data; mechanism unclear)
- Vitamin B12: reduces B12 absorption; check B12 annually in long-term users; supplement if deficient

**Side Effects:**
- GI (most common): nausea, diarrhea, abdominal discomfort; occurs in 20-30%; usually improves over weeks
- Mitigation: start low, titrate slowly, take with food, switch to XR formulation
- Lactic acidosis: rare (<1 per 100,000 patient-years); risk elevated in renal failure, hepatic failure, sepsis, hypoxia
- Metallic taste

#### Add-On Therapy — Patient-Centered Algorithm

The ADA recommends selecting add-on agents based on:
1. Presence of established cardiovascular disease (ASCVD) or high CV risk
2. Heart failure (HF) — HFrEF or HFpEF
3. Chronic kidney disease (CKD)
4. Need for weight loss
5. Need to minimize hypoglycemia
6. Cost/access considerations

### GLP-1 Receptor Agonists

**Mechanism:** Activate GLP-1 receptor → increase glucose-stimulated insulin secretion, suppress glucagon, slow gastric emptying, reduce appetite centrally.

**Cardiovascular Outcome Trials (CVOTs):**

| Drug | Trial | CV Outcome |
|------|-------|-----------|
| Liraglutide | LEADER | 13% MACE reduction vs placebo |
| Semaglutide injectable | SUSTAIN-6 | 26% MACE reduction |
| Dulaglutide | REWIND | 12% MACE reduction; broad T2DM population |
| Exenatide ER | EXSCEL | Neutral (non-inferior) |
| Albiglutide | HARMONY | 22% MACE reduction |
| Semaglutide oral | PIONEER 6 | Non-inferior (trend toward benefit) |

**Individual Agents:**

**Liraglutide (Victoza):**
- Dosing: 0.6 mg SQ daily x 1 week → 1.2 mg daily → max 1.8 mg daily
- A1c reduction: 1.0-1.5%
- Weight reduction: 3-5 kg
- CV: LEADER trial — MACE reduction, HF hospitalization reduction; indicated for CV risk reduction in T2DM + CVD
- Obesity dose (Saxenda): 3.0 mg daily

**Semaglutide injectable (Ozempic):**
- Dosing: 0.25 mg SQ weekly x 4 weeks → 0.5 mg weekly → may increase to 1 mg or 2 mg weekly
- A1c reduction: 1.5-2.0%
- Weight reduction: 4-6 kg (2 mg dose)
- CV: SUSTAIN-6 — MACE reduction; SELECT trial (semaglutide 2.4 mg) — 20% MACE reduction in overweight/obese adults with CVD but without diabetes
- Obesity dose (Wegovy): 0.25 mg/week, titrate over 16 weeks to 2.4 mg/week

**Semaglutide oral (Rybelsus):**
- Dosing: 3 mg daily x 30 days → 7 mg daily → max 14 mg daily
- Administration: take on empty stomach with ≤4 oz (120 mL) plain water; wait 30 minutes before eating or other medications
- A1c reduction: 1.0-1.5% (slightly less than injectable due to bioavailability ~1% oral)
- Weight reduction: 2-4 kg

**Dulaglutide (Trulicity):**
- Dosing: 0.75 mg SQ weekly → 1.5 mg → 3 mg → max 4.5 mg weekly
- Auto-injector single-use pen; refrigerated (can store at room temp up to 14 days)
- A1c reduction: 1.1-1.5%
- Weight reduction: 2-4 kg
- CV: REWIND — MACE reduction; included patients with low CV risk

**Exenatide (Byetta):**
- Dosing: 5 mcg SQ twice daily within 60 minutes before the 2 largest meals; increase to 10 mcg BID after 1 month if tolerated
- Extended-release: Bydureon BCise 2 mg SQ weekly
- Older agent; more GI side effects than newer agents
- Renal: avoid if eGFR <30 mL/min

**Tirzepatide (Mounjaro) — Dual GIP/GLP-1 Agonist:**
- Mechanism: activates both GIP and GLP-1 receptors → additive/synergistic effects on insulin secretion, glucagon suppression, weight loss
- Dosing: 2.5 mg SQ weekly x 4 weeks → increase by 2.5 mg every 4 weeks as tolerated → maximum 15 mg weekly
- A1c reduction: 2.0-2.5% (superior to all GLP-1 monotherapy in head-to-head trials — SURPASS series)
- Weight reduction: 12-22.5% body weight (SURMOUNT-1 trial — 15 mg dose)
- CV: SURMOUNT-MMO trial — significant MACE reduction in obese/overweight adults
- Obesity dose (Zepbound): same dosing, approved for chronic weight management
- Side effects: same as GLP-1 class (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea predominate during titration)

**GLP-1 Class Side Effects:**
- GI: nausea (most common, 20-40%), vomiting, diarrhea — typically transient, improves over 4-8 weeks; slow titration reduces incidence
- Pancreatitis: rare but reported; discontinue if suspected pancreatitis; avoid in history of pancreatitis
- Thyroid C-cell tumors: rodent data only; no confirmed human risk, but black box warning requires avoiding in: personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC) or Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia type 2 (MEN2)
- Gallbladder: cholelithiasis and cholecystitis (increased risk with rapid weight loss)
- Injection site reactions: typically mild
- Tachycardia: mild heart rate increase (~2-4 bpm)
- Diabetic retinopathy worsening: with rapid A1c reduction (semaglutide SUSTAIN-6); monitor ophthalmology if pre-existing retinopathy

**GLP-1 Contraindications:**
- Personal or family history of MTC
- MEN2 syndrome
- Pregnancy (teratogenic in animal studies; discontinue 2 months before planned conception)
- Hypersensitivity to the agent

### SGLT2 Inhibitors (Sodium-Glucose Cotransporter-2 Inhibitors)

**Mechanism:** Block SGLT2 protein in the proximal renal tubule → prevent renal glucose reabsorption → glucosuria (~60-70g glucose excreted per day). Secondary effects: osmotic diuresis, natriuresis, weight loss, blood pressure reduction, reduced preload and afterload on heart, erythropoietin stimulation.

**Individual Agents:**

**Empagliflozin (Jardiance):**
- Dosing: 10 mg daily; can increase to 25 mg for additional A1c/weight benefit
- A1c reduction: 0.5-1.0%
- Weight reduction: 2-4 kg
- CV: EMPA-REG OUTCOME — 38% reduction in CV death, 35% reduction in HF hospitalization vs placebo in T2DM + CVD
- HF: EMPEROR-Reduced (HFrEF) and EMPEROR-Preserved (HFpEF) — reduced HF hospitalization regardless of diabetes status; approved for HF in both settings
- Renal: EMPA-KIDNEY — significant renal protection; approved for CKD (eGFR ≥20); initiated in patients with eGFR ≥20 if UACR ≥200 mg/g or eGFR 20-45 regardless of UACR

**Dapagliflozin (Farxiga):**
- Dosing: 5 mg daily; may increase to 10 mg for additional glycemic benefit (T2DM indication requires eGFR ≥45)
- CV: DECLARE-TIMI 58 — reduced HF hospitalization; neutral MACE overall; benefit in those with prior MI
- HF: DAPA-HF (HFrEF) and DELIVER (HFpEF) — reduced HF hospitalization/CV death regardless of diabetes status
- Renal: DAPA-CKD — significant renal protection in CKD with or without T2DM; approved for CKD with UACR ≥200 and eGFR ≥25
- Additional approval: acute myocardial infarction (reduces worsening HF after MI)

**Canagliflozin (Invokana):**
- Dosing: 100 mg daily before first meal; max 300 mg daily (for A1c and renal benefit, if eGFR ≥45)
- CV: CANVAS — 14% MACE reduction vs placebo; but significantly increased amputation risk (black box warning — monitor closely; avoid in PAD or prior amputation)
- Renal: CREDENCE — reduced renal outcomes in T2DM + DKD with macroalbuminuria

**Ertugliflozin (Steglatro):**
- 5-15 mg daily; VERTIS-CV trial — neutral MACE; reduced HF hospitalization
- Less data vs other agents; less commonly used

**SGLT2i Class Side Effects:**

| Side Effect | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| Genital mycotic infections (GMIs) | Vulvovaginal candidiasis (women ~10%), balanitis (men ~3%); treat with antifungal; recurrent GMIs may warrant stopping |
| UTI | Modestly increased risk; symptomatic UTIs reported |
| Fournier gangrene | Rare necrotizing fasciitis of perineum; FDA black box; discontinue immediately if suspected |
| Euglycemic DKA | Glucose may be only mildly elevated (150-250 mg/dL); mechanism: SGLT2i lower glucose while ketones rise; triggered by: fasting, surgery, low carb diet, alcohol, illness; check ketones (blood or urine) in sick patients on SGLT2i |
| Volume depletion | Orthostatic hypotension; reduce diuretics if needed; caution in elderly |
| Acute kidney injury (AKI) | From volume depletion; risk higher with concomitant diuretics/ACEi/NSAIDs in volume-depleted states; hold during acute illness |
| LDL increase | 4-8 mg/dL modest increase; monitor lipids |
| Lower limb amputation (canagliflozin) | Black box warning; higher risk in PAD |
| Bone fractures (canagliflozin) | Modest increased risk; mechanism unclear |

**SGLT2i Contraindications:**
- Type 1 diabetes without insulin coverage (euglycemic DKA risk; off-label only under specialist supervision)
- eGFR below drug-specific threshold (see individual agents above for glycemic indication; some have lower thresholds for CV/renal indications)
- Recurrent UTI or urogenital infections
- Pregnancy

**Perioperative Management:**
- Hold SGLT2 inhibitors 3-4 days before any elective surgery, major procedures, or anticipated prolonged fasting
- Restart after patient is tolerating oral intake and hemodynamically stable

### Sulfonylureas

**Mechanism:** Bind to SUR1 subunit of pancreatic K-ATP channels → channel closure → beta-cell depolarization → calcium influx → insulin secretion. Effect is glucose-independent (major limitation: hypoglycemia regardless of glucose level).

**Agents and Dosing:**

| Agent | Starting Dose | Max Dose | Renal Notes |
|-------|--------------|----------|-------------|
| Glipizide (Glucotrol) | 5 mg before breakfast | 40 mg/day (20 mg BID) | Preferred in CKD; inactive metabolites; can use to eGFR 10 |
| Glimepiride (Amaryl) | 1-2 mg daily | 8 mg/day | Use with caution in CKD; reduce dose |
| Glyburide (DiaBeta, Micronase) | 1.25-2.5 mg daily | 20 mg/day | AVOID in elderly and CKD; active metabolites accumulate; Beers Criteria |
| Glibenclamide | Same as glyburide | — | Avoid in CKD |

**A1c Lowering:** 1.0-1.5%

**Side Effects:**
- Hypoglycemia: most significant risk; potentiated by: missed meals, exercise, renal failure, drug interactions (fluconazole, CYP2C9 inhibitors), alcohol
- Weight gain: 2-4 kg (insulin secretagogue → anabolic effect)
- Sun sensitivity (photosensitivity)
- Disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol (glyburide, chlorpropamide)
- Hematologic: rare thrombocytopenia, hemolytic anemia

**Drug Interactions:**
- Fluconazole, clarithromycin, trimethoprim: increase sulfonylurea levels → profound hypoglycemia
- Beta-blockers: mask tachycardia symptoms of hypoglycemia; mask all but diaphoresis

### DPP-4 Inhibitors (Dipeptidyl Peptidase-4 Inhibitors)

**Mechanism:** Inhibit DPP-4 enzyme → prevent degradation of endogenous GLP-1 and GIP → modest increase in active incretin levels → glucose-dependent insulin secretion enhancement.

**Comparison to GLP-1 RA:** DPP-4i are oral, better tolerated (no GI side effects), but far less effective for weight loss and A1c reduction; no proven CV benefit.

| Agent | Dose | Renal Dose Adjustment |
|-------|------|-----------------------|
| Sitagliptin (Januvia) | 100 mg daily | 50 mg if eGFR 30-50; 25 mg if eGFR <30 |
| Saxagliptin (Onglyza) | 2.5-5 mg daily | 2.5 mg if eGFR ≤50 |
| Alogliptin (Nesina) | 25 mg daily | 12.5 mg if eGFR 30-60; 6.25 mg if eGFR <30 |
| Linagliptin (Tradjenta) | 5 mg daily | No adjustment (biliary excretion) — preferred in CKD |
| Vildagliptin | 50 mg BID | 50 mg once daily if eGFR <50 — not available in US |

**A1c Lowering:** 0.5-0.8%

**Side Effects:**
- Generally well tolerated; weight neutral
- Nasopharyngitis, upper respiratory infections
- Urinary tract infections
- Pancreatitis: rare but reported; FDA warning
- Arthralgia: FDA warning (severe joint pain, may resolve with discontinuation)
- Heart failure hospitalization: increased with saxagliptin (SAVOR-TIMI trial) and alogliptin (EXAMINE trial) — avoid in symptomatic HF; linagliptin and sitagliptin appear safer in HF
- Rare bullous pemphigoid (skin blistering)

### Thiazolidinediones (TZDs)

**Mechanism:** PPARgamma agonists → increase insulin sensitivity in adipose tissue, skeletal muscle, and liver → transcriptional regulation of glucose and lipid metabolism.

**Pioglitazone (Actos):**
- Dosing: 15 mg daily; increase to 30-45 mg daily as needed
- A1c reduction: 0.8-1.4%
- Weight change: gain 2-4 kg (fluid retention + adipogenesis)
- CV: PROactive trial — trend toward MACE reduction; significant reduction in MI and stroke secondary endpoint; shown to reduce recurrent MI/stroke in T2DM with prior stroke (IRIS trial)
- NASH benefit: reduces liver inflammation and fibrosis (FDA-approved for NASH as of 2024 at 15-45 mg)
- No dose adjustment for renal impairment (hepatically metabolized)

**Rosiglitazone (Avandia):**
- Increased LDL and MI risk vs pioglitazone; restricted use in US; generally avoided
- Pioglitazone is the preferred TZD

**TZD Class Side Effects:**

| Side Effect | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| Fluid retention and edema | Sodium and water retention; risk of HF decompensation |
| Heart failure | CONTRAINDICATED in NYHA class III-IV HF; use with caution in HF with symptoms |
| Bone fractures | Increased distal fractures (wrist, foot) — especially in women (PPARgamma shifts MSC differentiation away from osteoblasts toward adipocytes) |
| Bladder cancer | Pioglitazone — modest increased risk with long duration (>12 months); FDA warning; avoid in active/history of bladder cancer |
| Weight gain | Via adipogenesis and fluid retention |
| Macular edema | Rare; monitor vision |
| Hepatotoxicity | Rare with pioglitazone; monitor LFTs if symptomatic |

### Alpha-Glucosidase Inhibitors

**Mechanism:** Inhibit intestinal alpha-glucosidases → delay carbohydrate digestion and glucose absorption → reduce postprandial glucose excursions.

**Acarbose (Precose):**
- Dosing: 25 mg TID with first bite of each meal; increase to 50 mg TID after 4-8 weeks; max 100 mg TID (if >60 kg body weight)
- A1c reduction: 0.5-0.8% (primarily postprandial)
- Weight neutral
- Side effects: flatulence, bloating, diarrhea (very common — 30-60%; limits adherence; may improve over time); hepatotoxicity (rare)
- Contraindications: eGFR <30, IBD, bowel obstruction, cirrhosis
- Drug interaction: reduces digoxin absorption; hypoglycemia from acarbose alone treated with GLUCOSE (not sucrose — sucrose requires alpha-glucosidase to be absorbed)

**Miglitol (Glyset):**
- 25-100 mg TID with meals; similar profile to acarbose

### Insulin in T2DM

#### When to Initiate Insulin in T2DM

- A1c >10-12% at diagnosis with symptoms of hyperglycemia
- FPG >300 mg/dL
- Failure of 2-3 oral agents to achieve glycemic targets
- Pregnancy (T2DM or GDM requiring medication)
- Hospitalization or perioperative period

#### Basal Insulin Initiation

- Starting dose: 10 units SQ once daily at bedtime, OR 0.1-0.2 units/kg once daily
- Preferred agents: glargine (Lantus/Basaglar), degludec (Tresiba), or detemir (Levemir)
- Injection timing: bedtime preferred (targets fasting glucose); degludec can be given at any time of day

**Titration Algorithms:**

| Algorithm | Protocol |
|-----------|---------|
| 2-2-2 rule | Increase by 2 units every 2 days if fasting glucose >130 mg/dL |
| 3-3 rule | Increase by 3 units every 3 days if fasting glucose 121-160; 6 units if >160 mg/dL |
| 10% rule | Increase by 10% of current dose every 3 days |

- Stop titration if fasting glucose 80-130 mg/dL
- Reduce by 10-20% if fasting glucose <80 mg/dL or hypoglycemia occurs

#### Basal-Plus Regimen

- Add 1 dose of rapid-acting insulin at the largest meal if A1c still above target after optimizing basal
- Start at 4 units or 10% of basal dose
- Titrate based on postprandial glucose readings

#### Combination Injectable Therapies

| Product | Components | Dosing |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| Xultophy (iDegLira) | Degludec + Liraglutide 3.6 units:0.13 mg per unit dose | 10-50 dose steps (10 steps = 36 units degludec + 1.3 mg liraglutide) |
| Soliqua (iGlarLixi) | Glargine + Lixisenatide | 15-60 dose units |

- Advantages: fewer injections, less weight gain vs insulin alone, less GI side effects vs GLP-1 alone

## Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

### Pathophysiology

DKA results from absolute or relative insulin deficiency combined with excess counterregulatory hormones (glucagon, cortisol, epinephrine, growth hormone). The result is:

1. Unrestrained lipolysis → free fatty acids → hepatic ketogenesis (acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetone)
2. Impaired glucose uptake + increased gluconeogenesis → hyperglycemia
3. Osmotic diuresis → dehydration + electrolyte loss
4. Metabolic acidosis from ketones (anion gap acidosis)
5. Potassium shifts: acidosis drives K extracellularly → total body K depleted (via osmotic diuresis) but serum K may be normal or high initially

### Diagnosis

**ADA Diagnostic Criteria for DKA:**

| Parameter | Mild DKA | Moderate DKA | Severe DKA |
|-----------|----------|-------------|-----------|
| Plasma glucose | >250 mg/dL | >250 mg/dL | >250 mg/dL |
| Arterial pH | 7.25-7.30 | 7.00-7.24 | <7.00 |
| Serum bicarbonate | 15-18 mEq/L | 10-14 mEq/L | <10 mEq/L |
| Urine ketones | Positive | Positive | Positive |
| Serum ketones | Positive | Positive | Positive |
| Beta-hydroxybutyrate | >3 mmol/L | >3 mmol/L | >3 mmol/L |
| Anion gap | >10 | >12 | >12 |
| Mental status | Alert | Alert/drowsy | Stupor/coma |

**Euglycemic DKA:**
- Glucose may be <200 mg/dL in: SGLT2i use, pregnancy, partial insulin treatment, prolonged fasting
- Ketones and acidosis still present
- Increasing incidence due to widespread SGLT2i use
- Diagnosis requires high index of suspicion; check ketones in any patient on SGLT2i presenting with nausea/vomiting or acidosis

**Precipitating Factors — "6 I's":**
1. Infection (most common — 30-40%; UTI, pneumonia)
2. Insulin omission/non-compliance
3. Ischemia (MI, CVA, mesenteric ischemia)
4. Intoxication (alcohol, cocaine)
5. Infarction (myocardial, cerebral)
6. Iatrogenic (SGLT2i, steroids, antipsychotics, immunotherapy)

**Initial Workup:**
- CBC, BMP, serum ketones or beta-hydroxybutyrate (BHB), blood gas (VBG acceptable for monitoring), UA with ketones, ECG, blood cultures, urine culture, chest X-ray
- Anion gap = Na - (Cl + HCO3); normal 8-12; elevated in DKA typically >20
- Corrected sodium: add 1.6 mEq/L Na per 100 mg/dL glucose above 100 (dilutional hyponatremia from osmotic shift)

### Management Protocol

#### Hour 0-1: Initial Resuscitation and Assessment

1. IV access (large bore x2), cardiac monitor, O2 if SpO2 <92%
2. Labs: BMP, blood gas, ketones, CBC, cultures, ECG
3. IV fluid: **Normal saline (0.9% NaCl) 1 L IV over first hour** (or 15-20 mL/kg/hr in hypotension)
4. Check potassium BEFORE starting insulin

#### Potassium Management (Critical Step)

| Potassium Level | Action |
|-----------------|--------|
| K <3.5 mEq/L | HOLD INSULIN — give 40 mEq/hr KCl until K ≥3.5 mEq/L |
| K 3.5-5.5 mEq/L | Add 20-40 mEq KCl to each liter of IV fluid; start insulin |
| K >5.5 mEq/L | Start insulin; no potassium replacement; recheck q2h |

**Rationale:** Insulin drives potassium intracellularly. Starting insulin with hypokalemia can cause fatal hypokalemia-induced arrhythmia.

#### Fluid Replacement

- Typical total body water deficit: 3-6 L in adults (DKA); 8-10 L in HHS
- After initial NS bolus: switch to 0.45% NaCl (half-normal saline) at 250-500 mL/hr
- Add KCl to fluids as per table above
- When glucose reaches 200-250 mg/dL: switch to **D5W/0.45% NaCl** at 150-250 mL/hr
- Goal: correct fluid deficit over 24-48 hours (avoid over-rapid correction — cerebral edema risk in pediatric patients)

#### Insulin Protocol

**Adult Fixed-Rate Intravenous Insulin Infusion:**
- Regular insulin 0.1 units/kg/hr continuous IV infusion
- Alternative: 0.1 units/kg IV bolus, then 0.05-0.1 units/kg/hr infusion
- Infusion preparation: 1 unit/mL (25 units regular insulin in 250 mL NS)
- Do NOT decrease infusion rate until anion gap is closed
- When glucose ≤200-250 mg/dL: reduce infusion by 50% AND add dextrose to IV fluids (maintain glucose 150-200 mg/dL while finishing DKA treatment)

**Resolution Criteria (all must be met):**
- Anion gap ≤12 mEq/L
- Bicarbonate ≥18 mEq/L (some protocols ≥15)
- pH >7.3
- Able to tolerate oral intake

**Transition to Subcutaneous Insulin:**
- Give first SQ dose of long-acting insulin (and rapid-acting if eating)
- Overlap IV insulin infusion by **2 hours** after giving SQ dose (wait for SQ absorption before stopping infusion)
- Patients with known T1DM: resume prior home regimen
- New T1DM: start 0.5 units/kg/day in basal-bolus regimen

#### Bicarbonate Therapy

- Not routinely recommended (bicarbonate does not improve outcomes in pH >6.9)
- Consider if pH <6.9: 100 mEq sodium bicarbonate in 400 mL sterile water + 20 mEq KCl over 2 hours; repeat until pH >7.0
- Risks of bicarbonate: hypokalemia, paradoxical CSF acidosis, delayed ketone clearance, alkalosis rebound

#### Phosphate Replacement

- Routine phosphate replacement is NOT recommended
- Replace if: serum phosphate <1.0 mg/dL, OR symptomatic hypophosphatemia (weakness, hemolytic anemia, respiratory compromise)
- If replacing: 20-40 mmol potassium phosphate over 4-6 hours

### Monitoring During DKA Treatment

| Parameter | Frequency |
|-----------|----------|
| Blood glucose | Every 1 hour |
| BMP (Na, K, Cl, HCO3, BUN, Cr) | Every 2-4 hours |
| Anion gap calculation | Every 2-4 hours |
| Venous blood gas (pH, HCO3) | Every 4 hours |
| Urine output | Continuous (Foley catheter recommended) |
| Clinical exam | Every 2-4 hours |

### DKA Complications

**Cerebral Edema:**
- Almost exclusively in pediatric patients (<20 years)
- Presentation: headache, vomiting, behavior change, deteriorating consciousness hours after treatment initiation
- Mechanism: rapid osmotic shifts during treatment
- Risk factors: younger age, new onset T1DM, severe presentation, rapid fluid correction, bicarbonate therapy
- Treatment: mannitol 0.5-1 g/kg IV over 20 minutes OR 3% NaCl 5-10 mL/kg over 30 minutes; head of bed elevation; reduce IV fluid rate; neurology/neurosurgery consultation
- High mortality (21-25%) and morbidity

**Other DKA Complications:**
- Hypokalemia (most common — from treatment)
- Hypoglycemia (from excessive insulin)
- Aspiration (impaired consciousness + vomiting)
- Rhabdomyolysis
- Thrombosis (hypercoagulable state)

## Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)

### Overview

HHS (formerly HONK — hyperosmolar nonketotic coma) is a severe hyperglycemic emergency occurring predominantly in elderly T2DM patients with relative insulin deficiency insufficient to prevent hyperglycemia but sufficient to suppress lipolysis and ketogenesis.

### Diagnostic Criteria

| Parameter | HHS | DKA |
|-----------|-----|-----|
| Plasma glucose | >600 mg/dL (often 800-1200) | >250 mg/dL |
| Effective osmolality | >320 mOsm/kg | <320 mOsm/kg |
| Arterial pH | >7.30 | <7.30 |
| Bicarbonate | >18 mEq/L | <18 mEq/L |
| Ketones | Minimal (absent to trace) | Moderate to large |
| Mental status | Stupor to coma (90%) | Alert to drowsy |

**Effective Osmolality Calculation:**
```
Effective Osm = 2[Na+] + [Glucose (mg/dL)]/18
(BUN/2.8 excluded as urea freely crosses cell membranes)
```

**Corrected Sodium in HHS:**
```
Corrected Na = Measured Na + 1.6 × [(Glucose - 100) / 100]
```
(Severe hyperglycemia causes dilutional hyponatremia — corrected sodium reflects true sodium status)

### Clinical Features

- Severe dehydration (typical deficit: 8-10 L or 20-25% total body water)
- Neurological: confusion, lethargy, focal deficits, seizures, coma
- Common precipitants: infection, stroke, MI, medications (diuretics, steroids, antipsychotics), dialysis, tube feeding
- Mortality: 5-20% (much higher than DKA) — largely due to age and precipitating illness

### Treatment

**Fluids (Higher Priority than Insulin in HHS):**
- Initial: NS 1 L/hr x 1-2 hours (aggressive resuscitation for hypotension)
- After hemodynamic stabilization: 0.45% NaCl at 250-500 mL/hr (correct free water deficit)
- Add dextrose when glucose <300 mg/dL (lower threshold than DKA to prevent osmolarity correction too fast)
- Goal: correct osmolality over 24-48 hours; reduce by no more than 3-8 mOsm/kg/hr

**Insulin:**
- Start only after adequate fluid resuscitation (fluids alone may reduce glucose significantly)
- Use lower doses than DKA: regular insulin 0.05-0.1 units/kg/hr IV infusion
- Potassium management same as DKA

**Potassium:** Total body potassium highly depleted; replace aggressively

## Hypoglycemia

### Classification (ADA 2024)

| Level | Glucose Threshold | Clinical Significance |
|-------|------------------|-----------------------|
| Level 1 (Alert value) | <70 mg/dL | Requires treatment; alert patient |
| Level 2 (Clinically significant) | <54 mg/dL | Serious event; requires intervention |
| Level 3 (Severe) | No specific threshold | Altered consciousness/behavior requiring assistance |

### Causes

**Insulin-treated diabetes:**
- Too much insulin (dose error, incorrect timing)
- Insufficient carbohydrate intake (skipped/delayed meal)
- Unplanned exercise (increases glucose uptake; can persist 12-24h)
- Alcohol consumption (inhibits gluconeogenesis; no warning symptoms if masked)
- Renal failure (reduced insulin clearance + reduced gluconeogenesis)
- Gastroparesis (delayed food absorption with premeal insulin)

**Non-insulin agents:**
- Sulfonylureas (most common oral agent cause); risk highest with glyburide, renally impaired, elderly
- Meglitinides (repaglinide, nateglinide)

**Other causes of hypoglycemia:**
- Insulinoma (fasting hypoglycemia; high insulin + C-peptide)
- Factitious hypoglycemia (exogenous insulin — low C-peptide; oral agent — screen plasma/urine)
- Post-bariatric hypoglycemia (reactive, postprandial — 1-3 hours after eating)
- Adrenal insufficiency (glucocorticoid deficiency → impaired counterregulatory response)
- Sepsis, liver failure, prolonged starvation

### Symptoms

**Adrenergic/Autonomic (early warning, typically <70 mg/dL):**
- Diaphoresis (sweating) — most reliable symptom
- Tremor, shakiness
- Tachycardia, palpitations
- Anxiety, nervousness
- Pallor
- NOTE: beta-blockers blunt ALL of these except diaphoresis

**Neuroglycopenic (severe; typically <50 mg/dL):**
- Difficulty concentrating, confusion
- Slurred speech
- Visual changes, diplopia
- Behavior change, irritability
- Focal neurological deficits
- Seizures
- Loss of consciousness, coma

### Treatment

**Conscious Patient — "Rule of 15":**
1. Take 15g of fast-acting carbohydrates:
   - 4 oz (120 mL) regular fruit juice or soda (not diet)
   - 3-4 glucose tablets (4g each)
   - 1 tablespoon sugar or honey
   - 5-6 hard candies
   - Glucose gel (15g dose)
2. Wait 15 minutes; recheck blood glucose
3. Repeat if still <70 mg/dL
4. Once glucose ≥70 mg/dL, eat a snack (15g carb + protein) if next meal >1 hour away

**Unconscious / Unable to Swallow:**

| Treatment | Dose | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| Glucagon IM/SQ kit | 1 mg (adults); 0.5 mg (<25 kg children) | GlucaGen or generic kits; may cause vomiting |
| Nasal glucagon (Baqsimi) | 3 mg intranasally (one actuation in one nostril) | No reconstitution; caregiver can administer |
| Dasiglucagon SQ (Zegalogue) | 0.6 mg SQ | Ready-to-use auto-injector |
| IV Dextrose (hospital) | D50W 25-50 mL IV (12.5-25g dextrose) | Rapid reversal; first-line in hospital |
| D10W (hospital) | 100-200 mL IV | Less hyperglycemia overshoot; safer for peripheral IV |

**Post-hypoglycemia:**
- Identify and address cause
- Reduce insulin dose if appropriate
- Review timing of meals and medications
- Educate caregiver/family
- Consider CGM with low glucose alarms

### Hypoglycemia Unawareness

- Recurrent hypoglycemia → habituation of counterregulatory response → blunted adrenergic symptoms
- "HAAF" (Hypoglycemia-Associated Autonomic Failure)
- Risk factors: T1DM >5 years, tight glycemic control, nighttime hypoglycemia, autonomic neuropathy
- Management:
  - Strict avoidance of hypoglycemia for 2-4 weeks may restore awareness (upregulate counterregulation)
  - Relax glycemic targets temporarily (A1c 7.5-8% acceptable)
  - CGM with alarm thresholds set at 70 mg/dL (or 80 mg/dL to prevent level 1)
  - Patient and caregiver education on glucagon use
  - Evaluate for islet transplantation or pancreas transplant in severe cases

## Diabetic Complications

### Diabetic Kidney Disease (DKD)

DKD affects 20-40% of patients with diabetes and is the leading cause of chronic kidney disease (CKD) in developed nations. Progression follows: normoalbuminuria → microalbuminuria → macroalbuminuria → declining GFR → ESKD.

**Screening:**
- Annual urine albumin-creatinine ratio (ACR) and eGFR in all patients with diabetes
- T1DM: begin screening 5 years after diagnosis
- T2DM: begin screening at diagnosis

**ACR Classification:**

| Category | ACR (mg/g creatinine) | Notes |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Normal | <30 | No albuminuria |
| Moderately increased (microalbuminuria) | 30-300 | Two of three samples positive over 3-6 months to confirm |
| Severely increased (macroalbuminuria) | >300 | Overt nephropathy |

**CKD Staging (eGFR + Albuminuria — KDIGO classification):**

| CKD Stage | eGFR (mL/min/1.73m2) | Severity |
|-----------|----------------------|---------|
| G1 | ≥90 | Normal or high (if albuminuria present) |
| G2 | 60-89 | Mildly decreased |
| G3a | 45-59 | Mild-moderately decreased |
| G3b | 30-44 | Moderate-severely decreased |
| G4 | 15-29 | Severely decreased |
| G5 | <15 | Kidney failure |

**DKD Treatment:**

1. **Glucose control:** A1c <7% slows DKD progression (UKPDS, ADVANCE)
2. **Blood pressure:** Target <130/80 mmHg; ACEi or ARB first-line (reduce intraglomerular pressure, reduce proteinuria ~30-40%)
   - ACEi + ARB combination: NOT recommended (increased AKI, hyperkalemia without added benefit — ONTARGET trial)
3. **SGLT2 inhibitors:** First-line for CKD + T2DM
   - Dapagliflozin: DAPA-CKD — 39% reduction in kidney failure, sustained eGFR decline, or death from renal/CV cause; works in non-diabetic CKD too
   - Empagliflozin: EMPA-KIDNEY — significant renal protection (eGFR ≥20 + UACR ≥200 OR eGFR 20-45)
   - Canagliflozin: CREDENCE — significant renal protection (eGFR ≥30 + macroalbuminuria)
4. **Finerenone (Kerendia):** Non-steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA)
   - Dose: 10 mg daily if eGFR 25-60; 20 mg daily if eGFR ≥60 (titrate based on K levels)
   - FIDELIO-DKD trial: 18% reduction in CKD progression; FIGARO-DKD trial: 13% reduction in MACE
   - Complementary mechanism to SGLT2i; can combine
   - Side effect: hyperkalemia (monitor K at 4 weeks, then q3-6 months)
   - Contraindication: K >5.0 mEq/L, severe hepatic impairment, concomitant strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (ketoconazole)
5. **Dietary protein restriction:** 0.8 g/kg/day (ADA recommendation) — may slow progression
6. **Lipid management:** Statin therapy; reduce cardiovascular risk

### Diabetic Retinopathy (DR)

Leading cause of new blindness in working-age adults in developed nations.

**Screening:**
- T1DM: dilated fundus exam within 5 years of diagnosis, then annually
- T2DM: dilated fundus exam at time of diagnosis, then annually
- More frequent if abnormal findings; may reduce to every 1-2 years if normal and good glycemic control
- Pregnancy with pre-existing diabetes: exam before conception or in first trimester; monitor each trimester

**Classification:**

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| No DR | Normal retina |
| Mild NPDR | Microaneurysms only |
| Moderate NPDR | More than microaneurysms; less than severe NPDR |
| Severe NPDR | Any of: >20 intraretinal hemorrhages per quadrant, venous beading ≥2 quadrants, IRMA ≥1 quadrant (4-2-1 rule) |
| PDR (Proliferative DR) | Neovascularization of retina or optic disc; vitreous or preretinal hemorrhage |
| DME (Diabetic Macular Edema) | Retinal thickening involving or threatening fovea; can occur at any DR stage |

NPDR = Non-Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy; PDR = Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy
IRMA = Intraretinal microvascular abnormalities

**Treatment:**

| Condition | Treatment |
|-----------|----------|
| Mild-moderate NPDR | Optimize glucose and BP; annual monitoring |
| Severe NPDR | Consider pan-retinal photocoagulation (PRP); anti-VEGF |
| PDR | Pan-retinal photocoagulation; anti-VEGF injections; vitrectomy if vitreous hemorrhage |
| DME involving center | Anti-VEGF first-line (ranibizumab, bevacizumab, aflibercept, faricimab — Vabysmo); focal laser; steroid implants |

**Anti-VEGF for DME:** Intravitreal injections monthly for 3-6 doses loading, then PRN or treat-and-extend.

**Rapid A1c correction warning:** Rapid reduction in A1c can transiently worsen retinopathy (mechanism: restored perfusion exposes vulnerable vessels). Gradual A1c reduction recommended (≤1-2% per 3 months) if baseline A1c very high.

### Diabetic Peripheral Neuropathy (DPN)

Most common complication of diabetes; affects 50% of patients with >25 years of T2DM.

**Types:**

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Distal Symmetric Polyneuropathy (DSPN) | Most common; stocking-glove distribution; sensorimotor |
| Autonomic neuropathy | Multiple organ systems affected |
| Mononeuropathy | Cranial nerves (CN III most common — painful ophthalmoplegia with pupil sparing), compression neuropathies (carpal tunnel, foot drop) |
| Diabetic amyotrophy (Bruns-Garland) | Lumbosacral radiculoplexopathy; proximal leg pain and weakness; usually asymmetric |
| Acute painful neuropathy | Rapid onset after A1c change; may improve with glucose stabilization |
| CIDP | Chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy — higher prevalence in diabetes |

#### Distal Symmetric Polyneuropathy (DSPN)

**Symptoms:**
- Burning, tingling, electric-like pain (positive symptoms)
- Numbness, loss of sensation (negative symptoms)
- Pain worse at night
- "Walking on sand" or "pebbles" sensation
- Loss of balance (proprioception impaired)

**Annual Screening:**
- 10-gram monofilament (Semmes-Weinstein): applied to 10 plantar sites; failure to feel = loss of protective sensation (LOPS)
- 128 Hz tuning fork: applied to hallux/medial malleolus; reduced vibration sense
- Pinprick sensation (disposable pin or Wartenberg wheel)
- Ankle reflexes (diminished/absent early)
- Temperature discrimination (may use side of tuning fork)

**Diagnosis:** DSPN is clinical; NCS/EMG if atypical features or diagnosis uncertain.

**Treatment of Painful DPN:**

| Tier | Agent | Dose | Notes |
|------|-------|------|-------|
| First-line | Pregabalin (Lyrica) | 150-300 mg/day BID-TID | FDA-approved for DPN; adjust for renal; sedation, weight gain |
| First-line | Duloxetine (Cymbalta) | 60-120 mg/day | FDA-approved for DPN; also treats depression; GI side effects |
| First-line | Gabapentin (Neurontin) | 300-3600 mg/day TID | Off-label; sedation; adjust for renal |
| First-line | TCAs (amitriptyline, nortriptyline) | 25-100 mg QHS | Effective; limited by anticholinergic SE; Beers Criteria caution in elderly |
| Second-line | Tapentadol ER (Nucynta) | 50-500 mg/day | FDA-approved; mu-opioid + NRI; abuse potential |
| Second-line | Lidocaine patch 4-5% | Up to 3 patches/24h for 12h | Topical; minimal systemic absorption |
| Third-line | Capsaicin 0.075% cream | TID-QID topical | Depletes substance P; burning sensation initially |
| Third-line | High-concentration capsaicin 8% patch (Qutenza) | One application q3 months | Specialist application; significant burning during application |
| Third-line | Opioids (tramadol, oxycodone) | Individualized | Limited evidence; abuse potential; last resort |
| Adjunct | TENS, acupuncture, spinal cord stimulation | Variable | Refractory cases |

**Glucose control:** Most important intervention — DCCT showed 64% reduction in neuropathy with intensive glycemic control in T1DM.

#### Autonomic Neuropathy

**Cardiovascular Autonomic Neuropathy (CAN):**
- Resting tachycardia (heart rate >100), fixed heart rate (loss of heart rate variability)
- Orthostatic hypotension: drop ≥20 mmHg systolic or ≥10 mmHg diastolic within 3 minutes of standing
- Silent MI: painless ischemia (absent angina due to sensory denervation of myocardium)
- Sudden death risk (abnormal QTc, ventricular arrhythmias)
- Treatment: fludrocortisone, midodrine, compression stockings, head of bed elevation

**Gastrointestinal Autonomic Neuropathy:**
- Gastroparesis: delayed gastric emptying → postprandial fullness, nausea, vomiting, unpredictable glucose
  - Diagnosis: nuclear gastric emptying study (4-hour protocol); >10% retention at 4 hours = gastroparesis
  - Treatment: metoclopramide (10 mg AC and QHS; FDA warns use >12 weeks due to tardive dyskinesia); erythromycin (motilin agonist; tachyphylaxis); domperidone (not FDA-approved); dietary modification (small frequent meals, low fat/fiber)
  - Insulin adjustment: postprandial dosing or extended bolus on pump; premixed insulins problematic
- Constipation: most common GI autonomic symptom; treat with fiber, osmotic laxatives
- Diabetic diarrhea: nocturnal, watery; treat with loperamide, clonidine, bile acid binders
- Fecal incontinence: internal sphincter dysfunction; biofeedback, loperamide

**Genitourinary Autonomic Neuropathy:**
- Neurogenic bladder: impaired sensation + detrusor dysfunction → urinary retention + overflow incontinence; treat with timed voiding, bethanechol, alpha-blockers; catheterization if needed
- Erectile dysfunction (ED): 50% of men with T2DM >10 years; multifactorial (autonomic, vascular, psychogenic); treat with PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil); exclude hypogonadism; penile prosthesis if refractory
- Female sexual dysfunction: vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, reduced arousal; topical lubricants, systemic hormones if appropriate

### Diabetic Foot

Leading cause of non-traumatic lower extremity amputation. Multifactorial: neuropathy + PAD + infection.

**Annual Foot Examination — ADA Minimum:**
- Visual inspection (skin integrity, deformity, calluses, nail pathology)
- Monofilament testing (loss of protective sensation)
- Assessment for PAD: pedal pulses, ABI
- Identify deformity: hallux valgus, hammertoe, Charcot foot

**Risk Stratification:**

| Category | Risk Level | Screening Frequency |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| No LOPS, no PAD | Low risk | Annual |
| LOPS or PAD | Moderate risk | Every 3-6 months |
| LOPS + PAD, or foot deformity | High risk | Every 1-3 months |
| Prior ulcer or amputation | Very high risk | Every 1-3 months (or each visit) |

**Wagner Classification of Diabetic Foot Ulcers:**

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0 | No open lesion; pre- or post-ulcer; intact skin (bony prominence, callus) |
| 1 | Superficial ulcer; no infection; partial or full thickness |
| 2 | Deep ulcer; extends to tendon, capsule, or bone without osteomyelitis |
| 3 | Deep ulcer with abscess, osteomyelitis, or joint sepsis |
| 4 | Localized gangrene (forefoot or heel) |
| 5 | Extensive gangrene of entire foot |

**Charcot Neuroarthropathy:**
- Acute phase: erythema, warmth, swelling of foot/ankle; often precipitated by minor trauma in neuropathic foot; X-ray initially normal → progressive joint destruction (midfoot typical — "rocker-bottom" deformity)
- Diagnosis: clinical + MRI (early); X-ray shows fragmentation and disorganization later
- Differential: cellulitis, osteomyelitis (probe-to-bone helps), DVT, gout
- Treatment acute phase: complete non-weight-bearing (total contact cast or removable cast walker) until skin temperature normalizes (typically 4-6 months)
- Bisphosphonates (pamidronate, zoledronic acid): may shorten acute phase; used in some centers
- Chronic phase: custom footwear; Charcot-restraint orthotic walker (CROW); surgical reconstruction in selected cases

**Osteomyelitis in Diabetic Foot:**
- Probe-to-bone test: sterile probe inserted into ulcer — palpating hard gritty surface = osteomyelitis; sensitivity 66%, specificity 85-89%
- Plain X-ray: initially normal; periosteal reaction and bone destruction appear 2-4 weeks after infection
- MRI: gold standard; sensitivity 90%, specificity 79%; shows marrow edema, periosteal reaction
- ESR >70 mm/hr + clinical features: high PPV for osteomyelitis
- Treatment: antibiotics 4-6 weeks (bone-penetrating agents preferred)
  - Clindamycin: good bone penetration; covers MRSA (clindamycin-susceptible)
  - Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin): good bone penetration; cover Gram-negative
  - Linezolid: excellent MRSA coverage; 100% oral bioavailability; toxicity with prolonged use (thrombocytopenia, optic neuropathy)
  - Rifampin: add to regimen for biofilm-active coverage if implants or prosthetics (never monotherapy)
  - Bone biopsy for culture preferred before starting antibiotics in non-urgent cases

**Antibiotic Selection for DFI (IDSA Classification):**

| Severity | Typical Coverage | Agent |
|----------|-----------------|-------|
| Mild (superficial, limited cellulitis <2 cm) | Staph aureus, Streptococci | Amoxicillin-clavulanate PO, cefalexin PO, dicloxacillin PO |
| Moderate (deep tissue, >2 cm cellulitis) | Staph, Strep, Gram-neg | Amoxicillin-clavulanate, ertapenem, cefazolin/ceftriaxone IV |
| Moderate-severe (limb-threatening) | Polymicrobial + anaerobes | Pip-tazo IV, ertapenem IV, ampicillin-sulbactam IV |
| MRSA risk factors | Add MRSA coverage | + Vancomycin IV, daptomycin IV, or linezolid PO/IV |
| Severe (sepsis, systemic) | Broad polymicrobial | Pip-tazo + vancomycin, or meropenem + vancomycin |

**Vascular Assessment:**
- ABI (Ankle-Brachial Index): normal 1.0-1.3; PAD if <0.9; critical limb ischemia (CLI) if <0.4
- ABI >1.3: non-compressible vessels (falsely high — common in diabetic calcific arteriopathy); use toe-brachial index (TBI) instead (normal >0.7)
- Revascularization consideration for CLI: ABI <0.4, non-healing wound, rest pain
- Options: endovascular (balloon angioplasty, stenting) or open bypass surgery; decision based on anatomy and comorbidities

## Special Populations

### Diabetes in Pregnancy

#### Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM)

**Definition:** Diabetes first diagnosed in the second or third trimester of pregnancy that was not clearly overt diabetes prior to gestation.

**Screening (ADA/ACOG):**
- Universal screening at 24-28 weeks gestation
- Two-step approach (most common in US):
  1. 1-hour 50g glucose challenge test (GCT): positive if ≥130 mg/dL (some centers use 140 mg/dL cutoff)
  2. If GCT positive: 3-hour 100g OGTT with Carpenter-Coustan criteria:
     - Fasting: ≥95 mg/dL
     - 1-hour: ≥180 mg/dL
     - 2-hour: ≥155 mg/dL
     - 3-hour: ≥140 mg/dL
     - GDM diagnosed if ≥2 values meet/exceed threshold
- One-step approach (IADPSG/ADA alternative): single 75g OGTT at 24-28 weeks
  - Fasting: ≥92 mg/dL
  - 1-hour: ≥180 mg/dL
  - 2-hour: ≥153 mg/dL
  - GDM diagnosed if ANY single value meets/exceeds threshold (more sensitive, higher GDM prevalence)
- Screen earlier if high risk factors: prior GDM, BMI >35, prior macrosomic infant, PCOS, strong family history, glucosuria

**Complications of Untreated GDM:**
- Macrosomia (birth weight >4 kg) → shoulder dystocia, C-section
- Neonatal hypoglycemia (fetal hyperinsulinism from maternal glucose)
- Polyhydramnios, pre-eclampsia, preterm labor
- Long-term: increased maternal risk of T2DM (50% within 10 years); increased child risk of obesity and T2DM

**GDM Management:**
- Step 1: Medical nutrition therapy (MNT) + physical activity → monitor glucose SMBG for 1-2 weeks
- Step 2: If glucose targets not met → insulin (safest, no placental transfer of concern)
- Oral agents: metformin crosses placenta (long-term safety in offspring unclear, but no structural defects); glyburide — less effective, higher neonatal hypoglycemia
- Insulin regimens: NPH + Regular (traditional); rapid analogs (lispro, aspart) are safe and preferred; glargine — some data supporting safety

**Glucose Targets in GDM:**

| Timing | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Fasting | <95 mg/dL |
| 1-hour postprandial | <140 mg/dL |
| 2-hour postprandial | <120 mg/dL |

**Postpartum:**
- 75g OGTT at 4-12 weeks postpartum (A1c not sensitive immediately postpartum)
- Annual A1c or FPG screening thereafter
- Breastfeeding: reduces T2DM risk; continue blood glucose monitoring; insulin dose adjustment needed (lower requirements)

#### Pre-existing Diabetes in Pregnancy

- Target A1c: <7% before conception (ideally 6.0-6.5% if achievable without severe hypoglycemia)
- Folic acid: 4 mg/day (vs 0.4-0.8 mg for general population) — reduces neural tube defect risk
- Retinopathy: screen and treat before and during pregnancy (rapid glucose correction can worsen)
- Nephropathy: ACEi and ARB MUST be discontinued before pregnancy (teratogenic — category D); switch to methyldopa, nifedipine, labetalol for BP control
- Insulin requirements increase dramatically in second and third trimesters (especially T1DM); decrease abruptly after delivery

### Diabetes Management in the Hospital

**Inpatient Glucose Targets:**

| Setting | Target Range | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| ICU | 140-180 mg/dL | Tight control (80-110) increases mortality — NICE-SUGAR trial |
| General ward | 140-180 mg/dL | Avoid hyperglycemia + hypoglycemia |
| Cardiac surgery | 140-180 mg/dL | Some centers use 110-140 for perioperative; evidence limited |

**Principles:**
- Basal-bolus-correction protocol preferred over sliding scale insulin alone
- Avoid oral antidiabetics on admission: hold metformin for contrast, illness; hold SGLT2i for any significant illness or surgery; hold GLP-1 RA only if NPO
- Continue basal insulin (reduce dose 20-50% if eating poorly to avoid hypoglycemia)
- Point-of-care (POC) glucose monitoring: AC and bedtime (minimum); q1h if on insulin infusion

**Perioperative Management:**

| Timing | Action |
|--------|--------|
| Evening before surgery | Take usual basal insulin (some reduce by 20-25%) |
| Morning of surgery | Skip rapid-acting insulin (no food); give 50-80% of usual basal insulin |
| Intraoperative | Glucose checks q1-2h; insulin infusion if prolonged surgery or glucose >180 |
| Postoperative | Resume home regimen when tolerating oral intake |

**Stress Hyperglycemia (No Prior Diabetes):**
- Any hospitalized patient with glucose >140 mg/dL should be assessed and treated
- Insulin treatment with target 140-180 mg/dL improves outcomes
- Approximately 30% may have undiagnosed diabetes; obtain A1c; follow up after discharge

### Diabetes in the Elderly (Age ≥65)

**Revised A1c Targets (ADA Older Adults Framework):**

| Health Status | A1c Target | Rationale |
|---------------|-----------|-----------|
| Healthy (few chronic conditions, intact cognition) | <7.0-7.5% | Benefits from tight control may be realized |
| Intermediate (multiple chronic conditions, early cognitive impairment) | <8.0% | Hypoglycemia risk outweighs tight control benefit |
| Very complex/poor health (end-stage CKD, metastatic cancer, advanced dementia) | <8.5% or less stringent | Avoid hypoglycemia; quality of life focus |

**Key Considerations in Elderly:**
- Hypoglycemia: most dangerous complication — increases fall risk, fractures, MI, cognitive decline, mortality
- Cognitive decline: hypoglycemia is both a consequence and cause; patients with dementia have reduced hypoglycemia awareness
- Polypharmacy: high drug interaction risk; DPP-4i and SGLT2i better tolerated overall
- Renal function: declines with age; adjust drug doses (sitagliptin, metformin, SGLT2i)
- Frailty: sarcopenia may reduce ability to sense or respond to hypoglycemia symptoms

**Preferred Agents in Elderly:**

| Agent Class | Status in Elderly | Notes |
|-------------|------------------|-------|
| Metformin | First-line if tolerated | Watch eGFR; B12 monitoring |
| DPP-4i | Preferred (sitagliptin, linagliptin) | Low hypoglycemia, well-tolerated, renal dosing needed (except linagliptin) |
| GLP-1 RA | Preferred if CV benefit needed | GI side effects may cause weight loss (harmful in frail elderly) |
| SGLT2i | Preferred if HF or CKD | Risk of volume depletion, UTI, falls in very elderly/frail |
| Sulfonylureas | Use with caution (if needed) | Prefer glipizide or glimepiride; AVOID glyburide (Beers Criteria) |
| Basal insulin | Acceptable | Use low doses; beware nocturnal hypoglycemia; consider degludec (lower hypoglycemia) |
| Sliding scale insulin | Avoid as sole regimen | Reactive only; not physiologic |

**Beers Criteria (AGS) Drugs to Avoid in Elderly:**
- Glyburide (DiaBeta, Glynase): inappropriate in adults ≥65 — prolonged hypoglycemia
- Glipizide and glimepiride: use with caution
- Sliding scale insulin: may use as supplement to scheduled insulin but not as sole regimen

## Cardiovascular Risk Management in Diabetes

Diabetes confers a 2-4x increased cardiovascular risk. Comprehensive cardiovascular risk factor management is integral to diabetes care.

### Lipid Management

**Statin Therapy:**

| Patient Category | Statin Intensity | Target |
|-----------------|-----------------|--------|
| Diabetes + established ASCVD | High-intensity (atorvastatin 40-80 mg, rosuvastatin 20-40 mg) | LDL <70 mg/dL; if ASCVD + very high risk, LDL <55 mg/dL |
| Diabetes, age 40-75, no ASCVD | Moderate-to-high intensity | LDL <100 mg/dL |
| Diabetes, age <40 or >75 | Individualized; consider moderate statin | Based on risk-benefit discussion |
| Diabetes + CKD | Moderate-intensity statin (rosuvastatin 10 mg preferred in CKD 4-5 — less renal metabolism) | LDL <70 mg/dL |

**Additional Lipid Agents:**
- Ezetimibe: add if LDL not at goal on maximum tolerated statin (IMPROVE-IT trial — added benefit in post-ACS with diabetes)
- PCSK9 inhibitors (evolocumab, alirocumab): for established ASCVD or familial hypercholesterolemia with LDL not at goal on statin + ezetimibe
- Fibrates: primarily for triglyceride reduction; modest HDL increase; gemfibrozil (avoid with statins — myopathy); fenofibrate preferred
- Omega-3 fatty acids: icosapentaenoic acid (EPA — Vascepa 4g/day) — REDUCE-IT trial showed 25% MACE reduction in patients with T2DM and elevated TG >150 mg/dL on statin; indicated for CV risk reduction
- Niacin: not recommended for CV outcomes (AIM-HIGH, HPS2-THRIVE trials showed no benefit, possible harm)

### Blood Pressure Management

- Target: <130/80 mmHg for most adults with diabetes
- Less strict target (<140/90) acceptable in elderly or limited life expectancy
- First-line agents: ACEi or ARB (especially with CKD/proteinuria); avoid combining ACEi + ARB
- Second-line: CCB (amlodipine preferred), diuretics (thiazide preferred — chlorthalidone for CV benefit)
- Avoid: beta-blockers as first-line for HTN in T2DM (mask hypoglycemia symptoms, worsen glucose tolerance — exception: post-MI, HF, angina)

### Antiplatelet Therapy

- Aspirin 75-162 mg/day: established ASCVD (secondary prevention) — recommended
- Primary prevention aspirin: only if high ASCVD risk (10-year risk >10%), age 50-70, no high bleeding risk; shared decision-making required; NNT vs NNH consideration
- Do NOT use aspirin in age <30 (Reye syndrome potential) or >80 (bleeding risk)
- P2Y12 inhibitors (clopidogrel, ticagrelor, prasugrel): DAPT after ACS/PCI per standard cardiology protocols

### Smoking Cessation

- Every patient who smokes should receive counseling at every visit
- Pharmacotherapy: nicotine replacement therapy (NRT), varenicline (Chantix/Champix), bupropion
- Varenicline: most effective pharmacotherapy; monitor neuropsychiatric symptoms in high-risk patients
- E-cigarettes/vaping: not recommended as cessation tools
