## Anemia — Overview and Classification

### Classification by MCV

| Category | MCV | Common Causes |
|---|---|---|
| Microcytic | <80 fL | Iron deficiency, thalassemia, anemia of chronic disease (ACD), sideroblastic |
| Normocytic | 80-100 fL | ACD, hemolysis, aplastic anemia, early deficiencies, renal failure |
| Macrocytic | >100 fL | B12 deficiency, folate deficiency, hypothyroidism, liver disease, MDS, medications |

## Microcytic Anemia

### Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA)

**Pathophysiology:** Reduced iron stores deplete ferritin, increase transferrin synthesis, lower transferrin saturation, reduce hemoglobin synthesis — RBCs become microcytic and hypochromic.

**Causes:**
- Blood loss (GI tract most common in adults — must rule out malignancy)
- Menstrual loss (most common in premenopausal women)
- Malabsorption (celiac disease, post-gastrectomy, H. pylori)
- Inadequate dietary intake (vegetarians, infants)
- Increased demand (pregnancy, growth)

**Labs:**

| Test | Finding in IDA |
|---|---|
| Serum ferritin | Low (<12 mcg/L definitive; <30 mcg/L suggestive) |
| TIBC (transferrin) | Elevated (>360 mcg/dL) |
| Transferrin saturation | Low (<16%) |
| Serum iron | Low |
| Reticulocyte count | Low (hypoproliferative) |
| Peripheral smear | Microcytic, hypochromic RBCs, pencil cells, anisocytosis, poikilocytosis |
| RDW | Elevated (reflects size variation) |

Note: Ferritin is an acute-phase reactant — can be falsely normal in IDA + inflammation. Check CRP; if elevated, ferritin <100 still consistent with IDA.

**Oral Iron Replacement:**

| Formulation | Elemental Iron | Dose |
|---|---|---|
| Ferrous sulfate 325 mg | 65 mg | 1-2 tabs TID on empty stomach |
| Ferrous gluconate 325 mg | 38 mg | 1-2 tabs TID |
| Ferrous fumarate 325 mg | 108 mg | 1 tab TID |

- Take 30-60 min before meals for best absorption
- Vitamin C co-administration increases absorption
- Side effects: constipation, nausea, dark stools
- Expect hemoglobin to rise 1-2 g/dL over 3-4 weeks
- Continue oral iron for 3-6 months after hemoglobin normalizes to replete stores

**IV Iron Formulations:**

| Agent | Dose | Infusion Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Iron sucrose (Venofer) | 200-300 mg IV per session | Multiple sessions needed; low anaphylaxis risk |
| Ferric carboxymaltose (Injectafer) | 750 mg IV over 15 min; max 1500 mg/course | High single-dose; can cause hypophosphatemia |
| Low molecular weight iron dextran (INFeD) | Calculated total dose infusion | Test dose required; higher anaphylaxis risk |
| Ferumoxytol (Feraheme) | 510 mg IV x2 doses 3-8 days apart | Fastest option; MRI artifact; FDA black box for hypersensitivity |
| Ferric gluconate (Ferrlecit) | 125 mg IV x8 sessions | Dialysis patients; low reaction rate |

**Indications for IV Iron Over Oral:**
- Malabsorption (IBD, celiac, post-bariatric)
- Oral intolerance or non-compliance
- Chronic kidney disease on ESA therapy
- Rapid repletion needed (pre-op, late pregnancy)
- Ongoing blood loss exceeding oral absorption capacity
- Heart failure with IDA (IV iron improves outcomes)

### Thalassemia

**Pathophysiology:** Reduced synthesis of globin chains (alpha or beta) → imbalanced chain production → ineffective erythropoiesis + hemolysis of RBCs containing excess chains.

**Alpha Thalassemia:**

| Genotype | Missing Alpha Genes | Clinical |
|---|---|---|
| Silent carrier | 1 | Normal, microcytosis possible |
| Alpha thalassemia trait | 2 | Mild microcytic anemia, usually asymptomatic |
| Hemoglobin H disease | 3 | Moderate hemolytic anemia, splenomegaly |
| Hydrops fetalis | 4 | Lethal in utero or at birth; Hgb Barts (gamma4) |

**Beta Thalassemia:**

| Type | Beta Gene Status | Clinical |
|---|---|---|
| Beta thalassemia minor (trait) | One beta mutation | Mild microcytic anemia; Hgb A2 >3.5% (diagnostic) |
| Beta thalassemia intermedia | Two partial mutations | Moderate anemia; transfusion-independent or occasional |
| Beta thalassemia major (Cooley's) | Two severe mutations | Severe; transfusion-dependent from infancy; iron overload |

**Diagnosis:**
- Hemoglobin electrophoresis: elevated Hgb A2 (>3.5%) in beta thalassemia trait; Hgb H inclusions on brilliant cresyl blue stain in alpha thal; Hgb Barts in newborn screen
- DNA analysis for alpha thalassemia (HPLC electrophoresis less reliable)
- Peripheral smear: target cells, basophilic stippling, microcytosis, hypochromia

**Key Distinguishing Point from IDA:** Normal ferritin, normal/elevated serum iron, elevated Hgb A2 on electrophoresis (beta thal); RDW often normal in thalassemia (uniform microcytosis) vs elevated in IDA.

**Management:**
- Thalassemia trait: no treatment; genetic counseling
- Thalassemia major: chronic transfusion every 3-4 weeks (goal pre-transfusion Hgb 9-10), iron chelation (deferasirox, deferoxamine, deferiprone), splenectomy if hypersplenism, allogeneic HSCT curative; luspatercept (Reblozyl) reduces transfusion burden in beta thalassemia (TGF-beta ligand trap)
- Folic acid supplementation

### Anemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)

**Pathophysiology:** Chronic inflammation (infection, autoimmune, malignancy, CKD) elevates IL-6, IL-1, TNF → liver produces hepcidin → hepcidin degrades ferroportin → iron trapped in macrophages + enterocytes → functional iron deficiency despite adequate stores.

**Labs:**

| Test | ACD | IDA | ACD + IDA |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ferritin | Normal or high | Low | Low-normal (may be falsely elevated by inflammation) |
| TIBC | Low or normal | High | Variable |
| Transferrin saturation | Low-normal | Low | Low |
| Serum iron | Low | Low | Low |
| Soluble transferrin receptor (sTfR) | Normal | Elevated | Elevated |
| sTfR/log ferritin index | <1 | >2 | >2 |

**Treatment:**
- Treat underlying cause (primary)
- Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs) in CKD: epoetin alfa or darbepoetin; target Hgb 10-11.5 g/dL (avoid >13 — increased CV events)
- IV iron supplementation if TSAT <30% and starting ESA
- Avoid ESAs in cancer patients receiving chemotherapy with curative intent
- Roxadustat and other HIF-PH inhibitors (approved in some countries): oral, reduce hepcidin, stimulate erythropoiesis

## Normocytic Anemia

### Hemolytic Anemia

**Approach to Hemolysis:**

Key lab markers of hemolysis:
- LDH elevated (released from lysed RBCs)
- Haptoglobin low or undetectable (binds free Hgb → cleared)
- Indirect bilirubin elevated (unconjugated from Hgb catabolism)
- Reticulocytosis (bone marrow compensatory response)
- Peripheral smear: morphology guides etiology

| Peripheral Smear Finding | Diagnosis |
|---|---|
| Schistocytes (fragmented RBCs) | Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (TMA): TTP, HUS, DIC, HELLP |
| Spherocytes | AIHA, hereditary spherocytosis |
| Sickle cells | Sickle cell disease |
| Target cells | Thalassemia, liver disease, hemoglobinopathies |
| Elliptocytes | Hereditary elliptocytosis |
| Bite cells / blister cells | G6PD deficiency (Heinz bodies on supravital stain) |
| Acanthocytes (spur cells) | Abetalipoproteinemia, severe liver disease |
| Burr cells (echinocytes) | Uremia, artifact |
| Teardrop cells | Myelofibrosis, extramedullary hematopoiesis |

**Autoimmune Hemolytic Anemia (AIHA):**

| Type | Antibody | Temperature | Causes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm AIHA | IgG | 37°C | Idiopathic, SLE, CLL, lymphoma, drugs (methyldopa, penicillin) |
| Cold agglutinin disease | IgM | <37°C | Mycoplasma, EBV, lymphoma, idiopathic |
| Mixed | IgG + IgM | Both | SLE, idiopathic |
| Drug-induced | Various | — | Many medications |

- Direct Coombs (DAT): detects antibody or complement on RBC surface
  - IgG positive = warm AIHA
  - C3 positive = cold agglutinin disease
  - Both = mixed or drug-induced

**Warm AIHA Treatment:**
- First line: prednisone 1-1.5 mg/kg/day; taper once Hgb stable
- Second line: rituximab 375 mg/m2 x4 weeks; splenectomy
- Refractory: azathioprine, mycophenolate, cyclosporine, danazol
- Fostamatinib (spleen tyrosine kinase inhibitor) — approved for warm AIHA
- Avoid cold exposure irrelevant for warm type

**Cold Agglutinin Disease Treatment:**
- Cold avoidance
- Rituximab (B-cell depletion)
- Sutimlimab (Enjaymo) — complement C1s inhibitor — FDA approved for cold agglutinin disease; prevents C3-mediated hemolysis
- Avoid warming IV fluids; minimize cold saline

**TTP (Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura):**
- Pentad: microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, neurologic symptoms, fever, renal failure (rarely all 5 present; first 2 sufficient to act)
- Pathophysiology: deficiency of ADAMTS13 (autoimmune or congenital) → ultra-large vWF multimers → platelet microthrombi
- PLASMIC score predicts ADAMTS13 deficiency
- Treatment: URGENT plasma exchange (PEX/TPE) 1-1.5 plasma volumes daily until platelet count >150k x2 days; glucocorticoids; rituximab for immune TTP; caplacizumab (anti-vWF nanobody) reduces time to normalization; do NOT give platelets (thrombosis risk)
- ADAMTS13 activity <10% confirms TTP; send before PEX

**HUS:**
- Typical (STEC-HUS): E. coli O157:H7 (Shiga toxin) — supportive care; avoid antibiotics and antimotility (worsen toxin release); dialysis if needed
- Atypical HUS (aHUS): complement dysregulation — eculizumab (terminal complement inhibitor) is treatment of choice

### Aplastic Anemia

**Definition:** Pancytopenia with hypocellular bone marrow (<25% cellularity with <30% hematopoietic cells) due to destruction or failure of hematopoietic stem cells.

**Causes:**
- Idiopathic/autoimmune (most common — T-cell mediated attack on HSCs)
- Drugs: chloramphenicol, NSAIDs, anticonvulsants, carbimazole, sulfonamides
- Viruses: EBV, CMV, hepatitis-associated aplastic anemia (non-A, B, C, E)
- PNH (paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria) — closely related; flow cytometry for GPI-anchored proteins
- Inherited: Fanconi anemia (chromosomal fragility test), dyskeratosis congenita (telomerase mutations), Shwachman-Diamond

**Severity Classification:**

| Severity | Criteria |
|---|---|
| Severe (SAA) | 2 of 3: ANC <500, platelets <20k, reticulocytes <20k; plus hypocellular marrow |
| Very Severe (VSAA) | SAA criteria + ANC <200 |
| Non-severe | Cytopenias not meeting SAA criteria; bone marrow hypocellular |

**Treatment:**

- Preferred for age <40 with matched sibling donor: allogeneic HSCT (curative, ~90% OS)
- Preferred for age >40 or no matched sibling: immunosuppressive therapy (IST)
  - Horse anti-thymocyte globulin (hATG, ATGAM) 40 mg/kg/day x4 days IV — superior to rabbit ATG for first-line
  - Cyclosporine 5 mg/kg/day PO (target trough 200-400); taper slowly over 2 years
  - Eltrombopag 150 mg/day added at start of IST (improves response rates); monitor liver function
  - Supportive: RBC transfusions (threshold Hgb 7-8), platelets (threshold 10k or 20k if bleeding), G-CSF (no clear OS benefit but reduces infections), infection prophylaxis (fluconazole, acyclovir)
  - Response assessed at 3-6 months; non-responders: matched unrelated donor HSCT or second course of IST

## Macrocytic Anemia

### Vitamin B12 (Cobalamin) Deficiency

**Causes:**
- Pernicious anemia (anti-intrinsic factor antibodies, anti-parietal cell antibodies) — autoimmune
- Gastrectomy, gastric bypass (loss of parietal cells)
- Ileal disease/resection (site of B12 absorption)
- Vegan diet (animal products only source)
- Metformin (reduces ileal B12 absorption — check B12 yearly)
- Proton pump inhibitors (long-term, modest effect)
- Nitrous oxide (inactivates B12; acute neurologic crisis)

**Clinical Findings:**
- Hematologic: macrocytic anemia, hypersegmented neutrophils (>5 lobes, or single 6-lobed = pathognomonic), pancytopenia in severe cases; oval macrocytes
- Neurologic (subacute combined degeneration of spinal cord):
  - Posterior column dysfunction: loss of vibration, proprioception, positive Romberg
  - Lateral column dysfunction: spastic weakness, hyperreflexia
  - Peripheral neuropathy: glove-stocking paresthesias
  - Cognitive impairment, dementia, psychiatric symptoms
  - Neurologic symptoms can occur WITHOUT anemia
- Glossitis, angular cheilitis

**Labs:**
- Serum B12 <200 pg/mL = deficient; 200-300 = borderline (check MMA)
- Methylmalonic acid (MMA): elevated in B12 deficiency; confirms deficiency when B12 borderline
- Homocysteine: elevated in both B12 and folate deficiency (less specific)
- Antiparietal cell antibodies (sensitive, 80-90% but not specific)
- Anti-intrinsic factor antibodies (specific for pernicious anemia, less sensitive)
- Reticulocytopenia, hypersegmented neutrophils on smear

**Treatment:**
- IM cyanocobalamin 1000 mcg daily x7 days, then weekly x4 weeks, then monthly
- High-dose oral B12 1000-2000 mcg daily (adequate even without intrinsic factor — passive absorption); equivalent efficacy to IM in most studies; preferred by some patients
- Nasal (Nascobal): 500 mcg weekly
- Expect reticulocyte peak at 5-7 days ("reticulocyte crisis"), then Hgb rise
- Hypokalemia can occur as new RBC production consumes potassium — monitor
- Neurologic recovery: may be incomplete if prolonged deficiency; treat promptly

### Folate Deficiency

**Causes:**
- Poor dietary intake (alcoholism, elderly, food insecurity)
- Malabsorption (celiac, IBD)
- Increased demand (pregnancy, hemolytic anemia, dialysis)
- Drugs: methotrexate, trimethoprim, phenytoin, sulfasalazine

**Clinical Findings:**
- Macrocytic anemia identical to B12 on smear and CBC
- NO neurologic involvement (critical distinction from B12)
- Glossitis possible
- Elevated homocysteine; MMA normal (distinguishes from B12)
- Neural tube defects if deficient periconception

**Treatment:**
- Folic acid 1 mg PO daily
- Prophylaxis in pregnancy: 400-800 mcg folic acid starting 1-3 months before conception
- Methotrexate toxicity: leucovorin (folinic acid) rescue, not folic acid

## Sickle Cell Disease

### Pathophysiology

- HbS (glutamate → valine substitution at beta-globin position 6)
- HbS polymerizes under low oxygen, acidosis, dehydration → RBC sickling
- Sickling causes vascular occlusion, hemolysis, endothelial injury, inflammation
- Chronic hemolysis → low haptoglobin, elevated LDH, pigment gallstones
- HbSS (homozygous) most severe; HbSC, HbS-beta thalassemia — intermediate

### Vaso-Occlusive Crisis (VOC)

**Clinical:** Severe bone/joint pain; most common in femoral head, lumbar spine, chest
**Triggers:** Cold, dehydration, hypoxia, infection, stress, menses

**Management:**
- IV fluids: 1.5x maintenance (avoid hyperhydration)
- Opioid analgesia:
  - Hydromorphone or morphine IV; titrate every 15-30 min until pain controlled
  - PCA preferred for inpatient
  - Ketorolac 30 mg IV q6h x5 days (adjunct; reduces opioid requirements)
  - Avoid meperidine (normeperidine seizures)
- Incentive spirometry every 2 hours (prevents ACS)
- NSAIDs as adjunct
- Acetaminophen for mild-moderate pain
- Discharge when oral opioids adequate, tolerating PO

### Acute Chest Syndrome (ACS)

**Definition:** New pulmonary infiltrate on CXR + any of: fever, chest pain, cough, hypoxia, respiratory symptoms

**Pathophysiology:** Fat embolism (from necrotic marrow), in situ sickling, infection (atypical organisms, RSV), pulmonary infarction

**Management:**
- Supplemental oxygen (maintain SaO2 >95%)
- IV fluids (moderate — avoid overload)
- Antibiotics: cover atypical organisms — azithromycin + ceftriaxone (or respiratory fluoroquinolone)
- Bronchodilators (albuterol)
- Simple transfusion: if Hgb >1 g/dL below baseline
- Exchange transfusion (RED CELL EXCHANGE): if Hgb >9 g/dL and worsening, or severe ACS; goal HbS <30%; requires apheresis or manual exchange
- Incentive spirometry
- Analgesia
- ICU if deteriorating

### Hydroxyurea

**Mechanism:** Increases fetal hemoglobin (HbF) production — HbF inhibits HbS polymerization; also reduces neutrophil/monocyte count, adhesion molecules, RBC dehydration

**Indications:** Recurrent VOC (>3/year), ACS, severe anemia, priapism, ALL patients ≥9 months per 2014 NHLBI guidelines

**Dosing:**
- Starting: 15 mg/kg/day (usually 500-1000 mg/day)
- Titrate by 5 mg/kg every 8-12 weeks to maximum tolerated dose (up to 35 mg/kg/day)
- Target: maximize HbF, maintain MCV >100, avoid cytopenias

**CBC Monitoring:**
- Every 2 weeks during dose titration
- Every 2-3 months when stable
- Hold if: ANC <2000, platelets <80k, Hgb <5 g/dL or >2 g/dL below baseline

**Benefits:** Reduces VOC frequency ~50%, reduces ACS, reduces mortality, may slow organ damage

### Transfusion in Sickle Cell Disease

**Simple Transfusion:**
- Indications: symptomatic anemia, Hgb drop >2 g/dL below baseline, aplastic crisis, sequestration crisis
- Goal: raise Hgb to ~10 g/dL (avoid >11 — hyperviscosity risk if HbS not adequately diluted)
- Use leukoreduced, sickle-negative (Hgb S negative), phenotype-matched units (at minimum, ABO/Rh/C/E/K matching reduces alloimmunization)

**Exchange Transfusion:**
- Indications: stroke, severe/worsening ACS, priapism >4-6h unresponsive to other treatment, pre-operative preparation (selected)
- Goal: HbS <30% while avoiding post-transfusion Hgb >10-11 g/dL
- Automated apheresis preferred; manual exchange possible

**Stroke Prevention:**
- Transcranial Doppler (TCD) screening annually from age 2 to 16
- If TCD velocity >200 cm/s: chronic monthly simple transfusion (goal HbS <30%)
- Hydroxyurea is alternative for primary prevention if transfusion not available

**Iron Overload:**
- Occurs with chronic transfusion (each unit adds ~200-250 mg iron)
- Monitor: serum ferritin every 3 months; liver iron concentration by MRI (R2/FerriScan); cardiac T2* MRI
- Chelation therapy:
  - Deferasirox (Exjade/Jadenu): oral, once daily; preferred; monitor creatinine, LFTs, audiology
  - Deferoxamine (Desferal): SQ/IV infusion 8-12 hours/night; older standard; used if deferasirox intolerant
  - Deferiprone: oral, TID; good cardiac iron removal; risk of agranulocytosis — weekly ANC monitoring

### Functional Asplenia and Infection Prevention

- Functional asplenia begins in infancy; progressive with age in HbSS
- Encapsulated organisms: Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae type b, Neisseria meningitidis — highest risk

**Vaccinations:**
- Pneumococcal conjugate (PCV15 or PCV20) AND pneumococcal polysaccharide (PPSV23) — series per ACIP guidelines
- Meningococcal conjugate (MenACWY) — doses at 2, 4, 6, 12 months then booster every 3-5 years
- MenB — 2 or 3-dose series based on product
- Hib — routine childhood vaccination
- Influenza annually
- COVID-19 per current ACIP recommendations

**Penicillin Prophylaxis:**
- Penicillin VK 125 mg BID from age 2 months to 3 years; 250 mg BID age 3-5
- Continue beyond age 5 if history of pneumococcal sepsis or splenectomy

**Priapism:**
- Ischemic (low-flow) priapism most common in SCD
- Management of acute episode:
  - Hydration, opioids, ice/heat
  - Oral pseudoephedrine (alpha-agonist, first-line oral agent)
  - If >2-4 hours: penile aspiration and irrigation with dilute phenylephrine (alpha-agonist)
  - If >4-6 hours: surgical shunt
- Prevention: hydroxyurea, sildenafil (paradoxically beneficial in some), GnRH analogues

## Bleeding Disorders

### Hemophilia A and B

**Hemophilia A:**
- Factor VIII deficiency; X-linked recessive (males affected, females carriers but can bleed if low factor)
- Prevalence: 1 in 5000 male births

**Hemophilia B:**
- Factor IX deficiency; X-linked recessive
- "Christmas disease" — named after Stephen Christmas
- Prevalence: 1 in 25,000 male births

**Severity Classification:**

| Severity | Factor Activity | Clinical |
|---|---|---|
| Mild (5-40%) | 5-40% | Bleeding with major trauma/surgery; rarely spontaneous |
| Moderate (1-5%) | 1-5% | Bleeding with minor trauma; occasional spontaneous |
| Severe (<1%) | <1% | Spontaneous hemarthroses, muscle bleeds, life-threatening bleeds |

**Lab Pattern:**
- Elevated PTT (intrinsic pathway)
- Normal PT, platelet count, thrombin time
- Low factor VIII or IX activity (specific assay)
- vWF antigen normal (distinguishes from vWD which can lower factor VIII)

**Factor Replacement Principles:**
- Factor VIII: 1 unit/kg raises FVIII by 2%; half-life ~8-12 hours
  - Minor bleed: target 30-50%; major bleed/surgery: 80-100%
- Factor IX: 1 unit/kg raises FIX by 1%; half-life ~18-24 hours
- Recombinant products preferred over plasma-derived (no viral transmission risk)
- Extended half-life (EHL) products: PEGylated or Fc-fusion; allow less frequent dosing

**DDAVP (Desmopressin):**
- Stimulates endothelial release of VWF and FVIII from Weibel-Palade bodies
- Dose: 0.3 mcg/kg IV/SQ (nasal spray 150 mcg per nostril up to 300 mcg)
- Only for mild/moderate Hemophilia A; not for B
- Tachyphylaxis after 2-3 doses
- Test dose to confirm response before relying on in emergency
- Side effects: hyponatremia (fluid restriction post-dose), flushing, headache
- Contraindicated: age <2, history of hyponatremia, cardiovascular disease

**Prophylaxis:**
- Primary prophylaxis (before first joint bleed): all severe Hemophilia A and B
- Standard: FVIII every other day or 3x/week; FIX twice weekly
- EHL FVIII products: twice weekly or less; EHL FIX: once weekly or every 10-14 days

**Emicizumab (Hemlibra):**
- Bispecific antibody: binds FIXa and FX simultaneously, mimicking cofactor function of FVIII
- Approved: prophylaxis in Hemophilia A with or without inhibitors
- Route: SQ; weekly (3 mg/kg x4 loading), then biweekly (3 mg/kg) or monthly (6 mg/kg)
- Does not correct PTT; PTT remains elevated during monitoring (not a reflection of hemostatic efficacy)
- WARNING: if patient on emicizumab bleeds and needs bypassing agents, do NOT use aPCC (FEIBA) — risk of thrombotic microangiopathy and thromboembolism; use recombinant FVIIa at low doses or hold and monitor

**Inhibitors:**
- Inhibitory alloantibodies against FVIII develop in ~30% severe Hemophilia A, 3-5% Hemophilia B
- Bethesda assay quantifies inhibitor (Nijmegen modification more specific)
- High-titer (>5 BU): bypassing agents required

**Bypassing Agents:**

| Agent | Mechanism | Dose |
|---|---|---|
| Recombinant FVIIa (NovoSeven) | Activates factor X directly on platelet surface | 90 mcg/kg IV every 2-3h |
| aPCC/FEIBA | Activated prothrombin complex; contains FVIIa, Xa, IIa | 50-100 units/kg IV every 8-12h |

**Immune Tolerance Induction (ITI):**
- High-dose FVIII (50-200 units/kg daily) for months-years to eradicate inhibitor
- Success rate 60-80% in hemophilia A; poorer in hemophilia B (risk of nephrotic syndrome/anaphylaxis with ITI)
- Emicizumab has largely reduced urgency of ITI

### von Willebrand Disease (vWD)

**Epidemiology:** Most common inherited bleeding disorder; affects 1% of population

**Types:**

| Type | Defect | Prevalence | DDAVP Response |
|---|---|---|---|
| Type 1 | Partial quantitative deficiency of vWF | 75-80% | Yes (usually) |
| Type 2A | Qualitative: decreased large multimers, decreased platelet binding | 5-10% | Variable (may worsen) |
| Type 2B | Qualitative: increased affinity for GpIbα → platelet clumping, low large multimers | 5% | Contraindicated (thrombocytopenia worsens) |
| Type 2M | Qualitative: decreased platelet binding, normal multimers | Rare | No |
| Type 2N | Qualitative: decreased FVIII binding (mimics hemophilia A) | Rare | No |
| Type 3 | Severe quantitative deficiency | 1-5% | No |

**Lab Evaluation:**

| Test | Finding |
|---|---|
| vWF antigen (vWF:Ag) | Low in types 1, 2, 3; normal in 2N/2M |
| vWF activity (ristocetin cofactor, vWF:RCo) | Low in most types |
| Factor VIII level | Low (particularly type 2N and type 3) |
| Ristocetin-induced platelet aggregation (RIPA) | Low-dose RIPA increased in type 2B |
| vWF multimer analysis | Absent large multimers in 2A, 2B |
| Platelet count | Low in type 2B |

**Clinical Manifestations:**
- Mucocutaneous bleeding: epistaxis, gum bleeding, heavy menstrual bleeding, easy bruising
- Post-procedural bleeding (dental extraction, surgery)
- GI bleeding (especially type 2A, type 3)
- Less commonly: hemarthroses (type 3)

**Treatment:**

| Treatment | Indications | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| DDAVP 0.3 mcg/kg | Type 1, Type 2A (selected) | Releases stored vWF; test dose first; repeat q12-24h x2-3 doses |
| vWF concentrate (Humate-P, Wilate, Vonvendi) | Type 2 (most), Type 3, DDAVP failure | Plasma-derived or recombinant; also contains FVIII |
| Tranexamic acid 1g TID or 25 mg/kg | Mucosal bleeding; dental procedures; menorrhagia | Antifibrinolytic; can combine with DDAVP |
| Epsilon-aminocaproic acid | Similar to tranexamic acid | Alternative antifibrinolytic |
| Combined oral contraceptives | Heavy menstrual bleeding | Raises vWF levels |
| Levonorgestrel IUD | Heavy menstrual bleeding | Effective local option |

### Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT)

**Pathophysiology:**
- Unfractionated heparin (UFH) > LMWH; fondaparinux very low risk
- Heparin binds platelet factor 4 (PF4) → forms PF4-heparin complex → immune system generates IgG antibodies
- IgG-PF4-heparin complex binds FcγRIIa on platelets → platelet activation → thrombosis + consumption of platelets
- Paradox: thrombocytopenia but prothrombotic

**4T Score:**

| Category | 2 Points | 1 Point | 0 Points |
|---|---|---|---|
| Thrombocytopenia | >50% fall; nadir >20k | 30-50% fall; nadir 10-19k | <30% fall; nadir <10k |
| Timing | Days 5-10, or ≤1 day (prior heparin 30-100 days) | >10 days; or timing unclear | <4 days (no prior heparin) |
| Thrombosis | New confirmed thrombosis, skin necrosis | Progressive or recurrent thrombosis; erythematous skin lesions | None |
| oTher cause | No other cause | Possible other cause | Definite other cause |

- Score 0-3: low probability (HIT unlikely)
- Score 4-5: intermediate
- Score 6-8: high probability

**Diagnosis:**
- PF4-heparin ELISA (immunoassay): high sensitivity (~99%), lower specificity; OD >2.0 highly predictive
- Serotonin release assay (SRA): gold standard; 95-99% specificity; functional assay; send when ELISA positive
- Do not wait for SRA results to start treatment in high-probability cases

**Treatment — CRITICAL SEQUENCE:**
1. IMMEDIATELY STOP all heparin (UFH drips, LMWH injections, heparin flushes, heparin-coated catheters)
2. START non-heparin anticoagulant immediately:

| Agent | Dose | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Argatroban | 2 mcg/kg/min IV; titrate to aPTT 1.5-3x baseline; max 10 mcg/kg/min | Hepatically metabolized — preferred in renal failure; monitor LFTs; affects INR |
| Bivalirudin | 0.15 mg/kg/hr; adjust per aPTT | Renally and enzymatically cleared; good option in hepatic and renal failure |
| Fondaparinux | 5-10 mg SQ daily (weight-based) | Off-label; NOT for HIT with active thrombosis (slower onset); renally cleared |
| Danaparoid | 750 units IV bolus + infusion | Not available in US |

3. Do NOT start warfarin until platelet count recovers to >150k
4. Transition to warfarin: overlap with DTI for minimum 5 days AND therapeutic INR for 2 consecutive days before stopping DTI
5. AVOID platelet transfusions: may fuel thrombosis

**Duration of Anticoagulation:**
- HIT without thrombosis: at least 4 weeks
- HIT with thrombosis (HITT): 3 months minimum

**Long-term Considerations:**
- Document HIT as allergy in medical record
- Antibodies may wane over 3-6 months
- If future cardiac surgery requiring heparin and antibodies negative: single brief intraoperative heparin exposure may be acceptable with cardiology guidance
- If antibodies persist: use bivalirudin for cardiopulmonary bypass

### Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC)

**Pathophysiology:**
- Systemic activation of coagulation cascade (via tissue factor exposure)
- Simultaneous widespread microvascular thrombosis (end-organ ischemia)
- Consumption of clotting factors, platelets, fibrinogen
- Secondary fibrinolysis → elevated D-dimer and FDPs
- Net result: paradoxical bleeding AND thrombosis

**Causes:**
- Sepsis (most common): gram-negative endotoxin, gram-positive toxins
- Malignancy: AML-M3/APL (promyelocytes release tissue factor — chronic/subacute DIC), mucin-secreting adenocarcinomas
- Obstetric: placental abruption, amniotic fluid embolism, eclampsia, retained dead fetus
- Trauma and burns: tissue factor release
- Transfusion reactions (ABO incompatibility)
- Snake envenomation (some species)
- Liver failure, heat stroke

**Lab Findings:**

| Lab | DIC Finding |
|---|---|
| Platelets | Decreased (consumption) |
| PT/PTT | Prolonged (factor consumption) |
| Fibrinogen | Decreased (<150 mg/dL in overt DIC; may be normal early) |
| D-dimer | Markedly elevated |
| Fibrin degradation products (FDPs) | Elevated |
| Antithrombin III | Low |
| Protein C | Low |
| Peripheral smear | Schistocytes (microangiopathic component) |

**ISTH DIC Score:**

| Variable | Score |
|---|---|
| Platelets >100k: 0; 50-100k: 1; <50k: 2 | 0-2 |
| D-dimer normal: 0; moderate increase: 2; strong increase: 3 | 0-3 |
| PT prolonged <3s: 0; 3-6s: 1; >6s: 2 | 0-2 |
| Fibrinogen >1g/L: 0; <1g/L: 1 | 0-1 |
| Score ≥5 = overt DIC | |

**Treatment:**

Primary: Treat underlying cause

Supportive hemostasis (for active bleeding or pre-procedure):
- FFP: for PT/PTT >1.5x normal; dose 15-20 mL/kg
- Cryoprecipitate: for fibrinogen <150 mg/dL; 10 units raises fibrinogen ~50 mg/dL; each unit contains ~250 mg fibrinogen, vWF, FVIII, fibronectin
- Platelets: for platelet count <50k with active bleeding; <20k with high bleeding risk
- Vitamin K: if prolonged PT and not taking anticoagulants (correct nutritional deficiency)

Anticoagulation in DIC:
- Heparin infusion: thrombosis-predominant DIC (APL, purpura fulminans, acral ischemia, arterial thrombosis)
- Low-dose heparin: controversial; consider when fibrin deposition ongoing despite replacement therapy

Avoid:
- Antifibrinolytics (tranexamic acid, epsilon-aminocaproic acid) in most DIC — worsens thrombotic end-organ damage; EXCEPTION: hyperfibrinolysis-dominant DIC (trauma-associated, APL)

APL-specific DIC:
- ATRA (all-trans retinoic acid) + arsenic trioxide differentiate promyelocytes → reduces DIC source
- Heparin often used for APL coagulopathy while awaiting ATRA effect

## Tumor Lysis Syndrome (TLS)

### Definition and Pathophysiology

Rapid tumor cell lysis releases intracellular contents:
- Purines → uric acid (hyperuricemia → urate nephropathy, gout)
- Potassium → hyperkalemia (cardiac arrhythmias)
- Phosphate → hyperphosphatemia → secondary hypocalcemia (tetany, seizures, arrhythmias)
- Lactate, cytokines → acute kidney injury (multifactorial)

### Cairo-Bishop Criteria

**Laboratory TLS** (within 3 days before or 7 days after therapy): 2 or more of the following (from baseline or threshold):

| Lab | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Uric acid | >8 mg/dL or 25% increase from baseline |
| Potassium | >6 mEq/L or 25% increase |
| Phosphate | >4.5 mg/dL (adults) or 25% increase |
| Calcium | <7 mg/dL corrected or 25% decrease |

**Clinical TLS:** Laboratory TLS + one of:
- Serum creatinine >1.5x ULN
- Cardiac arrhythmia or sudden death
- Seizure

### Risk Stratification

| Risk | Tumors | Approach |
|---|---|---|
| High | Burkitt's lymphoma, ALL (WBC >100k), AML (WBC >100k), DLBCL bulky, lymphoblastic lymphoma | Rasburicase, aggressive IVF, inpatient monitoring |
| Intermediate | DLBCL non-bulky, CLL treated with venetoclax/rituximab, AML moderate | Allopurinol, IVF, close monitoring |
| Low | Solid tumors (most), CLL not treated, indolent lymphomas | Standard hydration, oral allopurinol, outpatient |

### Prevention

**IV Hydration:**
- Adults: 200-300 mL/hr NS (adjust for cardiac/renal function)
- Goal urine output: >80-100 mL/hr (or 3 mL/kg/hr in children)
- Avoid bicarbonate alkalinization (increases xanthine and calcium phosphate precipitation; rarely recommended)

**Allopurinol:**
- Xanthine oxidase inhibitor — reduces uric acid production from hypoxanthine and xanthine
- Dose: 100-300 mg PO TID (adults); start 2-3 days before chemotherapy
- Limitations: does not reduce existing uric acid; hypersensitivity syndrome (HLA-B*5801); interacts with azathioprine and mercaptopurine (increase levels — reduce dose 50-75% or use allopurinol cautiously)
- Adjust dose for renal impairment

**Rasburicase (Elitek):**
- Recombinant urate oxidase: converts uric acid to allantoin (much more soluble); rapidly lowers uric acid within hours
- Dose: 0.2 mg/kg IV daily x5 days (may use single dose 3-7.5 mg fixed dose in low-risk/adults based on protocols)
- Indications: high-risk TLS, elevated uric acid at diagnosis, intermediate risk with additional risk factors
- CONTRAINDICATED in G6PD deficiency — hydrogen peroxide byproduct causes severe hemolysis; screen for G6PD (particularly in males of African, Asian, Mediterranean descent) before administration
- Blood samples must be collected in pre-cooled tubes on ice and processed within 4 hours (rasburicase continues converting uric acid ex vivo, falsely lowering measured uric acid)

### Treatment of Established TLS

**Hyperkalemia:**
- Continuous cardiac monitoring
- Calcium gluconate 1g IV (membrane stabilization if ECG changes — peaked T waves, wide QRS, sine wave)
- Insulin 10 units IV + D50W 50 mL (shifts K into cells; onset 15-30 min)
- Sodium bicarbonate IV (shifts K; less effective alone)
- Patiromer (Veltassa), sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (SZC/Lokelma), or sodium polystyrene sulfonate (Kayexalate) — GI cation exchangers; eliminate K (onset hours)
- Furosemide if urine output adequate
- Dialysis for refractory or severe hyperkalemia

**Hyperphosphatemia:**
- Dietary phosphate restriction
- Oral phosphate binders: sevelamer (Renvela, no calcium load, preferred); calcium carbonate; lanthanum
- Dialysis for severe hyperphosphatemia or acute kidney injury

**Hypocalcemia:**
- Treat only if symptomatic (tetany, seizures, prolonged QTc, arrhythmia) — treating asymptomatic hypocalcemia in the setting of hyperphosphatemia risks calcium phosphate precipitation
- Symptomatic: calcium gluconate 1-2g IV slow infusion; calcium chloride for severe

**Acute Kidney Injury:**
- Maintain hydration
- Rasburicase if uric acid elevated
- Avoid nephrotoxins
- Dialysis indications: oliguria/anuria refractory to diuretics, severe electrolyte disturbances, volume overload

## Neutropenic Fever

### Definition
- ANC <500 cells/mcL (or <1000 with expected fall to <500 within 48h)
- Temperature: single oral temp >38.3°C (101°F), or ≥38.0°C (100.4°F) sustained x1 hour

### MASCC Risk Score

| Criterion | Points |
|---|---|
| Burden of illness: no or mild symptoms | 5 |
| No hypotension (systolic >90) | 5 |
| No COPD | 4 |
| Solid tumor or no prior fungal infection | 4 |
| No dehydration requiring IV fluids | 3 |
| Burden of illness: moderate symptoms | 3 |
| Outpatient status at fever onset | 3 |
| Age <60 | 2 |

- Score ≥21 = low risk (consider outpatient oral regimen)
- Score <21 = high risk (hospitalize, IV therapy)

**CISNE score** for solid tumor patients: better stratifies truly low-risk outpatient candidates (ECOG 0, no COPD, no CKD, no stress hyperglycemia, no mucositis grade 2+, monocytes ≥200)

### Empiric Antibiotic Regimens

**Low-Risk (MASCC ≥21):**
- Ciprofloxacin 500 mg PO BID + amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg PO BID
- Requires: tolerating PO, no recent fluoroquinolone prophylaxis, reliable follow-up within 24h, no prior resistant organism
- If FQ prophylaxis was used: admit and treat as high-risk

**High-Risk — Monotherapy (Anti-Pseudomonal):**

| Agent | Dose | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Cefepime | 2g IV q8h | First-line; good Pseudomonas + gram-positive coverage |
| Piperacillin-tazobactam | 4.5g IV q6h (or extended infusion) | Broad spectrum; avoid if ESBL risk |
| Meropenem | 1g IV q8h | Reserve for high-risk resistance; ESBL coverage |
| Imipenem-cilastatin | 500mg IV q6h | Alternative carbapenem |

**Add Vancomycin if ANY of:**
- Hemodynamic instability
- Clinically apparent catheter-related infection
- Soft tissue or skin infection
- MRSA colonization known
- Severe mucositis (streptococcal viridans bacteremia risk)
- Recent FQ prophylaxis with breakthrough fever (resistant gram-positives)
- Positive blood culture gram-positive cocci pending speciation

**Antifungal Escalation:**
- Persistent fever day 4-7 despite broad antibacterials, or new fever after defervescence after being afebrile
- Empiric antifungal: caspofungin 70 mg IV x1, then 50 mg IV daily; OR liposomal amphotericin B 3-5 mg/kg/day
- CT chest/sinuses to evaluate for mold (Aspergillus) if prolonged neutropenia
- Galactomannan antigen (Aspergillus) and beta-D-glucan from blood
- Consider voriconazole if strong concern for Aspergillus

**Duration of Antibiotics:**
- Afebrile and ANC >500 for 48 hours AND clinically stable: stop antibiotics
- If source identified: pathogen-directed therapy for appropriate duration
- If persistent neutropenia but afebrile x5-7 days: may consider stopping if clinically well (high-risk neutropenia excepted)
- Do not prolong antibiotics beyond 14 days without source or sepsis justification

### Prophylaxis During Chemotherapy

| Drug | Indication | Dose |
|---|---|---|
| Fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin or ciprofloxacin) | AML induction, allo-HSCT, anticipated ANC <100 x7+ days | Levofloxacin 500 mg daily; ciprofloxacin 500 mg BID |
| Fluconazole | Allo-HSCT (through engraftment) | 400 mg daily |
| Micafungin | Allo-HSCT (pre-engraftment) | 50 mg IV daily |
| Voriconazole/posaconazole | Allo-HSCT GVHD, AML induction/consolidation | Posaconazole 300 mg daily or BID loading |
| Acyclovir/valacyclovir | HSV seropositive allo-HSCT, CLL treated with alemtuzumab | Acyclovir 400 mg BID; valacyclovir 500 mg BID |
| TMP-SMX (Bactrim) | PCP prophylaxis: allo-HSCT, prolonged steroids, T-cell depleting agents | DS tablet BID 3x/week or daily |
| G-CSF (filgrastim, pegfilgrastim) | Primary prophylaxis if expected febrile neutropenia rate >20%, or secondary prophylaxis | Peg-filgrastim 6 mg SQ x1 per cycle (24h post-chemo) |

## Anticoagulation and Thrombophilia

### Thrombophilia Workup

**Indications:** Unprovoked VTE (DVT or PE) especially at young age (<50), unusual site (mesenteric, cerebral, portal), recurrent VTE, strong family history, pregnancy-related VTE

**Timing:** Do NOT test while on anticoagulation (most tests affected); test 4-6 weeks after stopping anticoagulation, or use DNA-based tests (Factor V Leiden, prothrombin gene) which are unaffected

| Thrombophilia | Prevalence | DVT Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Factor V Leiden (heterozygous) | 5% white; <1% Black, Asian | 3-7x increased |
| Factor V Leiden (homozygous) | 0.02-0.1% | 50-80x increased |
| Prothrombin G20210A (heterozygous) | 2-3% | 3x increased |
| Protein C deficiency | 0.2-0.4% | 7-10x increased |
| Protein S deficiency | 0.03-0.13% | 5-10x increased |
| Antithrombin deficiency | 0.02% | 20-50x increased |
| Antiphospholipid syndrome | 1-5% VTE population | 5-10x increased (arterial and venous) |

**Test Affected by Anticoagulation:**

| Test | Effect of Warfarin | Effect of Heparin/LMWH | Effect of DOAC |
|---|---|---|---|
| Protein C activity | Decreased | Minimal | Variable |
| Protein S activity | Decreased | Minimal | Variable |
| Antithrombin | Normal | Decreased | Minimal |
| Lupus anticoagulant (LA) | Affects (Dilute Russell viper venom test) | Affects | Affects (especially anti-Xa DOACs) |
| Factor V Leiden PCR | Not affected | Not affected | Not affected |
| Prothrombin gene PCR | Not affected | Not affected | Not affected |
| Anticardiolipin / anti-β2GP1 | Not affected | Not affected | Not affected |

### Antiphospholipid Syndrome (APS)

**Revised Sapporo/Sydney Criteria (2006):**
At least ONE clinical criterion AND one laboratory criterion:

Clinical criteria:
- Vascular thrombosis (arterial, venous, or small vessel)
- Pregnancy morbidity (≥3 unexplained early losses, or late loss ≥10 weeks without other explanation, or premature birth <34 weeks with severe preeclampsia)

Laboratory criteria (positive on 2+ occasions ≥12 weeks apart):
- Lupus anticoagulant (LA) — detected by clotting-based assay (dRVVT, aPTT-based)
- Anticardiolipin antibody IgG or IgM >40 GPL/MPL units (medium-high titer)
- Anti-beta-2 glycoprotein I IgG or IgM >99th percentile

**Triple Positive (LA + aCL + anti-β2GP1):** Highest risk for recurrent thrombosis; warfarin indefinitely; DOAC inferior

**Treatment:**
- Thrombotic APS: warfarin (INR 2-3); DOACs are inferior to warfarin (rivaroxaban TRAPS trial — increased recurrent events with rivaroxaban in triple positive)
- Obstetric APS (pregnancy morbidity without thrombosis): low-dose aspirin + prophylactic LMWH during pregnancy
- Triple positive with any thrombosis: INR 2-3 warfarin, indefinitely

### VTE Duration of Anticoagulation

| Scenario | Duration |
|---|---|
| Provoked by major transient risk factor (surgery ≥30 min, hospitalization, trauma) | 3 months |
| Provoked by minor transient risk factor (travel, oral contraceptive use, estrogen, minor procedure) | 3-6 months; consider extended if multiple risk factors |
| Unprovoked first DVT (isolated distal) | 3 months; reassess |
| Unprovoked first proximal DVT or PE | Minimum 3-6 months; assess recurrence risk; consider extended |
| Recurrent unprovoked VTE | Indefinite anticoagulation |
| Active cancer (cancer-associated VTE) | LMWH or DOAC (apixaban, rivaroxaban) while cancer active and/or chemotherapy ongoing |
| APS | Warfarin indefinitely (see above) |
| Inherited thrombophilia, high risk (AT deficiency, homozygous FVL, double heterozygous) | Indefinite after unprovoked VTE |

**Recurrence Risk Assessment Tools:**
- HERDOO2 score (women): low score → safe to stop at 3-6 months
- Vienna nomogram
- D-dimer testing 1 month after stopping: elevated D-dimer → consider re-starting (PROLONG study)

### DOACs vs Warfarin

| Drug | Target | Indication | Reversal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Apixaban (Eliquis) | Factor Xa | VTE, AF, ACS adjunct | Andexanet alfa (Andexxa) |
| Rivaroxaban (Xarelto) | Factor Xa | VTE, AF, ACS, CAD | Andexanet alfa |
| Edoxaban (Savaysa) | Factor Xa | VTE (after parenteral), AF | Andexanet alfa |
| Betrixaban (Bevyxxa) | Factor Xa | Extended VTE prophylaxis hospitalized | Andexanet alfa |
| Dabigatran (Pradaxa) | Thrombin (IIa) | VTE, AF | Idarucizumab (Praxbind) |
| Warfarin | Vitamin K (II, VII, IX, X) | APS, mechanical valves, AF, VTE | Vitamin K, 4F-PCC, FFP |

**Situations Favoring Warfarin Over DOAC:**
- Mechanical heart valve (warfarin only — RE-ALIGN trial dabigatran inferior)
- Antiphospholipid syndrome (especially triple positive)
- Severe renal failure (GFR <15-25 mL/min; though warfarin also complex)
- Drug interactions limiting DOAC use

### Extravasation of Vesicant Chemotherapy

**Vesicants (cause tissue necrosis):**
- Anthracyclines (doxorubicin, daunorubicin, epirubicin)
- Vinca alkaloids (vincristine, vinblastine)
- Mitomycin C
- Nitrogen mustard
- Cisplatin (>0.5 mg/mL)
- Taxanes (paclitaxel, docetaxel)

**Irritants (cause inflammation without necrosis):**
- Carboplatin, oxaliplatin, etoposide, ifosfamide, topotecan

**Immediate Management of Extravasation:**
1. Stop infusion immediately
2. Leave needle/catheter in place; aspirate residual drug
3. Mark erythema borders with pen
4. Notify oncology/pharmacy
5. Apply specific antidote based on agent
6. Document and photograph

**Antidotes:**

| Vesicant | Antidote | Application |
|---|---|---|
| Anthracyclines | Dexrazoxane (Totect) | IV 1000 mg/m2 day 1-2; 500 mg/m2 day 3; within 6h of extravasation; over large vein distant from site |
| Vinca alkaloids | Hyaluronidase (150-900 units) | Intradermal or SQ injection at margins of extravasation; warm compresses |
| Mitomycin C | DMSO 99% solution | Topical application to area every 8h x7 days; DO NOT cover with dressing |
| Nitrogen mustard | Sodium thiosulfate 1/6 molar | Intradermal injections at margins; neutralizes alkylating agent |
| Anthracyclines (alternative/older) | DMSO 99% topical | If dexrazoxane not available; every 8h x7 days; avoid occlusive dressings |

| Risk | Agents | Expected Vomiting Without Prophylaxis |

### Immunotherapy (Checkpoint Inhibitor) Toxicities

**Mechanism:** Anti-PD-1 (nivolumab, pembrolizumab), anti-PD-L1 (atezolizumab, durvalumab, avelumab), anti-CTLA-4 (ipilimumab, tremelimumab) — remove immune brakes → autoimmune inflammation in any organ

**General Grading and Management:**

| Grade | Definition | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Grade 1 | Mild; asymptomatic or mild symptoms | Hold ICI only if certain organs; monitor closely |
| Grade 2 | Moderate; some interference with daily activities | Hold ICI; prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day; reassess |
| Grade 3 | Severe; significant limitation | Hold or permanently discontinue ICI; prednisone 1-2 mg/kg/day |
| Grade 4 | Life-threatening | Permanently discontinue; IV methylprednisolone 1-2 mg/kg/day; escalate |

**Organ-Specific irAEs:**

| Organ | irAE | Specific Management |
|---|---|---|
| Lung | Pneumonitis (cough, dyspnea, infiltrates) | Hold ICI; prednisone 1-2 mg/kg; if grade 3-4: IV methylprednisolone; infliximab or cyclophosphamide for refractory |
| GI | Colitis (diarrhea, hematochezia, abdominal pain) | Hold ICI grade 2+; prednisone 1-2 mg/kg; if grade 3-4: methylprednisolone IV; infliximab 5 mg/kg x1 (if steroid-refractory, check TB status first); vedolizumab for anti-CTLA-4 colitis |
| Liver | Hepatitis (elevated transaminases) | Hold ICI; prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg for grade 2; 1-2 mg/kg grade 3+; mycophenolate for steroid-refractory; avoid infliximab (hepatotoxic) |
| Endocrine | Thyroiditis (hypo or hyperthyroidism) | Grade 1-2: continue ICI; levothyroxine for hypothyroidism; methimazole/beta-blocker for hyperthyroidism until resolution |
| Endocrine | Hypophysitis (headache, fatigue, anterior pituitary failure) | Hold ICI for grade 2+; steroid replacement (cortisol first priority); hormone replacement as needed; MRI pituitary |
| Endocrine | Primary adrenal insufficiency | Hydrocortisone replacement; hold ICI grade 2+ |
| Endocrine | Type 1 diabetes (insulin-deficient, can present as DKA) | Hold ICI; insulin therapy; DKA protocol; may be permanent |
| Skin | Rash, pruritus, vitiligo | Topical steroids; oral antihistamines; hold for grade 3 |
| Skin | Stevens-Johnson (rare, grade 4) | Permanently discontinue; dermatology/burn unit |
| Rheumatologic | Arthritis, myositis, polymyalgia | NSAIDs grade 1; prednisone; infliximab/anti-IL-6 for severe |
| Renal | Nephritis (rising creatinine) | Hold ICI; prednisone 1-2 mg/kg; renal biopsy to guide therapy |
| Neurologic | Meningitis, neuropathy, myasthenic syndrome, encephalitis | Permanently discontinue in grade 3-4; IV steroids; IVIG/PLEX for myasthenic syndrome |
| Cardiac | Myocarditis (troponin elevation, arrhythmia, heart block) — rare but fatal | Permanently discontinue ICI; high-dose IV methylprednisolone 1g/day; cardiology urgently; consider abatacept |

**Restarting ICI After irAE:**
- May restart for grade 1-2 irAEs after resolution to grade 0-1 and steroid taper to ≤10 mg/day prednisone equivalent
- Do NOT restart for grade 3-4 pneumonitis, myocarditis, neurologic, colitis recurrence, or any grade 4 toxicity
- Consider switching agent class (anti-PD-1 to anti-PD-L1) if benefit clearly outweighs risk

## Selected Hematologic Malignancies

### Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML)

**Diagnosis:** ≥20% blasts in peripheral blood or bone marrow (by WHO 2022); or diagnostic cytogenetic abnormality regardless of blast count (t(8;21), inv(16), t(15;17))

**Key Genetic/Molecular Markers:**

| Mutation | Prevalence | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| FLT3-ITD | 25-30% | Poor prognosis; FLT3 inhibitor targeted therapy |
| NPM1 mutation | 25-30% | Favorable prognosis (if FLT3-WT); minimal residual disease marker |
| CEBPA biallelic | 8-10% | Favorable prognosis |
| IDH1/IDH2 | 15-20% | Targeted therapy available (ivosidenib IDH1, enasidenib IDH2) |
| TP53 | 5-10% | Very poor prognosis |
| Core-binding factor AML (CBF): t(8;21), inv(16)/t(16;16) | 15% | Favorable; gemtuzumab ozogamicin benefit |
| KIT mutation in CBF | 20-30% of CBF | Intermediate/poor prognosis modifier |

**APL (AML-M3, t(15;17), PML-RARA):**
- Unique: DIC (see above)
- Treatment: ATRA + arsenic trioxide (ATO) — non-cytotoxic differentiation therapy
- Low-risk (WBC <10k): ATRA + ATO alone (no chemotherapy required — APOLLO/LPA2005 regimens)
- High-risk (WBC ≥10k): ATRA + ATO + gemtuzumab ozogamicin or ATRA + idarubicin
- Monitor for differentiation syndrome: fever, weight gain, pulmonary infiltrates, edema, hypotension — steroids (dexamethasone 10 mg BID) + hold ATRA if severe

**Induction Chemotherapy (Standard AML, non-APL):**
- "7+3": cytarabine 100-200 mg/m2/day continuous infusion x7 days + daunorubicin 60-90 mg/m2 or idarubicin 12 mg/m2 IV x3 days
- Venetoclax (BCL-2 inhibitor) + azacitidine: frontline for elderly/unfit patients; CR rate ~65-70%
- CPX-351 (liposomal cytarabine + daunorubicin): for therapy-related AML or AML with myelodysplasia-related changes (AML-MRC); superior OS in these subtypes
- FLT3-positive: add midostaurin (Rydapt) to 7+3; gilteritinib in relapsed/refractory FLT3+ AML

### Chronic Myeloid Leukemia (CML)

**Pathophysiology:** t(9;22) Philadelphia chromosome → BCR-ABL1 fusion protein (constitutively active tyrosine kinase)

**Phases:**
- Chronic phase (CP): WBC >100k, basophilia, splenomegaly, <10% blasts — most patients
- Accelerated phase (AP): blasts 10-19%, basophils >20%, persistent thrombocytopenia/thrombocytosis
- Blast phase (BP): ≥20% blasts (myeloid or lymphoid) = acute leukemia

**Treatment — Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitors (TKIs):**

| Generation | Drug | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | Imatinib (Gleevec) | Milestone therapy; generic available; once daily |
| 2nd | Dasatinib (Sprycel) | More potent; CNS penetration; pleural effusions |
| 2nd | Nilotinib (Tasigna) | More potent; QT prolongation; peripheral vascular events |
| 2nd | Bosutinib (Bosulif) | Diarrhea GI toxicity; less cardiovascular |
| 3rd | Ponatinib (Iclusig) | For T315I mutation (resistant to 1st/2nd gen); arterial occlusive events |
| 3rd | Asciminib (Scemblix) | STAMP inhibitor; T315I indication at higher dose; favorable safety |

**Response Milestones:**

| Milestone | Time | Definition | Action If Not Met |
|---|---|---|---|
| Complete hematologic response (CHR) | 3 months | Normal CBC, no splenomegaly | Check compliance, consider switch |
| Complete cytogenetic response (CCyR) | 12 months | 0% Ph+ metaphases | Switch TKI |
| Major molecular response (MMR) | 12-18 months | BCR-ABL1 ≤0.1% IS | Reassess TKI choice |
| Deep molecular response (DMR) | Ongoing | BCR-ABL1 ≤0.01% (MR4) or ≤0.0032% (MR4.5) | Eligibility for TKI discontinuation (TFR) |

**Treatment-Free Remission (TFR):** Discontinue TKI after sustained DMR ≥2 years on TKI; ~50% maintain remission; restart TKI if molecular relapse (regain remission rapidly)

### Multiple Myeloma

**Diagnostic Criteria (SLiM-CRAB):**
- Clonal bone marrow plasma cells ≥10% or biopsy-proven plasmacytoma
- PLUS any of:
  - C: Calcium elevated (>11 mg/dL)
  - R: Renal (creatinine >2 mg/dL or CrCl <40)
  - A: Anemia (Hgb <10 g/dL)
  - B: Bone disease (osteolytic lesion by CT/PET-CT/X-ray)
  - SLiM criteria (biomarkers of near-inevitable progression):
    - S: >60% clonal plasma cells
    - Li: free light chain ratio >100
    - M: >1 focal lesion on MRI

**Workup:** SPEP + immunofixation; serum free light chains (kappa/lambda ratio); 24h urine UPEP + immunofixation; bone marrow biopsy with cytogenetics (FISH for del17p, t(4;14), t(14;16) — high risk); whole body low-dose CT or PET-CT; LDH, beta-2 microglobulin, albumin (staging)

**Revised ISS (R-ISS) Staging:**

| Stage | Criteria | Median OS |
|---|---|---|
| I | ISS I + standard-risk cytogenetics + LDH normal | Not reached |
| II | Not I or III | ~83 months |
| III | ISS III + high-risk cytogenetics (del17p/t(4;14)/t(14;16)) OR high LDH | ~43 months |

**Treatment Framework:**
- Transplant-eligible: induction (VRd: bortezomib + lenalidomide + dexamethasone, or Dara-VRd) x 3-4 cycles → stem cell mobilization → ASCT → maintenance (lenalidomide ± bortezomib)
- Transplant-ineligible: DRd (daratumumab + lenalidomide + dexamethasone) or VRd-lite; maintenance lenalidomide
- High-risk disease (del17p, t(4;14), t(14;16)): proteasome inhibitor-based maintenance; bortezomib preferred

**Key Drug Classes:**

| Class | Examples | Notable Toxicities |
|---|---|---|
| Proteasome inhibitors | Bortezomib, carfilzomib, ixazomib | Peripheral neuropathy (bortezomib), cardiovascular (carfilzomib) |
| IMiDs | Thalidomide, lenalidomide, pomalidomide | VTE (prophylaxis required), cytopenias, teratogenicity (REMS) |
| Anti-CD38 monoclonal antibodies | Daratumumab, isatuximab | Infusion reactions, interference with Coombs testing and blood bank crossmatch |
| Anti-BCMA | Belantamab mafodotin (ADC), elranatamab (bispecific), teclistamab (bispecific) | Keratopathy (belantamab), CRS (bispecifics), neurotoxicity |
| CAR-T (anti-BCMA) | Idecabtagene vicleucel (Abecma), ciltacabtagene autoleucel (Carvykti) | CRS, neurotoxicity (ICANS), infections; reserved for relapsed/refractory ≥4 prior lines |

**Bone Disease:** Bisphosphonates (zoledronic acid 4 mg IV monthly or denosumab 120 mg SQ monthly) for all active myeloma patients with bone disease or osteopenia; reduce skeletal-related events; monitor creatinine and calcium; osteonecrosis of jaw risk (dental evaluation before starting)

### Diffuse Large B-Cell Lymphoma (DLBCL)

**First-Line Treatment:**
- R-CHOP (rituximab, cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, prednisone) x6 cycles
- Interim PET-CT after 2-4 cycles (Deauville 4-5 = poor response — escalate therapy)
- Pola-R-CHP (polatuzumab vedotin replaces vincristine) — superior PFS to R-CHOP in frontline (POLARIX trial); preferred for higher-risk disease
- CNS prophylaxis: intrathecal MTX or high-dose MTX IV for high CNS-IPI score (>2 extranodal sites, elevated LDH, performance status ≥2, testicular involvement, renal/adrenal involvement)

**Relapsed/Refractory:**
- Salvage chemo (R-ICE, R-DHAP, R-GDP) → response assessment → autologous HSCT if chemo-sensitive
- CAR-T cell therapy (axicabtagene ciloleucel [Yescarta], tisagenlecleucel [Kymriah], lisocabtagene maraleucel [Breyanzi]) — approved for relapsed/refractory after ≥2 prior lines; now second-line eligible per ZUMA-7/TRANSFORM
- Loncastuximab tesirine, tafasitamab + lenalidomide, selinexor — additional options

## Supportive Care

### Growth Factors

**G-CSF (Filgrastim, Pegfilgrastim, Tbo-filgrastim):**
- Stimulates neutrophil production; shortens duration of neutropenia
- Primary prophylaxis: chemotherapy regimens with febrile neutropenia risk >20%, or patient-specific risk factors
- Pegfilgrastim 6 mg SQ x1 dose per cycle (24h after chemotherapy completion; avoid same day as chemo or within 14 days of next cycle)
- Filgrastim 5 mcg/kg/day SQ starting day +1 post-chemo; continue until ANC recovery
- Biosimilars: filgrastim-sndz (Zarxio), pegfilgrastim-jmdb (Fulphila), etc.

**Erythropoiesis-Stimulating Agents (ESAs):**
- Epoetin alfa, darbepoetin: stimulate erythropoiesis
- Cancer patients on chemotherapy (palliative intent only): target Hgb 10-12 g/dL; risk of thromboembolism; may stimulate tumor growth in some malignancies
- CKD: target Hgb 10-11.5 g/dL; avoid >13 (increased CV events per TREAT/CHOIR trials)
- Ensure adequate iron stores before and during ESA therapy

**Thrombopoietin Receptor Agonists:**
- Romiplostim (Nplate) and eltrombopag (Promacta): stimulate platelet production
- Approved: ITP, aplastic anemia (eltrombopag), HCV-related thrombocytopenia (eltrombopag), myelodysplastic syndrome (luspatercept/eltrombopag)
- Avatrombopag, lusutrombopag: short-course for procedural thrombocytopenia in CLD patients

### Transfusion Thresholds (General Guidelines)

| Blood Product | Threshold (Stable Inpatient) | Special Considerations |
|---|---|---|
| Packed RBCs | Hgb <7 g/dL (restrictive) | <8 in cardiac disease, postoperative cardiac surgery, symptomatic anemia |
| Platelets (prophylactic) | <10k (stable) | <20k if bleeding risk, fever, rapid drop; <50k before invasive procedure; <100k for neurosurgery/ophthalmology |
| FFP | PT/INR >1.5x or active bleeding with coagulopathy | Not indicated for mild prolongation or nutritional repletion |
| Cryoprecipitate | Fibrinogen <150 mg/dL with bleeding | Massive transfusion protocol: give based on ratio (1 pool cryo per 10 RBCs) |

**Massive Transfusion Protocol (MTP):** Hemorrhage >10 units pRBC in 24h or active uncontrolled bleeding
- Balanced resuscitation: 1:1:1 ratio pRBC:FFP:platelets
- Cryoprecipitate early
- TXA (tranexamic acid) 1g IV within 3h of injury (CRASH-2 trial)
- Calcium replacement (citrate toxicity from rapid transfusion)
- Monitor TEG/ROTEM to guide factor product use
- Avoid aggressive crystalloid resuscitation (dilutional coagulopathy)
