## Emergency Department Triage

### Overview

Triage is the process of sorting patients by urgency of need and prioritizing care based on available resources. The goal is to identify the most critically ill patients immediately while ensuring that all patients receive appropriate evaluation.

---

### ESI (Emergency Severity Index) — Five-Level Triage System

The ESI is the most widely adopted triage system in the United States. It classifies patients into five levels based on acuity and anticipated resource needs.

#### ESI Level 1 — Immediate / Resuscitation

- **Definition:** Requires immediate life-saving intervention.
- **Criteria:** Patient is in immediate danger of death or severe disability without intervention. Unresponsive, pulseless, apneic, or in active hemorrhagic shock.
- **Examples:** Cardiac arrest, respiratory failure, unresponsive trauma, status epilepticus with airway compromise, active airway obstruction.
- **Resource use:** Unlimited; full resuscitation team activates immediately.
- **Disposition time:** Zero — begins in triage room or at bedside.

#### ESI Level 2 — Emergent / High Risk

- **Definition:** High-risk situation, confused or lethargic, or in severe pain or distress.
- **Criteria:** Vital signs may be abnormal. Patient is not in immediate danger but may deteriorate rapidly. Clinical presentation suggests a high-risk condition requiring rapid evaluation.
- **Key screening question:** Should this patient wait?
- **Examples:** Chest pain with ST changes, altered mental status, active stroke, severe sepsis, ectopic pregnancy with hemodynamic instability, severe allergic reaction without airway compromise.
- **Resource use:** Multiple; labs, imaging, and procedures expected.
- **Disposition time:** Less than 15 minutes to provider evaluation.

#### ESI Level 3 — Urgent

- **Definition:** Stable vital signs but expected to require two or more resources.
- **Criteria:** Patient is stable but requires evaluation, testing, or treatment. Vital signs within normal limits or mildly abnormal.
- **Examples:** Moderate abdominal pain, urinary tract infection with normal vitals, minor fracture, headache without red flags (stable), laceration requiring repair.
- **Resource use:** Two or more expected (e.g., labs AND imaging).
- **Disposition time:** Can wait but should be evaluated within 30 minutes to 1 hour.

#### ESI Level 4 — Less Urgent / Semi-Urgent

- **Definition:** Stable patient requiring one resource.
- **Criteria:** One diagnostic test or procedure expected; no anticipated clinical deterioration.
- **Examples:** Sore throat requiring rapid strep test, minor extremity injury requiring x-ray, rash requiring examination only, medication refill request with one lab check.
- **Resource use:** One expected.
- **Disposition time:** Patient may wait; typically evaluated within 1-2 hours.

#### ESI Level 5 — Non-Urgent

- **Definition:** Stable patient requiring no resources; examination or history only.
- **Criteria:** No labs, no imaging, no procedures anticipated. Conditions that could be managed in an outpatient primary care setting.
- **Examples:** Cold symptoms, prescription refills, minor skin complaint with no testing needed.
- **Resource use:** None expected.
- **Disposition time:** Lowest priority for evaluation; appropriate for redirection to urgent care or primary care.

#### ESI Vital Sign Criteria for Considering Upgrade

| Age Group | High-Risk HR | High-Risk RR | SpO2 Threshold |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| < 3 months | > 180 | > 50 | < 92% |
| 3–36 months | > 160 | > 40 | < 92% |
| 3–8 years | > 140 | > 30 | < 92% |
| > 8 years | > 100 | > 20 | < 92% |

Patients with abnormal vital signs who would otherwise be classified ESI Level 3 should be considered for upgrade to Level 2.

### START Triage — Mass Casualty Incident (MCI)

START (Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment) is designed for use in mass casualty incidents where resources are overwhelmed. Assessment takes approximately 30 seconds per patient.

#### Sequence of Assessment

1. **Respirations** — Is the patient breathing? If no: reposition airway. If still no: tag Black (Expectant/Deceased). If breathing rate > 30/min: tag Red (Immediate).
2. **Perfusion** — Radial pulse present? Capillary refill > 2 seconds? If no radial pulse or delayed refill: tag Red (Immediate). Control life-threatening hemorrhage with direct pressure or tourniquet.
3. **Mental Status** — Can patient follow simple commands (e.g., "squeeze my hand")? Failure to obey: tag Red. Obeys commands with normal respirations and perfusion: tag Yellow.
4. **Walking wounded** — Prior to individual assessments, call out for any walking wounded. Those who walk to the designated area are tagged Green (Minor).

#### START Categories

| Tag Color | Category | Criteria | Action |
|-----------|----------|----------|--------|
| Red | Immediate | Respiratory rate > 30, capillary refill > 2 sec, absent radial pulse, cannot follow commands | Treat first; life-threatening but salvageable |
| Yellow | Delayed | Can follow commands, perfusion intact, RR normal, injuries significant but stable | Treat second after Immediate; can wait minutes to hours |
| Green | Minor | Ambulatory, minor injuries, no respiratory or perfusion compromise | "Walking wounded"; defer treatment until Red/Yellow addressed |
| Black | Expectant/Deceased | Apneic after airway repositioning, or injuries incompatible with survival | No resuscitation in MCI context; comfort only if resources allow |

#### START Limitations

- Does not address burns, pediatrics (use JumpSTART for pediatrics), or blast injury specifically.
- Triage categories are dynamic — reassess all patients periodically.

---

### JumpSTART Triage — Pediatric MCI Modification

Used for patients appearing younger than approximately 8 years old or under 5 feet tall.

- Modification: If child is apneic after airway repositioning, give 5 rescue breaths before tagging Black.
- Rationale: Children have higher vagal tone and respiratory arrest may precede cardiac arrest; rescue breaths may resuscitate.
- Respiratory rate threshold: < 15 or > 45 breaths/min = Immediate (Red).
- Mental status: AVPU scale — Alert, responds to Voice, responds to Pain, Unresponsive. "P" or "U" = Immediate.

### SALT Triage — Sort, Assess, Lifesaving Interventions, Treatment/Transport

SALT is the federally recommended MCI triage algorithm in the United States (CHEMPACK, National Response Framework).

#### Step 1 — Sort

- Global sorting into three groups using voice commands and observation:
  - **Group 1 (Still/Obvious life threat):** Non-ambulatory, not waving or responding to commands. Assessed first.
  - **Group 2 (Wave/Purposeful movement):** Non-ambulatory but responsive. Assessed second.
  - **Group 3 (Walk):** Ambulatory patients walk to a designated area. Assessed last.

#### Step 2 — Assess (Individual Assessment)

Each patient receives individual rapid assessment after initial sorting.

#### Step 3 — Lifesaving Interventions (LSI)

Performed immediately during assessment if needed, before moving to next patient:
- Control major hemorrhage (tourniquet or direct pressure).
- Open airway (repositioning, nasopharyngeal airway).
- Needle decompression for tension pneumothorax.
- Autoinjector antidotes (nerve agent exposure: atropine + pralidoxime).

#### Step 4 — Treatment/Transport Priority

| Category | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| Immediate (Red) | Likely to survive with immediate intervention; life-threatening but treatable |
| Delayed (Yellow) | Serious injuries; can wait without immediate risk to life |
| Minimal (Green) | Minor injuries; can self-treat or minimal care needed |
| Expectant (Gray/Black) | Unlikely to survive given available resources; comfort care only |
| Dead (Black) | No signs of life; no response after LSI |

#### SALT vs START Key Differences

- SALT permits performing LSI before completing triage of all patients.
- SALT uses a global sorting phase before individual assessment.
- SALT uses "Minimal" rather than "Minor" for Green category.
- SALT has a separate "Expectant" from "Dead" category.

## Resuscitation

### Cardiac Arrest

#### AHA 2020 ACLS Guidelines — Core Principles

- High-quality CPR is the most important intervention prior to defibrillation.
- Minimize interruptions to chest compressions — pauses should not exceed 10 seconds.
- Defibrillation should occur as rapidly as possible for shockable rhythms.
- Medications are adjuncts — do not delay defibrillation or CPR for IV access.
- Team communication and role assignment prior to arrest improves outcomes.

#### Basic Life Support (BLS) Standards

| Parameter | Adult | Child (1–puberty) | Infant (< 1 year) |
|-----------|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Compression rate | 100–120 /min | 100–120 /min | 100–120 /min |
| Compression depth | 2–2.4 inches (5–6 cm) | At least 2 inches (5 cm) | 1.5 inches (4 cm) |
| Compression-to-breath ratio (1 rescuer) | 30:2 | 30:2 | 30:2 |
| Compression-to-breath ratio (2 rescuers) | 30:2 | 15:2 | 15:2 |
| Allow full chest recoil | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Chest compression fraction (CCF) | > 60% (target > 80%) | > 60% | > 60% |

- Full chest recoil between compressions allows ventricular filling and reduces intrathoracic pressure.
- Avoid excessive ventilation — hyperventilation increases intrathoracic pressure, reduces venous return, and decreases cardiac output.
- Once advanced airway placed: deliver 1 breath every 6 seconds (10/min) without pausing compressions.

#### Cardiac Arrest Algorithm — Shockable Rhythms: VF / Pulseless VT (pVT)

1. Confirm pulselessness, call for help, begin CPR immediately.
2. Charge defibrillator while CPR continues.
3. Deliver shock — biphasic 200J (manufacturer-specific recommended dose; if unknown, use maximum available).
4. Resume CPR immediately for 2 minutes (5 cycles) without pulse check.
5. After 2 minutes: check rhythm. If shockable — shock again.
6. **Epinephrine 1mg IV/IO** q3–5 min (begin after second shock or as soon as IV access available).
7. After 2 more minutes of CPR: check rhythm. If shockable — shock again.
8. **Amiodarone 300mg IV/IO** push for persistent VF/pVT (second dose: 150mg).
9. **Lidocaine** alternative to amiodarone: 1–1.5mg/kg IV first dose, 0.5–0.75mg/kg subsequent doses.
10. Continue CPR-shock-drug cycles. Search for and treat reversible causes.

#### Cardiac Arrest Algorithm — Non-Shockable Rhythms: PEA / Asystole

1. Begin CPR immediately.
2. Establish IV/IO access.
3. **Epinephrine 1mg IV/IO** as soon as possible, then every 3–5 minutes.
4. CPR for 2 minutes, reassess rhythm.
5. If asystole or organized electrical activity without pulse — continue CPR and epinephrine.
6. Identify and treat reversible causes (H's and T's).
7. If patient has ROSC — begin post-arrest care.

#### The H's and T's — Reversible Causes of Cardiac Arrest

| Cause | Assessment | Treatment |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Hypovolemia | Flat neck veins, history of hemorrhage/dehydration | Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation; blood products |
| Hypoxia | Verify airway and ventilation; check SpO2 | Ensure proper ventilation, suction, airway repositioning |
| Hydrogen ion (Acidosis) | Blood gas; known DKA, metformin, toxic ingestion | Sodium bicarbonate 1–2 mEq/kg IV for severe acidosis |
| Hypokalemia / Hyperkalemia | BMP or point-of-care electrolytes; ECG changes | Potassium replacement for hypo; calcium + bicarb + insulin/glucose for hyperK |
| Hypothermia | Core temperature; exposure or submersion history | Active rewarming; ECPR consideration; "not dead until warm and dead" |
| Tension Pneumothorax | Absent breath sounds, tracheal deviation, JVD, decreased compliance | Needle decompression (2nd ICS MCL or 4th ICS AAL), then chest tube |
| Cardiac Tamponade | JVD, muffled heart sounds, hypotension (Beck's triad); electrical alternans | Emergent pericardiocentesis; pericardial window |
| Toxins | History of ingestion; toxidrome features | Specific antidote (naloxone, bicarb, Cyanokit, etc.); GI decontamination if applicable |
| Thrombosis (PE) | Risk factors, S1Q3T3, RV strain; known PE | Empiric thrombolytics: alteplase 50mg IV; ECPR |
| Thrombosis (MI/ACS) | STEMI on ECG; known CAD | ECPR; emergent cath lab activation; thrombolytics if cath unavailable |

#### Post-ROSC (Return of Spontaneous Circulation) Care

Goals of post-cardiac arrest care focus on preventing secondary injury, optimizing hemodynamics, and identifying reversible etiology.

**Targeted Temperature Management (TTM)**
- Prevent fever in all comatose post-arrest patients for 72 hours.
- Target temperature range: 32–36°C (89.6–96.8°F) for 24 hours in patients who remain comatose.
- Historical standard was 33°C; 2021 TTM2 trial demonstrated non-inferiority of normothermia (36°C) vs hypothermia (33°C) — avoid fever in all patients.
- Active fever prevention: acetaminophen, cooling blankets, intravascular cooling catheters.

**Oxygenation and Ventilation**
- Avoid hyperoxia: titrate FiO2 to maintain SpO2 94–98%.
- Avoid hypoxia: do not decrease FiO2 to subtherapeutic levels.
- Target normocapnia: PaCO2 35–45 mmHg. Avoid hypocapnia (vasoconstriction worsens cerebral ischemia).

**Hemodynamic Targets**
- Avoid hypotension: MAP > 65 mmHg minimum; many centers target MAP > 70–80 mmHg post-arrest.
- Vasopressors as needed: norepinephrine first-line for vasodilatory shock post-arrest.
- Fluid resuscitation: isotonic crystalloid for hypovolemia; avoid excessive free water.

**Coronary Angiography**
- Emergent coronary angiography recommended for STEMI post-arrest.
- Consider urgent angiography for non-STEMI arrest with suspected cardiac etiology (age, history, ECG pattern).
- ARREST and COACT trials suggest non-STEMI/non-obstructive ECG may not require emergent cath — individualize decision.

**Neurologic Prognostication**
- Do not prematurely prognosticate — sedation and hypothermia confound neurologic exam.
- Wait minimum 72 hours after ROSC and normothermia before prognostication.
- Multimodal prognostication: clinical exam + EEG + SSEP + CT/MRI + NSE (neuron-specific enolase).

**Extracorporeal CPR (ECPR)**
- Indication: refractory cardiac arrest (typically > 20 minutes without ROSC despite optimal ACLS).
- Best candidates: witnessed arrest, shockable rhythm (VF/pVT), presumed reversible etiology (PE, drug OD, hypothermia), age < 60–65.
- Requires ECMO capability and rapid cannulation team.
- Evidence: ARREST trial showed survival benefit for VF arrest in cath lab-capable centers.

### Rapid Sequence Intubation (RSI)

RSI is the simultaneous administration of an induction agent and a paralytic to achieve rapid, safe intubation of the trachea while minimizing the risk of aspiration and failed airway.

#### Indications for Intubation

- **Failure to oxygenate:** SpO2 < 90% despite high-flow oxygen, NIPPV failure.
- **Failure to ventilate:** Rising PaCO2, respiratory fatigue, decreasing respiratory rate, paradoxical breathing.
- **Airway protection:** Absent gag reflex, aspiration risk, hematemesis, angioedema, burns to airway.
- **Anticipated deterioration:** Combative trauma patient, clinical trajectory toward arrest, need for procedural sedation in high-risk patient.

#### Preoxygenation

Goal: extend apnea time by maximizing alveolar oxygen reserve before paralysis.

- Non-rebreather mask (NRB) at 15 LPM for 3–5 minutes — standard in spontaneously breathing patient.
- Head-of-bed elevation 20–30 degrees (reduces FRC-related desaturation).
- High-flow nasal cannula (HFNC) 40–70 LPM during laryngoscopy (apneic oxygenation) — delays desaturation by continuous oxygen insufflation during apneic period.
- In hypoxic patients: NIPPV (BiPAP) with FiO2 1.0 prior to RSI preoxygenates more effectively than NRB alone.

#### The 7 P's of RSI

| Step | Details |
|------|---------|
| 1. Preparation | Equipment check: laryngoscope (video preferred), ETT (sized), stylet, suction, BVM, ETCO2, drugs drawn, backup airway (LMA), surgical kit at bedside |
| 2. Preoxygenation | As above: NRB + HFNC, 3–5 minutes, SpO2 to 100% if possible |
| 3. Premedication | Adjunct medications given 3 minutes before induction to blunt physiologic responses |
| 4. Paralytic + Induction | Simultaneous administration; induction first, paralytic immediately after |
| 5. Positioning | Ramped/sniffing position; ear-to-sternal-notch alignment; head elevated 20–30 degrees |
| 6. Placement | Direct or video laryngoscopy; BURP maneuver if needed; tube placed under direct visualization |
| 7. Post-intubation management | Confirm placement, secure tube, initiate ventilator settings, reassess |

#### Premedication Options

| Agent | Dose | Indication |
|-------|------|-----------|
| Lidocaine | 1.5 mg/kg IV, 3 min before | Blunts sympathetic response; used in elevated ICP, reactive airways disease (evidence modest) |
| Fentanyl | 3 mcg/kg IV (slow push), 3 min before | Blunts laryngoscopy-induced hypertension; indicated in elevated ICP, severe HTN, aortic dissection |
| Atropine | 0.02 mg/kg IV (min 0.1 mg, max 0.5 mg) | Prevents succinylcholine-induced bradycardia in children < 5 years; vagolytic |

#### Induction Agents

| Agent | Dose | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-------|------|-----------|---------------|
| Ketamine | 1.5–2 mg/kg IV | Maintains BP and airway reflexes; bronchodilator; analgesic; preferred in hypotension, asthma, trauma | Increased secretions; laryngospasm (rare); emergence phenomenon; avoid in hypertensive emergency or elevated ICP (relative — evidence evolving) |
| Etomidate | 0.3 mg/kg IV | Hemodynamically neutral; excellent intubating conditions; short duration | Single dose adrenal suppression (cortisol inhibition 12–24h); avoid in sepsis if prolonged infusion; no analgesic properties |
| Propofol | 1.5–2.5 mg/kg IV | Smooth induction; antiemetic; reduces ICP; anticonvulsant | Significant hypotension (vasodilation + myocardial depression); avoid in hemodynamically unstable patients; pain on injection |
| Midazolam | 0.1–0.3 mg/kg IV | Widely available; amnestic | Slow onset; hypotension; long duration; not ideal for RSI |

#### Neuromuscular Blocking Agents (Paralytics)

**Succinylcholine (Depolarizing)**
- Dose: 1.5 mg/kg IV (intubating); 3–4 mg/kg IM if no IV access.
- Onset: 45–60 seconds.
- Duration: 8–12 minutes (pseudocholinesterase-dependent — prolonged in deficiency).
- Advantage: Fastest onset; short duration allows reassessment if intubation fails.
- Absolute contraindications:
  - Known personal or family history of malignant hyperthermia.
  - Skeletal muscle myopathy (Duchenne muscular dystrophy, myotonia).
  - Hyperkalemia (baseline K+ > 5.5 mEq/L or risk of acute hyperkalemia).
- Relative contraindications and risks of hyperkalemia:
  - Burns > 24 hours old (upregulation of extrajunctional acetylcholine receptors — hyperkalemia risk peaks at 1 week to 6 months; avoid after first 24 hours).
  - Crush injury > 24 hours.
  - Prolonged immobilization (> 72 hours in ICU patients, paraplegia, denervation).
  - Underlying myopathy.
  - Renal failure with baseline hyperkalemia.

**Rocuronium (Non-Depolarizing)**
- Dose: 1.2 mg/kg IV for RSI (high-dose intubating conditions).
- Onset: 60–75 seconds at 1.2 mg/kg dose.
- Duration: 60–90 minutes.
- Advantage: No depolarization-related hyperkalemia; safe in all contraindications to succinylcholine.
- Reversal: **Sugammadex 16 mg/kg IV** — encapsulates rocuronium, reversal within 3 minutes. Allows rapid reversal in cannot-intubate, cannot-oxygenate scenario.
- Caution: No reversal available if sugammadex not on formulary.

#### Laryngoscopy

**Cormack-Lehane Grading System**
| Grade | View | Interpretation |
|-------|------|----------------|
| I | Full vocal cords visible | Easy intubation |
| II | Partial cords (posterior) visible | Slightly difficult |
| III | Epiglottis only visible | Difficult; adjuncts needed |
| IV | No laryngeal structures visible | Very difficult / failed airway |

**Optimization Techniques**
- BURP maneuver (Backward, Upward, Rightward Pressure on thyroid cartilage): improves glottic view by 1 Cormack-Lehane grade.
- External laryngeal manipulation (ELM): operator-directed vs assistant-applied pressure.
- Ramp positioning (obese patients): elevate head/torso until ear aligns with sternal notch.
- Videolaryngoscopy (VL) advantages:
  - Improved glottic view in difficult airway (Cormack-Lehane III/IV).
  - Teaching tool — shared visual field.
  - Useful in cervical spine immobilization.
  - Hyperangulated blades (GlideScope, C-MAC D-blade): better view but tube manipulation more difficult.
  - Standard geometry blades with video (C-MAC): combines direct and indirect techniques.

#### ETT Placement Confirmation

- **Primary confirmation:** Continuous waveform capnography (ETCO2) — gold standard. Must see 3 consistent waveforms.
- Colorimetric CO2 detector: secondary; less sensitive in cardiac arrest or prolonged low-flow states.
- Bilateral breath sounds on auscultation: adjunct; can be falsely positive with esophageal intubation.
- Chest rise bilaterally.
- **Chest X-ray:** Confirms depth (tip 2–4 cm above carina); does not confirm tracheal vs esophageal placement.
- SpO2 improvement: confirmatory but delayed; unreliable in low-perfusion states.

#### Post-Intubation Management

- ETT depth: typically 21–23 cm at lip in adult female, 23–25 cm in adult male (3 x ETT size in pediatrics).
- Secure tube: commercial holder or tape; verify no movement with head repositioning.
- Initial ventilator settings: FiO2 1.0 initially (wean based on SpO2), TV 6–8 mL/kg IBW, RR 12–16, PEEP 5 cmH2O.
- Sedation and analgesia: initiate immediately — propofol or midazolam infusion + fentanyl infusion.
- Nasogastric tube: decompress stomach, confirm ETT position on CXR.

#### Failed Airway Algorithm

A failed airway is defined as failure to intubate after three attempts by an experienced provider or inability to maintain SpO2 > 90% with BVM ventilation.

1. **Call for help** — Activate additional resources immediately.
2. **Attempt BVM ventilation** — Two-person technique with oral/nasal airway adjuncts. Can you oxygenate?
3. **Supraglottic airway (SGA):**
   - LMA (Laryngeal Mask Airway): size 3–5 for adults; inserted blindly; buy time.
   - King LT airway: blind insertion; dual-lumen.
   - i-gel: no-cuff SGA; quick insertion.
4. **Reattempt laryngoscopy** with modified technique (different blade, operator, position, VL).
5. **Surgical airway (cricothyrotomy)** — MUST be performed promptly in "can't intubate, can't oxygenate" (CICO) scenario.
   - Identify cricothyroid membrane (CTM): midline, between thyroid and cricoid cartilages.
   - Bougie-assisted technique: vertical skin incision → horizontal CTM stab → bougie → tube over bougie.
   - Commercial kit (Melker): Seldinger technique.
   - Target: 6.0 ETT or dedicated cricothyrotomy tube.
6. **Surgical tracheotomy:** Operating room; ENT or surgical backup for anticipated difficult airway — "double setup" means prepping for both RSI and surgical airway simultaneously.

## Acute Coronary Syndrome (ED Management)

### Initial Assessment and Risk Stratification

- Obtain **12-lead ECG within 10 minutes** of arrival for any patient with chest pain, dyspnea, or symptoms suggestive of ACS.
- Serial ECGs: repeat at 15–30 minute intervals if initial ECG non-diagnostic in high-clinical-suspicion patients.
- IV access, continuous cardiac monitoring, pulse oximetry.
- Aspirin 325 mg PO (chewed) for all suspected ACS patients unless true aspirin allergy.

#### Troponin

- High-sensitivity cardiac troponin (hs-cTn) assays detect myocardial injury at 1 hour.
- **0/1h algorithm:** Draw at presentation and 1 hour. If both low: rule out with high sensitivity. If significant rise: rule in.
- **0/3h algorithm:** Alternative; useful when clinical picture is uncertain at 1 hour.
- Serial troponin must be interpreted alongside ECG and clinical presentation.
- Troponin elevation without obstructive CAD (MINOCA): demand ischemia, myocarditis, PE, Takotsubo cardiomyopathy — requires investigation.

### STEMI Management in the ED

#### Diagnosis

- ST elevation ≥ 2 mm in two contiguous precordial leads or ≥ 1 mm in two contiguous limb leads.
- New or presumably new LBBB with symptoms (Sgarbossa criteria: concordant ST elevation ≥ 1 mm, concordant ST depression ≥ 1 mm in V1–V3, or excessively discordant ST elevation ≥ 5 mm).

#### Immediate ED Interventions

- **Aspirin 325 mg** PO chewed (if not already given).
- **P2Y12 receptor inhibitor loading:**
  - Ticagrelor 180 mg PO (preferred over clopidogrel in most STEMI patients; do not use if CABG likely within 5 days).
  - Clopidogrel 600 mg PO (use if ticagrelor unavailable or patient on anticoagulation).
  - Prasugrel 60 mg PO (avoid if > 75 years, weight < 60 kg, prior TIA/stroke).
- **Anticoagulation:**
  - UFH (Unfractionated Heparin): 60 units/kg IV bolus (max 4,000 units), then 12 units/kg/hr infusion (max 1,000 units/hr). Goal: aPTT 50–70 seconds for medical management; anti-Xa based dosing for PCI.
  - Bivalirudin: 0.75 mg/kg bolus then 1.75 mg/kg/hr — alternative to UFH, lower bleeding risk.
  - Enoxaparin: 30 mg IV bolus then 1 mg/kg SQ q12h — option in STEMI for medical management.
- **Oxygen:** Only if SpO2 < 90%. Supplemental oxygen in normoxic STEMI patients is associated with larger infarct size.
- **Nitroglycerin:** 0.4 mg SL q5min x3 for ongoing ischemic pain; hold for SBP < 90, RV infarction (inferior STEMI), recent PDE5 inhibitor use.
- **Beta-blocker:** Metoprolol IV 5 mg q5min x3 if heart rate > 60, BP > 120, no evidence of heart failure or bronchospasm. Oral beta-blocker initiated within 24 hours in eligible patients.
- **Morphine/analgesia:** Fentanyl preferred over morphine (morphine delays P2Y12 absorption).

#### Reperfusion Strategy

- **Primary PCI (PPCI):** Gold standard when available. Door-to-balloon time target < 90 minutes (< 60 minutes if transfer-in for PPCI: door-to-balloon < 120 minutes from first medical contact).
- **Thrombolysis:** Indicated when PPCI is unavailable within 120 minutes of first medical contact.

#### Thrombolytic Therapy — Eligibility

**Absolute Contraindications to Thrombolysis:**
- Prior intracranial hemorrhage at any time.
- Ischemic stroke within 3 months.
- Significant closed-head or facial trauma within 3 months.
- Intracranial neoplasm, AVM, or aneurysm.
- Suspected aortic dissection.
- Active bleeding (not menses).
- Significant hypertension unresponsive to treatment (SBP > 180 or DBP > 110).

**Relative Contraindications:**
- History of chronic, severe, poorly controlled HTN.
- Prior ischemic stroke > 3 months.
- Traumatic or prolonged (> 10 min) CPR.
- Major surgery < 3 weeks.
- Recent internal bleeding (2–4 weeks).
- Non-compressible vascular puncture.
- Pregnancy.
- Active peptic ulcer.
- Oral anticoagulant use.

#### Thrombolytic Agents and Dosing

**Tenecteplase (TNK-tPA) — Weight-Based**
| Weight | Dose |
|--------|------|
| < 60 kg | 30 mg IV bolus |
| 60–69 kg | 35 mg IV bolus |
| 70–79 kg | 40 mg IV bolus |
| 80–89 kg | 45 mg IV bolus |
| ≥ 90 kg | 50 mg IV bolus |
- Single bolus over 5 seconds. Easiest to administer. Most widely used outside PCI-capable centers.

**Alteplase (tPA) — Accelerated Infusion for STEMI**
- 15 mg IV bolus over 1–2 minutes.
- Then 0.75 mg/kg over 30 minutes (max 50 mg).
- Then 0.5 mg/kg over 60 minutes (max 35 mg).
- Maximum total dose: 100 mg.

#### Post-Thrombolysis Assessment

- Reperfusion signs: ST resolution > 50% in index lead, relief of chest pain, reperfusion arrhythmia (accelerated idioventricular rhythm — benign, does not require treatment).
- Transfer for rescue PCI if no reperfusion signs at 60–90 minutes.
- Pharmacoinvasive strategy: routine angiography 3–24 hours post-thrombolysis even with apparent reperfusion.

#### Posterior STEMI

- Often missed — standard 12-lead does not directly image posterior wall.
- Clues on standard ECG: Tall broad R wave in V1–V2, horizontal ST depression V1–V3, upright T waves in V1–V2 (mirror image of posterior STEMI).
- **Posterior leads (V7–V9):** Place electrodes on left posterior chest; ST elevation ≥ 0.5 mm = posterior STEMI equivalent.
- Often associated with inferior STEMI (RCA territory) — check right-sided leads (V3R, V4R) for RV infarction.
- RV infarction: ST elevation in V4R; avoid nitrates and excessive diuresis (preload dependent).

## Hypertensive Emergencies

### Definitions

- **Hypertensive Emergency:** Blood pressure > 180/120 mmHg with evidence of end-organ damage.
- **Hypertensive Urgency:** Blood pressure > 180/120 mmHg without end-organ damage.

### End-Organ Damage Patterns

| System | Manifestation |
|--------|--------------|
| CNS | Hypertensive encephalopathy (headache, confusion, seizure, visual changes), ischemic stroke, hemorrhagic stroke, PRES |
| Cardiovascular | Acute MI, acute decompensated heart failure (ACLE), aortic dissection |
| Renal | Acute kidney injury, microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (TMA) |
| Pulmonary | Flash pulmonary edema (ACLE) |
| Ophthalmologic | Papilledema, retinal hemorrhages, visual disturbances |
| Obstetric | Eclampsia, HELLP syndrome |

### Blood Pressure Reduction Targets

| Condition | Target | Timeline |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Most hypertensive emergencies | Reduce MAP by 10–20% in first 1 hour, then 25% over next 2–6 hours | Hours |
| Aortic dissection | SBP < 120, HR < 60 | Within 20 minutes |
| Ischemic stroke — no tPA eligible | Treat only if BP > 220/120; reduce no more than 15% in first 24h | Permissive HTN first 24h |
| Ischemic stroke — tPA candidate | Reduce to < 185/110 before tPA; maintain < 180/105 after | Immediately before tPA |
| ACLE pulmonary edema | Rapid BP reduction improves cardiac output | 30–60 minutes |
| Hypertensive encephalopathy | Reduce MAP 20–25% over first hour | 1 hour |
| Eclampsia | SBP < 160, DBP < 105 | Prompt; delivery is definitive |

### IV Antihypertensive Agents

| Agent | Mechanism | Dose | Advantages | Disadvantages/Cautions |
|-------|----------|------|-----------|------------------------|
| Nicardipine | CCB (dihydropyridine) | 5 mg/hr; titrate by 2.5 mg/hr q5–15 min; max 15 mg/hr | Smooth, titratable; safe in most emergencies; good cerebral penetration | Reflex tachycardia; caution in advanced heart block |
| Labetalol | Alpha + beta blocker | 20 mg IV bolus, then 40–80 mg q10 min; or infusion 1–2 mg/min | Balanced HR and BP reduction; safe in stroke, dissection | Avoid in asthma, acute decompensated HF with low output, bradycardia |
| Clevidipine | Dihydropyridine CCB | Start 1–2 mg/hr, double q90 sec; max 32 mg/hr | Ultra-short acting (1–2 min half-life); metabolized by esterases | Hyperlipidemia (lipid emulsion); limited to ICU settings |

| Hydralazine | Direct vasodilator | 10–20 mg IV q20–30 min (bolus) | Widely available; effective in eclampsia | Unpredictable, prolonged effect; reflex tachycardia; avoid in dissection |

### Specific Hypertensive Emergency Scenarios

#### Aortic Dissection
- **Target:** SBP < 120 mmHg AND HR < 60 within 20 minutes.
- **First line:** Esmolol (rate and BP) + nicardipine/nitroprusside if needed.
- **Principle:** Reduce force of ventricular ejection (dP/dt) — beta-blocker BEFORE vasodilator.
- NEVER give vasodilator alone (reflex tachycardia increases aortic wall stress).

#### Pheochromocytoma Crisis
- Catecholamine excess from adrenal tumor; can be precipitated by anesthesia, biopsy, tumor manipulation.
- **First line: Phentolamine (alpha-blocker)** — 5–15 mg IV bolus, then infusion.
- Beta-blocker ONLY after alpha-blockade is established — beta-blockade alone causes paradoxical hypertension (unopposed alpha stimulation).
- Phenoxybenzamine (oral alpha-blocker) for pre-operative preparation.

#### Eclampsia and Severe Preeclampsia
- **Seizure prophylaxis and treatment: Magnesium sulfate** — 4–6 g IV loading dose over 20 minutes, then 2 g/hr maintenance infusion.
- Monitor for magnesium toxicity: loss of patellar reflexes (> 7 mEq/L), respiratory depression (> 10 mEq/L), cardiac arrest (> 15 mEq/L). Antidote: calcium gluconate 1 g IV.
- Antihypertensives for BP ≥ 160/110 in eclampsia:
  - Labetalol 20 mg IV then 40 mg q10 min.
  - Hydralazine 5–10 mg IV q20 min.
  - Oral nifedipine 10 mg (rapidly absorbed).
- **Definitive treatment:** Delivery. Neurologically indicated at any gestational age; obstetric team involved immediately.

## Stroke (Emergency Management)

### Time-Critical Activation

- **Stroke activation criteria:** Sudden onset neurologic deficit — facial droop, arm weakness, speech disturbance, gait disturbance, sudden severe headache, sudden vision change.
- FAST: Face, Arm, Speech, Time (call 911).
- BE-FAST: Balance, Eyes, Face, Arm, Speech, Time — improves detection of posterior circulation strokes.
- **ED door-to-CT target:** < 25 minutes.
- **Door-to-needle (tPA) target:** < 60 minutes.
- **Door-to-groin (thrombectomy) target:** < 90 minutes.

### Initial ED Workup

- **Non-contrast CT head:** Must be done first — exclude hemorrhage, large established infarct.
- CT angiography (CTA) head and neck: Detect large vessel occlusion (LVO); assess clot burden; evaluate for basilar occlusion.
- CT perfusion (CTP): Identify penumbra (salvageable) vs core (infarcted) tissue; extends thrombectomy window.
- Labs: CBC, BMP, coagulation (PT/INR, aPTT), glucose (point-of-care), HbA1c, lipid panel.
- ECG: Detect AF as cardioembolic source.
- Finger stick glucose at bedside — hypoglycemia mimics stroke.

### NIHSS (NIH Stroke Scale)

Scored 0–42. Assesses 11 domains: level of consciousness, gaze, visual fields, facial palsy, motor arm (bilateral), motor leg (bilateral), limb ataxia, sensory, language, dysarthria, extinction/inattention.
- 0–4: Minor stroke.
- 5–15: Moderate stroke.
- 16–20: Moderate-severe stroke.
- 21–42: Severe stroke.

### IV Thrombolysis — Alteplase

#### Indications
- Ischemic stroke diagnosis on clinical + CT (no hemorrhage).
- Age ≥ 18 years.
- Measurable neurologic deficit (NIHSS ≥ 1–2, not rapidly improving).
- Onset to treatment within 3 hours (expanded to 4.5 hours for eligible patients).

#### Absolute Contraindications
- Intracranial hemorrhage on CT.
- Subarachnoid hemorrhage.
- BP > 185/110 (must be treated and stable before administration).
- Platelets < 100,000.
- Anticoagulant with INR > 1.7 or elevated aPTT (heparin within 48h).
- Glucose < 50 mg/dL or > 400 mg/dL (treat and reassess).
- Prior ICH at any time.
- Ischemic stroke within 3 months.
- Head trauma within 3 months.
- Intracranial/spinal surgery within 3 months.
- GI or GU hemorrhage within 21 days.
- Arterial puncture at non-compressible site within 7 days.
- Active internal bleeding.
- Infective endocarditis.
- Aortic arch dissection.
- Intracranial neoplasm, AVM, or aneurysm.

#### 3 to 4.5 Hour Extended Window — Additional Exclusions
- Age > 80 years.
- NIHSS > 25.
- Oral anticoagulant use (regardless of INR).
- History of both prior stroke AND diabetes.
- Very large infarct core (> 1/3 MCA territory on imaging).

#### Alteplase Dosing
- **Total dose: 0.9 mg/kg IV (maximum 90 mg).**
- 10% of total dose as IV bolus over 1 minute.
- Remaining 90% as IV infusion over 60 minutes.
- Example: 80 kg patient → 72 mg total; 7.2 mg bolus over 1 min, 64.8 mg over 60 min.

#### Tenecteplase Alternative
- **Dose: 0.25 mg/kg IV (maximum 25 mg) as single bolus.**
- Non-inferior to alteplase in multiple trials (AcT, NOR-TEST).
- Advantages: Single push, easier to administer, no mixing required, fewer systemic fibrinogen effects.
- Currently approved for STEMI; off-label for stroke in some centers; replacing alteplase at many institutions.

### Mechanical Thrombectomy (Endovascular Therapy)

#### Eligibility
- Large vessel occlusion (LVO): ICA, M1 or M2 segment MCA, basilar artery, PCA.
- Age ≥ 18 (no upper age limit based on trials).
- Pre-stroke mRS 0–1 (functionally independent).
- NIHSS ≥ 6.

#### Time Windows
| Criteria | Window |
|----------|--------|
| Standard (DAWN, DEFUSE-3 excluded) | 0–6 hours from onset |
| DAWN trial: wake-up/unknown onset, clinical-imaging mismatch, DWI ≤ 70 mL | 6–24 hours |
| DEFUSE-3: ischemic core < 70 mL, penumbra:core ratio ≥ 1.8 | 6–16 hours |
| Basilar artery occlusion (ATTENTION, BAOCHE) | 0–24 hours, some extend to 72h |

### Post-tPA Monitoring Protocol

- Neurologic assessment every 15 minutes for 2 hours.
- Then every 30 minutes for 6 hours.
- Then every 60 minutes for 16 hours.
- Blood pressure checks with neuro checks; maintain BP < 180/105 for 24 hours post-tPA.
- **Hold all antiplatelets and anticoagulants for 24 hours** post-tPA.
- No arterial or venous punctures at non-compressible sites for 24 hours.

### Hemorrhagic Transformation After tPA

- Symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage (sICH) occurs in approximately 6% of tPA-treated patients.
- **Management:**
  1. Stop tPA infusion immediately.
  2. Urgent non-contrast CT head.
  3. Cryoprecipitate 10 units IV (replenish fibrinogen — target fibrinogen > 150 mg/dL).
  4. Fresh frozen plasma (FFP) if cryoprecipitate not available or coagulopathy suspected.
  5. Platelets if thrombocytopenic.
  6. Tranexamic acid (TXA) 1 g IV in some protocols.
  7. Urgent neurosurgery consultation.

### Hemorrhagic Stroke Management

#### Intracerebral Hemorrhage (ICH)
- Urgent neurosurgery and neurology consultation.
- **Anticoagulation reversal:**
  - Warfarin: 4-factor PCC (Kcentra) 25–50 units/kg + Vitamin K 10 mg IV.
  - Direct thrombin inhibitors (dabigatran): Idarucizumab (Praxbind) 5 g IV.
  - Factor Xa inhibitors (rivaroxaban, apixaban): Andexanet alfa (Andexxa) or 4-factor PCC as alternative.
  - Heparin: Protamine sulfate 1 mg per 100 units heparin given in last 2 hours.
- **BP management (ICH):** Target SBP < 140 mmHg (INTERACT2) — IV nicardipine or labetalol.
- Surgical evacuation: Consider for cerebellar hemorrhage > 3 cm, hydrocephalus, or accessible lobar hemorrhage with deterioration.

#### Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH)
- Sudden onset "thunderclap" (worst headache of life), meningismus, loss of consciousness.
- Non-contrast CT: 97–98% sensitive within 6 hours; sensitivity decreases over time.
- Lumbar puncture: If CT negative and high clinical suspicion; xanthochromia confirms SAH.
- **BP management (SAH):** SBP 140–160 mmHg — balance rebleeding risk vs cerebral perfusion.
- **Vasospasm prevention:** Nimodipine 60 mg PO/NG q4h for 21 days (calcium channel blocker — reduces vasospasm-related deficits).
- Urgent neurosurgery: Aneurysm securing (coiling vs clipping), external ventricular drain (EVD) for hydrocephalus.

## Respiratory Emergencies

### Acute Severe Asthma / Status Asthmaticus

Status asthmaticus is severe bronchospasm unresponsive to initial bronchodilator therapy, with persistent airflow obstruction and hypoxia or hypercapnia.

#### Severity Assessment

| Parameter | Mild | Moderate | Severe | Near-Fatal |
|-----------|------|----------|--------|-----------|
| PEF | > 70% predicted | 40–69% | < 40% | < 25% |
| SpO2 | > 95% | 91–95% | < 91% | < 90% on O2 |
| RR | < 20 | 20–30 | > 30 | Variable (slow = fatigue) |
| HR | < 100 | 100–120 | > 120 | Variable |
| Speech | Full sentences | Partial | Single words | Silent chest |
| Accessory muscles | None | Mild | Significant | Paradoxical breathing |

#### Treatment Ladder for Severe Asthma

1. **Short-Acting Beta-2 Agonist (SABA):** Albuterol 2.5–5 mg via nebulizer every 20 minutes x3, then continuous nebulization 10–15 mg/hr. MDI with spacer equally effective in moderate cases.

2. **Short-Acting Muscarinic Antagonist (SAMA):** Ipratropium bromide 0.5 mg via nebulizer every 20 minutes x3 (synergistic with albuterol — combination superior to albuterol alone in ED).

3. **Systemic Corticosteroids:** Methylprednisolone 80–125 mg IV or prednisone 60–80 mg PO. Oral and IV equally bioavailable if patient can swallow. Onset within 4–6 hours. Continue 5–7 day course on discharge.

4. **Magnesium Sulfate:** 2 g IV over 20 minutes. Inhibits smooth muscle calcium entry → bronchodilation. Most benefit in severe/near-fatal asthma (FEV1 < 25% predicted). Reduces hospital admission.

5. **Heliox (70:30 Helium:Oxygen Mixture):** Lower density than air; reduces turbulent to laminar flow; improves drug delivery to distal airways. Useful as temporizing measure while other therapies take effect. Dilutes FiO2 — use only if SpO2 maintained.

6. **Intravenous Ketamine:** 0.3–0.5 mg/kg IV bolus for sedation/analgesia during severe exacerbation; also potent bronchodilator (sympathomimetic + direct smooth muscle relaxation). Use as pre-intubation sedation.

7. **NIPPV (BiPAP):** Consider in impending respiratory failure (RR > 35, declining mental status, fatigue) if patient is cooperative. IPAP 10–12, EPAP 5; titrate. Avoid in obtunded or vomiting patients.

8. **Intubation — Last Resort:**
   - High risk in asthma: dynamic hyperinflation (breath stacking), auto-PEEP, barotrauma, hypotension.
   - Ketamine preferred induction agent (bronchodilator).
   - Initial ventilator settings: low RR (10–12), low TV (6–7 mL/kg), high flow rate (80–100 L/min), low PEEP (3–5), long I:E ratio (1:4 or 1:5).
   - **Permissive hypercapnia:** Tolerate PaCO2 up to 80 mmHg to avoid barotrauma — pH > 7.20.
   - Auto-PEEP assessment: Perform inspiratory hold maneuver; plateau pressure > 30 cmH2O suggests dangerous hyperinflation.
   - Consider manual decompression: disconnect circuit to allow complete exhalation if acute hemodynamic deterioration.

### Anaphylaxis

#### Diagnostic Criteria

Anaphylaxis is highly likely when any ONE of the following criteria is fulfilled:

1. Acute onset of illness with skin or mucosal tissue involvement (hives, pruritus, flushing, swollen lips/tongue/uvula) AND respiratory compromise OR reduced BP.

2. Two or more of the following occurring after exposure to likely allergen: skin/mucosal symptoms, respiratory symptoms, reduced BP, persistent GI symptoms.

3. Reduced BP after exposure to known allergen.

#### Treatment Protocol — Priority Order

**Step 1: Epinephrine IM — First Line. No Absolute Contraindications.**
- Adult: 0.3–0.5 mg (0.3–0.5 mL of 1:1,000 solution) IM into anterolateral thigh (vastus lateralis).
- Pediatric: 0.01 mg/kg IM (max 0.5 mg).
- Repeat every 5–15 minutes if no improvement.
- IM thigh delivers faster, higher peak levels than IM deltoid.

**Step 2: Position**
- Supine with legs elevated — maintains venous return.
- Critical: Do NOT allow patient to sit up or stand if hypotensive — sudden death from vasovagal collapse in upright position (empty ventricle syndrome).
- Exception: Respiratory compromise — head elevation preferred.

**Step 3: Airway and Oxygenation**
- High-flow oxygen (NRB 15 LPM).
- Early intubation if angioedema is progressing (delay = cannot intubate).
- Cricothyrotomy as surgical backup for failed airway in severe angioedema.

**Step 4: Vascular Access and Fluids**
- Two large-bore IVs.
- Normal saline 1–2 L rapid IV infusion for hypotension.
- Third-spacing and vasodilation can result in up to 35% plasma volume loss in severe anaphylaxis.

**Step 5: Adjunct Medications (Secondary — Do Not Delay Epinephrine)**
- H1 antihistamine: Diphenhydramine 50 mg IV — treats urticaria and pruritus; does NOT treat bronchospasm or hypotension; does NOT prevent anaphylaxis or biphasic reaction.
- H2 blocker: Famotidine 20–40 mg IV or ranitidine 50 mg IV — adjunct to H1 antihistamine.
- Corticosteroids: Methylprednisolone 125 mg IV or dexamethasone 8 mg IV — theoretical reduction of biphasic reaction; evidence for this benefit is weak. Do not delay other treatments for steroids.

**Refractory Anaphylaxis:**
- Epinephrine IV infusion: 0.1–0.5 mcg/kg/min (titrate); requires cardiac monitoring, arterial line preferred.
- Glucagon 1–2 mg IV (or IM): Bypasses beta-adrenergic receptor; essential in patients on beta-blockers who fail to respond to epinephrine. Follow with infusion 5–15 mcg/min.
- Vasopressin 0.03–0.04 units/min: For refractory vasodilatory shock.
- Methylene blue 1–2 mg/kg IV: Inhibits NO synthase; used in refractory distributive shock.

#### Biphasic Anaphylaxis

- Recurrence of anaphylaxis 1–72 hours after apparent recovery (typically 4–8 hours).
- Incidence: approximately 3–20% depending on definition.
- Risk factors: Severe initial reaction, unknown trigger, delayed epinephrine administration, cutaneous manifestations only in initial phase.
- **Observation period:**
  - Mild-moderate initial reaction: 4–6 hours minimum.
  - Severe initial reaction: 8–24 hours observation.

#### Discharge Planning
- Prescribe two epinephrine autoinjectors (EpiPen 0.3 mg adult; EpiPen Jr 0.15 mg pediatric).
- Written anaphylaxis action plan.
- Allergen avoidance counseling.
- Allergy/immunology referral for desensitization planning.
- Medical alert bracelet recommendation.

## Toxicology

### General Toxicology Principles

#### GI Decontamination

**Activated Charcoal (AC)**
- Dose: 1 g/kg PO (maximum 50 g in adults; 25 g in children).
- Indications: Recent ingestion (within 1–2 hours); patient has intact and protected airway; non-caustic, non-hydrocarbon ingestion.
- Contraindications: Altered mental status without definitive airway, bowel obstruction or perforation, caustic ingestion, hydrocarbon ingestion, lithium, iron, metals (charcoal does not bind).
- Multiple-dose AC (MDAC): Every 4–6 hours for drugs with enterohepatic recirculation (theophylline, phenobarbital, carbamazepine, dapsone, quinine).

**Whole Bowel Irrigation (WBI)**
- Polyethylene glycol electrolyte solution (PEG-ELS): Adults 2 L/hr PO or NG; children 0.5–1 L/hr.
- Continue until rectal effluent is clear.
- Indications:
  - Sustained-release or enteric-coated medications.
  - Body packing (drug mules with packages in GI tract).
  - Metals not adsorbed by charcoal (iron, lead, lithium, barium).
  - Late presentation when charcoal is no longer useful.
- Contraindications: Bowel obstruction, perforation, significant vomiting, unprotected airway.

**Gastric Lavage**
- Rarely indicated in modern toxicology; consider only within 60 minutes of massive, life-threatening ingestion with intact airway.
- Not recommended for most overdoses — does not improve outcomes beyond charcoal alone.

#### Urinary Alkalinization
- Sodium bicarbonate 1–2 mEq/kg IV to achieve urine pH > 7.5.
- Enhances renal elimination of: salicylates, phenobarbital, methotrexate, chlorpropamide, herbicides (2,4-D).
- Mechanism: Ionizes weak acids in renal tubule → trapping → increased excretion.

#### Hemodialysis in Toxicology
Indicated for: severe metabolic acidosis, fluid/electrolyte unresponsive to supportive care, renal failure impairing drug elimination, or high drug levels with clinical deterioration.
- Readily dialyzed: Salicylates, lithium, methanol, ethylene glycol, theophylline, valproic acid, metformin.
- Poorly dialyzed: Highly protein-bound drugs (TCAs, benzodiazepines), large volume of distribution drugs.

### Toxidromes

A toxidrome is a constellation of signs and symptoms produced by a specific class of toxin.

| Toxidrome | HR | BP | Temp | Pupils | Skin | MS | Examples |
|-----------|----|----|------|--------|------|----|----------|
| Anticholinergic | Tachycardia | Elevated | Elevated | Dilated | Dry, flushed | Agitated, delirium | Atropine, scopolamine, TCAs, diphenhydramine, jimsonweed |
| Cholinergic (muscarinic) | Bradycardia | Low | Normal | Miotic | Diaphoretic, wet | Variable | Organophosphates, carbamates, nerve agents, physostigmine |
| Sympathomimetic | Tachycardia | Elevated | Elevated | Dilated | Diaphoretic | Agitated, psychosis | Cocaine, methamphetamine, MDMA, pseudoephedrine, caffeine |
| Opioid | Bradycardia | Low | Low-normal | Miotic (pinpoint) | Dry, pale | CNS depression | Heroin, morphine, fentanyl, methadone, codeine |
| Sedative-Hypnotic | Variable | Low | Low-normal | Normal or small | Dry | CNS depression | Benzodiazepines, barbiturates, alcohol, GHB, carisoprodol |
| Serotonin Syndrome | Tachycardia | Elevated | Elevated | Dilated | Diaphoretic | Agitated, altered | SSRIs + MAOIs, tramadol + SSRIs, linezolid, meperidine |
| NMS | Tachycardia | Labile/elevated | Very high (> 40°C) | Normal | Diaphoretic | Altered, lead-pipe rigidity | Antipsychotics (haloperidol, olanzapine), metoclopramide |
| Cholinergic (nicotinic) | Tachycardia | Elevated | Normal | Variable | Diaphoretic | Agitated, weakness | High-dose OPs, nicotine toxicity |

**Distinguishing Serotonin Syndrome vs NMS:**

| Feature | Serotonin Syndrome | NMS |
|---------|-------------------|-----|
| Onset | Hours (acute) | Days to weeks (insidious) |
| Onset after drug | Rapid (< 24h) | Slow (days–weeks) |
| Rigidity | Hyperreflexia, clonus (lower extremity > upper) | Lead-pipe rigidity (uniform) |
| Cause | Serotonergic drug excess | Dopamine antagonist |
| Temperature | Moderate elevation | Extreme hyperthermia |
| Treatment | Cyproheptadine, BZDs, supportive; stop offending agents | Stop antipsychotic; bromocriptine, dantrolene; supportive |

**Anticholinergic Mnemonic:**
"Blind as a bat (dilated pupils), hot as a hare (hyperthermia), dry as a bone (dry skin), red as a beet (flushing), mad as a hatter (delirium), full as a flask (urinary retention), the bowel and bladder lose their tone (ileus, retention)."

**Cholinergic SLUDGE/DUMBELS Mnemonic:**
- SLUDGE: Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI distress, Emesis.
- DUMBELS: Diarrhea, Urination, Miosis, Bradycardia/Bronchospasm/Bronchorrhea, Emesis, Lacrimation, Salivation.

### Specific Antidotes and Toxin Management

#### Acetaminophen (APAP) Overdose

- Most common cause of acute liver failure in the United States.
- Toxic metabolite: NAPQI (N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone imine) — normally detoxified by glutathione; overwhelms at toxic doses.
- **Clinical phases:**
  - Phase 1 (0–24h): Nausea, vomiting, malaise (or asymptomatic).
  - Phase 2 (24–72h): Apparent improvement; elevated LFTs begin.
  - Phase 3 (72–96h): Peak hepatotoxicity — jaundice, coagulopathy, encephalopathy, AKI.
  - Phase 4 (4 days–2 weeks): Recovery or fulminant liver failure.
- **Rumack-Matthew nomogram:** Plot APAP level and time post-ingestion. Level above "treatment line" indicates N-acetylcysteine (NAC) therapy.
- **N-Acetylcysteine (NAC):**
  - IV (Acetadote): Loading dose 150 mg/kg over 60 min, then 50 mg/kg over 4h, then 100 mg/kg over 16h.
  - Oral: 140 mg/kg loading dose, then 70 mg/kg q4h x17 doses.
  - Initiate within 8 hours of acute ingestion for maximum efficacy; still beneficial beyond 8 hours.
  - Continue until LFTs improving and APAP level undetectable.
- Hepatology consult for ALT > 1000 U/L, INR > 2, or encephalopathy; liver transplant evaluation if King's College criteria met.

#### Opioid Toxidrome and Naloxone

- Classic triad: Miosis, CNS depression, respiratory depression.
- **Naloxone (Narcan):**
  - IV/IM/IN: 0.4–2 mg; titrate to respiratory rate > 12 and protective airway reflexes.
  - IN (intranasal): 4 mg per nostril; effective and needle-free.
  - Duration: 30–90 minutes (shorter than most opioids — monitor for re-sedation).
  - In opioid-dependent patients: Start 0.04–0.1 mg IV to avoid precipitating withdrawal.
  - Infusion for sustained opioids (methadone, buprenorphine, extended-release): 2/3 of effective dose per hour.
- Fentanyl/carfentanil: May require multiple high-dose naloxone doses. Doses up to 10–40 mg have been reported.

#### Organophosphate / Nerve Agent Poisoning

- Mechanism: Irreversible acetylcholinesterase inhibition → acetylcholine accumulation at muscarinic and nicotinic synapses.
- Presentation: SLUDGE + DUMBELS + nicotinic effects (fasciculations, muscle weakness, paralysis) + CNS (seizures, coma).
- Life threats: Bronchospasm, bronchorrhea, respiratory paralysis, seizures.
- **Atropine:**
  - Dose: 2–4 mg IV q5–10 minutes; titrate to **dry secretions** (not heart rate or pupil size).
  - No maximum dose in severe poisoning — continue until secretions resolve.
  - Endpoint: Dry oropharynx, resolved bronchospasm, SpO2 improving.
- **Pralidoxime (2-PAM):**
  - Dose: 1–2 g IV over 15–30 minutes, then 0.5 g/hr infusion.
  - Must be given within 24–48 hours of exposure before "aging" of organophosphate-cholinesterase bond (irreversible).
  - Reactivates acetylcholinesterase — targets nicotinic effects and CNS (atropine does not).
- Decontamination: Remove clothing, copious irrigation with water. Protect rescuers (secondary exposure).

#### Tricyclic Antidepressant (TCA) Overdose

- Mechanisms: Sodium channel blockade, alpha-1 blockade, anticholinergic, antihistamine, GABA antagonism.
- Cardiac toxicity: QRS prolongation > 100 ms = seizure risk; QRS > 160 ms = ventricular arrhythmia risk.
- **Sodium bicarbonate:**
  - Dose: 1–2 mEq/kg IV bolus for QRS > 100 ms, hypotension, dysrhythmia, or seizure.
  - Target serum pH 7.45–7.55.
  - Mechanism: Increases extracellular sodium (overcomes Na channel blockade), alkalemia (reduces drug binding to channel).
  - Infusion: 2–3 ampules NaHCO3 (88–132 mEq) in 1L D5W; titrate to pH.
- Seizures: Benzodiazepines first-line; phenytoin CONTRAINDICATED (blocks Na channels further).
- Hypotension: Sodium bicarb, norepinephrine (preferred vasopressor); avoid dopamine.
- **Physostigmine CONTRAINDICATED** in TCA overdose — precipitates seizures and asystole.
- **NEVER** use flumazenil in unknown overdose with TCA co-ingestion.

#### Digoxin Toxicity

- Mechanism: Na/K-ATPase inhibition → increased intracellular calcium → automaticity.
- Acute toxicity: GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain), hyperkalemia.
- Chronic toxicity: More common; visual disturbances (xanthopsia — yellow-green halos), confusion, GI symptoms.
- ECG: PAT with block (paroxysmal atrial tachycardia with AV block), bidirectional VT, junctional rhythms, slow AF with regularization.
- **Digoxin immune Fab (Digibind / DigiFab):**
  - Indicated for: Life-threatening arrhythmia, hyperkalemia > 5.5 mEq/L, digoxin level > 10 ng/mL, ingestion > 10 mg adult (4 mg child).
  - Dosing: Based on estimated total body load; for empiric dosing: 10 vials initially.
  - Monitor for rebound toxicity and hypokalemia after Fab administration.
- Magnesium sulfate 2 g IV for ventricular arrhythmias.
- Avoid calcium administration in digoxin toxicity (worsens intracellular calcium overload — "stone heart").

#### Beta-Blocker Overdose

- Presentation: Bradycardia, hypotension, AV block, bronchospasm, hypoglycemia (especially in diabetics).
- **Treatment:**
  1. Atropine 1 mg IV (usually insufficient but try).
  2. **Glucagon 5–10 mg IV bolus** then 1–10 mg/hr infusion — bypasses beta-receptor; activates adenylyl cyclase directly.
  3. **High-dose insulin therapy (HDIT):** Insulin 1 unit/kg IV bolus then 0.5–1 unit/kg/hr infusion with glucose 0.5–1 g/kg/hr (dextrose infusion); monitor glucose q30 min; potassium supplementation.
  4. Calcium gluconate 3 g IV or calcium chloride 1 g IV.
  5. Vasopressors: Norepinephrine, epinephrine for hypotension.
  6. **Lipid emulsion therapy (IVLE — Intralipid 20%):** 1.5 mL/kg IV bolus over 1 minute, then 0.25 mL/kg/min x30–60 min. For lipophilic beta-blockers (propranolol, metoprolol) with refractory cardiovascular toxicity.
  7. Transvenous pacing for refractory high-degree AV block.
  8. VA-ECMO for refractory cardiogenic shock.

#### Calcium Channel Blocker Overdose

- Presentation: Bradycardia, hypotension, AV block (similar to beta-blocker), hyperglycemia (non-dihydropyridines), reflex tachycardia with dihydropyridines.
- **Treatment:**
  1. Calcium gluconate 3 g IV (30 mL of 10% calcium gluconate) or calcium chloride 1 g IV; repeat as needed.
  2. Atropine 1 mg IV (limited benefit in severe poisoning).
  3. **High-dose insulin therapy (HDIT):** Same protocol as beta-blocker.
  4. Glucagon 5–10 mg IV bolus.
  5. Vasopressors: Norepinephrine, vasopressin.
  6. **Lipid emulsion therapy:** For highly lipophilic CCBs (verapamil, diltiazem).
  7. VA-ECMO for refractory hemodynamic failure.

#### Methanol and Ethylene Glycol

- Methanol metabolism: Formate → metabolic acidosis + optic nerve toxicity (blindness).
- Ethylene glycol metabolism: Oxalate → AKI (calcium oxalate crystalluria).
- Both: Elevated osmolar gap (early), elevated anion gap metabolic acidosis (late), elevated lactate (methanol).
- **Fomepizole (4-methylpyrazole):**
  - Dose: 15 mg/kg IV loading dose over 30 minutes, then 10 mg/kg q12h x4 doses, then 15 mg/kg q12h thereafter.
  - Mechanism: Inhibits alcohol dehydrogenase — prevents formation of toxic metabolites.
  - Preferred over ethanol (easier dosing, no sedation, no hypoglycemia).
- Ethanol: IV or oral 10% ethanol; competitive substrate for alcohol dehydrogenase; more difficult to titrate.
- **Hemodialysis indications:** Severe acidosis (pH < 7.25), methanol level > 50 mg/dL or ethylene glycol level > 50 mg/dL, AKI, visual symptoms (methanol), refractory acidosis.
- Folic acid 50 mg IV q6h (methanol: enhances formate metabolism).
- Thiamine 100 mg IV + pyridoxine 50 mg IV (ethylene glycol: redirects oxalate pathway).

#### Cyanide Poisoning

- Sources: Smoke inhalation (plastics, synthetic fabrics), industrial exposure, nitroprusside toxicity.
- Mechanism: Inhibits cytochrome c oxidase → histotoxic hypoxia → lactic acidosis.
- Suspect in: Smoke inhalation with elevated lactate (> 10 mmol/L highly specific), persistent acidosis despite resuscitation.
- **Hydroxocobalamin (Cyanokit):** 5 g IV over 15 minutes; binds cyanide → cyanocobalamin. First line. No hypotension risk.
- Sodium thiosulfate 12.5 g IV: Provides sulfur donor for rhodanese enzyme; use with hydroxocobalamin or sodium nitrite.
- Sodium nitrite 300 mg IV: Induces methemoglobin (competes with cytochrome for cyanide binding). Do NOT use in co-exposure with CO poisoning (reduces oxygen carrying capacity).

#### Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

- Sources: Combustion (house fires, generators, exhaust, heaters).
- COHb levels poorly correlate with symptoms (smokers have baseline 5–10%).
- SpO2 falsely normal on standard pulse oximetry.
- **Symptoms by COHb level:**

| COHb Level | Symptoms |
|------------|---------|
| < 10% | Usually none; mild headache in sensitive patients |
| 10–20% | Headache, exertional dyspnea |
| 20–40% | Confusion, visual disturbance, fatigue, nausea |
| 40–60% | Altered consciousness, seizure, tachycardia |
| > 60% | Coma, cardiovascular collapse, death |

- **Treatment:**
  - 100% oxygen via NRB mask — reduces COHb half-life from 5 hours (room air) to 60–90 minutes.
  - **Hyperbaric oxygen (HBO) therapy:** Reduces half-life to 20–30 minutes; reduces risk of delayed neurologic sequelae.
  - HBO indications: COHb > 25%, loss of consciousness, neurologic symptoms or cognitive deficits, chest pain/ECG changes, age > 36, pregnant patient (any COHb level), severely ill patient, metabolic acidosis.

#### Serotonin Syndrome — Treatment

- Remove all serotonergic agents.
- **Cyproheptadine (5-HT2A antagonist):** 12 mg PO/NG then 2 mg q2h (max 32 mg/day) — only oral formulation available.
- Benzodiazepines for agitation, hyperthermia management (muscle relaxation), hypertension.
- Severe hyperthermia (> 41°C): Immediate external cooling, paralysis (non-depolarizing NMBA) + intubation.
- Avoid antipyretics (temperature is not centrally mediated).
- Avoid physical restraints (increases hyperthermia, rhabdomyolysis).
- Nitroprusside/nicardipine for hypertension if needed.

## Orthopedic Emergencies

### Fracture Principles

#### Closed Reduction Indications
- Unacceptable fracture alignment after clinical and radiographic assessment.
- Generally: > 10 degrees angulation in long bones; displaced intra-articular fractures; significant shortening.
- Vascular compromise — emergent reduction before definitive fixation.
- Skin tenting from fracture fragment.

#### Closed Reduction Technique
1. Adequate analgesia and sedation (procedural sedation: ketamine, propofol, or hematoma block with 1% lidocaine).
2. Traction: Restore length, correct displacement.
3. Disimpaction followed by alignment.
4. Post-reduction neurovascular check: pulse, capillary refill, sensation, motor function.
5. Post-reduction imaging.
6. Immobilization in position of function.

#### Splinting vs Casting
- Acute phase: Always splint rather than circumferential cast — allows for swelling.
- Circumferential casts within 48–72 hours risk compartment syndrome.
- Splint coverage: Covers 2/3 of limb circumference; secures with cotton webril and elastic bandage.
- Cast: Definitive immobilization once swelling subsides (5–7 days).

#### Common Upper Extremity Fractures

**Distal Radius Fracture (Colles Fracture)**
- Mechanism: Fall on outstretched hand (FOOSH).
- Radiographic features: Dorsal angulation, radial shortening, dorsal displacement of distal fragment.
- Reduction: Traction-countertraction, recreate deformity then dorsally flex to reduce.
- Immobilization: Sugar-tong splint (wrist neutral, slight ulnar deviation).
- Operative indications: > 20 degrees dorsal tilt, > 2 mm intra-articular step-off, > 3 mm radial shortening, unstable or open fracture.

**Boxer's Fracture (5th Metacarpal Neck)**
- Mechanism: Punch with closed fist.
- Acceptable angulation: Up to 40 degrees in 5th metacarpal, up to 20 degrees in 4th.
- Reduction if needed: Jahss maneuver (flex MCP and PIP 90 degrees, apply dorsal force to proximal phalanx).
- Immobilization: Ulnar gutter splint (4th and 5th fingers, wrist 30 degrees extension, MCP 70 degrees flexion, IPs 0 degrees).

**Scaphoid Fracture**
- Mechanism: FOOSH with wrist dorsiflexion.
- Anatomic snuffbox tenderness: Highly sensitive.
- X-ray negative in up to 20% acutely — do NOT rule out based on X-ray alone.
- **MRI is gold standard** (most sensitive in acute phase).
- CT: Good for identifying fracture line and assessing displacement.
- Treatment: Thumb spica splint/cast for non-displaced. Surgical fixation for displaced (≥ 1 mm) or proximal pole fractures (avascular necrosis risk).
- Avascular necrosis risk: Proximal third (retrograde blood supply).

**Lisfranc Fracture-Dislocation**
- Tarsometatarsal joint complex injury — commonly missed.
- Mechanism: Axial load on plantarflexed foot (fall from height, motor vehicle).
- X-ray findings: Diastasis between 1st and 2nd metatarsal bases > 2 mm, avulsion fracture at base of 2nd MT ("fleck sign").
- Weight-bearing X-rays: Increase sensitivity.
- CT if X-ray negative but high suspicion.
- Orthopedic emergency: Even minimally displaced Lisfranc injuries require urgent orthopedic consultation and non-weight-bearing.

**Jones Fracture**
- Fracture at base of 5th metatarsal, specifically at the metaphyseal-diaphyseal junction (Zone II).
- Distinguish from Dancer's fracture (Zone I — avulsion of 5th MT base by peroneus brevis — benign).
- Jones fracture: Poor blood supply; high nonunion and re-fracture rate.
- Treatment: Non-weight-bearing cast; operative fixation increasingly recommended for athletes.

#### Hip Dislocation

**Posterior Hip Dislocation (Most Common — 90%)**
- Mechanism: Dashboard injury; high-energy axial load with hip flexed, adducted.
- Clinical appearance: Hip flexed, adducted, internally rotated; shortened limb.
- Associated injuries: Sciatic nerve injury (10–20%); posterior acetabular wall fracture; femoral head fracture.
- **Emergent reduction — target within 6 hours** (risk of avascular necrosis increases dramatically after 6 hours; acceptable rate < 12h).
- Avascular necrosis rates: < 6 hours: 3–5%; 6–12 hours: 10–15%; > 12 hours: up to 40–50%.
- Reduction techniques (procedural sedation required):
  - **Stimson technique (gravity-assisted):** Patient prone, hip off table edge, gentle downward traction on knee.
  - **Bigelow maneuver:** Traction-countertraction with circumduction.
  - **Allis maneuver:** Supine, traction inline with deformity then flex hip 90 degrees, gentle internal-external rotation, upward traction.
- Post-reduction: CT pelvis to detect occult acetabular or femoral head fractures; neurovascular reassessment.

**Anterior Hip Dislocation (Less Common — 10%)**
- Clinical appearance: Hip extended, abducted, externally rotated.
- Reduction: Traction inline with deformity + internal rotation.

### Compartment Syndrome

Compartment syndrome is a surgical emergency involving elevated pressure within a closed osteofascial compartment that compromises perfusion to contained muscles and nerves.

#### Etiology
- Fractures (most common — tibial shaft > 50% of cases).
- Tight circumferential dressings or casts.
- Crush injury.
- Burns.
- Reperfusion injury.
- Nephrotic syndrome / anasarca.
- Prolonged limb compression (drug-induced coma).
- Vascular injury with hemorrhage or ischemia-reperfusion.

#### Clinical Features (6 P's)

| Sign | Significance |
|------|-------------|
| Pain (out of proportion) | Earliest and most sensitive sign |
| Pain with passive stretch | Stretching of ischemic muscle → exquisite pain; most specific finding |
| Pressure (tense compartment) | Woody, indurated feel on palpation |
| Paresthesias | Ischemic neuropathy; suggests progressing injury |
| Paralysis | Late sign — motor function lost with prolonged ischemia |
| Pallor + Pulselessness | Very late signs; often absent until significant necrosis |

#### Diagnostic Criteria

- **Compartment pressure measurement:** Stryker device or improvised saline manometer technique.
- Absolute pressure ≥ 30 mmHg: Generally accepted indication for fasciotomy.
- **Delta P (perfusion pressure): ≤ 30 mmHg** = indication for fasciotomy.
  - Delta P = Diastolic BP − Compartment Pressure.
  - Example: DBP 70, compartment pressure 45 → Delta P = 25 mmHg → fasciotomy indicated.
- Delta P < 30 mmHg is more sensitive in hypotensive patients.

#### Treatment

1. **Remove all circumferential dressings** immediately — bivalve cast if present.
2. Position limb at level of heart (elevating reduces arterial perfusion; lowering worsens venous congestion).
3. **Emergent fasciotomy** — do not delay for imaging or observation.
4. Leg fasciotomy: Dual incision technique (anterolateral + posteromedial) releasing all 4 compartments (anterior, lateral, superficial posterior, deep posterior).
5. Forearm fasciotomy: Volar + dorsal incisions releasing all compartments.
6. Wound left open; delayed primary closure or skin grafting at 48–72 hours.
7. Monitor for rhabdomyolysis post-release: aggressive fluid hydration, urinary alkalinization.

## Genitourinary Emergencies

### Testicular Torsion

**Presentation:**
- Sudden onset of severe scrotal pain (classically peaks within minutes).
- Nausea and vomiting (visceral stretch response).
- High-riding testicle (cremasteric muscle spasm shortens spermatic cord).
- Horizontal lie of testis ("bell-clapper" deformity predisposition).
- Absent cremasteric reflex (most sensitive sign — 100% sensitive in some series).
- Scrotal swelling and erythema (later findings).

**Diagnosis:**
- **Color Doppler ultrasound:** Absent or severely diminished testicular blood flow. Sensitivity > 95%, specificity > 95%.
- Critical: Do NOT wait for ultrasound if clinical suspicion is high and emergent surgery is immediately available. Proceed to OR based on clinical diagnosis.

**Treatment — Time-Critical:**

| Time to Detorsion | Testicular Salvage Rate |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| < 6 hours | > 90–100% |
| 6–12 hours | 50–70% |
| 12–24 hours | 10–20% |
| > 24 hours | < 10% |

- **Emergency urologic consultation** and surgical detorsion/orchiopexy.
- **Manual detorsion (temporizing measure):**
  - Most torsions twist medially (inward).
  - "Open a book" maneuver: Externally rotate testicle (like opening a book from medial to lateral).
  - Successful if pain relief and improved blood flow on Doppler.
  - Not definitive — still requires surgical fixation.
- Bilateral orchiopexy performed at same operation (bell-clapper deformity is bilateral in 40%).

### Urinary Retention

**Common Causes:**

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Obstructive | BPH, prostate cancer, urethral stricture, bladder neck contracture, stone |
| Neurogenic | Spinal cord injury, multiple sclerosis, Cauda equina syndrome, diabetic neuropathy |
| Medications | Anticholinergics (diphenhydramine, oxybutynin, TCAs), alpha-1 agonists, opioids |
| Infectious | Prostatitis, urethritis |
| Post-surgical | Spinal, pelvic, perineal surgery |
| Other | Constipation, pelvic mass, phimosis |

**Management:**
- Foley catheter (urethral catheterization): First-line; drain bladder, measure residual urine.
- Post-obstructive diuresis: If bladder extremely distended (> 700–800 mL), monitor for post-obstructive diuresis — large urine output post-drainage requiring IV fluid replacement.
- Hematuria post-decompression: Common; usually resolves; suspect bladder tumor or stone.
- Alpha-blocker initiation: Tamsulosin 0.4 mg daily for BPH — facilitates voiding trial prior to definitive urology follow-up.
- Urologic consultation: For failed catheterization, suspected urethral stricture (suprapubic catheter consideration), new spinal pathology (Cauda equina).

### Priapism

**Ischemic (Low-Flow) Priapism — Emergency**
- Pathophysiology: Failure of venous outflow; blood pools, becomes hypoxic, acidotic.
- Presentation: Painful, rigid erection > 4 hours; corpora cavernosa rigid, glans soft.
- Etiology: Sickle cell disease (most common in children), medications (intracavernosal injections, trazodone, antipsychotics, cocaine), idiopathic.
- Corporal blood gas: pO2 < 30 mmHg, pCO2 > 60 mmHg, pH < 7.25 = ischemic.

**Non-Ischemic (High-Flow) Priapism**
- Pathophysiology: Unregulated arterial inflow from arteriovenous fistula (usually post-perineal trauma).
- Presentation: Painless, non-rigid erection; perineal trauma history.
- Not an emergency; managed electively with angiography and embolization.

**Ischemic Priapism Treatment:**
1. Corporal aspiration: 18–21 gauge needle inserted at base of corpora at 3 or 9 o'clock position; aspirate 10–20 mL; repeat until blood is bright red.
2. **Phenylephrine (drug of choice):**
   - 100–200 mcg in 1 mL injected intracavernously every 5 minutes (max 1 mg/hr).
   - Pediatric/cardiovascular risk: 100 mcg (dilute further).
   - Sympathomimetic alpha-1 agonist; induces detumescence.
3. Refractory cases (> 24–36 hours): Surgical shunt (Winter shunt, Ebbehoj shunt) required urgently — permanent erectile dysfunction likely if untreated > 48–72 hours.
4. Sickle cell: Hydration, oxygenation, exchange transfusion; penile treatment as above.

### Paraphimosis

- Foreskin retracted and cannot be replaced over glans.
- Constricting band causes glans edema → vascular compromise → gangrene if untreated.
- **Treatment:**
  1. Topical analgesia / dorsal penile block.
  2. Manual reduction: Bimanual compression of glans (reduce edema) then push foreskin forward over glans.
  3. Ice application for 5 minutes to reduce swelling prior to reduction.
  4. Osmotic agents: Apply granulated sugar or concentrated dextrose solution for 20 minutes to reduce edema.
  5. **Dorsal slit:** If manual reduction fails; urology consultation.
  6. **Circumcision:** Definitive management; performed electively after acute episode resolves.

## Ophthalmic Emergencies

### Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma (AACG)

**Pathophysiology:** Obstruction of aqueous humor outflow at trabecular meshwork by iris apposition → acute IOP elevation.

**Precipitating Factors:** Pupillary dilation (dark environment, mydriatic drops, anticholinergic medications, emotional stress).

**Presentation:**
- Severe unilateral eye pain (often boring, aching, can radiate to forehead or cause nausea/vomiting).
- Decreased vision, halos around lights (corneal edema).
- Nausea and vomiting (mistaken for GI illness).
- Mid-dilated, non-reactive (or sluggish) pupil.
- Rock-hard eye on palpation.
- Conjunctival injection (circumlimbal/ciliary flush).
- Corneal cloudiness/haziness (epithelial edema).
- Shallow anterior chamber.

**Treatment — Emergent:**
1. **Topical timolol 0.5%** (1 drop): Reduces aqueous production.
2. **Topical pilocarpine 1–2%** (1 drop): Miosis pulls iris away from trabecular meshwork; may be ineffective if IOP very high (ischemic iris sphincter).
3. **Topical apraclonidine 1%** (1 drop): Alpha-2 agonist; reduces aqueous production.
4. **Acetazolamide 500 mg IV** (or PO if IV unavailable): Systemic carbonic anhydrase inhibitor; reduces aqueous production.
5. **Mannitol 1–2 g/kg IV over 45 minutes:** Osmotic agent; reduces vitreous volume and IOP.
6. Supine positioning (lens moves posteriorly, relieves pupillary block).
7. Emergent ophthalmology consultation for laser peripheral iridotomy (definitive treatment).

### Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO)

**Presentation:**
- Sudden, painless, monocular vision loss (complete or partial/altitudinal field defect).
- Vision loss often described as "shade pulled over eye."
- Afferent pupillary defect (relative) in affected eye.

**Fundoscopic Findings:**
- **Cherry-red spot** at macula (foveal choroidal circulation spared; surrounding ischemic retina appears white).
- Retinal pallor/whitening (ischemia of retinal nerve fiber layer).
- Segmentation/boxcarring of retinal arterioles.

**CRAO as Stroke Equivalent:**
- 15% of CRAO patients have concurrent acute brain infarction on DWI MRI.
- Evaluate for cardioembolic source (ECG for AF, carotid US, echocardiogram).

**Treatment (Time-Dependent — Target < 4.5 Hours):**
1. Ocular massage: Firm, intermittent pressure on eye x10 seconds on/off for 5–10 minutes — dislodges clot.
2. **IOP-lowering agents:** Topical timolol, acetazolamide 500 mg IV — reduces IOP → increases arterial perfusion gradient.
3. **Anterior chamber paracentesis:** Rapidly lowers IOP; performed by ophthalmologist.
4. Hyperbaric oxygen: Some evidence for early use.
5. **IV tPA:** Emerging evidence; consider in patients presenting within 4.5 hours with significant visual deficit; same contraindications as stroke tPA; ophthalmology + neurology co-management.
6. Positioning: Flat (increases ocular perfusion pressure).
7. Rebreathing into paper bag: Hypercapnia → vasodilation (limited evidence).

### Chemical Eye Injury

**Severity:**
- **Alkali (lye, lime, ammonia):** More severe. Saponification of cell membranes; penetrates deeper into anterior chamber; risk of cataract, glaucoma, corneal opacification.
- **Acid:** Less severe. Protein coagulation creates barrier limiting penetration (exception: hydrofluoric acid penetrates deeply).

**Treatment — Copious Irrigation is Priority ONE:**
1. Immediate copious irrigation — begin before triage, before any other assessment.
2. Irrigate with normal saline or sterile water; minimum 2 liters (up to 4–10 L for alkali).
3. Morgan lens for continuous irrigation.
4. Check pH at conjunctival fornix after irrigation — target pH 7.0–7.4; repeat irrigation if abnormal.
5. Re-check pH 10–15 minutes after irrigation stops (off-gassing can cause pH rebound).
6. Topical anesthetic (proparacaine) to facilitate examination and irrigation.
7. Remove contact lenses; evert eyelids; remove particulate matter (lime, plaster).
8. Emergent ophthalmology consultation for all but the most minor acid splashes.

### Orbital vs Periorbital (Preseptal) Cellulitis

**Distinguishing Features:**

| Feature | Periorbital/Preseptal | Orbital |
|---------|----------------------|---------|
| Location | Anterior to orbital septum | Posterior to orbital septum |
| Proptosis | Absent | Present |
| Ophthalmoplegia | Absent | Present (pain with eye movement) |
| Vision | Normal | May be decreased |
| Pupil | Normal | May show APD |
| IOP | Normal | May be elevated |
| Pain with EOM | Absent | Present |
| Source | Superficial skin/sinuses | Sinusitis (ethmoid), dental |

**Orbital Cellulitis Treatment:**
- Emergent CT orbits with contrast: Detect orbital abscess (requires surgical drainage).
- IV antibiotics: Vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam (or cefepime for MRSA coverage).
- Emergent ophthalmology consultation.
- ENT consultation (concurrent sinusitis in > 85%).
- Surgical drainage if orbital abscess identified.
- Risks: Cavernous sinus thrombosis, intracranial extension, vision loss.

**Periorbital/Preseptal Cellulitis Treatment:**
- Minor/mild: Oral antibiotics (amoxicillin-clavulanate) outpatient with close follow-up.
- Moderate: IV antibiotics, possible admission.
- Children: Lower threshold for admission.

### Pediatric Resuscitation Fundamentals

### Pediatric Airway

- Pediatric larynx is more anterior, cephalad, and funnel-shaped (narrowest at cricoid ring — different from adult).
- **Blade selection:** Miller (straight) blade preferred in infants and young children (epiglottis is long, floppy, omega-shaped).
- **ETT size (uncuffed for age < 8):** Age/4 + 4 mm (e.g., 4-year-old: 4/4 + 4 = 5.0 mm ETT).
- **ETT size (cuffed, increasingly used):** Age/4 + 3.5 mm.
- **Insertion depth at lip:** ETT size × 3 (e.g., 4.0 ETT → 12 cm at lip).
- Pediatric BVM sizes: Neonatal, infant, child; adult BVM acceptable in adolescents.
- IO (intraosseous) access: Proximal tibia, distal femur, humerus; EZ-IO preferred device; all resuscitation drugs can be given IO.

### Febrile Seizure

**Simple Febrile Seizure:**
- Age 6 months–6 years.
- Generalized (tonic-clonic).
- Duration < 15 minutes.
- Single episode in 24-hour period.
- Full recovery within 1 hour.
- No intracranial infection or metabolic cause.

**Complex Febrile Seizure:**
- Any of: Focal onset, duration > 15 minutes, recurrent within 24 hours, incomplete recovery within 1 hour.

**Management:**
- Seizure termination (if ongoing):
  - Lorazepam IV/IO 0.1 mg/kg (max 4 mg) — first line if IV access.
  - Midazolam IN/IM 0.1–0.2 mg/kg (max 10 mg) — preferred if no IV (buccal midazolam also acceptable).
  - Diazepam PR 0.5 mg/kg — widely available, effective.
  - Second-line (if benzodiazepines fail): Fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg IV, levetiracetam 60 mg/kg IV, phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV.
- Lumbar puncture (LP): Strongly consider in age < 12 months after first febrile seizure (limited meningeal signs in infants); consider in unvaccinated children; consider if post-ictal period prolonged or complex features.
- Routine antiepileptic prophylaxis: NOT recommended for simple febrile seizures.
- Recurrence risk: 30% overall; higher with young age at first seizure, family history, low fever at onset.

### Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)

- Etiology: Parainfluenza virus (type 1 most common).
- Age: 6 months–3 years.
- Presentation: Barky (seal-like) cough, inspiratory stridor, hoarse voice, low-grade fever; worse at night.
- Steeple sign on AP neck X-ray (subglottic narrowing) — characteristic but not required for diagnosis.

**Severity Assessment:**
| Severity | Features |
|---------|---------|
| Mild | Barky cough; no stridor at rest; no retractions |
| Moderate | Stridor at rest; visible retractions; no agitation |
| Severe | Stridor at rest; marked retractions; agitation, decreased LOC |

**Treatment:**
- **All patients:** Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg PO/IM/IV (max 10 mg) — reduces severity and return visits.
- **Moderate-severe:** Racemic epinephrine 0.05 mL/kg of 2.25% solution in 3 mL NS via nebulizer (max 0.5 mL); or L-epinephrine 0.5 mg/kg of 1:1000 (max 5 mg) — observe minimum 2–4 hours for rebound stridor.
- Humidified air: Traditional recommendation; evidence mixed.
- Heliox: Consider for severe croup with impending respiratory failure.
- Intubation: Rarely required; use ETT 0.5–1 size smaller than predicted (subglottic narrowing); ENT standby for surgical airway.

### Epiglottitis

- Etiology: Historically Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib); now less common due to vaccination; Group A Strep, Staph aureus more prevalent.
- Now seen more in adults than children.
- Presentation: Rapid onset, high fever, drooling (inability to swallow secretions), muffled "hot potato" voice, dysphagia, tripod positioning (leaning forward, neck extended, hands on knees).

**Critical Management Principles:**
- **Do NOT examine the oropharynx** — may precipitate complete airway obstruction.
- **Do NOT agitate the child** — any agitation can precipitate laryngospasm.
- Keep child calm; parent holding; avoid IV placement until definitive airway secured.
- **Secure airway in controlled setting (OR) with anesthesia and ENT present.**
- Lateral neck X-ray: Thumbprint sign (enlarged epiglottis) — only if patient is stable and imaging does not delay airway management.

**Airway Management:**
- Direct laryngoscopy or flexible nasopharyngoscopy in OR with intubation tray and surgical airway backup.
- Intubate with ETT 0.5–1 size smaller than predicted.
- Antibiotics: Ceftriaxone 50–100 mg/kg/day IV after airway secured.

### Intussusception

- Most common cause of bowel obstruction in infants and toddlers.
- Peak age: 6 months–3 years (up to 80–90% are ileocolic).
- Pathophysiology: Intestine telescopes into adjacent distal segment → lymphatic obstruction → venous obstruction → arterial compromise → ischemia.

**Presentation:**
- Episodic crampy abdominal pain (child pulls legs to chest, cries intensely, then appears well between episodes).
- Bilious vomiting (with obstruction).
- **Currant-jelly stool:** Blood-tinged mucoid stool — late sign indicating mucosal sloughing; only present in 15–20%.
- Right lower quadrant sausage-shaped mass on palpation.
- **"Dance's sign":** Empty right iliac fossa on palpation (displaced intestinal contents).

**Diagnosis:**
- **Ultrasound (preferred — 95% sensitive):** Target sign (ring of echogenic center with hypoechoic rim) on transverse view; pseudokidney sign on longitudinal view.
- Abdominal X-ray: Soft tissue mass in RLQ, absence of normal cecal gas, air-fluid levels.

**Treatment:**
- Non-operative reduction (first-line):
  - **Air enema (pneumatic reduction):** Success rate 70–90%; 60–120 mmHg pressure; fluoroscopic guidance.
  - Contrast (hydrostatic) enema: Barium or water-soluble contrast under fluoroscopy; alternatives.
  - Ultrasound-guided hydrostatic reduction: Saline enema under ultrasound guidance; avoids radiation.
- Surgical reduction: Required for peritonitis, perforation, hemodynamic instability, failed non-operative reduction, or lead point (Meckel's diverticulum, polyp, lymphoma, duplication cyst).
- Recurrence after successful reduction: 5–10%; usually within 72 hours — observe post-reduction.
