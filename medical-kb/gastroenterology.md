# Gastroenterology — Clinical Reference

Structured reference for GI and hepatology topics. Optimized for RAG retrieval with structured headers, tables, and bullet points. Covers acute pancreatitis, GI bleeding, IBD, H. pylori, GERD, CRC screening, celiac disease, diverticular disease, IBS, and LFT interpretation.

---

## Acute Pancreatitis

### Definition and Diagnosis

Diagnosis requires 2 of 3 criteria (Revised Atlanta Classification):
1. Characteristic abdominal pain (acute onset, epigastric, radiates to back)
2. Serum lipase or amylase greater than 3x upper limit of normal
3. Characteristic imaging findings on CT, MRI, or ultrasound

Lipase is preferred over amylase — greater sensitivity and specificity. Amylase normalizes faster (24-72h); lipase remains elevated 7-14 days.

### Etiology

| Cause | Frequency | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Gallstones / Biliary | 40% | Most common overall; small stones, microlithiasis |
| Alcohol | 30% | Typically chronic heavy use; acute on chronic common |
| Hypertriglyceridemia (HTG) | 1-4% | TG >1000 mg/dL; treat with insulin drip, fibrates |
| Medications | 1-2% | See drug table below |
| ERCP-induced | 3-5% post-ERCP | Prophylaxis: rectal indomethacin + pancreatic stent |
| Autoimmune (Type 1/2 AIP) | Rare | IgG4 elevation; steroid responsive |
| Hypercalcemia | Rare | Hyperparathyroidism; malignancy |
| Pancreatic divisum | Rare | Incomplete ductal fusion |
| Idiopathic | 10-20% | Exclude microlithiasis, early chronic |
| Hereditary/Genetic | Rare | PRSS1, SPINK1, CFTR mutations |
| Trauma | Rare | Blunt abdominal trauma; ERCP |
| Ischemia | Rare | Post-cardiac surgery, vasculitis |
| Infectious | Rare | Mumps, CMV, HIV, Ascaris |
| Scorpion sting | Rare | Tityus trinitatis; tropical regions |

#### Drug-Induced Pancreatitis — Common Offenders

| Drug Class | Specific Agents |
|---|---|
| Antibiotics | Metronidazole, tetracyclines, sulfonamides |
| Immunosuppressants | Azathioprine, 6-mercaptopurine, mesalamine |
| HIV antiretrovirals | Didanosine (ddI), stavudine, pentamidine |
| Diuretics | Furosemide, thiazides |
| GLP-1 agonists | Exenatide, liraglutide (evidence debated) |
| Valproic acid | Especially in children |
| Statins | Rare; class effect |
| ACE inhibitors | Rare |
| Estrogens | Especially if hypertriglyceridemia coexists |
| Chemotherapy | L-asparaginase, cytarabine |
| Corticosteroids | Controversial |

### Revised Atlanta Severity Classification

| Severity | Definition |
|---|---|
| Mild | No organ failure, no local or systemic complications |
| Moderately Severe | Transient organ failure (<48h) AND/OR local or systemic complications |
| Severe | Persistent organ failure (>48h), single or multiorgan |

Organ failure defined using Modified Marshall Scoring System (respiratory, renal, cardiovascular). Score >2 in any system = organ failure.

Local complications:
- Acute peripancreatic fluid collection (APFC) — early, no wall
- Pancreatic pseudocyst — mature wall, no solid debris, >4 weeks
- Acute necrotic collection (ANC) — early, contains necrosis
- Walled-off necrosis (WON) — mature wall, contains necrosis, >4 weeks

### BISAP Score (Bedside Index for Severity in Acute Pancreatitis)

Score 1 point for each present within 24 hours of admission:

| Criterion | Score |
|---|---|
| BUN >25 mg/dL | 1 |
| Impaired mental status (GCS <15) | 1 |
| SIRS (2 or more criteria) | 1 |
| Age >60 years | 1 |
| Pleural effusion on imaging | 1 |

- Score 0-2: low risk (mortality <1%)
- Score 3-5: high risk (mortality up to 22%)

SIRS criteria: temp <36 or >38C, HR >90, RR >20 or PaCO2 <32, WBC <4 or >12k or >10% bands.

### Ranson Criteria

#### At Admission

| Parameter | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Age | >55 years |
| WBC | >16,000/mm3 |
| Blood glucose | >200 mg/dL |
| LDH | >350 IU/L |
| AST | >250 IU/L |

#### At 48 Hours

| Parameter | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Hematocrit decrease | >10% |
| BUN increase | >5 mg/dL |
| Calcium | <8 mg/dL |
| PaO2 | <60 mmHg |
| Base deficit | >4 mEq/L |
| Fluid sequestration | >6 L |

Scoring:
- 0-2: mortality <1%
- 3-4: mortality ~15%
- 5-6: mortality ~40%
- 7-11: mortality ~100%

Not validated for gallstone pancreatitis (modified Ranson criteria exist for gallstone etiology).

### CT Severity Index (Balthazar Score)

#### CT Grade (Balthazar Grade)

| Grade | CT Findings | Points |
|---|---|---|
| A | Normal pancreas | 0 |
| B | Pancreatic enlargement only | 1 |
| C | Pancreatic inflammation +/- peripancreatic fat | 2 |
| D | Single peripancreatic fluid collection | 3 |
| E | Two or more fluid collections or retroperitoneal gas | 4 |

#### Degree of Pancreatic Necrosis

| Necrosis | Points |
|---|---|
| None | 0 |
| <30% | 2 |
| 30-50% | 4 |
| >50% | 6 |

CT Severity Index = Balthazar grade points + necrosis points (max 10)
- 0-3: low severity (mortality <3%, morbidity <8%)
- 4-6: moderate severity (mortality ~6%, morbidity ~35%)
- 7-10: high severity (mortality ~17%, morbidity ~92%)

CT should be performed at 48-72h if diagnosis uncertain or to evaluate for necrosis, not routinely at admission.

### Management — Acute Pancreatitis

#### Fluid Resuscitation

- Lactated Ringer (LR) preferred over normal saline
  - LR reduces SIRS, may reduce severity compared to NS
  - Avoid in hypercalcemia (LR contains calcium)
- Initial rate: 250-500 mL/hr (goal-directed)
- Target: urine output >0.5 mL/kg/hr, HR <120, MAP >65
- Reassess volume status at 6 and 24 hours
- Avoid aggressive fluid resuscitation after initial 24-48h (associated with worse outcomes)
- Hematocrit and BUN as markers of hemoconcentration/inadequate resuscitation

#### Nutrition

- Early oral or nasogastric (NG) feeding within 24-48 hours is preferred in mild-moderate disease
- Early enteral nutrition reduces infectious complications, length of stay, mortality
- Soft/low-fat oral diet is safe; no need to wait for resolution of pain or normalization of lipase
- Nasojejunal (NJ) tube: reserved for patients who cannot tolerate NG feeds
- Total parenteral nutrition (TPN): only when enteral route is not feasible for >5-7 days
- Nothing by mouth (NPO) status should not be prolonged; resume feeding as soon as tolerated

#### Analgesia

- Adequate pain control is essential; do not withhold analgesia
- IV opioids: hydromorphone or morphine acceptable
- Meperidine not preferred (metabolite accumulation)
- NSAIDs can augment opioid analgesia

#### Antibiotics

- NO prophylactic antibiotics — not recommended even for predicted severe pancreatitis or sterile necrosis
- Antibiotics indicated ONLY for:
  - Confirmed or suspected infected pancreatic necrosis
  - Concurrent cholangitis
  - Concurrent bacteremia or other extrapancreatic infection
- Preferred agents for infected necrosis: carbapenems (imipenem, meropenem), fluoroquinolones + metronidazole, or piperacillin-tazobactam
- Duration guided by clinical response and culture data

#### ERCP in Acute Pancreatitis

- ERCP within 24 hours for acute pancreatitis with concurrent cholangitis
- ERCP not indicated for predicted severe biliary pancreatitis without cholangitis or persistent biliary obstruction
- Choledocholithiasis on imaging without cholangitis: intraoperative cholangiogram or MRCP to guide decision

#### Cholecystectomy in Gallstone Pancreatitis

- Same-admission laparoscopic cholecystectomy for mild gallstone pancreatitis
  - Reduces risk of recurrence; safe during same hospitalization
  - Target within index admission before discharge
- Severe pancreatitis: delay cholecystectomy until inflammatory process resolves (4-6 weeks minimum)
- Interval cholecystectomy if patient not surgical candidate: endoscopic sphincterotomy as bridge

### Infected Pancreatic Necrosis — Step-Up Approach

Infected necrosis suspected when: clinical deterioration at 7-10 days, persistent fever, rising WBC, gas in necrotic collection on CT.

Confirm with CT-guided fine needle aspiration (FNA) or treat empirically if high clinical suspicion.

Step-Up Approach (preferred over upfront open necrosectomy):

1. Antibiotics (carbapenem preferred for pancreatic necrosis penetration)
2. Minimally invasive drainage:
   - Percutaneous catheter drainage (PCD): first step, especially for APFC or early ANC
   - Endoscopic ultrasound (EUS)-guided transmural drainage for WON with gastric/duodenal proximity
3. If drainage fails (no improvement in 72h):
   - Video-assisted retroperitoneal debridement (VARD)
   - Endoscopic necrosectomy (through transluminal route)
4. Open surgical necrosectomy: reserved for failure of minimally invasive approaches

Timing: delay intervention for at least 4 weeks to allow WON to mature (liquify and wall off). Earlier intervention carries higher morbidity.

## Chronic Pancreatitis

### Overview

Progressive fibroinflammatory destruction of pancreatic parenchyma leading to irreversible loss of exocrine and endocrine function. Distinct from recurrent acute pancreatitis, though recurrent acute pancreatitis can progress to chronic.

### Etiology — TIGAR-O Classification

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Toxic-metabolic | Alcohol, tobacco, hypercalcemia, hypertriglyceridemia, chronic renal failure |
| Idiopathic | Early-onset, late-onset, tropical |
| Genetic | PRSS1 (hereditary), SPINK1, CFTR, CTRC |
| Autoimmune | Type 1 AIP (IgG4), Type 2 AIP |
| Recurrent/severe acute pancreatitis | Post-necrotic, recurrent acute |
| Obstructive | Pancreas divisum, sphincter of Oddi dysfunction, tumor |

Alcohol is most common identifiable cause in developed countries. Tobacco is an independent risk factor.

### Clinical Features

- Chronic abdominal pain: epigastric, radiates to back, often postprandial, partially relieved by leaning forward
- Exocrine insufficiency: steatorrhea (oily stools, malabsorption), weight loss, fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies (A, D, E, K)
- Endocrine insufficiency: Type 3c Diabetes Mellitus (pancreatogenic diabetes)
- Complications: pseudocyst, bile duct obstruction, duodenal obstruction, pancreatic cancer (20-fold increased risk)

### Exocrine Pancreatic Insufficiency (EPI)

Occurs when >90% of pancreatic exocrine function is lost.

Diagnosis:
- Fecal elastase-1: <100 mcg/g = severe EPI; 100-200 = moderate
- 72-hour fecal fat collection (gold standard, rarely used clinically)
- Secretin-stimulated MRCP/EUS

Treatment: Pancreatic Enzyme Replacement Therapy (PERT)
- Pancrelipase: enteric-coated microspheres
- Dosing: 40,000-50,000 lipase units per main meal; 25,000 per snack
- Take with first bite of meal; do not chew or crush
- Dose titration based on symptom response and nutritional parameters
- Concurrent PPI improves efficacy (reduces acid denaturation)
- Monitor fat-soluble vitamins and supplement as needed

### Type 3c Diabetes Mellitus (Pancreatogenic Diabetes)

- Caused by loss of islet cells (both alpha and beta)
- Brittle diabetes: prone to hypoglycemia due to glucagon deficiency
- Insulin required in many patients
- Avoid sulfonylureas (hypoglycemia risk without intact counterregulation)
- Incretin-based therapies may be considered but with caution
- Monitor for DKA (uncommon but possible if residual beta cells destroyed)

### Pain Management

- Abstinence from alcohol and tobacco (slow disease progression)
- Analgesic ladder: acetaminophen, NSAIDs, tramadol, opioids
- Pancreatic enzyme supplementation may reduce pain (reduces CCK stimulation)
- Nerve block: endoscopic ultrasound-guided celiac plexus block for refractory pain
- Endoscopic therapy: stone extraction, stent placement for ductal obstruction
- Surgery: Puestow procedure (lateral pancreaticojejunostomy) for dilated duct (>5mm); Whipple for head-predominant disease

## GI Bleeding — Upper Gastrointestinal Bleed (UGIB)

### Definition

Bleeding proximal to the ligament of Treitz. Presents as hematemesis, melena, or hematochezia (in massive UGIB with rapid transit).

### Causes

| Cause | Frequency |
|---|---|
| Peptic ulcer disease (PUD) | 40-50% |
| Variceal bleeding (esophageal/gastric) | 10-20% |
| Mallory-Weiss tear | 5-10% |
| Erosive esophagitis/gastritis/duodenitis | 5-15% |
| Dieulafoy lesion | 1-2% |
| Gastric antral vascular ectasia (GAVE) | 1-2% |
| Aortoenteric fistula | Rare, life-threatening |
| Hemobilia | Rare |
| Upper GI malignancy | 1-2% |

### Initial Assessment and Resuscitation

- Two large-bore IVs (14-16 gauge); central venous access if needed
- IV fluid resuscitation with crystalloid; type and crossmatch
- Transfusion threshold: Hemoglobin (Hgb) target >7 g/dL (restrictive strategy)
  - Target Hgb >8 in coronary artery disease or hemodynamic instability
  - Restrictive transfusion strategy (Hgb 7) associated with better outcomes in UGIB (TRICC, Villanueva trials)
- Platelet target: >50,000 for active bleeding with planned endoscopy
- Correct coagulopathy: FFP for INR >1.5-2.0, vitamin K
- Avoid overtransfusion in variceal bleeding (increases portal pressure)
- Intubate for airway protection: active hematemesis, altered mental status, respiratory compromise
- NG lavage: not required routinely; may help clear stomach before endoscopy

### Glasgow-Blatchford Score (GBS)

Identifies low-risk patients who may be managed as outpatients. Score 0 = very low risk; can discharge for elective outpatient EGD.

| Variable | Score |
|---|---|
| BUN 18.2-22.3 mg/dL | 2 |
| BUN 22.4-27.9 mg/dL | 3 |
| BUN 28-70 mg/dL | 4 |
| BUN >70 mg/dL | 6 |
| Hemoglobin 12-12.9 (male) | 1 |
| Hemoglobin 10-11.9 (male) | 3 |
| Hemoglobin <10 (male) | 6 |
| Hemoglobin 10-11.9 (female) | 1 |
| Hemoglobin <10 (female) | 6 |
| SBP 100-109 mmHg | 1 |
| SBP 90-99 mmHg | 2 |
| SBP <90 mmHg | 3 |
| HR >100 bpm | 1 |
| Melena | 1 |
| Syncope | 2 |
| Hepatic disease | 2 |
| Cardiac failure | 2 |

Score >1 requires inpatient management. Score 0 = low risk; consider outpatient EGD.

### Rockall Score

Used post-endoscopy to predict rebleeding and mortality risk.

| Variable | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Age | <60 | 60-79 | >80 | — |
| Shock | None (SBP>100, HR<100) | Tachycardia (HR>100, SBP>100) | Hypotension (SBP<100) | — |
| Comorbidity | None | — | CHF, IHD, major comorbidity | Renal/hepatic failure, malignancy |
| Diagnosis | MW tear / no lesion | All other | Upper GI malignancy | — |
| Endoscopic stigmata | None / dark spot | — | Blood in upper GI, adherent clot, visible vessel, active bleeding | — |

Total score:
- 0-2: low risk (rebleed <5%, mortality <0.1%)
- 3-4: intermediate
- 5+: high risk (rebleed >40%, mortality >11%)

### Medical Management

#### Proton Pump Inhibitor (PPI)

- IV pantoprazole: 80 mg IV bolus followed by 8 mg/hr continuous infusion
- Start before endoscopy (reduces need for endoscopic therapy, not shown to improve mortality)
- High-dose IV PPI reduces rebleeding in patients with high-risk stigmata post-endoscopy
- Switch to oral PPI (high dose, twice daily) after endoscopy for 72h, then standard dosing

#### Variceal Bleeding — Specific Management

- Octreotide (somatostatin analogue):
  - 50 mcg IV bolus followed by 50 mcg/hr infusion
  - Continue for 3-5 days
  - Reduces portal pressure, splanchnic blood flow
  - Terlipressin preferred in Europe (not available in US); vasopressin + nitroglycerin alternative
- Antibiotic prophylaxis (ceftriaxone 1g IV daily x 7 days):
  - Indicated in ALL patients with cirrhosis and acute variceal bleeding
  - Reduces bacterial infections, rebleeding, and mortality
  - Alternative: norfloxacin 400mg BID (oral) if no IV access
- TIPS (transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt): early TIPS within 72h for Child-Pugh B/C or failure of endoscopic therapy
- Balloon tamponade (Sengstaken-Blakemore tube): bridge to TIPS/endoscopy, short-term use only (max 24h)

### EGD Timing

| Clinical Status | EGD Timing |
|---|---|
| Hemodynamically stable, GBS low risk | Within 24 hours |
| High-risk features (hemodynamic instability, Rockall high, cirrhosis) | Within 6-12 hours |
| Active massive hemorrhage, shock | Emergent after resuscitation |
| Low-risk (GBS 0) | Consider outpatient within 24-72h |

### Forrest Classification — Endoscopic Stigmata of Peptic Ulcer

| Class | Finding | Rebleed Risk | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ia | Active spurting arterial bleed | 55-90% | Endoscopic therapy + high-dose IV PPI |
| Ib | Active oozing | 30-55% | Endoscopic therapy + high-dose IV PPI |
| IIa | Non-bleeding visible vessel | 40-50% | Endoscopic therapy + high-dose IV PPI |
| IIb | Adherent clot | 20-30% | Attempt clot removal; if vessel exposed — treat |
| IIc | Flat pigmented spot / hematin | 5-10% | Medical therapy alone (oral PPI) |
| III | Clean ulcer base | <2% | Medical therapy alone; may discharge early |

Endoscopic therapy options: epinephrine injection (1:10,000) + second modality (thermal coagulation, hemoclip, fibrin glue). Epinephrine alone is insufficient.

## GI Bleeding — Lower Gastrointestinal Bleed (LGIB)

### Definition

Bleeding originating distal to the ligament of Treitz. Typically presents as hematochezia or maroon stools.

### Causes

| Cause | Frequency | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Diverticulosis | 30-40% | Most common cause of major LGIB; right colon predominates |
| Anorectal (hemorrhoids, fissure) | 20-30% | Usually minor; bright red per rectum |
| Colonic ischemia | 5-10% | Watershed areas (splenic flexure, sigmoid); post-aortic surgery |
| Colorectal neoplasm | 5-10% | Polyps, cancer |
| IBD (UC, Crohn's colitis) | 5% | |
| Angiodysplasia/AVMs | 3-5% | Common in elderly; cecum and right colon |
| Post-polypectomy bleeding | 1-2% | Delayed up to 2 weeks |
| Radiation proctopathy | 1-2% | Post-pelvic radiation |
| Meckel's diverticulum | Rare | Children/young adults; 99mTc scan |
| Rectal varices | Rare | Portal hypertension |

### Oakland Score

Validated score to identify patients safe for early discharge:

| Variable | Score |
|---|---|
| Age 40-69 | 3 |
| Age >70 | 5 |
| Male sex | 1 |
| Previous LGIB admission | 1 |
| DRE: blood on exam | 1 |
| HR 70-89 | 3 |
| HR 90-109 | 5 |
| HR >110 | 7 |
| SBP <90 | 5 |
| Hemoglobin 3.6-6.9 g/dL | 22 |
| Hemoglobin 7-8.9 g/dL | 17 |
| Hemoglobin 9-10.9 g/dL | 13 |
| Hemoglobin 11-12.9 g/dL | 8 |
| Hemoglobin 13-15.9 g/dL | 4 |

Score <8: low risk; may consider early discharge with outpatient colonoscopy.

### Management

- Resuscitation as with UGIB; hemoglobin target >7 (>8 if cardiovascular disease)
- Colonoscopy within 24 hours after bowel prep (where feasible): preferred first-line investigation and treatment
  - Allows diagnosis and endoscopic therapy (epinephrine, clipping, thermal coagulation)
  - Urgent colonoscopy without prep if patient unstable and hemorrhage ongoing
- CT angiography (CTA): preferred for active bleeding if colonoscopy not immediately feasible
  - Detects bleeding at rates >0.3 mL/min
  - Guides selective embolization
- Mesenteric angiography + embolization: for active arterial bleeding identified on CTA or ongoing massive hemorrhage
  - Success rate 50-80%; ischemia complication risk 5-10%
- Tagged red blood cell scan (99mTc): detects bleeding >0.1 mL/min; may localize source before angiography
- Surgery: reserved for refractory hemorrhage, multiple failed endoscopic/angiographic attempts, or clinical deterioration

## Inflammatory Bowel Disease — Crohn's Disease

### Pathophysiology

Transmural (full-thickness) inflammation; can affect any segment of the GI tract from mouth to anus. Characterized by:
- Discontinuous involvement (skip lesions)
- Cobblestone mucosa
- Creeping fat, fistula, stricture, abscess
- Non-caseating granulomas (50% of biopsies)

### Montreal Classification — Crohn's Disease

#### Age at Diagnosis
- A1: <17 years
- A2: 17-40 years
- A3: >40 years

#### Location (L)
- L1: Terminal ileum
- L2: Colon
- L3: Ileocolon
- L4: Upper GI tract (add modifier to L1-L3)

#### Behavior (B)
- B1: Non-stricturing, non-penetrating (inflammatory)
- B2: Stricturing
- B3: Penetrating (fistulizing, abscess)
- Add "p" to any B category for perianal disease (e.g., B1p)

### Extraintestinal Manifestations (EIM)

| System | Manifestation | Relation to IBD Activity |
|---|---|---|
| Musculoskeletal | Peripheral arthropathy Type 1 (large joints, <5 joints) | Parallel to bowel activity |
| Musculoskeletal | Peripheral arthropathy Type 2 (small joints, polyarticular) | Independent of bowel activity |
| Musculoskeletal | Ankylosing spondylitis / sacroiliitis | Independent of bowel activity |
| Skin | Erythema nodosum | Parallel to bowel activity |
| Skin | Pyoderma gangrenosum | Independent of bowel activity |
| Skin | Sweet syndrome | Parallel to bowel activity |
| Eyes | Episcleritis | Parallel to bowel activity |
| Eyes | Uveitis/iritis | May be independent |
| Liver/biliary | Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC) | Independent (more common in UC) |
| Liver/biliary | Cholelithiasis | Increased risk (terminal ileal disease) |
| Renal | Nephrolithiasis (oxalate) | Terminal ileal disease |
| Renal | Ureteral obstruction/fistula | |
| Hematologic | DVT/PE (hypercoagulable state) | Parallel to activity |
| Metabolic | Osteoporosis | Steroids, malabsorption |
| Oral | Aphthous ulcers | Parallel to bowel activity |

### Treatment Ladder — Crohn's Disease

#### Induction of Remission

- Mild-moderate ileocecal:
  - Budesonide 9 mg/day x 8-12 weeks (controlled-release ileal delivery, low systemic absorption)
  - Mesalamine: limited efficacy in Crohn's; not strongly recommended
- Moderate-severe:
  - Prednisone 40-60 mg/day (tapered over 8-12 weeks)
  - Biologic therapy for steroid-dependent/refractory disease
- Severe / hospitalized:
  - IV methylprednisolone 40-60 mg/day
  - IV hydrocortisone 100 mg TID

Steroids should never be used as maintenance therapy. Steroid-sparing strategies required.

#### Maintenance of Remission

- Immunomodulators:
  - Azathioprine (AZA) 2-2.5 mg/kg/day
  - 6-Mercaptopurine (6-MP) 1-1.5 mg/kg/day
  - Check TPMT activity before initiation (risk of myelosuppression in poor metabolizers)
  - Onset of action 3-6 months
  - Methotrexate (MTX) 15-25 mg/week IM/SQ: alternative to AZA/6-MP; preferred in males (AZA lymphoma risk)
    - Teratogenic: contraception required in both sexes; folate supplementation

#### Biologic Agents

| Agent | Class | Dosing | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Infliximab (IFX) | Anti-TNF (IV) | 5 mg/kg at weeks 0, 2, 6, then q8 weeks | First biologic approved for Crohn's; fistula indication |
| Adalimumab | Anti-TNF (SQ) | 160 mg week 0, 80 mg week 2, then 40 mg q2w | Self-injectable; convenient |
| Certolizumab pegol | Anti-TNF (SQ) | 400 mg at weeks 0, 2, 4, then q4w | Pegylated; low placental transfer |
| Vedolizumab | Anti-integrin (IV) | 300 mg at weeks 0, 2, 6, then q8w | Gut-selective; favorable safety profile |
| Ustekinumab | Anti-IL12/23 (IV then SQ) | IV 6 mg/kg induction, then 90 mg SQ q8-12w | Effective in anti-TNF failures |
| Risankizumab | Anti-IL23 (p19) (IV then SQ) | 600 mg IV q4w x 3, then 360 mg SQ q8w | Approved Crohn's 2022; IL-23 selective |

Combination therapy (biologic + immunomodulator): reduces immunogenicity and improves outcomes vs. monotherapy (SONIC trial: IFX + AZA superior to either alone).

#### Perianal Crohn's Disease

- Fistulizing perianal disease: seton placement (non-cutting) to maintain drainage
- Infliximab 5 mg/kg at weeks 0, 2, 6 and maintenance q8 weeks (ACCENT II trial)
- Combination: seton + infliximab is standard
- MRI pelvis: preferred imaging for perianal anatomy
- Examine under anesthesia (EUA) with surgical drainage of abscesses
- Local injection of stem cells (darvadstrocel): approved in EU for complex perianal fistulas
- Proctectomy: last resort for refractory perianal disease

#### Treat-to-Target Strategy

Goal: mucosal healing (endoscopic remission) rather than symptom control alone.
- Targets: CDAI <150, CRP normalization, fecal calprotectin <250 mcg/g, endoscopic healing
- Monitoring: clinical assessment every 3 months; endoscopy/cross-sectional imaging every 6-12 months
- Tightening therapy if targets not met

## Inflammatory Bowel Disease — Ulcerative Colitis (UC)

### Pathophysiology

Continuous mucosal and submucosal inflammation, always involving the rectum, extending proximally in an uninterrupted pattern. Does not cause transmural inflammation (except toxic megacolon). No granulomas.

### Montreal Classification — UC

#### Extent (E)
- E1: Proctitis (distal to rectosigmoid junction)
- E2: Left-sided / distal colitis (distal to splenic flexure)
- E3: Extensive / pancolitis (proximal to splenic flexure including cecum)

#### Severity (S) — Modified Truelove-Witts / Mayo
- S0: Clinical remission
- S1: Mild (≤4 stools/day, no systemic symptoms)
- S2: Moderate (4-6 stools/day, minimal systemic features)
- S3: Severe (>6 bloody stools/day, systemic involvement)

### Mayo Score

| Variable | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Stool frequency | Normal | 1-2 above normal | 3-4 above normal | >4 above normal |
| Rectal bleeding | None | Streaks <50% | Most stools | Blood alone |
| Endoscopy findings | Normal | Mild (erythema, dec. vascularity) | Moderate (friability, erosions) | Severe (ulcers, spontaneous bleeding) |
| Physician global assessment | Normal | Mild | Moderate | Severe |

Total 0-12: Remission 0-2; Mild 3-5; Moderate 6-10; Severe 11-12.

### Medical Treatment — UC

#### Mild to Moderate Disease

- E1 (Proctitis):
  - Mesalamine suppository 1g/day (rectal delivery preferred for distal disease)
  - Alternative: mesalamine enema for proctosigmoiditis
  - Add oral mesalamine if rectal therapy alone inadequate
- E2-E3 (Left-sided / Extensive):
  - Oral mesalamine 2.4-4.8 g/day (e.g., Asacol HD, Lialda, Pentasa)
  - Combination oral + rectal mesalamine is superior to oral alone
  - Sulfasalazine: effective but higher side-effect burden (headache, male infertility)

#### Moderate to Severe Disease — Induction

- Oral prednisone 40-60 mg/day x 2-4 weeks, then taper over 8-12 weeks
- Budesonide MMX (9 mg): mild-moderate, some right-sided benefit; less systemic
- Biologics for steroid-dependent or refractory disease:
  - Azathioprine 2-2.5 mg/kg/day (check TPMT first)
  - Infliximab 5 mg/kg at weeks 0, 2, 6, q8w
  - Vedolizumab 300 mg IV at weeks 0, 2, 6, q8w (gut-selective, favorable safety)
  - Tofacitinib 10 mg BID x 8 weeks induction, then 5 mg BID maintenance (JAK inhibitor)
  - Ozanimod 0.92 mg daily (S1P receptor modulator)
  - Upadacitinib 45 mg daily x 8 weeks induction, then 15 or 30 mg daily (JAK inhibitor)
  - Etrasimod 2 mg daily (S1P receptor modulator)

#### Acute Severe Ulcerative Colitis (ASUC)

Definition — Truelove-Witts Criteria:
- >6 bloody stools per day PLUS any of:
  - HR >90 bpm
  - Temperature >37.8C
  - Hemoglobin <10.5 g/dL
  - ESR >30 mm/hr

Management:
1. Hospital admission; IV fluids; NPO or limited oral intake
2. IV methylprednisolone 60 mg/day (or IV hydrocortisone 100 mg TID, or IV prednisolone 40 mg/day)
3. Thromboprophylaxis: prophylactic heparin (high DVT risk in IBD)
4. Stool cultures; CMV testing if steroid refractory
5. Surgical consult on admission
6. Avoid antidiarrheals, NSAIDs, opioids (risk of toxic megacolon)

Assessment at Day 3-5 (Oxford Criteria / Swedish Short Health Assessment):
- If adequate response: continue steroids, plan transition to outpatient oral steroids
- If inadequate response (failing steroids): rescue therapy

Rescue Therapy Options:
- Infliximab 5 mg/kg IV (accelerated dosing protocol: 0, 3-7 days, 14 days for rapid cycling)
- Cyclosporine 2 mg/kg/day IV (bridge to thiopurine; requires close monitoring of levels, renal function, BP)
- Tacrolimus: alternative to cyclosporine
- If rescue fails or contraindicated: urgent colectomy (proctocolectomy with IPAA or end ileostomy)

Toxic Megacolon:
- Definition: transverse colon diameter >6 cm on plain film (or cecum >9 cm) with systemic toxicity
- High mortality if perforation occurs
- Management: bowel rest, NGT decompression, IV steroids, broad-spectrum antibiotics, surgical consult
- Urgent colectomy if no improvement in 24-72h

#### Colorectal Cancer (CRC) Surveillance in UC

- Start surveillance colonoscopy 8-10 years after symptom onset (regardless of extent at onset)
- Frequency:
  - Low risk (no dysplasia, no PSC, no extensive disease): q1-2 years
  - High risk (extensive colitis, PSC, pseudopolyps, family history of CRC, stricture): annually
- Chromoendoscopy with targeted biopsies preferred over random biopsies
- PSC patients: colonoscopy at diagnosis of PSC, then annually regardless of IBD duration
- Any grade of confirmed dysplasia in flat mucosa in UC: strong consideration for colectomy (multidisciplinary discussion)

## H. pylori and Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)

### H. pylori Epidemiology and Clinical Significance

- Infects ~50% of world population; lower prevalence in developed countries
- Causes: PUD (gastric and duodenal ulcers), gastric adenocarcinoma, MALT lymphoma, GERD (modest link)
- Type B gastritis (antral predominance)

### Diagnosis

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Urea Breath Test (UBT) | 93-96% | 93-96% | Preferred non-invasive; test-of-cure |
| Stool Antigen Test (SAT) | 94-96% | 93-96% | Preferred non-invasive; test-of-cure |
| Serology (IgG) | 85-90% | 79-90% | NOT for test-of-cure (remains positive after eradication) |
| Rapid urease test (CLO) | 90-95% | 95-99% | Endoscopic biopsy; affected by PPIs, antibiotics |
| Histology | 90-95% | 95-99% | Gold standard; multiple biopsies from antrum + body |
| Culture | 70-80% | ~100% | For susceptibility testing; rarely used clinically |

Important: Stop PPIs 2 weeks before testing and antibiotics 4 weeks before testing (reduces false negatives on UBT, SAT, rapid urease, histology). Serology is not affected but cannot confirm cure.

### Eradication Regimens

#### Clarithromycin Triple Therapy (14 days; where local clarithromycin resistance <15%)

- PPI (standard dose BID) + Clarithromycin 500 mg BID + Amoxicillin 1g BID
- Eradication rate: 70-85% (declining due to resistance)

#### Bismuth Quadruple Therapy (10-14 days; preferred first-line in high-resistance areas or penicillin allergy)

- PPI (standard dose BID) + Bismuth subsalicylate 525 mg QID + Metronidazole 500 mg TID-QID + Tetracycline 500 mg QID
- Eradication rate: 85-90%

#### Concomitant Therapy (14 days)

- PPI + Clarithromycin 500 mg BID + Amoxicillin 1g BID + Metronidazole 500 mg BID
- Overcomes metronidazole resistance; eradication rate ~85-90%

#### Levofloxacin Triple Therapy (salvage)

- PPI + Levofloxacin 500 mg daily + Amoxicillin 1g BID x 14 days
- Use as second-line after bismuth quadruple failure

#### Rifabutin Triple Therapy (third-line)

- PPI + Rifabutin 150 mg BID + Amoxicillin 1g BID x 10-14 days

### Test for Eradication

- Wait at least 4 weeks after completing antibiotic treatment
- Wait at least 2 weeks after stopping PPI
- Preferred method: UBT or stool antigen test
- Serology is NOT appropriate for test-of-cure

### NSAID-Associated Ulcers

- NSAIDs inhibit COX-1 (prostaglandin synthesis) — impair mucosal defense
- Risk factors: age >65, prior ulcer history, high-dose/multiple NSAIDs, anticoagulant use, corticosteroid use
- Prevention: PPI co-therapy in high-risk NSAID users; switch to COX-2 selective inhibitor
- H. pylori testing and eradication before starting chronic NSAID use

### PUD Complications

| Complication | Clinical Features | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Bleeding | Hematemesis, melena, Forrest classification | IV PPI, endoscopy (see UGIB section) |
| Perforation | Sudden severe abdominal pain, free air on imaging | Emergency surgery (laparoscopic primary repair) |
| Penetration | Ulcer erodes into adjacent organ (pancreas most common) | Medical management; surgery if refractory |
| Gastric outlet obstruction | Nausea, vomiting, succussion splash | Endoscopic balloon dilation; H. pylori eradication; surgery if refractory |

## GERD and Esophageal Disorders

### GERD

Gastroesophageal reflux disease — retrograde flow of gastric contents causing symptoms or complications.

#### Diagnosis

- Clinical diagnosis in typical cases (heartburn, regurgitation)
- Upper endoscopy (EGD): indicated for alarm symptoms (dysphagia, odynophagia, weight loss, anemia, palpable mass, age >60 with new symptoms)
- Ambulatory pH monitoring: definitive; useful when endoscopy normal and diagnosis uncertain
- Impedance-pH monitoring: detects non-acid reflux

#### Los Angeles (LA) Classification of Erosive Esophagitis

| Grade | Endoscopic Finding |
|---|---|
| A | 1 or more mucosal breaks, each <5 mm, not extending between mucosal folds |
| B | 1 or more mucosal breaks >5 mm, not extending between mucosal folds |
| C | Mucosal breaks extending between 2 or more mucosal folds, involving <75% of esophageal circumference |
| D | Mucosal breaks involving >75% of esophageal circumference |

#### Treatment — Step-Up Approach

1. Lifestyle modifications: weight loss, elevate HOB, avoid late meals, avoid trigger foods, stop smoking
2. Antacids / H2 blockers (H2RA): for mild, infrequent symptoms
3. PPI once daily before meals: standard therapy; 4-8 week course for erosive esophagitis
4. PPI twice daily: for LA C/D esophagitis, refractory symptoms
5. Add-on options: baclofen (reduces transient lower esophageal sphincter relaxations), sucralfate
6. Surgical: Nissen fundoplication (360-degree wrap) or LINX device (magnetic sphincter augmentation)

### Barrett's Esophagus

Metaplastic replacement of squamous epithelium by specialized intestinal metaplasia (columnar cells with goblet cells) in the distal esophagus. Pre-malignant; risk of esophageal adenocarcinoma.

#### Surveillance by Dysplasia Grade

| Dysplasia Grade | Management |
|---|---|
| No dysplasia | Surveillance EGD q3-5 years |
| Indefinite for dysplasia | Optimize PPI therapy; repeat EGD in 6 months |
| Low-grade dysplasia (LGD) | Endoscopic eradication therapy (EET) preferred; surveillance q6-12 months if eradication not pursued |
| High-grade dysplasia (HGD) | Endoscopic eradication therapy (EET) — radiofrequency ablation (RFA) ± endoscopic mucosal resection (EMR) |
| Intramucosal carcinoma (T1a) | EET (EMR/ESD); esophagectomy if submucosal invasion (T1b) |

Confirmed LGD/HGD should be read by two expert GI pathologists.

### Achalasia

Esophageal motility disorder: failure of lower esophageal sphincter (LES) relaxation + absent peristalsis. Due to loss of inhibitory myenteric neurons (nitric oxide/VIP).

#### Diagnosis

- Barium swallow: bird-beak appearance at GEJ; dilated esophagus
- High-resolution manometry (HRM): gold standard — absent peristalsis, elevated LES resting pressure, incomplete LES relaxation (elevated integrated relaxation pressure / IRP >15 mmHg)
- EGD: to exclude pseudoachalasia (malignancy at GEJ); normal appearing mucosa but tight LES

#### Chicago Classification (HRM Types)

- Type I: Classic achalasia — aperistalsis, no pressurization
- Type II: Aperistalsis with panesophageal pressurization (>20% of swallows) — best treatment response
- Type III: Spastic/premature contractions (>20% of swallows) — poor prognosis

#### Treatment

- Pneumatic dilation: effective, especially Type II; risk of perforation 2-3%
- Peroral endoscopic myotomy (POEM): endoscopic myotomy; high efficacy especially Type III and spastic variants; higher post-procedure GERD
- Heller myotomy (laparoscopic): surgical myotomy + partial fundoplication; durable
- Botulinum toxin injection (Botox): temporary (6-12 months); reserved for frail, elderly, poor surgical candidates
- Smooth muscle relaxants (calcium channel blockers, nitrates): minimal efficacy; bridge therapy only

### Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE)

Immune-mediated esophageal disease characterized by eosinophilic inflammation.

#### Diagnosis

- Symptoms: dysphagia (adults), food impaction, GERD-refractory symptoms; vomiting/failure to thrive in children
- Endoscopy: rings (trachealization), furrows, white exudates/plaques, edema, narrowing
- Biopsy: >15 eosinophils per high-power field (eos/hpf) in esophageal mucosa (multiple biopsies, proximal and distal esophagus)
- Exclude secondary causes (PPI-responsive eosinophilia now considered within EoE spectrum)

#### Treatment

- Proton pump inhibitor (PPI): first-line; PPI-responsive EoE responds to high-dose PPI therapy (omeprazole 20-40 mg BID x 8 weeks)
- Swallowed topical corticosteroids: fluticasone MDI swallowed (not inhaled) 440-880 mcg BID; or viscous budesonide 1 mg BID x 8-12 weeks; first-line for symptomatic/refractory
- Dupilumab (anti-IL4/IL-13): FDA-approved 2022; biologic option for moderate-severe or steroid-refractory EoE; reduces eosinophil count and improves symptoms
- Dietary elimination:
  - Six-food elimination diet (milk, wheat, eggs, soy, nuts, seafood) — most effective
  - Two-food elimination (milk, wheat) — simpler, reasonable response rate
  - Elemental diet: most effective but least tolerated
- Esophageal dilation: for symptomatic strictures/rings; does not treat underlying inflammation

## Colorectal Cancer (CRC) Screening

### Average-Risk Screening (Starting Age 45 — Updated USPSTF 2021)

| Test | Interval | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Colonoscopy | Every 10 years | Gold standard; diagnostic and therapeutic |
| Annual FIT (fecal immunochemical test) | Annually | Quantitative; preferred over gFOBT |
| Annual gFOBT (guaiac fecal occult blood) | Annually | High-sensitivity only; 3 samples |
| Cologuard (stool DNA + FIT) | Every 1-3 years | Higher sensitivity; lower specificity; high false positive rate |
| CT colonography (CTC) | Every 5 years | No sedation; bowel prep required; positive = colonoscopy |
| Flexible sigmoidoscopy | Every 5 years (or q10y + annual FIT) | Limited to left colon |
| Serology (Epi proColon cfDNA) | Annually | Lower performance; rarely used |

All positive non-colonoscopy tests require follow-up diagnostic colonoscopy.

### High-Risk Groups

#### Personal History of Polyps (Post-Polypectomy Surveillance)

| Finding | Surveillance Interval |
|---|---|
| 1-2 tubular adenomas <10 mm with low-grade dysplasia | 7-10 years |
| 3-4 tubular adenomas, or any tubular adenoma 10-20 mm, or any villous feature or HGD | 3 years |
| 5-10 adenomas | 3 years |
| >10 adenomas | 1 year; consider genetic evaluation |
| Serrated polyp <10 mm, no dysplasia | 5 years |
| Sessile serrated lesion >10 mm, or with dysplasia | 3 years |
| Traditional serrated adenoma | 3 years |
| Piecemeal resection of large polyp | 6 months |

#### Family History

| Risk | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| First-degree relative with CRC or advanced adenoma at age <60 | Colonoscopy at 40 or 10 years before proband's diagnosis (whichever earlier); repeat q5y |
| First-degree relative with CRC or advanced adenoma at age >60 | Colonoscopy at 40; standard interval if normal |
| Two or more first-degree relatives with CRC (any age) | Colonoscopy at 40 or 10 years before earliest diagnosis; q5y |

### Lynch Syndrome (Hereditary Non-Polyposis CRC — HNPCC)

- Autosomal dominant; germline mutations in MMR genes: MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2, EPCAM
- Amsterdam II criteria: 3-2-1 rule (3 relatives, 2 generations, 1 before age 50)
- Universal tumor MMR testing (IHC +/- MSI) on all CRC specimens
- Screening: colonoscopy starting age 20-25 (or 5 years before earliest family diagnosis) q1-2 years
- Extra-colonic surveillance: endometrial biopsy +/- transvaginal ultrasound annually (age 30-35); consider prophylactic hysterectomy/BSO after childbearing; gastroscopy, urinalysis

### Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP)

- Autosomal dominant; APC gene mutation; >100 colonic polyps by 2nd-3rd decade
- Near 100% lifetime CRC risk by age 40 if untreated
- Attenuated FAP (AFAP): 10-100 polyps; later onset
- Screening: sigmoidoscopy or colonoscopy starting age 10-12 annually until prophylactic surgery
- Prophylactic colectomy: by age 20-25; options: total proctocolectomy with IPAA or IRA (with continued rectal surveillance)
- Upper GI: EGD every 1-5 years (duodenal polyps and ampullary cancer risk)
- Extraintestinal: desmoid tumors, osteomas, CHRPE, thyroid cancer (Gardner syndrome)

### MUTYH-Associated Polyposis (MAP)

- Autosomal recessive; MUTYH biallelic mutations
- 10-100+ adenomas; phenotype overlaps AFAP
- Colonoscopy starting age 25-30; q1-2 years

## Celiac Disease

### Overview

Immune-mediated enteropathy triggered by ingestion of gluten (gliadin — in wheat, rye, barley) in genetically susceptible individuals (HLA-DQ2 / HLA-DQ8). Prevalence ~1% in Western populations.

### Clinical Presentations

- Classical: diarrhea, steatorrhea, bloating, weight loss, growth failure
- Non-classical: iron deficiency anemia, osteoporosis, infertility, neurologic (cerebellar ataxia, peripheral neuropathy), dermatitis herpetiformis
- Asymptomatic (subclinical): identified on screening in high-risk groups
- Refractory celiac disease: persistent villous atrophy despite strict GFD; evaluate for lymphoma

### Diagnosis

#### Serology

- Anti-tissue transglutaminase IgA (tTG-IgA): first-line test; sensitivity 90-98%, specificity 95-99%
- Anti-endomysial IgA (EMA-IgA): very high specificity; used for confirmation
- Anti-deamidated gliadin peptide IgG (DGP-IgG): useful when IgA deficient
- CRITICAL: Check total serum IgA level at time of testing — IgA deficiency (1:500 prevalence) will cause false negative tTG-IgA; if IgA deficient, use IgG-based tests (tTG-IgG, DGP-IgG)

#### Small Bowel Biopsy

- EGD with multiple duodenal biopsies (minimum 4 from duodenum + 1-2 from bulb)
- Required for definitive diagnosis; performed while patient is on gluten-containing diet

#### Marsh Classification

| Grade | Histology |
|---|---|
| 0 | Normal |
| 1 | Increased intraepithelial lymphocytes (>25 per 100 enterocytes), normal villi |
| 2 | Crypt hyperplasia added to Grade 1 findings |
| 3a | Partial villous atrophy |
| 3b | Subtotal villous atrophy |
| 3c | Total villous atrophy and crypt hyperplasia |

Modified Marsh-Oberhuber classification; Grades 3a-3c most consistent with celiac disease.

#### HLA Typing

- HLA-DQ2 (DQA1*05:01/DQB1*02:01) — 90-95% of celiac patients
- HLA-DQ8 — additional 5%
- Absence of both HLA-DQ2 and HLA-DQ8 has very high negative predictive value (virtually excludes celiac disease)
- HLA typing useful when diagnosis uncertain; does not confirm disease (30% of general population carries DQ2/DQ8)

### Management

- Strict lifelong gluten-free diet (GFD): cornerstone of treatment; avoid wheat, rye, barley; oats may be tolerated if certified gluten-free
- Dietitian referral: essential for GFD education
- Nutritional supplementation: iron, folate, calcium, vitamin D, B12 as indicated (correct deficiencies)
- Monitoring:
  - Repeat tTG-IgA at 6-12 months after GFD (antibodies should normalize)
  - BMD assessment at diagnosis (osteoporosis risk)
  - Repeat biopsy: consider at 1-2 years if serology does not normalize (to confirm mucosal healing)
  - Ongoing annual clinical review; labs (CBC, iron, B12, folate, calcium, vitamin D, LFTs)
- Vaccinations: pneumococcal vaccine (functional hyposplenism in some patients)
- Refractory celiac disease (RCD):
  - Type I: abnormal IEL phenotype but polyclonal; budesonide or immunosuppressants
  - Type II: aberrant IEL clonal expansion; high risk of enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma (EATL); capsule endoscopy, PET-CT staging

## Diverticular Disease

### Diverticulosis

- Pseudodiverticula (mucosa/submucosa herniate through weak points in muscularis)
- Common in left colon (sigmoid) in Western populations; right colon more common in Asian populations
- Prevalence increases with age; associated with low-fiber diet, obesity, physical inactivity, red meat intake
- Often asymptomatic; discovered incidentally on colonoscopy or imaging

### Acute Diverticulitis — Classification

| Type | Features |
|---|---|
| Uncomplicated | Acute inflammation of diverticulum without complications |
| Complicated | Abscess, perforation, fistula, obstruction |

#### Hinchey Classification (Complicated Diverticulitis)

| Stage | Description |
|---|---|
| I | Pericolic abscess (<4 cm), confined to mesocolon |
| II | Pelvic / distant intraabdominal abscess (>4 cm) |
| III | Purulent peritonitis (ruptured pericolic abscess, no free stool) |
| IV | Fecal peritonitis (free perforation with stool contamination) |

### Management

#### Uncomplicated Acute Diverticulitis

- Mild cases without systemic features: outpatient management safe
- Oral antibiotics: evidence for antibiotics in uncomplicated diverticulitis is WEAK; observational studies and RCTs (AVOD, DIABOLO, STAND trials) show similar outcomes with antibiotics vs. no antibiotics in mild uncomplicated cases
- Current practice: most guidelines still recommend antibiotics for first presentation in immunocompetent patients; trend toward selective use
- Antibiotic options (if given): ciprofloxacin + metronidazole OR amoxicillin-clavulanate x 7-10 days
- Clear liquid or low-residue diet during acute phase; advance as tolerated
- NSAIDs: avoid (worsen inflammation, association with perforation)
- Colonoscopy: 6-8 weeks after recovery to exclude malignancy (if not recently performed)

#### Hospitalization Indications

- Inability to tolerate oral intake / hydration
- Failure to improve on outpatient treatment
- Immunocompromised, elderly, significant comorbidities
- High fever, leukocytosis, peritoneal signs
- IV antibiotics: piperacillin-tazobactam OR ciprofloxacin + metronidazole OR cefazolin + metronidazole

#### Complicated Diverticulitis Management

| Stage | Management |
|---|---|
| Hinchey I (pericolic abscess <4 cm) | IV antibiotics; bowel rest; close observation |
| Hinchey II (abscess >4 cm) | CT-guided percutaneous drainage + IV antibiotics |
| Hinchey III (purulent peritonitis) | Emergency surgery: sigmoid colectomy + primary anastomosis (if patient stable) or Hartmann procedure |
| Hinchey IV (fecal peritonitis) | Emergency surgery: Hartmann procedure; high mortality |

#### Elective Sigmoidectomy

Indications (after recovery from acute episode):
- Classically recommended after 2 uncomplicated episodes (older guidelines); current evidence does not support routine surgery after 2 episodes
- Consider elective resection for: immunocompromised patients, complex fistula (colovesical, colovaginal), right-sided diverticulitis, persistent symptoms between episodes, inability to exclude malignancy
- Shared decision-making; individualize based on frequency, severity, patient factors

### Diverticular Bleeding — Management

See Lower GI Bleed section. Diverticulosis is the most common cause of major LGIB.

## Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

### Rome IV Diagnostic Criteria

Recurrent abdominal pain at least 1 day per week for the last 3 months, with symptom onset at least 6 months prior, associated with 2 or more of:
1. Related to defecation
2. Associated with a change in stool frequency
3. Associated with a change in stool form/appearance

No structural or biochemical abnormality to explain symptoms.

### IBS Subtypes (Bristol Stool Form Scale)

- IBS-C (constipation-predominant): >25% hard/lumpy stools; <25% loose/watery
- IBS-D (diarrhea-predominant): >25% loose/watery stools; <25% hard/lumpy
- IBS-M (mixed): >25% hard AND >25% loose/watery stools
- IBS-U (unclassified): does not meet criteria for C, D, or M

### Alarm Features (Warrant Investigation)

- Age >50 with new symptoms; rectal bleeding; nocturnal symptoms; weight loss; family history of CRC/IBD; abnormal labs (anemia, elevated CRP)

### Treatment — IBS

#### IBS-C (Constipation-Predominant)

| Agent | Mechanism | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Lubiprostone | Chloride channel (CIC-2) activator | 8 mcg BID; FDA-approved IBS-C |
| Linaclotide | GC-C agonist (increases intestinal secretion + reduces visceral afferent pain) | 290 mcg daily before meals; FDA-approved IBS-C |
| Plecanatide | GC-C agonist | 3 mg daily; FDA-approved IBS-C |
| Tenapanor | NHE3 inhibitor (reduces sodium absorption, increases secretion) | 50 mg BID before meals; FDA-approved IBS-C |
| PEG laxative | Osmotic laxative | Off-label; helps constipation, minimal effect on pain |
| Antispasmodics | Hyoscine, dicyclomine | Abdominal cramping/pain |
| Low-FODMAP diet | Reduces fermentable oligosaccharides/disaccharides/monosaccharides/polyols | Effective for bloating, overall IBS symptoms; requires dietitian |
| TCAs (low dose) | Amitriptyline 10-25 mg | Slows gut transit; analgesic; especially for IBS-C with pain |

#### IBS-D (Diarrhea-Predominant)

| Agent | Mechanism | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Loperamide | Opioid mu receptor agonist | Reduces stool frequency; does NOT improve pain |
| Rifaximin | Nonsystemic antibiotic (targets gut microbiome) | 550 mg TID x 14 days; FDA-approved IBS-D; re-treatment effective |
| Alosetron | 5-HT3 antagonist | 0.5-1 mg BID; FDA-approved IBS-D women only (REMS program due to ischemic colitis risk) |
| Eluxadoline | Mixed mu/kappa opioid agonist + delta antagonist | 75-100 mg BID; FDA-approved IBS-D; avoid in no gallbladder (pancreatitis risk) |
| TCAs (low dose) | Amitriptyline, nortriptyline | Slows transit, analgesic; particularly useful in IBS-D |
| Low-FODMAP diet | Reduces fermentable substrates | Effective across IBS subtypes |
| Peppermint oil | Antispasmodic (calcium channel antagonist in smooth muscle) | Enteric-coated capsule; reduces pain/bloating |

#### IBS-M (Mixed) and General IBS

| Approach | Notes |
|---|---|
| Low-FODMAP diet | Most evidence-based dietary intervention for all subtypes |
| Gut-directed psychotherapy (CBT, hypnotherapy) | Strong evidence; particularly for refractory IBS |
| SSRIs | Useful when IBS-D predominant with anxiety/depression comorbidity |
| TCAs | Useful across subtypes for pain; dose 10-50 mg (lower than antidepressant dose) |
| Probiotics | Modest benefit; specific strains (Bifidobacterium, Lactobacillus); evidence variable |
| Fiber (psyllium) | Soluble fiber helps IBS-C; insoluble fiber may worsen IBS |

## Liver Function Test (LFT) Interpretation

### Standard LFT Panel

| Test | Measures |
|---|---|
| AST (aspartate aminotransferase) | Hepatocellular damage (also skeletal muscle, cardiac) |
| ALT (alanine aminotransferase) | Hepatocellular damage (liver-specific) |
| ALP (alkaline phosphatase) | Cholestasis; biliary disease; bone |
| GGT (gamma-glutamyl transferase) | Biliary/cholestatic disease; alcohol; induced enzymes |
| Total bilirubin (direct + indirect) | Hepatic excretion; hemolysis (indirect); biliary obstruction (direct) |
| Albumin | Hepatic synthetic function; long half-life (~20 days) |
| PT/INR | Hepatic synthetic function; coagulation factors (shorter half-life) |
| LDH | Non-specific; hemolysis, liver, muscle, malignancy |

### Pattern Recognition — R Ratio

R ratio = (ALT/ULN) divided by (ALP/ULN)

Where ULN = upper limit of normal for each test.

| R Ratio | Pattern | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| >5 | Hepatocellular | Liver cell injury predominates |
| <2 | Cholestatic | Bile flow obstruction predominates |
| 2-5 | Mixed | Both hepatocellular and cholestatic components |

### Hepatocellular Pattern (Elevated AST/ALT)

| Cause | Key Features |
|---|---|
| Viral hepatitis (acute A, B, C, E) | Markedly elevated ALT (>500); check hepatitis serologies |
| Alcoholic hepatitis | AST:ALT ratio >2:1; AST rarely >300; GGT elevated |
| Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD/MASLD) | Mild-moderate elevation (1-3x); metabolic risk factors; ultrasound |
| Drug-induced liver injury (DILI) | Temporal relationship with drug; broad pattern |
| Ischemic hepatitis ("shock liver") | Massive elevation (>1000); hypoperfusion; rapid resolution |
| Autoimmune hepatitis | Young women; ANA, anti-smooth muscle antibody; IgG elevated |
| Wilson's disease | Young patient; Kayser-Fleischer rings; ceruloplasmin low |
| Hemochromatosis | Elevated iron studies, ferritin; HFE gene mutation |
| Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency | Low A1AT; PiZZ phenotype |
| Celiac disease | Mild elevation; tTG-IgA testing |
| Thyroid disease (hypo/hyper) | Check TSH |
| Muscle injury | Elevated CK; AST > ALT; normal GGT |

### Cholestatic Pattern (Elevated ALP, Bilirubin)

| Cause | Key Features |
|---|---|
| Choledocholithiasis | Sudden onset; biliary colic; RUQ ultrasound |
| Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC) | Beaded biliary ducts on MRCP; IBD association (70% UC) |
| Primary biliary cholangitis (PBC) | Middle-aged women; anti-mitochondrial antibody (AMA) >95% sensitive; ALP elevated |
| Intrahepatic cholestasis of pregnancy | Third trimester; elevated bile acids; resolves postpartum |
| Drug-induced cholestasis | Anabolic steroids, estrogens, amoxicillin-clavulanate, phenothiazines |
| Biliary stricture (post-op, PSC, malignant) | MRCP or ERCP for characterization |
| Malignant biliary obstruction | Pancreatic cancer, cholangiocarcinoma; painless jaundice; CT/MRCP |
| Hepatic sarcoidosis | Noncaseating granulomas; elevated ALP; chest findings |
| Infiltrative liver disease | Lymphoma, TB, amyloid; diffuse hepatomegaly; liver biopsy |

### Isolated Elevated Bilirubin

| Pattern | Cause |
|---|---|
| Elevated indirect (unconjugated) bilirubin, normal LFTs | Hemolysis; Gilbert syndrome (common, benign — UGT1A1 variant) |
| Elevated direct (conjugated) bilirubin | Hepatocellular disease; biliary obstruction; Dubin-Johnson syndrome (rare, benign) |

### Hepatic Synthetic Function

- Albumin: low albumin indicates chronic liver dysfunction or malnutrition; NOT elevated in acute liver failure (long half-life)
- INR/PT: prolongation reflects severity of acute or chronic liver failure; more sensitive to acute changes
- Both required for Child-Pugh score and MELD score (model for end-stage liver disease)

### MELD-Na Score (Model for End-Stage Liver Disease — Sodium)

MELD-Na = MELD + 1.32(137-Na) - [0.24 x MELD x (137-Na)]
Where MELD = 3.78(ln bilirubin mg/dL) + 11.2(ln INR) + 9.57(ln creatinine) + 6.43

- Used for liver transplant prioritization
- Score >15: benefit from transplant exceeds surgical risk
- Score >40: very high 3-month mortality
