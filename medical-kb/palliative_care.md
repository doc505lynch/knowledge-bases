# Palliative Care: Principles, Symptom Management, and End-of-Life Care

## Palliative Care Principles

### Definition and Scope

Palliative care is specialized medical care focused on providing relief from the symptoms, pain, and stress of serious illness. The World Health Organization (WHO) defines palliative care as "an approach that improves the quality of life of patients and their families facing the problems associated with life-threatening illness, through the prevention and relief of suffering by means of early identification and impeccable assessment and treatment of pain and other problems, physical, psychosocial and spiritual."

Key features of the WHO definition:
- Provides relief from pain and other distressing symptoms
- Affirms life and regards dying as a normal process
- Intends neither to hasten nor postpone death
- Integrates the psychological and spiritual aspects of patient care
- Offers a support system to help patients live as actively as possible until death
- Offers a support system to help the family cope during the patient's illness and bereavement
- Uses a team approach to address the needs of patients and their families
- Will enhance quality of life and may also positively influence the course of illness
- Applicable early in the course of illness, in conjunction with other therapies intended to prolong life

### Palliative Care vs. Hospice: Key Distinctions

| Feature | Palliative Care | Hospice |
|---|---|---|
| Prognosis requirement | None — any stage, any diagnosis | Prognosis of 6 months or less |
| Curative treatment | Can continue concurrently | Forfeited under Medicare Hospice Benefit |
| Setting | Hospital, clinic, home, nursing facility | Home, residential hospice, nursing facility |
| Payer | Standard insurance/Medicare Part A or B | Medicare Hospice Benefit (per diem) |
| Timing | Any point in illness trajectory | Terminal phase only |
| Goals | Symptom relief alongside disease treatment | Comfort-focused only |
| Team structure | Variable — palliative consult team | Interdisciplinary team required |

### Disease-Modifying vs. Comfort-Directed Care Continuum

The care continuum model rejects the false dichotomy between curative and palliative care. Instead, the intensity of each component shifts over time:

- Early illness: heavy disease-modifying treatment, light palliative/supportive care
- Middle course: balanced disease-directed and symptom-focused care
- Advanced illness: decreasing disease-directed therapy, increasing palliative focus
- Terminal phase: comfort-directed care only (hospice)

This model supports concurrent palliative care with curative treatment as standard of practice rather than a last resort.

### Evidence Base: Concurrent Palliative Care with Curative Treatment

The landmark NEJM 2010 Temel study (Early Palliative Care for Patients with Metastatic Non-Small-Cell Lung Cancer) demonstrated:
- Population: patients with newly diagnosed metastatic NSCLC
- Intervention: early integration of outpatient palliative care alongside standard oncologic care
- Results:
  - Better quality of life (FACT-L scores significantly higher)
  - Less aggressive end-of-life care (fewer chemotherapy infusions in last 14 days, less ICU use)
  - Longer median survival: 11.6 months vs. 8.9 months despite less aggressive treatment
  - Less depression (HADS scores lower)
- Significance: demonstrated that comfort-focused care does not shorten life and may extend it through reduced treatment toxicity, better symptom management, and earlier transitions to appropriate care

This study changed practice guidelines. ASCO now recommends early palliative care integration for patients with advanced solid tumors.

### Referral Triggers for Palliative Care Consultation

Automatic or strong referral indications:
- Serious illness with high symptom burden at any stage
- Stage IV solid malignancy or hematologic malignancy not in remission
- Frequent hospitalizations (3+ in 12 months for same condition)
- ICU admission lasting more than 7 days with uncertain prognosis
- CHF NYHA Class III-IV despite optimal medical therapy
- COPD GOLD Stage III-IV (FEV1 < 50% predicted, severe-very severe)
- End-stage renal disease on dialysis or choosing not to pursue dialysis
- End-stage liver disease (Child-Pugh Class C or MELD score > 20)
- Dementia with recurrent infections, aspiration, or inability to perform ADLs
- ALS or progressive neurological illness with functional decline
- HIV/AIDS with AIDS-defining illness and poor functional status
- Request for information about hospice, advance directives, or goals of care
- Patient or family expressing distress, existential suffering, or need for support
- Any patient for whom the clinician would "not be surprised" if they died within 12 months (the Surprise Question)

## Communication Skills in Palliative Care

### SPIKES Protocol for Delivering Bad News

SPIKES is a six-step protocol for delivering serious news in a structured, compassionate manner.

**S — Setting**
- Arrange a private setting free of interruption
- Seat the patient at eye level (avoid standing over them)
- Have key family members or support persons present if patient wishes
- Silence pagers and phones
- Allow adequate time (do not rush)

**P — Perception**
- Assess what the patient already knows or understands
- Use open-ended questions: "What have your doctors told you so far about your illness?" or "What is your understanding of where things stand with your health?"
- Calibrates your communication to their baseline; identifies misconceptions

**I — Invitation**
- Assess how much information the patient wants to know
- Ask: "Are you the kind of person who wants to know all the details of your situation, or would you prefer I give you a general overview?"
- Some patients want all facts; others prefer information parceled out or given to family first
- Respect patient autonomy and cultural preferences

**K — Knowledge**
- Deliver information in plain language, avoiding medical jargon
- Give a "warning shot" before delivering serious news: "I have some difficult news to share with you"
- Deliver in small chunks; pause frequently to allow processing
- Avoid false reassurance ("everything will be fine") and excessive negativity
- State the facts clearly: diagnosis, what it means, what comes next
- Verify understanding: "Can you tell me in your own words what you understood from what I just said?"

**E — Emotions / Empathy**
- Respond to the emotional reaction before proceeding to medical information
- Acknowledge, name, and validate the emotion: "I can see this is devastating news. It makes complete sense that you feel this way."
- Exploratory empathic response (NURSE): Name, Understand, Respect, Support, Explore
- Allow silence; do not rush past emotional expression to medical detail
- Most patients cannot absorb further medical information when in acute emotional distress

**S — Strategy and Summary**
- Offer a clear plan and next steps
- Summarize what was discussed
- Invite questions: "What questions do you have right now?"
- Arrange follow-up: "I'd like to meet with you again in a few days when you've had a chance to process this"
- Document the conversation in detail

### Serious Illness Conversations: REMAP Framework

REMAP is a five-step framework for goals of care and illness trajectory conversations:

**R — Reframe why the conversation is happening**
- Link the conversation to the patient's clinical situation
- "Your situation has changed, and I want to make sure we're on the same page about what's ahead"
- Sets context without immediately delivering catastrophic framing

**E — Expect emotion and empathize**
- Pause after reframing; emotions will emerge
- Respond before moving forward
- Use reflective listening: "It sounds like you've been carrying a lot of worry about this"

**M — Map the future**
- Elicit what the patient understands about their likely illness course
- "What is your understanding of what's likely to happen with your illness over the next few months?"
- Follow up: "What do you hope for? What are your fears?"
- Identify specific concerns: fear of pain, being a burden, dying alone, not having affairs in order

**A — Align with values**
- Explore what matters most: "What is most important to you right now?"
- Explore functional thresholds: "If you could no longer [important activity], how would that change how you think about treatment?"
- Quality vs. quantity of life: "Some people tell me that living longer is most important, even if that means more time in the hospital or with treatment side effects. Others tell me that how they feel day to day matters more than how long they live. What feels true for you?"
- Identify unfinished business, important milestones

**P — Plan and propose treatments matching goals**
- Translate values into medical recommendations
- "Based on what you've told me — that being at home and feeling as well as possible matters most — here is what I would recommend..."
- Present a concrete plan rather than asking the patient to choose between options they don't have technical expertise to evaluate
- Document and communicate across care team

### Best Case / Worst Case / Most Likely Framework

A structured approach for presenting treatment decisions under uncertainty:

- Best case: what could happen if everything goes well
- Worst case: what could happen if things go poorly
- Most likely: what most patients in this situation actually experience

Application:
- Gives patients a realistic range without requiring the clinician to make a single prognostic statement
- Allows patients to frame decisions against their own values and risk tolerance
- Particularly useful for surgical decisions or ICU admission decisions in seriously ill patients

### Handling Prognostic Uncertainty

Clinicians often avoid prognosis because they fear being wrong. Key principles:
- Acknowledge uncertainty honestly: "I don't know exactly how long you have, and I would not want to mislead you"
- Provide a range rather than a point estimate: "Most people in your situation live weeks to a few months, though some people do better or worse than expected"
- Redirect from time to what to expect: "I can't tell you exactly how long, but I can tell you what we typically see happening — what would be most useful for you to know?"
- Discuss trajectory: "Your condition is likely to progress; we want to make sure you have time to be at home, be with family, and do the things that matter most to you"
- Never give false hope; never strip away all hope: hope for a cure may shift to hope for a good day, a special occasion, a peaceful death

## Goals of Care

### Conducting Goals of Care Conversations

Core domains to explore:
- Understanding: "What is your understanding of where you are with your illness?"
- Hopes: "What are you hoping for? What would a good outcome look like to you?"
- Fears: "What are you most afraid of? What would be the hardest thing for you?"
- What matters most: "What gives your life meaning and purpose? What do you want to make sure happens?"
- Values: "How do you think about the trade-off between living longer and feeling as well as possible?"
- Functional thresholds: "Is there a condition you can imagine where life would no longer be worth living for you, or would not be the kind of life you'd want to extend?"
- Practical concerns: "Are there things you need to take care of, people you need to talk to, places you want to see?"

### Surrogate Decision-Making

Hierarchy when patient lacks decision-making capacity:
1. Healthcare proxy / durable power of attorney for healthcare (DPAHC) — legally designated
2. Next of kin hierarchy (varies by state): spouse, adult children, parents, adult siblings
3. Court-appointed guardian

Standards for surrogate decision-making:
- Substituted judgment standard: "What would the patient want?" — not what the surrogate wants for the patient, but what the patient themselves would choose
- Best interest standard: applied when patient preferences are unknown; "What would a reasonable person in this situation want?"

Common pitfalls:
- Surrogate experiencing grief, guilt, or family conflict may request more aggressive treatment than the patient would want
- Surrogates need explicit permission to choose comfort over life-prolongation
- Frame as honoring the patient's values, not "giving up" or "withdrawing care"

Clinician role:
- Support surrogates; do not burden them with decisions alone
- Offer recommendations: "Based on what you've told me about your mother, and given what we're seeing medically, my recommendation is..."
- Document conversations thoroughly

### Advance Directives

**Living will:**
- Document expressing patient's wishes about specific treatments (mechanical ventilation, CPR, artificial nutrition) if incapacitated
- Advisory only — not a medical order
- Highly variable by state in legal weight and format
- Encourages advance care planning conversations; may not anticipate every clinical scenario

**Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare (DPAHC):**
- Legal document designating a healthcare proxy
- Gives the proxy authority to make medical decisions if patient lacks capacity
- More flexible than living will — applies to unanticipated scenarios
- Does not require physician signature; notarized or witnessed per state law

### POLST: Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment

Portable medical order form — distinct from an advance directive:
- Is a physician/provider-signed medical order (legally actionable by EMS, nursing staff)
- Designed for patients with serious illness, frailty, or advanced age
- Portable across care settings (home, hospital, nursing facility)
- Covers:
  - CPR preference (attempt resuscitation vs. do not attempt resuscitation)
  - Level of medical intervention (comfort only vs. limited interventions vs. full treatment)
  - Artificial nutrition (trial period vs. long-term vs. no artificial nutrition)
- Must be readily accessible (on refrigerator in home setting per state protocol)
- Not appropriate for healthy individuals (that is the role of advance directives)
- Requires conversation with patient/surrogate to complete — not a checkbox form

| Document | Type | Who Signs | Legal Mechanism | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Living will | Advisory | Patient | Advance directive statute | Healthy adults planning ahead |
| DPAHC | Advisory / Legal authority | Patient | Power of attorney statute | Healthy adults designating proxy |
| POLST | Medical order | Provider + patient/surrogate | Medical orders | Seriously ill, frail, advanced age |
| DNR order | Medical order | Provider | Standard orders | Inpatient setting |

## Hospice

### Eligibility Criteria

- Prognosis of 6 months or less if disease follows its natural course
- Two-physician certification required (attending + hospice medical director)
- Any diagnosis (not limited to cancer)
- Patient must elect to forgo curative/disease-modifying treatment for the terminal diagnosis under Medicare Hospice Benefit (can continue unrelated treatments)
- Patient must elect hospice voluntarily and can revoke at any time

### Medicare Hospice Benefit

- Structured as a benefit period: two 90-day periods followed by unlimited 60-day periods
- Renewable at each period if attending and hospice medical director re-certify prognosis of 6 months or less
- Per diem payment covers all hospice-related services:
  - Medications related to the terminal diagnosis
  - Durable medical equipment (hospital bed, wheelchair, commode, oxygen)
  - Nursing visits (frequency based on need)
  - Home health aide visits
  - Social worker visits
  - Chaplain/spiritual care
  - Volunteer services
  - Bereavement support for family for 13 months after death
- Four levels of hospice care:
  - Routine home care (most common): patient at home or nursing facility
  - Continuous home care (crisis care): intensive nursing for acute symptom management at home (8+ hours/day)
  - Inpatient respite care: short-term (up to 5 days) inpatient stay for caregiver relief
  - General inpatient care (GIP): inpatient admission for acute symptom management not manageable at home

### Interdisciplinary Hospice Team

Required by Medicare CoPs:
- Registered nurse (care coordinator, primary clinical contact)
- Physician (hospice medical director + attending)
- Social worker (psychosocial assessment, resource coordination, family support)
- Chaplain (spiritual care, non-denominational)
- Home health aide (personal care, hygiene)
- Volunteer (companionship, errands, caregiver respite)
- Bereavement counselor (supports family for 13 months post-death)

### NHPCO Disease-Specific Prognosis Guidelines

#### Cancer
- ECOG Performance Status 3 or greater (limited self-care, in bed >50% of waking hours)
- OR Karnofsky Performance Scale (KPS) 50 or less
- Weight loss > 10% of baseline body weight over 6 months
- Disease progression despite treatment or decision to stop treatment
- Hypercalcemia, pleural effusion, ascites indicating advanced disease
- Metastatic disease with poor performance status

#### Congestive Heart Failure
- NYHA Class IV symptoms at rest despite optimal medical therapy
- Ejection fraction < 20% (when available)
- Persistent symptoms despite ACE inhibitor/ARB + beta-blocker + diuretic at maximally tolerated doses
- Treatment-refractory arrhythmias
- History of cardiac arrest/resuscitation or unexplained syncope
- Cardiogenic brain embolism
- Concomitant HIV disease

#### COPD
- FEV1 < 30% predicted (GOLD Stage IV — very severe)
- Dyspnea at rest or with minimal exertion (MRC Grade 4-5)
- Cor pulmonale or right heart failure secondary to pulmonary disease
- Unintentional weight loss > 10% over 6 months
- Resting tachycardia > 100 bpm
- More than 3 hospitalizations in 12 months for acute exacerbations
- Hypoxemia at rest on supplemental oxygen (pO2 < 55 mmHg or SaO2 < 88%)
- Hypercapnia (pCO2 > 50 mmHg)
- Right heart failure secondary to pulmonary disease

#### Dementia
- Unable to ambulate without assistance
- Unable to dress or bathe independently
- Urinary and fecal incontinence
- Unable to speak meaningfully (< 6 intelligible words in a day)
- Recurrent infections: aspiration pneumonia, pyelonephritis, septicemia
- Inability to maintain adequate oral intake: malnutrition, weight loss
- Stage 7 on FAST (Functional Assessment Staging Tool)
- Modified: lost all intelligible speech and all ability to ambulate

#### ALS and Neurological Disease
- Respiratory insufficiency: vital capacity < 30% of predicted
- Significant dyspnea at rest
- Rapid progression of ALS (diagnosis to severe deficit within 12 months)
- Critically impaired nutritional status: weight loss, inability to maintain oral intake without assisted feeding
- Complications: aspiration, pneumonia, sepsis

#### Renal Failure (Not on Dialysis or Discontinuing Dialysis)
- Creatinine clearance < 15 mL/min (estimated)
- Serum creatinine > 8.0 mg/dL (or > 6.0 mg/dL in diabetics)
- Serum albumin < 3.5 g/dL
- Uremic symptoms: confusion, nausea/vomiting, pruritus
- Patient choosing not to pursue dialysis or discontinuing dialysis

#### Liver Disease
- Prothrombin time > 5 seconds above control (INR typically > 1.5)
- Serum albumin < 2.5 g/dL
- End-stage cirrhosis symptoms: ascites refractory to sodium restriction and diuretics
- Hepatic encephalopathy (HE): spontaneous or precipitated
- Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP)
- Hepatorenal syndrome (HRS)
- Recurrent variceal bleeding despite treatment
- Child-Pugh Class C (score 10-15)

## Pain Management in Palliative Care

### Assessment

Pain assessment principles:
- Patient self-report is the gold standard
- Numeric Rating Scale (NRS): 0-10; 0 = no pain, 10 = worst imaginable
- FACES scale for cognitive impairment
- PAINAD (Pain Assessment in Advanced Dementia) for non-verbal patients
- Characterize: location, quality, radiation, severity, temporal pattern (constant vs. breakthrough), aggravating and relieving factors, functional impact
- Distinguish nociceptive (somatic, visceral) from neuropathic pain — guides adjuvant selection
- Assess for total pain (physical, emotional, social, spiritual components)

### WHO Analgesic Ladder

Three-step framework for progressive pain management:

**Step 1 — Mild pain (NRS 1-3): Non-opioid analgesics**
- Acetaminophen: 650-1000 mg PO q6h; maximum 4 g/day (3 g/day in liver disease, elderly, frailty); available as IV for patients unable to take PO
- NSAIDs: ibuprofen 400-800 mg PO q8h, naproxen 500 mg PO q12h; COX-2 inhibitors (celecoxib); avoid in renal failure, GI bleeding risk, cardiovascular disease; use lowest effective dose shortest duration
- Adjuvants at any step: antidepressants, anticonvulsants, corticosteroids, bisphosphonates (bone pain)

**Step 2 — Moderate pain (NRS 4-6): Weak opioids or low-dose strong opioids**
- Codeine: 30-60 mg PO q4-6h; prodrug requires CYP2D6 conversion to morphine; avoid in ultra-rapid metabolizers (toxicity), poor metabolizers (no effect), children, renal failure; ceiling dose due to constipation and toxicity at higher doses
- Tramadol: 50-100 mg PO q6h; opioid + SNRI mechanism; avoid in seizure disorder, SSRI/SNRI combination (serotonin syndrome risk); limited ceiling due to seizure risk at > 400 mg/day
- Hydrocodone/acetaminophen combinations (limited by acetaminophen ceiling)
- Low-dose strong opioids now frequently substituted for Step 2 (morphine 5 mg q4h)

**Step 3 — Severe pain (NRS 7-10): Strong opioids**
- Morphine, oxycodone, hydromorphone, fentanyl, methadone
- No ceiling dose for pure opioid agonists (dose limited by side effects)
- Route preference: oral preferred; subcutaneous acceptable (avoid IM — painful, unpredictable absorption); IV for crisis management and rapid titration

### Opioid Reference: Individual Agents

**Morphine**
- Standard of reference for opioid equianalgesic conversions
- Oral (IR): 5-10 mg q4h in opioid-naive patients
- IV/SQ dose: one-third of oral dose (first-pass metabolism)
- Long-acting (SR) formulations: MS Contin, morphine sulfate ER q8-12h
- Active metabolite morphine-6-glucuronide (M6G) accumulates in renal failure — causes prolonged sedation and respiratory depression
- Avoid in renal failure; use hydromorphone instead
- Available as oral solution, tablets, suppositories, IV, SQ

**Oxycodone**
- Oral availability ~ 60-87% (better than morphine ~30%)
- IR: 5-10 mg q4h in opioid-naive
- SR: OxyContin 10-20 mg q12h
- No IV formulation available
- Less metabolite accumulation than morphine; can use cautiously in mild-moderate renal impairment
- Metabolized by CYP2D6 and CYP3A4 — drug interactions with inhibitors/inducers

**Hydromorphone (Dilaudid)**
- Oral IR: 1.5-4 mg q4h
- IV/SQ: 0.2-0.4 mg q4h (approximately 5:1 oral to parenteral ratio)
- More potent than morphine (equianalgesic: oral hydromorphone 7.5 mg = oral morphine 30 mg)
- Preferred in renal failure — less metabolite accumulation than morphine
- Available as oral tablets, liquid, suppository, IV, SQ

**Fentanyl Transdermal Patch**
- Highly lipophilic — absorbed through skin into fat depot, then slowly released
- Takes 12-17 hours to reach steady state after first patch application; takes 17+ hours to decline after removal
- Conversion: total 24-hour oral morphine equivalent (mg) divided by 2 = approximate patch strength in mcg/hr
- Example: 60 mg oral morphine/24h → 30 mcg/hr fentanyl patch
- Change patch every 72 hours (some patients every 48 hours due to variable absorption)
- Not appropriate for opioid-naive patients; start at lowest available strength (12 mcg/hr)
- Reduce dose by 50% in cachexia (reduced fat stores → unpredictable absorption)
- Fever increases absorption — monitor for increased opioid effect
- Remove patch before MRI (some formulations have metallic backing)
- Useful for patients unable to tolerate oral medications; avoid in acute, rapidly changing pain

**Methadone**
- Complex pharmacokinetics: highly variable equianalgesic ratio to morphine
  - Low morphine doses (30-90 mg/day): morphine:methadone ratio approximately 3:1 to 4:1
  - Moderate morphine doses (90-300 mg/day): ratio approximately 6:1 to 8:1
  - High morphine doses (> 300 mg/day): ratio approximately 10:1 to 12:1
- Long and unpredictable half-life (8-59 hours) — risk of accumulation and delayed toxicity
- NMDA receptor antagonism: useful for neuropathic pain and opioid rotation
- QTc prolongation: baseline and periodic ECG required; avoid with other QT-prolonging drugs
- Major drug interactions via CYP3A4 and CYP2D6 — rifampin, antifungals, antiretrovirals
- Should be initiated and titrated by experienced palliative care, pain, or addiction specialist
- Controlled substance; additional regulatory requirements for outpatient prescribing in some states

### Equianalgesic Conversion Table

| Opioid | Oral Dose | IV/SQ Dose |
|---|---|---|
| Morphine | 30 mg | 10 mg |
| Oxycodone | 20 mg | N/A (no IV formulation) |
| Hydromorphone | 7.5 mg | 1.5 mg |
| Oxymorphone | 10 mg | 1 mg |
| Codeine | 200 mg | N/A |
| Fentanyl transdermal | — | 12-15 mcg/hr patch = oral morphine 30-45 mg/day |

Notes on equianalgesic table:
- These are approximate conversions — individual patient variation is significant
- Always reduce calculated equivalent dose by 25-50% when rotating opioids (incomplete cross-tolerance)
- Re-titrate upward based on patient response
- Methadone conversions are dose-dependent and require specialist guidance

### Titration Principles

**Scheduled vs. Breakthrough Dosing**
- Chronic pain: scheduled (long-acting or regular short-acting) + breakthrough (PRN short-acting)
- Never use only PRN dosing for persistent pain — results in undertreated pain and demand-driven escalation
- Breakthrough (rescue) dose: 10-15% of the total 24-hour opioid dose; available q1h (oral IR) to q4h depending on formulation and route
- Example: total daily oral morphine 60 mg → breakthrough dose 6-9 mg oral morphine q1-4h PRN

**Dose Escalation**
- If patient uses 3 or more breakthrough doses in a 24-hour period: increase scheduled dose
- Method: total 24-hour opioid use (scheduled + all PRN doses used) → calculate new scheduled dose from that total; divide appropriately for formulation/frequency
- Example: scheduled 20 mg oral morphine q4h = 120 mg/day; patient uses 4 breakthrough doses of 15 mg = 60 mg additional; new total = 180 mg/day; new scheduled dose = 30 mg q4h

**Continuous Infusion Titration**
- Increase continuous infusion rate by 25-50% if ongoing uncontrolled pain
- Rescue (bolus) dose for continuous infusion: 25-50% of the hourly infusion rate
  - IV route: bolus available q15 minutes
  - SQ route: bolus available q30-60 minutes
- Reassess pain control after each dose change; adjust breakthrough based on new scheduled rate
- Reassess frequency: every 4 hours in acute or crisis settings; daily in stable hospice patients

### Opioid Rotation

Indications:
- Inadequate pain control despite dose escalation
- Intolerable side effects (cognitive impairment, myoclonus, hyperalgesia)
- Route change required (patient no longer able to swallow)
- Drug interaction

Procedure:
1. Calculate total 24-hour dose of current opioid
2. Convert to equianalgesic dose of new opioid using conversion table
3. Reduce the calculated new dose by 25-50% to account for incomplete cross-tolerance
4. Establish new breakthrough dose at 10-15% of the reduced scheduled dose
5. Re-titrate based on patient response; reassess frequently

### Opioid Side Effects and Management

**Constipation**
- Universal with opioid use — does not diminish with time (unlike nausea, sedation)
- Prophylactic bowel regimen mandatory at opioid initiation; do not wait for symptoms
- First-line: stimulant laxative + softener
  - Senna 2 tablets twice daily (stimulant)
  - Docusate sodium 100-200 mg twice daily (softener) — the "senna-docusate rule"
  - Note: docusate alone is insufficient; needs stimulant component
- Escalation options:
  - Bisacodyl 10 mg PO or rectal suppository
  - Polyethylene glycol (MiraLax) 17 g daily
  - Lactulose 15-30 mL BID-QID (osmotic; avoid in severe debility due to gas and cramping)
  - Magnesium hydroxide (Milk of Magnesia)
- Refractory opioid-induced constipation (OIC):
  - Methylnaltrexone (Relistor) SQ: peripheral mu-opioid receptor antagonist; does not cross BBB — no effect on central analgesia; indicated for OIC when laxatives inadequate
  - Naloxegol (Movantik) PO: peripherally acting mu-opioid receptor antagonist
  - Lubiprostone (Amitiza): chloride channel activator; softens stool by increasing intestinal fluid
  - Naldemedine (Symproic)

**Nausea**
- Common at opioid initiation; typically resolves within 7-10 days
- Mechanisms: central (chemoreceptor trigger zone), vestibular, gastric motility
- Management by mechanism:
  - CTZ/central: haloperidol 0.5-1 mg PO/SQ q6-8h (most effective for opioid-induced nausea); prochlorperazine 5-10 mg q6-8h
  - Motility: metoclopramide 10 mg PO/IV/SQ q6h (prokinetic — avoid in complete bowel obstruction)
  - Vestibular: scopolamine transdermal patch; meclizine 12.5-25 mg q6-8h
  - Broad: ondansetron (5-HT3 antagonist) 4-8 mg q8h; dexamethasone 4-8 mg daily
- Reassess at 7-10 days — if resolved, taper antiemetic

**Sedation**
- Common at opioid initiation and with dose escalation; typically improves over days
- Distinguish opioid sedation from disease progression, hypercalcemia, hyponatremia, brain metastases
- Management:
  - Reassurance and time (if new opioid start or recent increase)
  - Reduce opioid dose if pain controlled (address side effect without sacrificing analgesia)
  - Methylphenidate (Ritalin) 2.5-5 mg PO at 8 AM and noon — psychostimulant; improves opioid-induced sedation, fatigue, and depression; avoid evening dosing (insomnia)
  - Dextroamphetamine 2.5-5 mg PO AM
  - Modafinil (Provigil) 100-200 mg PO AM

**Pruritus**
- Less common with oral/SQ opioids than with neuraxial (epidural/intrathecal) opioids
- Management:
  - Antihistamines: diphenhydramine 25-50 mg q6h (limited evidence; add sedation)
  - Opioid rotation to different mu agonist
  - Low-dose naloxone infusion (0.25 mcg/kg/hr) — reverses pruritus without reversing analgesia
  - Ondansetron 4-8 mg q8h
  - Nalbuphine (kappa agonist / mu partial antagonist) 2.5-5 mg IV q6h

**Opioid-Induced Delirium and Neurotoxicity**
- Opioid rotation is first-line (change to different opioid at reduced equianalgesic dose)
- Ensure adequate hydration (dehydration concentrates active metabolites)
- Haloperidol 0.5-2 mg PO/SQ/IV q6-8h for agitation or delirium
- Reduce opioid dose if pain adequately controlled
- Evaluate for concurrent causes: infection, metabolic derangement, drug interactions, brain metastases

**Respiratory Depression**
- Uncommon with appropriate titration; respiratory drive returns before consciousness
- Significant respiratory depression from opioids: naloxone 0.04-0.4 mg IV/IM q2-3 min (dilute 0.4 mg in 10 mL NS; titrate 0.04 mg aliquots to effect in opioid-tolerant patients to avoid precipitating withdrawal and acute pain crisis)
- In hospice patients: do not automatically reverse opioids for slower respiratory rate — assess level of consciousness, comfort; slower rate may be normal dying process

## Dyspnea Management

### Overview

Dyspnea is one of the most distressing symptoms at end of life, affecting up to 70% of patients with advanced cancer or COPD and 60% of patients with CHF. It is a subjective experience; objective measurements (SaO2, respiratory rate) do not reliably correlate with patient-reported severity.

### Pathophysiology and Causes

- Hypoxemia (SaO2 < 90%): pulmonary parenchymal disease, pleural effusion, anemia, pulmonary embolism
- Hypercarbia: ventilatory failure in COPD, neuromuscular disease
- Pulmonary edema: CHF, hypoalbuminemia
- Pleural effusion: malignancy, CHF, infection
- Airway obstruction: bronchospasm, tumor, tracheal compression
- Pericardial effusion with tamponade
- Massive ascites (diaphragm elevation)
- Anxiety and panic (dyspnea-anxiety cycle)
- Deconditioning and weakness
- Anemia
- Pain (splinting, poor respiratory effort)

### Pharmacological Management

**Opioids — first-line pharmacological treatment for dyspnea**
- Most evidence-based pharmacological treatment for dyspnea in palliative care
- Mechanism: reduces ventilatory drive and the sensation of air hunger; reduces respiratory muscle effort
- Evidence consistently demonstrates opioids relieve dyspnea without accelerating death when dosed appropriately
- Opioid-naive: morphine 2-4 mg PO/SQ q4h scheduled; 2 mg SQ q1h PRN for breakthrough
- Opioid-tolerant: increase total dose by 25% for dyspnea management
- IV/SQ infusion for continuous dyspnea: morphine 1-2 mg/hr continuous with 1-2 mg bolus q15-30 min PRN
- Other opioids equivalent at equianalgesic doses

**Benzodiazepines — for anxiety component of dyspnea**
- Dyspnea triggers anxiety; anxiety worsens dyspnea (positive feedback cycle)
- Not first-line as monotherapy for dyspnea (opioids superior)
- Additive benefit when combined with opioids for refractory dyspnea
- Lorazepam (Ativan): 0.5-1 mg SL/SQ/IV q1-4h; fast-acting
- Midazolam: 1-2 mg SQ/IV q1h PRN (crisis dyspnea); 1-5 mg/hr continuous SQ/IV for refractory terminal dyspnea
- Diazepam 2-5 mg PO q6-8h for baseline anxiety with dyspnea

**Oxygen**
- Helpful only in hypoxic patients (SaO2 < 90%)
- For non-hypoxic dyspnea: no benefit over room air (multiple RCTs including ORIGINAL trial — oxygen vs. medical air; no difference in dyspnea relief in non-hypoxic patients)
- Fan blowing air on the face: effective for non-hypoxic dyspnea by stimulating trigeminal V2 branch (nasal mucosa cold receptors); can be as effective as supplemental oxygen
- In hospice: do not automatically continue home oxygen; reassess if patient is non-hypoxic; oxygen can interfere with family closeness at bedside

**Bronchodilators**
- Nebulized albuterol (2.5 mg q4-6h) for bronchospasm component
- Ipratropium for COPD exacerbation
- Systemic corticosteroids (dexamethasone 4-8 mg daily) for inflammatory airway obstruction, lymphangitic carcinomatosis, superior vena cava syndrome

### Death Rattle (Terminal Secretions)

- Audible gurgling respirations in dying patients due to pooled secretions in hypopharynx
- Occurs because patient lacks strength to clear secretions by coughing or swallowing
- Typically occurs in unconscious or obtunded patients — patient is generally not aware or distressed
- Distressing to family and bedside visitors — requires explanation and reassurance

Management:
- Anticipatory guidance for family: "The gurgling sound is caused by relaxed throat muscles. Your loved one is not choking or suffocating. They are not aware of this."
- Repositioning: semi-prone or lateral position may allow passive drainage of secretions
- Anticholinergic medications to reduce secretion production:
  - Glycopyrrolate 0.2 mg SQ q4h (preferred: does not cross blood-brain barrier — no CNS side effects, no added sedation)
  - Scopolamine (hyoscine) 0.4 mg SQ q4h or transdermal patch (crosses BBB — may cause confusion, sedation)
  - Hyoscine butylbromide (where available)
- Oropharyngeal suctioning: rarely helpful; often distressing; mobilizes secretions upward temporarily but secretions replenish; avoid in comfort-focused care
- Atropine 1% ophthalmic drops sublingually (off-label): 1-2 drops SL q4h; rapid onset

## Nausea and Vomiting in Palliative Care

### Causes and Assessment

Common causes in palliative patients (assess before treating):
- Constipation: most common and most treatable cause — always assess and address first
- Opioid-induced: CTZ stimulation and delayed gastric emptying
- Bowel obstruction: malignant or non-malignant
- Increased intracranial pressure: brain metastases, leptomeningeal carcinomatosis
- Metabolic: hypercalcemia (cancer), hyponatremia, uremia, hepatic failure
- Medication-induced: NSAIDs, antibiotics, iron supplements, digoxin toxicity, chemotherapy
- Vestibular: motion sensitivity, opioid-induced vestibular stimulation
- Anxiety and psychological factors
- Gastroparesis: diabetes, opioid-related, paraneoplastic

### Antiemetic Selection by Mechanism

| Drug | Class | Mechanism | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Haloperidol | Dopamine antagonist | CTZ blockade | Opioid-induced, metabolic, general |
| Metoclopramide | Prokinetic / dopamine antagonist | Motility + CTZ | Gastroparesis, opioid motility |
| Ondansetron | 5-HT3 antagonist | Vagal/CTZ | Chemotherapy, post-radiation, post-op |
| Prochlorperazine | Phenothiazine | CTZ + vestibular | General nausea |
| Dexamethasone | Corticosteroid | ICP reduction, anti-inflammatory | Bowel obstruction, ICP, chemo |
| Scopolamine patch | Anticholinergic | Vestibular | Motion-related, vestibular component |
| Meclizine | Antihistamine | Vestibular | Vestibular component |
| Levomepromazine | Phenothiazine | Broad multimodal | Refractory nausea (CSCI pump) |
| Octreotide | Somatostatin analogue | Reduces GI secretions | Malignant bowel obstruction |

### Dosing Guide

- Haloperidol: 0.5-2 mg PO/SQ q6-8h; effective at low doses for opioid-related and metabolic nausea; available in CSCI (continuous subcutaneous infusion)
- Metoclopramide: 10 mg PO/SQ/IV q6h; do not use in complete bowel obstruction (worsens colic)
- Ondansetron: 4-8 mg PO/IV q8h; ODT (orally disintegrating tablet) useful when unable to swallow
- Dexamethasone: 4-8 mg PO/IV/SQ daily-BID; useful for bowel obstruction, brain metastases, hepatomegaly

### Malignant Bowel Obstruction

Management approach (surgery often not appropriate in advanced cancer):
- Corticosteroids: dexamethasone 8 mg IV/SQ daily; reduces perilesional inflammation; may partially resolve obstruction within 3-5 days
- Octreotide: 100-300 mcg SQ TID or CSCI infusion; reduces gastric, pancreatic, and intestinal secretions; decreases bowel distension and vomiting episodes; superior to hyoscine butylbromide for secretion reduction
- Analgesics: buprenorphine patch, parenteral opioid for colicky pain
- Venting gastrostomy (percutaneous gastrostomy tube to drain without feeding): for comfort in patients with persistent vomiting who are not surgical candidates
- Surgical bypass or diverting stoma: selected patients with good performance status, localized obstruction, adequate nutritional reserve
- Nasogastric tube decompression: temporary bridge; uncomfortable; not appropriate for long-term comfort care

## Last Days and Hours of Life

### Clinical Recognition of Actively Dying

Signs indicating death is likely within hours to days:
- Mottled, blotchy skin (livedo reticularis pattern) beginning in knees and feet, spreading proximally
- Cold extremities, cyanosis of digits and lips
- Cheyne-Stokes breathing (crescendo-decrescendo pattern with apneic pauses)
- Extended apneic periods (> 30-45 seconds)
- Agonal respirations (mandibular breathing — irregular, slow, gasping)
- Decreasing level of consciousness, unresponsive to verbal stimulation
- Inability to swallow; drooling; mouth open and dry
- Oliguria or anuria (< 100 mL urine output per day)
- Eyes partially open (relaxation of orbicularis oculi)
- Jaw relaxation, facial muscle slackening
- Loss of peripheral pulses; weak central pulse

### Comfort-Focused Care in Active Dying

Discontinue:
- Non-comfort medications: antihypertensives, statins, vitamins, insulin (adjust for comfort), diuretics (unless for dyspnea), antibiotics (unless for symptom control), anticoagulants
- Monitoring: discontinue blood pressure monitoring, pulse oximetry, telemetry, daily labs
- Blood draws and IV access (unless needed for medication delivery)
- Tube feedings and IV fluids (may increase secretions, edema, respiratory distress in dying patients)
- Turn-and-reposition schedule if causing distress (repositioning for comfort only)

Continue:
- Opioids (convert to subcutaneous or IV infusion if unable to take PO)
- Benzodiazepines for anxiety or agitation
- Anticholinergics for secretions
- Dexamethasone if being used for symptom control (ICP, dyspnea)
- Anticonvulsants in patients with seizure history
- Mouth care (lip moistening, oral swabs — does not require swallowing)

### Anticipatory Prescribing

Critical principle: ensure PRN medications are prescribed and physically available before they are needed. In home hospice, medications must be at the bedside.

Standard anticipatory medications for last days:
- Pain: morphine or hydromorphone SQ/IV PRN q1-4h
- Dyspnea: same opioid as above; lorazepam or midazolam SQ PRN
- Agitation/terminal restlessness: haloperidol 1-2 mg SQ/IV q1-4h; midazolam 2.5-5 mg SQ/IV for severe agitation
- Secretions: glycopyrrolate 0.2-0.4 mg SQ q4h PRN
- Seizures: lorazepam 2-4 mg SQ/IV/rectal PRN; midazolam 5-10 mg buccal/nasal/SQ
- Nausea: haloperidol 1 mg SQ q4-6h PRN or ondansetron 4 mg ODT

### Continuous Subcutaneous Infusion (CSCI)

When patients can no longer swallow:
- Subcutaneous butterfly needle (27 gauge) inserted in anterior chest wall, outer thigh, or abdomen
- Compatible drugs for CSCI: morphine, hydromorphone, midazolam, haloperidol, glycopyrrolate, dexamethasone, metoclopramide, ondansetron, ketamine
- Incompatible combinations: check compatibility charts — not all drugs mix safely
- Advantage: continuous drug delivery without IV access; manageable at home

### Palliative Sedation

Definition: use of sedating medications to reduce consciousness proportionate to the level of refractory suffering.

Indications:
- Refractory symptoms not controlled by standard measures (pain, dyspnea, agitation, delirium)
- Patient expected to die within hours to days
- Intolerable existential or psychological suffering (controversial — requires careful ethical review)

Distinguishing from euthanasia:
- Palliative sedation: intent is to relieve suffering; death results from underlying disease; dosing proportionate to symptom relief
- Euthanasia: intent is to cause death; medication administered at dose intended to cause death

Standard approach:
- Midazolam continuous SQ/IV infusion: 1-5 mg/hr titrated to comfort
- Phenobarbital 100-200 mg SQ q4h or infusion for refractory seizures or agitation
- Propofol (ICU setting) for refractory terminal agitation when IV access available

Documentation requirements:
- Document refractory nature of symptoms and prior failed interventions
- Document patient/surrogate consent
- Document that intent is symptom relief, not hastening death
- Involve ethics consultation if any team member raises concerns

## Neuropathic Pain: Adjuvant Analgesics

Neuropathic pain characteristics: burning, shooting, electric, stabbing quality; dermatomal distribution; allodynia (pain from non-painful stimulus); hyperalgesia; dysesthesia.

### First-Line Adjuvants

**Gabapentinoids**
- Gabapentin (Neurontin): 100-300 mg TID initially, titrate to 300-1200 mg TID as tolerated; maximum 3600 mg/day; dose reduce in renal failure; sedation limits titration
- Pregabalin (Lyrica): 25-75 mg BID-TID; faster titration than gabapentin; linear absorption; controlled substance (Schedule V); dose reduce in renal failure

**Antidepressants**
- TCAs (amitriptyline, nortriptyline): 10-25 mg QHS initially; titrate q1-2 weeks; nortriptyline preferred (better side effect profile than amitriptyline); avoid in elderly, cardiac arrhythmias, urinary retention; anticholinergic burden
- SNRIs: duloxetine 30-60 mg daily (FDA-approved for diabetic neuropathy, fibromyalgia); venlafaxine 37.5-150 mg daily; well tolerated

**Topical Agents**
- Lidocaine 5% patch: applied to area of allodynia for 12 hours on/12 hours off; minimal systemic absorption; useful for postherpetic neuralgia
- Capsaicin (topical): depletes substance P; burning with application; 8% patch (Qutenza) — single application, clinic procedure

**Corticosteroids**
- Dexamethasone: for nerve compression, spinal cord compression, brain metastases, lymphangitis carcinomatosa; 4-16 mg/day; taper to minimum effective dose; short-term use preferred

## Refractory Symptoms and Existential Distress

### Psychological Assessment

Common psychiatric presentations in palliative patients:
- Adjustment disorder with depressed or anxious mood: most common; proportionate to illness severity; normalize and support
- Major depressive disorder (MDD): anhedonia, persistent hopelessness, guilt, suicidal ideation (passive death wish is common and not equivalent to suicidality); screen with PHQ-9 (adjusted for somatic symptoms in medically ill)
- Anxiety disorder: generalized anxiety, panic disorder, anticipatory anxiety
- Demoralization: distinct from MDD; sense of hopelessness, helplessness, meaninglessness without anhedonia; responds to hope, meaning, connection rather than antidepressants alone; Viktor Frankl's logotherapy framework applicable

### Pharmacological Management of Psychological Symptoms

- SSRIs: escitalopram 5-10 mg daily (well tolerated, low drug interactions); sertraline 25-50 mg daily; onset 4-6 weeks; preferred for longer life expectancy
- SNRIs: duloxetine 30-60 mg daily (bonus: neuropathic pain benefit); venlafaxine 37.5-150 mg daily
- Mirtazapine: 7.5-15 mg QHS; appetite stimulant, anxiolytic, antiemetic, antidepressant; rapid onset of sedation and appetite benefit; useful when life expectancy weeks to months
- Methylphenidate: 2.5-5 mg PO at 8 AM and noon; rapid onset (days); preferred for short life expectancy when waiting for SSRI effect not feasible; benefit for depression, fatigue, opioid sedation
- Lorazepam: 0.5-1 mg PO/SL q6-8h PRN or scheduled for anxiety; use cautiously in delirium-prone patients
- Olanzapine: 2.5-5 mg QHS or BID; useful for agitation, anxiety, nausea, and appetite stimulation in advanced cancer

### Spiritual and Existential Care

Spiritual assessment tools:
- FICA (Faith, Importance, Community, Address): structured spiritual history
- HOPE (Hope, Organized religion, Personal spirituality, Effect on care)

Interventions:
- Chaplaincy referral: for all seriously ill patients regardless of religious affiliation
- Life review / dignity therapy: structured interview documenting life narrative, values, accomplishments, messages to loved ones; shown to improve dignity, meaning, and will to live
- Meaning-centered psychotherapy: derived from Viktor Frankl; group or individual; reduces existential distress and desire for hastened death in advanced cancer
- Mindfulness-based approaches: adapted for serious illness; reduces anxiety, increases acceptance

## Bereavement

### Types of Grief

**Normal (uncomplicated) grief**
- Waves of sadness, tearfulness, intrusive memories, yearning for the deceased
- Intensity typically peaks 1-6 months, gradually diminishes
- Does not require treatment; benefits from social support, normalization
- Physical symptoms common: fatigue, insomnia, appetite changes, somatic pain
- Acute grief reactions lasting days to weeks are normal even if intense

**Anticipatory grief**
- Grief that begins before the death, during the illness trajectory
- Normal and adaptive; allows some preparation for loss
- Support during this period is part of palliative care for families
- Does not "use up" grief — family will still grieve after death

**Prolonged grief disorder (formerly complicated grief)**
- DSM-5-TR 2022 criteria: intense grief persisting beyond 12 months after loss in adults (6 months in children and adolescents)
- Core features: intense yearning or longing for the deceased, preoccupation with the deceased, difficulty accepting the death, bitterness or anger about the loss, difficulty engaging in activities or relationships
- Impaired functioning in occupational, social, or personal domains
- Prevalence: approximately 10% of bereaved individuals; higher after sudden or traumatic death, loss of child, dependent relationship

**Distinguishing prolonged grief disorder from MDD:**

| Feature | Prolonged Grief | Major Depression |
|---|---|---|
| Yearning for deceased | Central feature | Not specific |
| Anhedonia | Preserved capacity for pleasure if connected to deceased | Pervasive |
| Trigger | Loss-specific | Global |
| Positive memories | Present | Often absent (negative cognitive bias) |
| Functional impairment | Related to grief | Broader |
| Suicidality | Passive (wanting to be with deceased) | Active suicidal ideation common |

### Bereavement Interventions

- First-line: cognitive-behavioral grief therapy (CBGT) — grief-focused CBT; prolonged exposure elements
- Complicated grief treatment (CGT): developed by Shear; dual-process model; shown effective in RCTs
- Antidepressants: for concurrent MDD; escitalopram or sertraline; limited direct evidence for prolonged grief disorder without MDD
- Group support: bereavement groups (hospice-provided, community-based, disorder-specific)
- Under Medicare Hospice Benefit: bereavement services provided to family for 13 months after patient death

### Hospice Bereavement Support

Required under Medicare Hospice CoPs:
- Bereavement assessment of family and caregivers during and after hospice enrollment
- Bereavement plan of care
- Ongoing support for 13 months after death (phone calls, letters, support groups, counseling referral)
- Identify high-risk bereaved family members: unexpected death, traumatic circumstances, prior psychiatric history, limited social support, dependent relationship with deceased

## Common Syndromes in Palliative Care

### Cancer Anorexia-Cachexia Syndrome (CACS)

- Characterized by weight loss, muscle wasting, anorexia, fatigue, metabolic abnormalities
- Distinct from simple starvation: driven by pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1, IL-6); not reversible with nutritional supplementation alone
- Prevalence: up to 80% in advanced cancer patients
- Management:
  - Corticosteroids: dexamethasone 2-4 mg daily or methylprednisolone 16-32 mg daily; short-term benefit in appetite and energy (weeks); avoid long-term use
  - Megestrol acetate 400-800 mg daily: appetite stimulant; increases weight (mostly fat, not lean mass); risk of DVT, adrenal suppression; limited benefit in survival
  - Mirtazapine: appetite stimulant + antidepressant; well tolerated
  - Olanzapine 2.5-5 mg daily: appetite stimulant; antiemetic
  - Dronabinol (THC): modest benefit; less effective than megestrol; may help nausea and appetite
  - Omega-3 fatty acids (fish oil): some evidence for weight stabilization; limited data
  - Artificial nutrition: does not improve survival or quality of life in advanced cancer cachexia; may increase complications

### Malignant Hypercalcemia

- Paraneoplastic (PTHrP) or osteolytic; breast cancer, multiple myeloma, squamous cell carcinoma, lymphoma
- Symptoms: nausea, constipation, confusion, polyuria, polydipsia, lethargy, cardiac arrhythmias
- Corrected calcium > 12 mg/dL: symptomatic hypercalcemia
- Treatment: IV hydration (normal saline 200-500 mL/hr); bisphosphonates (zoledronic acid 4 mg IV single dose; effect onset 2-4 days, duration 4-6 weeks); calcitonin 4-8 units/kg IM/SQ q12h (rapid but tachyphylaxis); denosumab for bisphosphonate-refractory cases
- In advanced cancer: recurrence is common; discuss goals of care before aggressive treatment of repeated episodes

### Spinal Cord Compression

- Oncologic emergency; most common from breast, lung, prostate, myeloma
- Symptoms: back pain (often preceding weakness by weeks), lower extremity weakness, sensory loss, bowel/bladder dysfunction
- Management: dexamethasone 10-16 mg IV loading dose → 4-8 mg q6h; urgent radiation oncology and neurosurgery consultation; surgery for instability or radioresistant tumors; prognosis for neurological recovery depends on degree of deficit at presentation
- In palliative context: goals of care conversation critical — aggressive treatment appropriate if prognosis supports it; comfort measures if not

### Terminal Delirium

- Hyperactive, hypoactive, or mixed delirium in the last days of life
- Common causes: opioid metabolite accumulation, infection, metabolic derangement, brain metastases, uremia, hypercalcemia
- Management:
  - Rule out reversible causes if consistent with goals of care
  - Haloperidol: 0.5-2 mg PO/SQ/IV q6-8h scheduled + PRN; first-line for terminal delirium
  - Chlorpromazine: 12.5-50 mg PO/SQ/IV q6-8h; more sedating than haloperidol
  - Benzodiazepines: lorazepam for acute agitation; avoid as monotherapy for delirium (may worsen in non-alcohol-withdrawal delirium)
  - Midazolam CSCI for severe, refractory terminal agitation unresponsive to antipsychotics
  - Non-pharmacological: minimize unnecessary stimulation, consistent orientation cues, family presence and familiar voices, maintain day/night light cycle, avoid physical restraints

## Prognostic Tools

### Performance Status Scales

**ECOG Performance Status**

| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 | Fully active, no restriction |
| 1 | Restricted in physically strenuous activity; ambulatory; light work |
| 2 | Ambulatory, capable of self-care; unable to work; up > 50% of waking hours |
| 3 | Limited self-care; confined to bed/chair > 50% of waking hours |
| 4 | Completely disabled; no self-care; totally confined to bed/chair |
| 5 | Dead |

ECOG 3-4 = typical hospice-eligible performance status (prognosis weeks-months)

**Karnofsky Performance Scale (KPS)**

| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 100 | Normal; no complaints; no evidence of disease |
| 80-90 | Normal activity with effort; some signs of disease |
| 60-70 | Cares for self; unable to do normal activity |
| 40-50 | Requires occasional assistance; can care for most needs |
| 20-30 | Disabled; requires special care and assistance |
| 10 | Moribund |

KPS ≤ 50 = hospice-eligible performance status

### Palliative Performance Scale (PPS)

Designed specifically for palliative populations; incorporates ambulation, activity, self-care, oral intake, and level of consciousness. PPS ≤ 40% associated with survival of days to weeks.

### The Surprise Question

"Would I be surprised if this patient died in the next 12 months?"
- If answer is "No, I would not be surprised" → patient warrants palliative care referral and goals of care conversation
- Validated as a simple prognostic screening tool across multiple disease populations
- Sensitivity for 12-month mortality approximately 67%; specificity approximately 80%

## Ethical Principles in Palliative Care

### Core Bioethical Principles Applied

- Autonomy: patient's right to make informed decisions about their own care, including declining treatment
- Beneficence: obligation to provide treatments that benefit the patient
- Non-maleficence: obligation to avoid harm; aggressive treatment in dying patients may cause harm
- Justice: equitable access to palliative care regardless of race, socioeconomic status, geography

### Doctrine of Double Effect

Applies when an action intended to relieve suffering may have a foreseen but unintended harmful consequence (e.g., opioid dosing for dyspnea at end of life):
- The action itself must not be inherently harmful
- The clinician must intend the good effect (comfort), not the harmful one (hastened death)
- The harmful effect must not be the means to achieving the good effect
- There must be proportionate reason (sufficient benefit to justify risk)
- Evidence: appropriately dosed opioids for pain and dyspnea in palliative patients do not significantly hasten death; this doctrine is applied but may be invoked unnecessarily due to clinician misunderstanding of opioid pharmacology

### Withholding vs. Withdrawing Treatment

- Ethically and legally equivalent: no moral distinction between withholding a treatment never started and withdrawing one already in progress
- Clinicians and families often find withdrawal more psychologically difficult (commission vs. omission)
- Time-limited trial: offer a defined period of aggressive treatment with pre-agreed reassessment — helps families accept withdrawal if treatment fails

### Medically Futile Treatment

- Quantitative futility: treatment has less than 1% chance of physiological benefit
- Qualitative futility: treatment is not in accord with the patient's values or desired quality of life
- Process for disagreement: ethics consultation; patient/family meetings; time-limited trial; institutional policies; transfer of care as last resort; most states have procedural protections for unilateral withdrawal of futile treatment
