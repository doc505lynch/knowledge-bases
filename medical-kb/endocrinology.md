## Hypothyroidism

### Definition and Epidemiology

Hypothyroidism is a clinical syndrome resulting from deficient thyroid hormone production or action. Prevalence 4-10% in women, 1-2% in men. Subclinical hypothyroidism (elevated TSH with normal free T4) affects an additional 10% of the population.

### Etiology

#### Primary Hypothyroidism (High TSH, Low/Normal Free T4)

- **Hashimoto's thyroiditis (Chronic autoimmune thyroiditis)** — most common cause in iodine-replete countries
  - TPO antibodies (anti-thyroid peroxidase) positive in >90% of cases
  - Anti-thyroglobulin antibodies positive in 60-80%
  - Lymphocytic infiltration with follicular destruction
  - May present with transient hyperthyroidism (Hashitoxicosis) early
  - Goiter typically firm, rubbery, non-tender (may be atrophic in late disease)
  - Increased risk: other autoimmune disease (T1DM, Addison's, pernicious anemia, vitiligo)

- **Post-radioactive iodine (RAI) therapy**
  - Hypothyroidism develops in 80-90% within first year
  - Risk increases with dose; essentially all patients hypothyroid long term

- **Post-thyroidectomy**
  - Total thyroidectomy: universal hypothyroidism
  - Subtotal thyroidectomy: variable depending on remnant

- **Drug-induced hypothyroidism**

| Drug | Mechanism |
|------|-----------|
| Amiodarone | Iodine excess — Wolff-Chaikoff effect; also inhibits T4→T3 conversion |
| Lithium | Inhibits thyroid hormone synthesis and release |
| Interferon-alpha | Triggers autoimmune thyroiditis |
| Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (sunitinib, sorafenib) | Direct thyroid toxicity |
| Checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab, nivolumab) | Immune-related thyroiditis |
| Thionamides (methimazole, PTU) | Block synthesis — therapeutic cause |
| Excess iodine (CT contrast, supplements) | Wolff-Chaikoff effect in susceptible patients |

- **Iodine deficiency** — leading cause globally; endemic goiter in iodine-deficient regions
- **Congenital hypothyroidism** — thyroid dysgenesis, inborn errors of synthesis
- **Infiltrative disease** — amyloidosis, sarcoidosis, hemochromatosis, Riedel's thyroiditis
- **Radiation to neck** — head and neck malignancy treatment

#### Secondary Hypothyroidism (Low TSH, Low Free T4)

Caused by TSH deficiency from pituitary pathology.

- Pituitary adenoma (mass effect)
- Pituitary surgery or radiation
- Sheehan's syndrome (postpartum pituitary necrosis)
- Hypophysitis (autoimmune, lymphocytic, IgG4)
- Traumatic brain injury
- Empty sella syndrome

#### Tertiary Hypothyroidism (Low TRH, Low TSH, Low Free T4)

Hypothalamic dysfunction; rare; TSH may be measurably low or inappropriately normal.

### Clinical Features

#### Symptoms

- Fatigue, lethargy, somnolence
- Cold intolerance
- Weight gain (modest — mainly fluid; rarely >5-10 kg)
- Constipation
- Dry skin, coarse hair, hair loss, brittle nails
- Bradycardia
- Slowed mentation, poor concentration, depression
- Menstrual irregularities (menorrhagia most common)
- Hoarse voice
- Muscle cramps, myalgias, carpal tunnel syndrome
- Decreased libido

#### Signs

- Bradycardia
- Hypertension (diastolic)
- Periorbital puffiness (non-pitting)
- Macroglossia
- Delayed relaxation phase of deep tendon reflexes (pathognomonic when present)
- Dry, coarse, pale/yellowish skin (carotenemia)
- Diffuse alopecia
- Non-pitting edema (myxedema) — infiltration of dermis with glycosaminoglycans
- Pericardial effusion
- Pleural effusion
- Hypothermia (severe cases)

#### Complications

- Hyperlipidemia (elevated LDL, triglycerides — risk of atherosclerosis)
- Hyponatremia (SIADH-like mechanism)
- Hyperprolactinemia (TRH stimulates prolactin; may cause galactorrhea)
- Sleep apnea
- Infertility, miscarriage
- Carpal tunnel syndrome
- Myopathy with elevated CK
- Pericardial and pleural effusions
- Myxedema coma (see below)

### Laboratory Evaluation

#### Primary Hypothyroidism Pattern

| Lab | Result |
|-----|--------|
| TSH | Elevated (>4.5 mIU/L) |
| Free T4 | Low (<0.8 ng/dL) |
| Total T3 | Low to normal |
| TPO Ab | Positive (Hashimoto's) |
| Anti-Tg Ab | Positive (Hashimoto's) |
| Lipids | LDL elevated, triglycerides elevated |
| CBC | Normocytic or macrocytic anemia |
| CK | May be elevated |
| Sodium | May be low |

#### Secondary Hypothyroidism Pattern

| Lab | Result |
|-----|--------|
| TSH | Low or inappropriately normal |
| Free T4 | Low |
| Other pituitary hormones | May be deficient |
| MRI pituitary | Often abnormal |

#### Subclinical Hypothyroidism

| Lab | Result |
|-----|--------|
| TSH | 4.5-10 mIU/L |
| Free T4 | Normal |

### Treatment

#### Levothyroxine (LT4) — Pharmacotherapy

- **Full replacement dose:** 1.6 mcg/kg/day lean body weight
  - Average adult: 100-150 mcg/day
  - Obese patients: dose based on lean body weight

- **Starting dose adjustments:**

| Population | Starting Dose | Rationale |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|
| Young, healthy adult | Full calculated dose | Well tolerated |
| Elderly (>65) | 25-50 mcg/day | Avoid cardiac stress |
| Known or suspected CAD | 12.5-25 mcg/day | Prevent angina/MI |
| Severe longstanding hypothyroidism | 25-50 mcg/day | Risk of adrenal crisis if AI coexists |
| Pregnancy (hypothyroid) | Increase dose 20-30% immediately | Fetal neurodevelopment |

- **Titration:** Recheck TSH 6-8 weeks after each dose change
  - Adjust by 12.5-25 mcg increments
  - TSH has long half-life; changes not reliably detectable before 4-6 weeks
  - Target TSH: 0.5-2.5 mIU/L (general adults)
  - Target TSH in pregnancy: trimester-specific (first trimester <2.5, second/third <3.0)
  - Target TSH in elderly: 1.0-4.0 mIU/L (allow slightly higher to avoid over-treatment)

- **Administration:**
  - Take on empty stomach, 30-60 minutes before food/coffee
  - OR at bedtime (2-3 hours after last meal) — improved absorption in some patients
  - Consistent timing important for stable levels

#### Drug Interactions with Levothyroxine

**Drugs that DECREASE LT4 absorption — separate by at least 4 hours:**

| Drug/Supplement | Effect |
|----------------|--------|
| Calcium carbonate | Binds LT4 in GI tract |
| Ferrous sulfate (iron) | Binds LT4 in GI tract |
| Antacids (aluminum/magnesium) | Reduces absorption |
| Proton pump inhibitors | Reduce gastric acid needed for dissolution |
| Cholestyramine / colestipol | Binds LT4 |
| Sucralfate | Impairs absorption |
| Sevelamer | Binds LT4 |

**Drugs that INCREASE LT4 metabolism (may need dose increase):**

| Drug | Effect |
|------|--------|
| Phenytoin | Induces hepatic metabolism |
| Carbamazepine | Induces hepatic metabolism |
| Rifampin | Induces hepatic metabolism |
| Phenobarbital | Induces hepatic metabolism |
| Sertraline | Increases clearance |

**Other interactions:**

- Warfarin — LT4 increases anticoagulant effect; monitor INR closely when starting/changing dose
- Digoxin — thyroid status affects digoxin levels and toxicity threshold
- Insulin/oral hypoglycemics — correction of hypothyroidism changes glucose metabolism

#### Subclinical Hypothyroidism — Treatment Thresholds

| TSH Level | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| >10 mIU/L | Treat regardless of symptoms |
| 4.5-10 mIU/L with symptoms | Consider treatment trial |
| 4.5-10 mIU/L asymptomatic | Observe; treat if TPO Ab positive, goiter, planning pregnancy, or progressive |
| Any level in pregnancy | Treat if TSH above trimester-specific upper limit |

#### Combination T4/T3 Therapy

- Most patients do well on LT4 monotherapy
- Small subset report persistent symptoms despite normal TSH
- Consider adding liothyronine (T3) 2.5-5 mcg/day in select patients
- Monitor carefully — T3 has shorter half-life, may cause hyperthyroid symptoms if overreplaced
- No strong evidence for universal benefit

### Myxedema Coma

#### Definition

Severe, life-threatening decompensation of hypothyroidism. Misnomer — coma not always present. Mortality 20-40% even with optimal treatment.

#### Precipitants

- Cold exposure
- Infection (pneumonia most common)
- Medications: sedatives, narcotics, anesthetics
- Trauma
- Surgery
- Stroke
- Cardiac events
- Discontinuation of thyroid hormone

#### Clinical Features

- Altered mental status (confusion to coma)
- Hypothermia (cardinal feature — may be severe <30 C)
- Bradycardia
- Hypotension
- Hyponatremia (often severe)
- Hypoglycemia
- Respiratory failure (hypercapnia — CO2 retention)
- Macroglossia, periorbital edema
- Non-pitting edema
- Absent or markedly reduced DTRs
- Pericardial/pleural effusions

#### Lab Findings

- TSH markedly elevated (primary) or low (central)
- Free T4 very low
- Hyponatremia (often <125 mEq/L)
- Hypoglycemia
- Elevated CK
- Anemia
- Hypercholesterolemia
- Elevated CO2 on ABG

#### Management — Myxedema Coma

ICU admission mandatory.

**1. Thyroid hormone replacement (IV preferred — unreliable GI absorption):**
- IV T4 (levothyroxine): loading dose 200-400 mcg IV, then 50-100 mcg IV daily
- IV T3 (liothyronine): 5-20 mcg IV loading then 2.5-10 mcg IV q8h (faster onset but risk of cardiac arrhythmia)
- Combined T4+T3 protocol used by many centers

**2. Glucocorticoids (mandatory before or simultaneously with thyroid hormone):**
- Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV q8h
- Rationale: concurrent adrenal insufficiency may be present; accelerated cortisol clearance as thyroid hormone raises metabolic rate
- Do NOT withhold steroids waiting for cortisol level in acute setting

**3. Supportive care:**
- Passive rewarming (avoid active warming — causes vasodilation and hypotension)
- Mechanical ventilation if respiratory failure
- Hypertonic saline for severe symptomatic hyponatremia
- Glucose correction
- Treat underlying precipitant (antibiotics for infection)
- Vasopressors for refractory hypotension
- Avoid sedatives and narcotics

## Hyperthyroidism

### Definition and Epidemiology

Hyperthyroidism: excess thyroid hormone production by the thyroid gland (vs. thyrotoxicosis: excess thyroid hormone from any source). Prevalence 1-2%; women affected 5-10x more than men.

### Etiology

#### Graves' Disease

- Most common cause of hyperthyroidism in iodine-replete regions (~70-80%)
- Autoimmune — TSH receptor antibodies (TRAb) stimulate thyroid autonomously
- Three hallmark features (triad not required for diagnosis):
  1. Diffuse goiter — soft, smooth, may have bruit/thrill
  2. Ophthalmopathy (Graves' orbitopathy) — proptosis, periorbital edema, lid lag, lid retraction, diplopia (extraocular muscle involvement); can occur without hyperthyroidism
  3. Dermopathy — pretibial myxedema (rare; localized non-pitting edema over shins)
  4. Acropachy (rare) — subperiosteal new bone formation, clubbing
- TRAb (TSI — thyroid stimulating immunoglobulins) positive in >95%
- Family history positive in 50%
- May remit spontaneously (~30-40% remission with antithyroid drug therapy)

#### Toxic Multinodular Goiter (Plummer's Disease)

- Multiple autonomously functioning nodules
- Common in iodine-deficient areas; older patients
- Lower T3/T4 elevation than Graves'
- Does not remit with antithyroid drugs — definitive treatment required
- Radioiodine uptake: patchy, heterogeneous ("hot" and "cold" areas)

#### Toxic Adenoma (Solitary Toxic Nodule)

- Single hyperfunctioning nodule suppresses rest of gland
- Technetium/radioiodine scan: "hot" nodule with suppression of surrounding tissue
- Surgery or RAI preferred (antithyroid drugs control symptoms but do not cure)

#### Subacute (De Quervain's) Thyroiditis

- Viral or post-viral inflammation; painful, tender thyroid
- Triphasic course: hyperthyroid (2-6 weeks) → hypothyroid (weeks-months) → return to euthyroid
- Low radioiodine uptake (<5%) — differentiates from Graves'
- Treatment: NSAIDs, corticosteroids for severe pain; beta-blockers for symptoms
- No antithyroid drugs (not synthesis-driven)

#### Painless (Silent/Lymphocytic) Thyroiditis

- Autoimmune; associated with Hashimoto's; common postpartum
- Similar triphasic course to subacute; no pain
- Low radioiodine uptake
- Often no treatment needed; beta-blockers for symptoms

#### Amiodarone-Induced Thyrotoxicosis (AIT)

Two types requiring different management:

| Feature | Type 1 AIT | Type 2 AIT |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Mechanism | Iodine excess stimulates synthesis | Destructive thyroiditis, hormone release |
| Background | Pre-existing thyroid disease | Normal thyroid |
| Color flow Doppler | Increased vascularity | Absent/reduced vascularity |
| Radioiodine uptake | Variable | Low |
| Treatment | Methimazole + potassium perchlorate | Prednisone 40-60 mg/day |
| Mixed type | Both treatments combined |

#### Other Causes

- Exogenous thyroid hormone (factitious or iatrogenic)
- Struma ovarii (ovarian teratoma with thyroid tissue)
- TSH-secreting pituitary adenoma (rare; elevated TSH with elevated free T4)
- Iodine-induced (Jod-Basedow effect)
- hCG-mediated (first trimester pregnancy, gestational transient thyrotoxicosis, trophoblastic disease)

### Clinical Features of Hyperthyroidism

#### Symptoms

- Heat intolerance, excessive sweating
- Palpitations, rapid heart rate
- Weight loss despite increased appetite
- Fatigue, exercise intolerance
- Nervousness, anxiety, irritability, emotional lability
- Tremor (fine)
- Diarrhea, increased bowel frequency
- Menstrual irregularities (oligomenorrhea, amenorrhea)
- Decreased libido
- Sleep disturbance, insomnia
- Dyspnea

#### Signs

- Tachycardia (often >90 bpm at rest)
- Atrial fibrillation (in 10-15%; risk increases with age)
- Hypertension (systolic; widened pulse pressure)
- Warm, moist, smooth skin
- Fine tremor
- Hyperreflexia
- Proximal muscle weakness
- Brisk, "staring" eye findings (lid lag, lid retraction)
- Diffuse goiter (Graves') or nodular goiter (toxic MNG)
- Bruit over thyroid (Graves')
- Hair thinning
- Onycholysis (Plummer's nails)

#### Apathetic Hyperthyroidism (Elderly)

- Classic symptoms absent
- Presents with: unexplained atrial fibrillation, weight loss, depression, cognitive decline
- High index of suspicion needed; check TSH

| Overt hyperthyroidism | Suppressed (<0.01) | Elevated | Elevated | |
| T3 toxicosis | Suppressed | Normal | Elevated | Often early toxic adenoma/MNG |
| Subclinical hyperthyroidism | Suppressed | Normal | Normal | |
| TSH-oma | Elevated or normal | Elevated | Elevated | |
| Resistance to thyroid hormone | Elevated or normal | Elevated | Elevated | |

#### Antibody Testing

| Antibody | Condition | Sensitivity |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| TRAb (TSH receptor Ab) | Graves' disease | >95% |
| TSI (thyroid stimulating Ig) | Graves' disease | Similar |
| TPO Ab | Graves', Hashimoto's | 70-80% in Graves' |
| Anti-Tg Ab | Non-specific | Variable |

### Thyroid Uptake Scan Interpretation

| Finding | Interpretation |
|---------|---------------|
| Diffusely elevated uptake (30-50% at 24h) | Graves' disease |
| Patchy uptake, multiple hot areas | Toxic multinodular goiter |
| Single hot nodule, suppressed background | Toxic adenoma |
| Low/absent uptake (<5%) | Subacute thyroiditis, painless thyroiditis, factitious, iodine excess |
| Low uptake with elevated hCG | Gestational thyrotoxicosis |

### Treatment of Hyperthyroidism

#### Antithyroid Drugs (ATDs)

**Methimazole (MMI) — first-line for most patients**

- Mechanism: blocks thyroid peroxidase → inhibits T4 and T3 synthesis
- Does NOT block release of stored hormone
- Dose: 10-30 mg/day (single daily dose acceptable)
- Onset: 2-6 weeks; normalize thyroid function in 6-12 weeks
- Duration: 12-18 months; then trial off to assess remission
- Remission rate: ~30-40% after complete course (better if small goiter, mild disease, TRAb negative)

**Propylthiouracil (PTU)**

- Mechanism: blocks synthesis AND inhibits peripheral T4→T3 conversion
- Preferred situations:
  1. First trimester of pregnancy (MMI associated with embryopathy: aplasia cutis, choanal atresia)
  2. Thyroid storm (additional peripheral conversion blockade)
  3. MMI allergy
- Dose: 100-200 mg q8h (must dose 3x daily — shorter half-life)
- Higher risk of hepatotoxicity than MMI (including fulminant hepatic failure — black box warning)

**Side Effects of ATDs:**

| Side Effect | Frequency | Action |
|------------|-----------|--------|
| Minor rash, urticaria | 5% | Switch drug or add antihistamine |
| Agranulocytosis | 0.3-0.5% | STOP drug immediately — medical emergency |
| Hepatotoxicity (PTU) | Rare | STOP PTU; LFT monitoring |
| Cholestatic jaundice (MMI) | Rare | STOP MMI |
| Arthralgia, vasculitis (PTU) | <1% | Monitor ANCA |
| Hypothyroidism | With overtreatment | Dose reduction |

**Agranulocytosis Warning:**
- Patients MUST be instructed: if fever or sore throat develops, STOP the drug and seek emergency evaluation immediately
- CBC with differential; neutrophils <500/mm3 confirms agranulocytosis
- Do NOT rechallenge with either ATD
- Treat with G-CSF, antibiotics for infection

#### Radioactive Iodine (I-131, RAI)

- Preferred definitive therapy for Graves', toxic MNG, toxic adenoma in non-pregnant adults
- Contraindicated: pregnancy, breastfeeding, active moderate-severe Graves' ophthalmopathy
- Goal: destroy sufficient thyroid tissue to render euthyroid or hypothyroid
- Hypothyroidism expected and preferred outcome (lower recurrence risk than euthyroid)
- Start levothyroxine once hypothyroid (typically within 3-6 months)
- Temporary worsening of ophthalmopathy possible — pretreat with prednisone if at risk
- Radiation precautions: avoid close contact with children/pregnant women for ~3-7 days post-treatment

#### Thyroidectomy

**Indications:**
- Large goiter with compressive symptoms
- Coexisting thyroid malignancy suspected or confirmed
- Pregnancy where ATDs poorly tolerated
- Patient preference for definitive non-radiation treatment
- Moderate-severe Graves' ophthalmopathy (RAI contraindicated)
- Failure of or contraindication to other treatments

**Preoperative preparation:**
- Must achieve euthyroid state with ATDs first
- Lugol's iodine 3-5 drops TID x 10 days preoperatively (reduces vascularity, bleeding risk)
- Beta-blockers for heart rate control

**Complications:**

| Complication | Frequency | Management |
|-------------|-----------|-----------|
| Hypocalcemia (hypoparathyroidism) | 1-10% (transient), <2% permanent | Calcium/vitamin D supplementation; IV calcium gluconate if symptomatic |
| Recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN) injury | 1-2% permanent | Hoarseness, voice changes; requires ENT evaluation |
| Hematoma | <1% | Airway emergency — open wound immediately at bedside if stridor |
| Thyroid storm (perioperative) | Rare with proper prep | See thyroid storm section |
| Hypothyroidism | Universal (total thyroidectomy) | Lifelong levothyroxine |

#### Beta-Blockers (Symptomatic Control)

- Propranolol 10-40 mg q6-8h — first choice (also blocks T4→T3 conversion)
- Atenolol 25-100 mg/day (once daily; preferred in asthma but some beta-1 selectivity lost at high doses)
- Metoprolol 25-50 mg q12h
- Do NOT use beta-blockers as monotherapy (no effect on thyroid hormone levels or long-term course)
- Essential adjunct in thyroid storm and perioperative management

## Thyroid Storm (Thyrotoxic Crisis)

### Definition

Life-threatening exacerbation of thyrotoxicosis with multi-organ dysfunction. Mortality 10-30% even with treatment.

### Precipitants

- Surgery (especially thyroid surgery without adequate preparation)
- Radioiodine therapy (rare)
- Infection (most common medical precipitant)
- Trauma
- Acute illness: MI, stroke, pulmonary embolism
- Iodine load: contrast dye, amiodarone
- Medication nonadherence (stopping ATDs)
- Emotional stress
- Parturition

### Diagnostic Scoring — Burch-Wartofsky Point Scale (BWPS)

Scores ≥45 = thyroid storm; 25-44 = impending storm; <25 = unlikely.

### Treatment Protocol — Order is Critical

**Admission: ICU mandatory**

#### Step 1 — PTU (First drug given)

- **Propylthiouracil 500-1000 mg loading dose PO/NG, then 250 mg q4h**
- Dual mechanism: blocks synthesis AND blocks peripheral T4→T3 conversion
- Must be given BEFORE iodine to prevent Jod-Basedow effect

#### Step 2 — Iodine (Given ONE HOUR after PTU)

- **Lugol's iodine solution: 5-10 drops q6-8h PO**
- OR Saturated solution of potassium iodide (SSKI): 5 drops q6h
- OR Sodium iodide 500-1000 mg IV q12h
- Mechanism: Wolf-Chaikoff effect — high iodine acutely inhibits hormone release
- CRITICAL: Must give PTU at least 1 hour before iodine — if iodine given first, provides substrate for additional hormone synthesis (Jod-Basedow phenomenon)
- If iodine allergy: use lithium carbonate 300 mg q6-8h (inhibits release)

#### Step 3 — Beta-Blockade

- **Propranolol 60-80 mg q4h PO** (or IV propranolol 0.5-1 mg IV slow push for acute control)
- Controls heart rate, tremor, agitation
- Propranolol preferred over selective agents — also blocks T4→T3 at high doses
- Target heart rate <90 bpm
- Use esmolol IV infusion for rapid titration in severe cases

#### Step 4 — Glucocorticoids

- **Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV q8h** (or dexamethasone 2 mg IV q6h)
- Rationale: adrenal reserve may be depleted; glucocorticoids also block T4→T3 conversion
- Continue until clinical improvement

#### Step 5 — Cholestyramine (adjunct)

- **Cholestyramine 4 g QID**
- Binds thyroid hormones in gut; reduces enterohepatic recirculation
- Useful adjunct; not always available emergently

#### Additional Supportive Measures

- Cooling blankets for hyperthermia (avoid aspirin — displaces T4 from binding proteins)
- Acetaminophen preferred for fever
- IV fluids for hydration
- Glucose monitoring and supplementation
- Treat precipitating cause (antibiotics for infection)
- Digoxin or rate control for atrial fibrillation (may be refractory until euthyroid)
- Plasmapheresis or plasma exchange: last resort for refractory storm

## Adrenal Insufficiency

### Definition and Classification

Inadequate cortisol production. Aldosterone deficiency only in primary disease.

### Primary Adrenal Insufficiency (Addison's Disease)

Both cortisol AND aldosterone deficient. ACTH elevated (no negative feedback).

#### Etiology of Primary AI

- **Autoimmune adrenalitis (~80%)** — most common in developed countries
  - Anti-21-hydroxylase antibodies (most sensitive/specific)
  - Associated with APS type 1 (Addison's + hypoparathyroidism + mucocutaneous candidiasis)
  - Associated with APS type 2 (Addison's + autoimmune thyroiditis + T1DM)
- **Tuberculosis** — historically most common cause worldwide; still leading cause in TB-endemic regions
- **Bilateral adrenal hemorrhage/infarction** — Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome (meningococcemia), anticoagulation, antiphospholipid syndrome
- **Adrenal metastases** — bilateral lung, breast, colon, melanoma (need >90% adrenal destruction for AI)
- **Fungal infection** — histoplasmosis, coccidioidomycosis, cryptococcosis
- **HIV/AIDS** — CMV, mycobacteria, fungal
- **Infiltrative** — sarcoidosis, amyloidosis, hemochromatosis
- **Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH)** — 21-hydroxylase deficiency most common (95%); salt-wasting form; ambiguous genitalia in females
- **Adrenoleukodystrophy** — X-linked; affects young males
- **Adrenalectomy** — bilateral; or residual after unilateral if contralateral suppressed

### Secondary Adrenal Insufficiency

Cortisol deficient only; aldosterone intact (regulated by RAAS). ACTH low or inappropriately normal.

#### Etiology of Secondary AI

- **Exogenous corticosteroids (most common cause)** — HPA axis suppression
  - Any route: oral, inhaled (high dose), intranasal, topical, epidural, intra-articular
  - Risk after: prednisone >5 mg/day for >3-4 weeks
  - Duration of suppression: months after prolonged use
- Pituitary adenoma — mass effect on corticotrophs
- Pituitary surgery or radiation
- Pituitary apoplexy
- Sheehan's syndrome
- Hypophysitis (autoimmune, checkpoint inhibitor-related)
- Craniopharyngioma or other parasellar mass

### Tertiary Adrenal Insufficiency

TRH/CRH deficiency from hypothalamic disease. Clinically similar to secondary.

### Clinical Features

| Feature | Primary AI | Secondary AI |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Hyperpigmentation | Yes (elevated ACTH/MSH) | No |
| Salt craving | Yes | No |
| Hyponatremia | Yes | Yes (different mechanism) |
| Hyperkalemia | Yes (aldosterone deficient) | No |
| Hypoglycemia | Yes | Yes |
| Hypotension/postural | Yes (volume depletion) | Milder |
| Vitiligo | Sometimes (autoimmune) | No |
| Autoimmune features | Common | Uncommon |

#### Symptoms

- Fatigue (most common and prominent)
- Anorexia, nausea, weight loss
- Abdominal pain
- Salt craving (primary)
- Dizziness, orthostasis
- Myalgias, arthralgias
- Depression, cognitive slowing
- Amenorrhea (women)

#### Signs

- Hyperpigmentation (primary) — skin creases, buccal mucosa, areolae, surgical scars, pressure points
- Hypotension (orthostatic → supine in crisis)
- Weight loss
- Vitiligo patches (autoimmune origin)
- Sparse axillary/pubic hair (women — DHEA/androgen deficiency)

### Laboratory Findings

| Lab | Primary AI | Secondary AI |
|-----|-----------|-------------|
| Morning cortisol | Low (<3 mcg/dL diagnostic; >18 rules out) | Low |
| ACTH | Elevated (>200 pg/mL typical) | Low or inappropriately normal |
| Sodium | Low | Low |
| Potassium | High (aldosterone deficiency) | Normal |
| Glucose | Low | Low |
| Renin | High | Normal |
| Aldosterone | Low | Normal |
| Anti-21-hydroxylase Ab | Positive (autoimmune) | Negative |
| Eosinophilia | May be present | May be present |

### Cosyntropin (ACTH) Stimulation Test

- Standard test for adrenal insufficiency
- Synthetic ACTH 250 mcg IV or IM
- Cortisol measured at 0 and 30-60 minutes
- Peak cortisol <18-20 mcg/dL = adrenal insufficiency
- Tests adrenal reserve directly
- May miss early secondary AI (adrenal not yet atrophied); in this case use low-dose cosyntropin test (1 mcg) or insulin tolerance test
- Morning cortisol <3 mcg/dL without stimulation: strongly suggests AI
- Morning cortisol >18 mcg/dL: AI unlikely

### Replacement Therapy

#### Hydrocortisone (Cortisol Replacement)

- **Dose: 15-25 mg/day in divided doses**
- **Schedule:** larger dose in morning (mimics diurnal cortisol rhythm)
  - Two-dose: 10-15 mg AM + 5-10 mg early afternoon (early afternoon prevents insomnia)
  - Three-dose: 10 mg AM + 5 mg noon + 5 mg at 4-5 PM
- Monitor for over-replacement: weight gain, hypertension, hyperglycemia, osteoporosis
- Monitor for under-replacement: fatigue, hypotension, hypoglycemia

#### Fludrocortisone (Mineralocorticoid — Primary AI Only)

- **Dose: 0.05-0.2 mg/day (typically 0.1 mg/day)**
- Not needed in secondary AI (aldosterone intact)
- Assess adequacy: no orthostasis, sodium normal, renin normal to slightly elevated, potassium normal
- May need higher doses in hot weather/salt restriction
- Signs of excess: hypertension, edema, hypokalemia

#### DHEA Supplementation

- Dehydroepiandrosterone 25-50 mg/day
- Consider in women with primary AI who have persistent fatigue, low libido, depressed mood despite adequate glucocorticoid/mineralocorticoid replacement
- Not standard of care; individual decision

### Sick Day Rules

- Minor illness (fever, vomiting, moderate stress): **double or triple hydrocortisone dose**
  - Example: if on 15 mg/day, take 30-45 mg/day during illness
  - Resume normal dose when recovered
- Unable to tolerate oral medication (vomiting): **parenteral glucocorticoids mandatory**
  - Patient should carry IM hydrocortisone 100 mg emergency injection kit
  - Hydrocortisone 100 mg IM → seek emergency care
- Surgery/major illness/ICU: **50-100 mg IV hydrocortisone q6-8h** (stress dosing)

## Adrenal Crisis

### Definition

Acute life-threatening adrenal insufficiency with hemodynamic instability. Medical emergency.

### Precipitants

- Infection (most common)
- Surgery
- Trauma
- Bilateral adrenal hemorrhage
- Sudden withdrawal of exogenous steroids
- Pituitary apoplexy
- Failure to increase steroid dose during intercurrent illness (known AI)
- First presentation of undiagnosed AI under stress

### Clinical Features

- **Refractory hypotension** — vasopressor-resistant in severe cases
- **Hyponatremia** — often severe (<125 mEq/L)
- **Hyperkalemia** (primary AI)
- **Hypoglycemia**
- Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
- Fever
- Altered mental status
- Extreme fatigue, weakness
- Signs of volume depletion

### Management — Adrenal Crisis

**Do NOT delay treatment pending laboratory confirmation if clinical suspicion high.**

**1. Hydrocortisone:**
- **100 mg IV bolus immediately**
- Then 50 mg IV q6-8h OR continuous infusion 200 mg over 24h
- Once stable: taper to oral maintenance over 1-3 days

**2. Fluid Resuscitation:**
- **Normal saline (0.9% NaCl) 1-2 L bolus rapidly**
- Corrects hypovolemia and hyponatremia simultaneously
- Avoid hypotonic solutions acutely

**3. Dextrose:**
- **D5W or D5NS** if hypoglycemic
- Check fingerstick glucose frequently

**4. Identify and Treat Precipitant:**
- Blood cultures → broad-spectrum antibiotics if infection suspected
- CT adrenals if hemorrhage suspected
- Evaluate for other acute stressors

**5. Monitoring:**
- Frequent vital signs
- Serial glucose, electrolytes
- Volume status assessment

**Note:** Fludrocortisone not needed acutely — hydrocortisone at these doses has sufficient mineralocorticoid activity. Resume when transitioning to oral maintenance.

## Cushing's Syndrome

### Definition

Syndrome resulting from prolonged exposure to excess glucocorticoids.

### Etiology

| Cause | Frequency | ACTH |
|-------|-----------|------|
| Exogenous corticosteroids | Most common overall | Suppressed |
| Cushing's disease (ACTH-secreting pituitary adenoma) | 70% of endogenous | Elevated |
| Ectopic ACTH secretion (SCLC, carcinoid, medullary thyroid Ca) | 15% of endogenous | Very elevated |
| Adrenal adenoma | 10-15% of endogenous | Suppressed |
| Adrenal carcinoma | <5% of endogenous | Suppressed |
| Bilateral adrenal hyperplasia | Rare | Variable |

### Clinical Features

#### Classic Features (Discriminating)

- **Central (truncal) obesity** with peripheral wasting — redistribution
- **Moon facies** (rounded, plethoric face)
- **Buffalo hump** (dorsocervical fat pad)
- **Purple striae >1 cm wide** — distinguishes from simple obesity (pale/pink striae)
- **Proximal muscle weakness** — unable to rise from chair without arm assistance; difficulty climbing stairs
- **Easy bruising**
- **Thin skin**, poor wound healing

#### Common but Non-Discriminating Features

- Hypertension
- Hyperglycemia/Type 2 diabetes
- Osteoporosis, vertebral fractures
- Hyperlipidemia
- Menstrual irregularities, hirsutism (women)
- Decreased libido, erectile dysfunction (men)
- Depression, cognitive changes, psychosis
- Hypokalemia (especially ectopic ACTH — very high cortisol acts on mineralocorticoid receptors)
- Acne
- Opportunistic infections (immunosuppression)

### Diagnostic Algorithm

**Phase 1: Confirm Hypercortisolism (at least 2 tests recommended)**

| Test | Method | Interpretation |
|------|--------|---------------|
| 24-hour urinary free cortisol (UFC) | Urine collection | >3x ULN strongly suggests; false positives: depression, alcoholism, obesity |
| Late-night salivary cortisol | Salivary sample at 11 PM to midnight | Elevated (cortisol should be near nadir); two abnormal values increase specificity |
| 1 mg overnight dexamethasone suppression test (DST) | 1 mg dexamethasone at 11 PM; 8 AM cortisol | Post-DST cortisol <1.8 mcg/dL normal; >=1.8 abnormal |
| 2-day low-dose DST | Dexamethasone 0.5 mg q6h x 48h | Cortisol >1.8 mcg/dL at end = abnormal |

**Phase 2: Determine ACTH Dependency**

- Plasma ACTH level (8 AM):
  - ACTH <5 pg/mL: ACTH-independent (adrenal source) → adrenal CT
  - ACTH >15-20 pg/mL: ACTH-dependent → proceed to phase 3

**Phase 3: Distinguish Pituitary vs. Ectopic ACTH**

| Test | Cushing's Disease | Ectopic ACTH |
|------|------------------|-------------|
| High-dose DST (8 mg overnight) | Suppresses >50% | No suppression |
| CRH stimulation | ACTH/cortisol rises >35%/20% | Minimal response |
| MRI pituitary | Adenoma in 50-60% | Normal |
| IPSS (gold standard) | IPS:peripheral ACTH ratio >2 baseline, >3 post-CRH | No gradient |

**IPSS — Inferior Petrosal Sinus Sampling:**
- Bilateral simultaneous samples from petrosal sinuses vs. peripheral blood
- Most accurate test for localizing ACTH source
- Can lateralize tumor to left or right side of pituitary
- Reserved for discordant biochemical/imaging results

**Phase 4: Localize Adrenal Source (if ACTH-independent)**

- CT adrenal: adenoma vs. carcinoma vs. bilateral hyperplasia
- Features of malignancy: size >4 cm, irregular margins, high HU (>10 unenhanced), washout <60%

### Treatment

| Cause | Primary Treatment | Secondary Options |
|-------|-----------------|------------------|
| Exogenous steroids | Taper gradually | — |
| Cushing's disease | Transsphenoidal surgery | Radiation, bilateral adrenalectomy |
| Ectopic ACTH | Resect primary tumor | Bilateral adrenalectomy, medical |
| Adrenal adenoma | Laparoscopic adrenalectomy | — |
| Adrenal carcinoma | Open adrenalectomy + mitotane | Chemotherapy |

#### Medical Therapy (Pre-surgical or when surgery not possible)

| Drug | Mechanism | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Ketoconazole | Blocks adrenal steroidogenesis (CYP17, CYP11B1) | Drug interactions; LFT monitoring; available and inexpensive |
| Metyrapone | Blocks 11-beta-hydroxylase (final step cortisol synthesis) | Can worsen hirsutism, HTN (mineralocorticoid precursors) |
| Osilodrostat | Blocks 11-beta-hydroxylase | FDA approved 2020; more targeted |
| Cabergoline | Inhibits ACTH secretion (dopamine agonist) | Used for Cushing's disease; partial response |
| Pasireotide | Somatostatin analogue; inhibits ACTH | SC injection; significant hyperglycemia side effect |
| Mifepristone | Glucocorticoid receptor antagonist | For hyperglycemia/DM in Cushing's; cannot monitor cortisol on drug |
| Mitotane | Adrenolytic; cytotoxic | Adrenal carcinoma; side effects common |

#### Nelson's Syndrome

- Occurs after bilateral adrenalectomy for Cushing's disease
- Loss of cortisol negative feedback → ACTH-secreting adenoma expands aggressively
- Hyperpigmentation + mass effect
- Prevention: pituitary radiation before adrenalectomy

## Pheochromocytoma and Paraganglioma

### Definition

- **Pheochromocytoma:** catecholamine-secreting tumor of adrenal medulla chromaffin cells
- **Paraganglioma:** extra-adrenal catecholamine-secreting tumor (sympathetic chain, organ of Zuckerkandl)
- Rule of 10s (historical, less accurate now): 10% malignant, 10% bilateral, 10% extra-adrenal, 10% familial (now 25-35% have germline mutation)

### Classic Triad

- Episodic **headache** (90%)
- **Diaphoresis** (60-70%)
- **Palpitations/tachycardia** (60-70%)

Plus paroxysmal or sustained hypertension.

### Clinical Presentations

- Paroxysmal hypertensive episodes (minutes to hours)
- Sustained hypertension (more common than classically taught)
- Hypertensive crisis triggered by: surgery, anesthesia, medications (beta-blockers, TCAs, metoclopramide, glucagon, opioids, cocaine, tyramine-containing foods on MAOIs)
- Pallor during attacks (catecholamine-induced vasoconstriction — distinguishes from carcinoid which causes flushing)
- Triad with hypertension: PPV for pheo 94%
- "5 Ps": pressure (HTN), pain (headache), perspiration, palpitations, pallor

### Diagnosis

**First-line test: Plasma free metanephrines (sensitivity 96-99%)**

- Metanephrines are continuous metabolites (not episodic like catecholamines themselves)
- 24-hour urine fractionated metanephrines + catecholamines: sensitivity 87-98%
- Plasma normetanephrine >3x ULN: essentially diagnostic
- Mildly elevated levels: may be false positive (medications, stress, lab error)

**Drugs causing false-positive results:**
- Tricyclic antidepressants
- Labetalol (interferes with HPLC method)
- Buspirone
- Acetaminophen (some assays)
- Amphetamines, cocaine

**Imaging (only after biochemical confirmation):**
- CT abdomen/pelvis: first-line imaging; most pheos are adrenal and >3 cm
- MRI: preferred if concern for malignancy, paraganglioma, iodine contrast allergy
- MIBG scintigraphy: functional imaging; useful for metastatic disease, extra-adrenal tumors
- Ga-68 DOTATATE PET: emerging; superior sensitivity especially for head/neck paraganglioma, metastatic disease

### Hereditary Associations

| Gene/Syndrome | Features |
|--------------|----------|
| RET / MEN2A | Pheo + medullary thyroid Ca + primary hyperparathyroidism |
| RET / MEN2B | Pheo + medullary thyroid Ca + marfanoid habitus + mucosal neuromas |
| VHL | Pheo + clear cell RCC + hemangioblastoma + pancreatic cysts |
| NF1 (neurofibromatosis) | Pheo + cafe-au-lait spots + neurofibromas |
| SDHB | High risk of malignant paraganglioma; extra-adrenal; aggressive |
| SDHD | Parasympathetic paraganglioma (head/neck); usually non-secreting |
| SDHC, SDHA | Variable phenotype |

**All patients with pheochromocytoma should be offered genetic testing.**

### Treatment

#### Preoperative Medical Management — Critical Sequence

**Step 1: Alpha-blockade FIRST (10-14 days minimum)**

- **Phenoxybenzamine** (non-selective, irreversible): 10 mg BID, titrate to 20-40 mg BID
  - Preferred by many for perioperative alpha-blockade
  - Side effects: orthostatic hypotension, nasal stuffiness, reflex tachycardia, retrograde ejaculation
- **Doxazosin or prazosin** (selective alpha-1, competitive): alternative; less post-op hypotension
- Goal: blood pressure <130/80 sitting; allow orthostasis to 100 systolic standing

**Step 2: Beta-blockade ONLY after adequate alpha-blockade (3-4 days after starting alpha)**

- **NEVER give beta-blocker first or without adequate alpha-blockade**
- Rationale: if beta given first, alpha-mediated vasoconstriction unopposed → severe hypertensive crisis
- Propranolol 10-40 mg q6-8h for tachycardia
- Target HR 60-70 bpm

**Step 3: High-sodium diet and hydration**
- Counters volume depletion from chronic catecholamine exposure
- 5000 mg sodium diet for 2 weeks preoperatively
- IV fluid hydration on day of surgery

#### Surgical Treatment

- **Laparoscopic adrenalectomy** — standard of care for unilateral adrenal pheo
- Open surgery for large (>6 cm) or potentially malignant tumors
- Intraoperative hypertensive crises managed with: phentolamine, sodium nitroprusside, nicardipine
- Post-resection hypotension: IV fluids, vasopressors if needed; phenoxybenzamine cleared, alpha-tone lost

#### Malignant/Metastatic Disease

- SDHB mutations highest risk
- High-dose I-131 MIBG therapy
- Chemotherapy: cyclophosphamide/vincristine/dacarbazine (CVD)
- Sunitinib (tyrosine kinase inhibitor)
- Radioembolization for hepatic metastases

## Osteoporosis

### Definition

Systemic skeletal disease characterized by low bone mass and microarchitectural deterioration leading to increased fracture risk.

### DXA Interpretation — T-Score

| T-Score | Classification |
|---------|---------------|
| >-1.0 | Normal |
| -1.0 to -2.5 | Osteopenia (low bone mass) |
| <=-2.5 | Osteoporosis |
| <=-2.5 + fragility fracture | Severe osteoporosis |

- T-score compares to peak bone mass of young adult reference
- Z-score compares to age-matched reference (use in premenopausal women, men <50, children)

### FRAX Thresholds for Treatment

- 10-year probability of major osteoporotic fracture (hip, spine, wrist, shoulder)
- Treatment recommended (with or without DXA T-score -2.5):
  - Major osteoporotic fracture probability **>=20%** OR
  - Hip fracture probability **>=3%**
- Calculate at: https://www.sheffield.ac.uk/FRAX

### Risk Factors

- Age, female sex, postmenopausal status
- Family history of hip fracture
- Previous fragility fracture
- Low body weight (<58 kg or BMI <19)
- Current tobacco smoking
- Excessive alcohol (>=3 units/day)
- Glucocorticoid use (>=5 mg prednisone equivalent/day for >=3 months)
- Rheumatoid arthritis
- Secondary osteoporosis causes: malabsorption, hypogonadism, hyperthyroidism, hyperparathyroidism, CKD

### Non-Pharmacologic Therapy

- **Calcium:** 1000 mg/day (ages 19-50), 1200 mg/day (women >50, men >70)
  - Prefer dietary sources; supplement if dietary inadequate
  - Divide doses <=500 mg per dose (absorption limited)
  - Calcium carbonate: take with food (requires acid for absorption)
  - Calcium citrate: take without food; preferred in achlorhydria, PPI users
- **Vitamin D:** 800-1000 IU/day (aim 25-OH vitamin D level 30-50 ng/mL)
- Weight-bearing and resistance exercise
- Fall prevention: remove hazards, improve lighting, strength/balance training, review medications
- Avoid smoking and excess alcohol
- Limit caffeine

### Pharmacotherapy

#### Bisphosphonates

**Alendronate (Fosamax)**
- 70 mg once weekly PO (or 10 mg daily)
- **Administration:** take fasting on empty stomach with full glass water; remain upright (no lying down) for at least 30-60 minutes; do not eat for 30-60 minutes after
- Reduces vertebral fracture 47%, hip fracture 51%, non-vertebral fracture 49% vs. placebo
- Side effects: esophageal irritation/ulceration (most important), GI upset
- Contraindications: esophageal disorders, inability to sit/stand 30 min, GFR <35 mL/min

**Risedronate (Actonel)**
- 35 mg once weekly PO or 150 mg once monthly
- Same administration requirements as alendronate
- Slightly less GI irritation than alendronate

**Ibandronate**
- 150 mg once monthly PO or 3 mg IV q3 months
- Proven efficacy for vertebral fracture only (insufficient hip fracture data)

**Zoledronic Acid (Reclast)**
- **5 mg IV infusion once annually** (over 15 minutes)
- Alternatively 5 mg IV every 2 years for prevention
- Advantages: no GI side effects; annual dosing improves adherence
- Side effects: acute phase reaction (flu-like symptoms 24-72h after first infusion; pretreat with acetaminophen/ibuprofen); hypocalcemia (ensure adequate calcium/vitamin D before infusion)
- Contraindicated: GFR <35 mL/min

**Duration of Bisphosphonate Therapy:**
- Oral bisphosphonate: 3-5 year treatment, then reassess risk
- Zoledronic acid: 3-year treatment, then reassess
- High-risk patients (prior hip/vertebral fracture, T-score <-2.5): continue or switch to another agent
- Drug holiday: pause therapy and reassess; bisphosphonates remain in bone for years

**Atypical femur fractures and osteonecrosis of the jaw (ONJ):**
- Rare but serious complications with long-term bisphosphonate use
- Atypical femur fractures: transverse fractures of subtrochanteric femur after minimal trauma; thigh pain may precede fracture
- ONJ: predominantly in patients receiving high-dose IV bisphosphonates for malignancy; rare with osteoporosis dosing

#### Denosumab (Prolia)

- **60 mg subcutaneous injection every 6 months**
- Mechanism: RANK-L inhibitor; blocks osteoclast formation and activation
- Reduces vertebral fracture 68%, hip fracture 40%
- Advantages: no renal dose adjustment; useful in CKD
- **Critical — Rebound fractures:** Multiple vertebral fractures reported after discontinuation
  - Must transition to bisphosphonate when stopping denosumab
  - Administer zoledronic acid within 6 months of last denosumab dose
- Side effects: hypocalcemia (especially CKD — ensure calcium/vitamin D), skin infections (cellulitis), hypersensitivity

#### Teriparatide (Forteo)

- **20 mcg subcutaneous injection daily for up to 24 months**
- Mechanism: intermittent PTH (1-34) — anabolic; stimulates bone formation
- Reduces vertebral fracture 65%, non-vertebral 53%
- Reserved for: severe osteoporosis (multiple fractures, T-score <-3.5), failure of antiresorptive therapy
- Contraindications:
  - Paget's disease of bone
  - Prior skeletal radiation
  - Unexplained elevated alkaline phosphatase
  - Pediatric patients or young adults with open epiphyses
  - Hypercalcemia
  - Previous osteosarcoma (black box warning based on rat studies; clinical risk appears low)
- After completion: must follow with antiresorptive therapy (bisphosphonate or denosumab) to preserve gains

#### Abaloparatide (Tymlos)

- 80 mcg SQ daily x 18 months
- PTHrP(1-34) analogue; anabolic
- Similar contraindications to teriparatide
- Follow with antiresorptive agent

#### Romosozumab (Evenity)

- **210 mg (two 105 mg injections) subcutaneously once monthly for 12 months**
- Mechanism: sclerostin inhibitor — dual action: increases bone formation AND decreases bone resorption
- Reduces vertebral fracture risk 73% vs. alendronate
- **BLACK BOX WARNING:** Increased risk of MI and stroke; contraindicated in patients with MI or stroke within previous year
- Assess cardiovascular risk before prescribing
- Follow 12-month course with antiresorptive agent (bisphosphonate or denosumab)

#### Raloxifene (SERM)

- 60 mg/day PO
- Selective estrogen receptor modulator
- Reduces vertebral fracture; no proven hip fracture benefit
- Benefits: reduces breast cancer risk
- Contraindicated: active or prior DVT/PE; venous thromboembolism risk
- Hot flashes common (not useful in women with severe menopausal symptoms)

#### Calcitonin

- Nasal spray 200 IU/day intranasal
- Modest anti-fracture efficacy; inferior to bisphosphonates
- Analgesic benefit for acute vertebral fracture pain
- Second-line agent

### Glucocorticoid-Induced Osteoporosis (GIOP)

- Most common cause of secondary osteoporosis
- Bone loss most rapid in first 6-12 months of therapy
- Treat with calcium + vitamin D + bisphosphonate if:
  - Planning glucocorticoid >3 months at >=5 mg prednisone equivalent/day, AND
  - Age >=40 with FRAX major fracture >=10% or hip >=1%
  - OR prior fragility fracture regardless of FRAX
  - OR age >=50 with T-score <=-2.5
- Teriparatide shown superior to bisphosphonate in severe GIOP

## Hyperparathyroidism and Hypercalcemia

### Primary Hyperparathyroidism

#### Etiology

| Cause | Frequency |
|-------|-----------|
| Single adenoma | 85-90% |
| Multiple gland hyperplasia | 10-15% |
| Double adenoma | 2-4% |
| Parathyroid carcinoma | <1% |

#### Clinical Features — "Bones, Stones, Groans, Moans"

- **Bones:** osteitis fibrosa cystica, subperiosteal bone resorption (radial aspect middle phalanges on X-ray), "salt and pepper" skull, brown tumors
- **Stones:** nephrolithiasis (calcium oxalate, calcium phosphate); nephrocalcinosis
- **Groans:** GI — constipation, nausea, anorexia, peptic ulcer disease, pancreatitis
- **Moans:** neuropsychiatric — depression, cognitive decline, fatigue, weakness, "psychic moans"
- **Most common presentation today:** Asymptomatic hypercalcemia found on routine labs

#### Laboratory Findings

| Lab | Result |
|-----|--------|
| Calcium | Elevated (>10.5 mg/dL) |
| PTH | Elevated or inappropriately normal |
| Phosphorus | Low-normal to low |
| Chloride | Elevated (hyperchloremic) |
| Bicarbonate | Low |
| Chloride:phosphorus ratio | >33 (classic teaching) |
| Urinary calcium | Elevated |
| 25-OH vitamin D | May be low |
| 1,25-OH vitamin D | Variable |

#### Differential Diagnosis of Hypercalcemia

| Cause | PTH | PTH-rP | 25-OH VitD | 1,25-OH VitD |
|-------|-----|--------|------------|--------------|
| Primary hyperparathyroidism | High | Low | Normal | High |
| Malignancy (osteolytic) | Low | Variable | Normal | Normal/low |
| Humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy | Low | High | Normal | Low |
| Vitamin D toxicity | Low | Low | Very high | Variable |
| Granulomatous disease (sarcoid, TB) | Low | Low | Normal/low | Very high |
| FHH (familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia) | High-normal | Low | Normal | Normal |
| Milk-alkali syndrome | Low | Low | Normal | Normal |

**Key distinction FHH vs. primary hyperPTH:**
- Urine calcium/creatinine clearance ratio <0.01 = FHH (calcium clearance low despite hypercalcemia)
- CASR gene mutation in FHH
- Surgery NOT indicated for FHH

#### Surgery Indications — Asymptomatic Primary Hyperparathyroidism (2022 Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold for Surgery |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Serum calcium | >1 mg/dL above ULN |
| Creatinine clearance | <60 mL/min |
| DXA T-score | <=-2.5 any site; or vertebral fracture on imaging |
| Age | <50 years |
| 24-hour urine calcium | Not used as criterion |
| Renal imaging | Kidney stones or nephrocalcinosis |

**If any criterion met, surgery recommended even without symptoms.**

#### Parathyroidectomy

- Cure rate >95% for single adenoma
- Preoperative localization: sestamibi scan, neck ultrasound, 4D-CT
- Intraoperative PTH monitoring: PTH drops >50% within 10 minutes of adenoma removal = cure
- Complications: hypocalcemia (hungry bone syndrome post-op), RLN injury, hematoma

#### Medical Management (Non-Surgical Candidates)

- **Cinacalcet (Sensipar):** calcimimetic — binds to calcium-sensing receptor → decreases PTH
  - 30 mg BID, titrate to 60-90 mg BID
  - Lowers serum calcium but does NOT improve bone density significantly
  - Used when surgery contraindicated or refused
- Bisphosphonates: treat osteoporosis component
- Adequate hydration; avoid thiazide diuretics and lithium (raise calcium)

### Hypercalcemic Crisis

- Serum calcium typically >14-15 mg/dL
- Symptoms: confusion, stupor, coma, nausea/vomiting, polyuria/polydipsia, ileus
- Treatment:
  1. **IV normal saline 200-300 mL/h** — volume expansion promotes calciuresis
  2. **Furosemide** — only after volume replete (calciuresis); NOT as initial treatment
  3. **Calcitonin 4 IU/kg IM/SC q12h** — rapid onset (hours); tachyphylaxis after 48h
  4. **Zoledronic acid 4 mg IV** — onset 2-4 days; duration weeks; for malignancy-associated hypercalcemia
  5. **Glucocorticoids** — if granulomatous disease, lymphoma, vitamin D toxicity
  6. **Hemodialysis** — for severe refractory cases or renal failure

### Hypoparathyroidism and Hypocalcemia

#### Etiology of Hypoparathyroidism

- Post-thyroidectomy or parathyroidectomy (most common)
- Autoimmune (isolated or APS type 1)
- DiGeorge syndrome (chromosome 22q11 deletion)
- Hypomagnesemia (impairs PTH secretion and action)
- Hemochromatosis, Wilson's disease
- Irradiation to neck

#### Hypocalcemia Features

- **Neuromuscular irritability:** perioral numbness, tingling fingers/toes
- **Tetany:** carpopedal spasm, laryngospasm (life-threatening)
- **Chvostek's sign:** facial twitch when tapping over facial nerve (low specificity)
- **Trousseau's sign:** carpal spasm with blood pressure cuff >systolic x 3 minutes (more specific)
- **Seizures**
- **QTc prolongation** on ECG (risk of arrhythmia)
- Long-term: cataracts, basal ganglia calcifications, dental anomalies, papilledema

#### Acute Hypocalcemia Treatment

- Symptomatic/severe (Ca <7.5 or tetany/seizures):
  - **Calcium gluconate 1-2 g IV over 10-20 minutes**, repeat as needed
  - Then calcium gluconate infusion: 10 g in 1 L D5W at 50-100 mL/h
- Replace magnesium if low
- Oral calcium + calcitriol once stable

#### Chronic Hypoparathyroidism Treatment

- Oral calcium carbonate or citrate 1000-3000 mg/day
- Calcitriol 0.25-2 mcg/day (active vitamin D — bypasses PTH requirement for activation)
- **PTH replacement: Natpara (PTH 1-84)** — approved for hypoparathyroidism uncontrolled on conventional therapy
- Target calcium: low-normal range (8-9 mg/dL); avoid normalization (risk of hypercalciuria and nephrolithiasis without PTH to modulate kidney calcium handling)
- Monitor urine calcium (24h) and serum calcium/creatinine

## Pituitary Disorders

### Hypopituitarism

#### Etiology

- Pituitary adenoma (mass effect) — most common
- Pituitary surgery or radiation
- Traumatic brain injury
- Sheehan's syndrome (postpartum pituitary necrosis from hemorrhagic shock)
- Lymphocytic hypophysitis (autoimmune; peripartum women)
- Checkpoint inhibitor-related hypophysitis
- Craniopharyngioma (especially in children)
- Infiltrative: sarcoidosis, hemochromatosis, Langerhans cell histiocytosis
- Meningitis, encephalitis
- Empty sella syndrome

#### Anterior Pituitary Hormone Deficiencies

| Hormone | Axis | Testing | Replacement |
|---------|------|---------|-------------|
| ACTH | HPA | Cosyntropin stimulation, insulin tolerance test | Hydrocortisone 15-25 mg/day |
| TSH | HPT | Free T4 (TSH unreliable) | Levothyroxine 1.6 mcg/kg/day |
| LH/FSH | HPG | LH, FSH, testosterone (men), estradiol (women) | Testosterone (men), estrogen/progesterone (women) |
| GH | | IGF-1, GHRH-arginine, insulin tolerance test | Somatropin (recombinant GH) |
| Prolactin | | Serum prolactin | None (deficiency usually clinically silent) |

**Priority in hormone replacement:** ACTH deficiency must be corrected before thyroid hormone replacement (risk of adrenal crisis with levothyroxine if AI untreated).

#### Posterior Pituitary

- ADH (vasopressin) deficiency → diabetes insipidus
- Oxytocin deficiency: clinically not replaced

### Prolactinoma

- Most common functioning pituitary adenoma
- Microadenoma <10 mm; macroadenoma >=10 mm

#### Causes of Elevated Prolactin

| Cause | Prolactin Level |
|-------|----------------|
| Pregnancy/lactation | Up to 300 ng/mL |
| Prolactinoma macro | Often >250 ng/mL |
| Prolactinoma micro | 20-250 ng/mL |
| Hypothyroidism (TRH stimulus) | Mild elevation |
| Antipsychotics (dopamine antagonism) | 25-100 ng/mL |
| Antidepressants, metoclopramide, verapamil | Mild elevation |
| Stalk effect (non-prolactin pituitary tumor blocking dopamine) | Usually <100 ng/mL |
| Hook effect (very high prolactin, immunoassay artifact) | Falsely low — dilute sample |

#### Clinical Features

- Women: galactorrhea, amenorrhea/oligomenorrhea, infertility
- Men: decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, infertility, galactorrhea (less common), hypogonadism
- Both: osteoporosis (from hypogonadism), headaches, visual field defects (macroadenoma)

#### Treatment

**First-line: Dopamine agonists**

- **Cabergoline:** 0.25 mg twice weekly; titrate to 0.5-1 mg twice weekly
  - Preferred — higher remission rate, better tolerated, less frequent dosing
  - Normalizes prolactin in 80-90%, reduces tumor size in 90%
  - Side effects: nausea, orthostatic hypotension, impulse control disorders (rare at low doses)
  - Monitor echocardiogram if high cumulative dose (cardiac valve fibrosis risk at Parkinson's doses)
- **Bromocriptine:** 2.5-7.5 mg/day in divided doses; older agent, less tolerated

**Surgery (transsphenoidal):**
- Macroadenoma with persistent visual deficit not responding to medical therapy
- Drug intolerance
- Pregnancy (rarely needed — bromocriptine safe in pregnancy; cabergoline growing evidence)

**Radiation:** rarely needed for aggressive prolactinomas

### Acromegaly

#### Pathophysiology

- GH-secreting pituitary adenoma → excess IGF-1 → somatic overgrowth
- Before growth plate closure: gigantism
- After: acromegaly

#### Clinical Features

| System | Features |
|--------|---------|
| Skeletal | Coarsened facies, frontal bossing, prognathism, malocclusion, large hands/feet (ring, shoe size increase) |
| Soft tissue | Skin thickening, macroglossia, organomegaly |
| Metabolic | Insulin resistance, DM (in ~25%), hypertension |
| Sleep | Obstructive sleep apnea (macroglossia, soft tissue hypertrophy) |
| Cardiac | Cardiomegaly, cardiomyopathy — leading cause of death |
| Joints | Arthropathy, carpal tunnel syndrome |
| Colon | Increased colon polyps/cancer risk |
| Pituitary | Headache, bitemporal hemianopsia (macroadenoma) |

#### Diagnosis

- **Screening:** IGF-1 level (age and sex-matched)
- **Confirmation:** Oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) — 75 g glucose; GH should suppress to <0.4 ng/mL; failure to suppress = acromegaly
- **MRI pituitary** after biochemical confirmation

#### Treatment

| Modality | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Transsphenoidal surgery | First-line; cure in 80-90% microadenoma, 40-70% macroadenoma |
| Somatostatin analogues | Octreotide LAR 20-40 mg IM monthly; lanreotide SQ monthly; adjuvant to surgery |
| Dopamine agonists | Cabergoline — useful for mixed GH/prolactin tumors |
| Pegvisomant | GH receptor antagonist; most effective for IGF-1 normalization; does not reduce tumor size |
| Radiation | Stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife) for residual tumor; slow effect |

**Biochemical cure criteria:** IGF-1 normal for age/sex; GH <0.4 ng/mL on OGTT

### Pituitary Apoplexy

#### Definition

Acute hemorrhage or infarction within pituitary adenoma. Endocrine emergency.

#### Clinical Features

- Sudden severe headache ("thunderclap")
- Meningismus
- Ophthalmoplegia (CN III, IV, VI involvement — cavernous sinus)
- Visual field defects (optic chiasm compression)
- Altered mental status
- Acute adrenal crisis (most dangerous — ACTH deficiency)

#### Management

- **Immediate IV hydrocortisone** (presume ACTH deficiency)
- MRI brain/pituitary (CT may miss if no hemorrhage)
- Urgent ophthalmology and neurosurgical consultation
- Emergency transsphenoidal surgery if: severe visual loss, rapidly progressive visual deterioration, decreasing consciousness
- Conservative management acceptable if: vision intact, mental status normal — with close monitoring

### Bitemporal Hemianopsia

- Loss of temporal (outer) visual fields bilaterally
- Due to optic chiasm compression (crossing nasal fibers)
- Causes: pituitary macroadenoma (most common), craniopharyngioma, meningioma
- Fundoscopy: may show optic atrophy (late)
- Formal visual field testing (Goldman or Humphrey perimetry) — essential for monitoring

### Pituitary Incidentaloma

- Incidentally discovered pituitary mass on MRI done for other reason
- Evaluate for:
  - Hormonal hypersecretion: prolactin, IGF-1, 24h UFC, alpha-subunit, free T4/TSH
  - Hormonal deficiency: full anterior pituitary panel if indicated
  - Visual field testing if near or touching chiasm
- Management by size:
  - Microadenoma (<10 mm): MRI at 1 year then every 1-2 years; no treatment if non-functioning
  - Macroadenoma (>=10 mm): MRI at 6 months; surgery if compressive symptoms or hormonal hypersecretion

## Adrenal Incidentaloma

### Definition

Adrenal mass >1 cm discovered incidentally on imaging performed for unrelated reasons. Found in ~5% of abdominal CT scans.

### Initial Evaluation

All adrenal incidentalomas require hormonal workup regardless of size.

#### Hormonal Workup

| Test | Screens For | Threshold |
|------|------------|----------|
| 1 mg overnight DST | Autonomous cortisol secretion (subclinical Cushing's) | Post-DST cortisol >=1.8 mcg/dL = abnormal |
| Plasma free metanephrines | Pheochromocytoma | Per laboratory ULN |
| Aldosterone-to-renin ratio (ARR) | Primary aldosteronism | If hypertensive or hypokalemic; ARR >30 with PA-CC |
| DHEAS/androgens | Adrenocortical carcinoma | If clinical signs of androgen excess |

#### Imaging Characteristics

| Feature | Benign Adenoma | Adrenal Carcinoma | Pheo |
|---------|---------------|------------------|------|
| Hounsfield units (HU) unenhanced | <10 HU (lipid-rich adenoma) | >25 HU | Variable, often >25 |
| Size | Usually <4 cm | Often >4-6 cm | Variable |
| Absolute washout at 15 min | >60% | <60% | <60% |
| Homogeneity | Homogeneous | Heterogeneous | Variable |
| Borders | Smooth | Irregular | Smooth |
| Calcifications | Rare | May have |  |

**HU <10 unenhanced = very likely lipid-rich benign adenoma** (PPV >98%)

### Surgery Indications

| Finding | Action |
|---------|--------|
| Biochemically active (any hormone excess) | Surgical resection |
| Pheo (biochemically confirmed) | Adrenalectomy (alpha-block first) |
| Size >=4 cm or growing >=0.8 cm/year | Consider resection (malignancy risk) |
| Imaging features suspicious for malignancy | Resection |
| Bilateral with AI | Bilateral resection only if absolutely necessary; need stress dosing |

### Follow-up for Non-Operative Management

- Imaging: repeat CT at 6-12 months, then annually x2 if stable
  - Growth >0.8 cm/year: consider surgery
  - No growth after 4 years: discontinue imaging
- Hormonal reassessment: annually x5 years (new secretion may develop)

### Primary Aldosteronism (Conn's Syndrome)

- Most common cause of secondary hypertension
- Excess aldosterone from adrenal adenoma or bilateral hyperplasia
- Hypertension + hypokalemia (but 40% normokalemic)
- Screening: ARR >30 (with PAC >15 ng/dL)
- Confirmation: salt loading test, fludrocortisone suppression test
- Subtype differentiation: adrenal CT + adrenal vein sampling (gold standard)
- Adenoma → adrenalectomy; Bilateral hyperplasia → mineralocorticoid antagonist (spironolactone or eplerenone)

## Diabetes Insipidus

### Central DI

- ADH deficiency from posterior pituitary or hypothalamus
- Causes: surgery, trauma, tumor, autoimmune, infiltrative
- Large volumes dilute urine (>250 mL/hr, >3 L/day), serum sodium elevated
- Water deprivation test: urine fails to concentrate; corrects with desmopressin
- Treatment: desmopressin (DDAVP) intranasal, oral, or IV

### Nephrogenic DI

- ADH present but kidney fails to respond
- Causes: lithium (most common drug), hypercalcemia, hypokalemia, congenital (AVPR2 mutation)
- Water deprivation: urine fails to concentrate AND does NOT correct with desmopressin
- Treatment: address cause; low sodium/low protein diet; hydrochlorothiazide (paradoxical); indomethacin; amiloride (lithium-induced)

## SIADH

### Diagnostic Criteria

- Serum osmolality <275 mOsm/kg
- Urine osmolality >100 mOsm/kg (inappropriately concentrated)
- Urine sodium >20-40 mEq/L (inappropriate natriuresis)
- Euvolemia (no edema, no volume depletion)
- Normal adrenal, thyroid, renal, and hepatic function

### Causes

- CNS: meningitis, SAH, head trauma, stroke, abscess
- Pulmonary: SCLC (ectopic ADH), pneumonia, TB, ARDS
- Drugs: SSRIs, carbamazepine, cyclophosphamide, oxcarbazepine, MDMA, NSAIDs, opioids, chlorpropamide, tricyclics

### Treatment

- Mild asymptomatic: fluid restriction 800-1000 mL/day
- Moderate (Na 125-130): fluid restriction + identify/treat cause
- Severe symptomatic (seizures, coma): 3% hypertonic saline IV
  - Correction rate: no more than 10-12 mEq/L in 24 hours; 8 mEq/L in first 24h in high-risk patients
  - Overly rapid correction: osmotic demyelination syndrome (central pontine myelinolysis) — irreversible
- Tolvaptan (vasopressin V2 receptor antagonist): for chronic SIADH; raises sodium by aquaresis; hepatotoxicity risk; limited to 30 days

## Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia Syndromes

### MEN1 (Wermer's Syndrome) — "3 P's"

| Component | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Parathyroid | Multigland hyperplasia; most common manifestation; hypercalcemia |
| Pancreas (islets) | Gastrinoma (Zollinger-Ellison), insulinoma, VIPoma, glucagonoma, non-functioning |
| Pituitary | Prolactinoma most common; GH-, ACTH-secreting adenomas |
| Genetics | MEN1 gene (menin) autosomal dominant |

### MEN2A (Sipple's Syndrome)

| Component | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Medullary thyroid carcinoma | 95%; often multifocal and bilateral; calcitonin marker |
| Pheochromocytoma | 50%; bilateral; rule out before thyroid surgery |
| Primary hyperparathyroidism | 20-30% |
| Genetics | RET proto-oncogene mutations (codon 618, 620, 634 most common) |
| Management | Prophylactic thyroidectomy (timing based on specific RET codon) |

### MEN2B

| Component | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Medullary thyroid carcinoma | Most aggressive; onset in infancy/childhood |
| Pheochromocytoma | 50% |
| Marfanoid habitus | Long limbs, joint laxity |
| Mucosal neuromas | Tongue, lips, eyelids |
| Ganglioneuromatosis | GI tract |
| Genetics | RET codon 918 mutation (>95%) |
| Management | Prophylactic thyroidectomy within first 6 months of life |

### MEN4

- CDKN1B (p27) mutations
- Similar phenotype to MEN1 but MEN1 gene negative
