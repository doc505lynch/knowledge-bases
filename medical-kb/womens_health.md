## Prenatal Care

### First Trimester (Weeks 1-13)

**Initial Visit Goals**
- Confirm intrauterine pregnancy (IUP) with transvaginal ultrasound
- Establish gestational dating (last menstrual period vs. ultrasound — use US dating if discrepancy >7 days in first trimester, >10 days in second trimester)
- Assess for multiple gestation, fetal cardiac activity, crown-rump length (CRL)
- Calculate estimated due date (EDD): Naegele's rule — LMP + 9 months + 7 days, or LMP + 280 days

**First Trimester Laboratory Screening**

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| CBC with differential | Baseline anemia, thrombocytopenia |
| Blood type and Rh factor | Identify Rh-negative status for RhoGAM prophylaxis |
| Antibody screen (indirect Coombs) | Alloimmunization risk |
| Rubella IgG | Immune status; counsel non-immune to avoid exposure |
| Varicella IgG | Immune status; VZV in pregnancy causes severe maternal disease |
| HBsAg | Hepatitis B; HBIG + vaccine for neonate if positive |
| HCV antibody | Hepatitis C; no prophylaxis for neonate, monitor |
| HIV 1/2 Ag/Ab | Universal offer; ARV therapy reduces vertical transmission to <1% |
| Syphilis RPR/VDRL | Universal screen; congenital syphilis prevention |
| GC/Chlamydia NAAT | Screen all women at first prenatal visit |
| Urine culture | Asymptomatic bacteriuria (ABU) — treat even if asymptomatic in pregnancy due to pyelonephritis risk |
| TSH | Subclinical hypothyroidism associated with adverse outcomes; overt hypothyroidism requires levothyroxine dose adjustment |
| Pap smear | If due (per cervical cancer screening interval) |
| 1-hour glucose challenge (optional) | Early GDM screen if high-risk (prior GDM, obesity, PCOS, family history) |

**Genetic Screening — First Trimester**

- Cell-free fetal DNA (cfDNA/NIPT): screens for trisomy 21 (Down syndrome), trisomy 18 (Edwards), trisomy 13 (Patau), sex chromosome aneuploidies; can determine fetal sex; preferred for high-risk (AMA, prior aneuploidy, abnormal anatomy); positive NIPT requires diagnostic confirmation
- Nuchal translucency (NT) ultrasound: performed at 11-14 weeks; NT ≥2.5-3.0mm increases risk for trisomy 21, cardiac defects, Turner syndrome
- Combined first-trimester screen: NT + PAPP-A + free beta-hCG; detection rate ~85-90% for trisomy 21
- Diagnostic testing: chorionic villus sampling (CVS) at 10-13 weeks (miscarriage risk 0.5-1%); amniocentesis at 15-20 weeks (miscarriage risk 0.1-0.3%)

**Prenatal Vitamins and Supplementation**

| Supplement | Dose | Indication |
|-----------|------|-----------|
| Folic acid | 0.4 mg/day standard; 4 mg/day if prior NTD, on antiepileptics (valproate, carbamazepine), celiac disease, or malabsorption | Neural tube defect prevention; begin pre-conception |
| Iron | 27 mg/day (most prenatal vitamins); 60-120 mg elemental iron for iron deficiency anemia | Increased maternal and fetal demand |
| DHA (omega-3) | 200-300 mg/day | Fetal brain and retinal development |
| Calcium | 1000-1200 mg/day total (diet + supplement) | Fetal skeletal development; may reduce preeclampsia risk in low-calcium populations |
| Vitamin D | 600 IU/day minimum; higher doses if deficient | Calcium absorption, fetal bone mineralization |
| Iodine | 150-220 mcg/day | Fetal thyroid function; ensure prenatal vitamin contains iodine |

**Common First Trimester Complaints**

- Nausea and vomiting of pregnancy (NVP): onset 4-9 weeks, peaks 7-12 weeks, resolves by 20 weeks in 90%
  - Non-pharmacologic: small frequent meals, avoid triggers, ginger (1 g/day), acupressure bands, bland foods
  - Pharmacologic: vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) 25 mg TID; doxylamine 12.5 mg (Unisom SleepTabs) + B6 (Bonjesta/Diclegis); ondansetron 4-8 mg q8h (off-label but widely used after first trimester); metoclopramide; promethazine
  - Hyperemesis gravidarum: >5% pre-pregnancy weight loss + ketonuria + dehydration; IV fluids, IV antiemetics, thiamine supplementation (deficiency causes Wernicke's), TPN for refractory cases

- Bleeding: evaluate for subchorionic hemorrhage, threatened/incomplete abortion, ectopic pregnancy; quantify loss, check Rh status (RhoGAM if Rh-negative and >8 weeks or significant bleeding)

### Second Trimester (Weeks 14-27)

**Screening and Testing**

- Quad screen (maternal serum analytes): performed at 15-20 weeks (optimal 16-18 weeks)
  - AFP: elevated in NTDs (anencephaly, open spina bifida), abdominal wall defects, multiple gestation, underestimated gestational age; low in trisomy 21
  - hCG: elevated in trisomy 21; low in trisomy 18
  - Unconjugated estriol (uE3): low in trisomy 21, trisomy 18, Smith-Lemli-Opitz
  - Inhibin A: elevated in trisomy 21
  - Detection rate ~80% for trisomy 21

- Anatomy ultrasound (18-20 weeks): detailed survey of fetal anatomy; assess placental location (previa), amniotic fluid index (AFI) or maximum vertical pocket (MVP), fetal growth, cervical length

- Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) screen: 24-28 weeks (see GDM section)

- Cell-free DNA: can be offered throughout pregnancy; second trimester cfDNA less sensitive than first trimester for open NTDs (use AFP instead)

**Common Second Trimester Complaints**

- Heartburn/gastroesophageal reflux: progesterone relaxes lower esophageal sphincter; antacids (calcium carbonate, magnesium hydroxide — safe); H2 blockers (famotidine — preferred; ranitidine withdrawn due to NDMA); PPIs (omeprazole, pantoprazole — safe in 2nd/3rd trimester)
- Constipation: increased progesterone slows motility; iron supplements worsen; fiber 25-35g/day; increased fluid; docusate sodium (stool softener); MiraLax (polyethylene glycol — safe); avoid stimulant laxatives
- Round ligament pain: sharp stabbing pain in lower abdomen/inguinal area with movement; caused by stretching of round ligaments; reassure; heat, positional changes, prenatal support belt
- Back pain: lordosis shifts center of gravity; low-impact exercise, prenatal yoga, physical therapy, acetaminophen (avoid NSAIDs after 20 weeks due to fetal renal effects and premature ductus arteriosus closure)

### Third Trimester (Weeks 28-40+)

**Third Trimester Visits and Testing**

| Gestational Age | Intervention |
|----------------|-------------|
| 28 weeks | Repeat CBC, glucose screen if not done; RhoGAM 300 mcg IM for Rh-negative women |
| 28-32 weeks | Fetal kick counts (patient education); discuss birth preferences |
| 35-37 weeks | GBS vaginal-rectal culture; repeat CBC; discuss labor signs, when to go to hospital |
| 36-38 weeks | Group B Strep results review; confirm fetal presentation |
| 39-40 weeks | Cervical exam if patient desires; discuss post-dates management |
| 41 weeks | Non-stress test (NST) + amniotic fluid assessment; offer induction |
| 42 weeks | Induction recommended (increased stillbirth risk beyond 42 weeks) |

**Group B Streptococcus (GBS)**
- Screen: low vaginal and rectal swab at 35-37 weeks
- Intrapartum prophylaxis (IAP) indications: GBS-positive culture; prior infant with GBS disease; GBS bacteriuria any concentration in current pregnancy; unknown GBS status with preterm labor <37 weeks, rupture of membranes >18 hours, or intrapartum fever ≥38.0°C
- Treatment:
  - Penicillin G 5 million units IV load, then 2.5-3 million units IV q4h until delivery (first-line)
  - Ampicillin 2g IV load, then 1g IV q4h until delivery (alternative)
  - Penicillin allergy (low risk of anaphylaxis): cefazolin 2g IV load, then 1g IV q8h
  - High allergy risk or anaphylaxis history: clindamycin (if susceptible by sensitivity testing); vancomycin 20 mg/kg IV q8h (max 3g/dose) if resistant or unknown
- GBS-negative cultures within 5 weeks are valid; if culture >5 weeks old, treat as unknown status

## Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy

### Classification

| Disorder | Definition | Key Features |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Chronic hypertension | BP ≥140/90 before 20 weeks gestation or pre-existing | Pre-dates pregnancy or early presentation |
| Gestational hypertension | BP ≥140/90 after 20 weeks, no proteinuria or end-organ damage | Resolves within 12 weeks postpartum |
| Preeclampsia | BP ≥140/90 after 20 weeks + proteinuria OR end-organ damage | New-onset; see severe features below |
| Superimposed preeclampsia | Chronic HTN + new-onset proteinuria or worsening BP or end-organ damage | Worse maternal/fetal outcomes |
| Eclampsia | Generalized tonic-clonic seizures in setting of preeclampsia | Neurologic emergency |
| HELLP syndrome | Hemolysis + elevated liver enzymes + low platelets | May present without hypertension |

### Preeclampsia Diagnostic Criteria

**Blood pressure:** Systolic ≥140 mmHg OR diastolic ≥90 mmHg on two occasions at least 4 hours apart (or ≥160/110 confirmed within minutes for severe)

**Proteinuria (any one):**
- 24-hour urine protein ≥300 mg
- Protein:creatinine ratio ≥0.3
- Urine dipstick 2+ (only when quantitative methods not available)

**OR end-organ damage without proteinuria (any one):**
- Platelets <100,000/mcL
- Serum creatinine >1.1 mg/dL or doubling from baseline
- Liver transaminases (AST or ALT) >2x upper limit of normal (ULN)
- Pulmonary edema
- New-onset headache unresponsive to medication and not accounted for by alternative diagnoses
- Visual disturbances (scotomata, blurred vision, photopsia)

### Severe Features of Preeclampsia

Any one of the following:
- Systolic BP ≥160 or diastolic BP ≥110 (on two occasions ≥4 hours apart while patient is on bed rest)
- Platelets <100,000/mcL (thrombocytopenia)
- Serum creatinine >1.1 mg/dL or doubling of baseline creatinine
- AST or ALT >2x ULN with new-onset right upper quadrant or epigastric pain
- Pulmonary edema
- New-onset headache unresponsive to acetaminophen
- Visual disturbances

### HELLP Syndrome

**Diagnostic Criteria (Tennessee Classification):**
- Hemolysis: abnormal peripheral smear (schistocytes, burr cells), LDH >600 IU/L, bilirubin >1.2 mg/dL
- Elevated liver enzymes: AST >70 IU/L (or >2x ULN), severe right upper quadrant/epigastric pain
- Low platelets: <100,000/mcL

**Mississippi Classification (partial vs. complete HELLP):**
- Class 1 (severe): platelets <50,000
- Class 2 (moderate): platelets 50,000-100,000
- Class 3 (mild/partial): platelets 100,000-150,000

**Management:**
- Stabilize BP, initiate magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis
- Deliver at ≥34 weeks; at <34 weeks consider 24-48 hour corticosteroids for fetal lung maturity then deliver
- Dexamethasone (10 mg IV q12h) may accelerate platelet recovery (controversial, not standard of care)
- Platelet transfusion if <50,000 before cesarean or <20,000 at any time

### Antihypertensive Agents in Pregnancy

**Acute Severe Hypertension (BP ≥160/110) — Treat within 30-60 minutes:**

| Agent | Dose | Notes |
|-------|------|-------|
| Labetalol IV | 20 mg IV over 2 min; if inadequate response, give 40 mg at 10 min, then 80 mg at 10 min; max cumulative 300 mg | First-line; onset 5 min; avoid in asthma, decompensated heart failure |
| Hydralazine IV | 5-10 mg IV over 2 min; repeat 10 mg q20-30 min; max 20-25 mg | Reflex tachycardia; less predictable response than labetalol |
| Nifedipine IR (PO) | 10 mg PO; repeat 10-20 mg in 20 min if needed; max 30 mg acute | Short-acting only; do NOT use sublingual (precipitous drop); concern for interaction with Mg (theoretical) |

- Goal: reduce SBP to 140-155 mmHg and DBP to 90-105 mmHg; avoid rapid reduction (uteroplacental insufficiency)
- Reassess BP 30-60 min after each dose; escalate if no response after 3 doses

**Chronic Oral Management:**

| Agent | Dose | Safety |
|-------|------|--------|
| Labetalol | 100-400 mg PO BID-TID | Safe; preferred; neonatal bradycardia risk (monitor) |
| Nifedipine XL | 30-120 mg PO daily | Safe; good efficacy; calcium channel blocker |
| Methyldopa | 250-500 mg PO BID-QID | Longest safety record; sedation limits use |
| Hydralazine | 10-25 mg PO BID-QID | Less frequently used oral form; reflex tachycardia |

**Contraindicated in Pregnancy:**
- ACE inhibitors (captopril, lisinopril, enalapril): fetal renal tubular dysplasia, oligohydramnios, renal failure, skull hypoplasia, death — category D in 2nd/3rd trimester
- ARBs (losartan, valsartan): same fetal renal toxicity as ACEi — category D
- Renin inhibitors (aliskiren): similar mechanism, avoid

### Magnesium Sulfate

**Indications:**
- Eclamptic seizure treatment
- Seizure prophylaxis in preeclampsia with severe features
- Fetal neuroprotection when delivery anticipated at <32 weeks (reduces risk of cerebral palsy — BEAM trial)

**Protocol:**
- Loading dose: 4-6 g IV over 20-30 minutes
- Maintenance: 1-2 g/hour continuous IV infusion
- Continue 24-48 hours postpartum (highest seizure risk is postpartum period)

**Monitoring Parameters:**

| Parameter | Safe Range | Action if Abnormal |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Urine output | >25-30 mL/hr | Decrease rate or hold if oliguria; Mg is renally cleared |
| Respiratory rate | >12 breaths/min | Hold infusion; prepare antidote |
| Deep tendon reflexes | Present | Loss of DTR at ~7-10 mEq/L (early toxicity sign) |
| Oxygen saturation | >95% | Supplemental O2; consider hold |

**Magnesium Toxicity Progression:**

| Serum Level (mEq/L) | Effect |
|--------------------|--------|
| 4-7 (therapeutic) | Seizure prophylaxis |
| 7-10 | Loss of deep tendon reflexes |
| 10-13 | Respiratory depression, respiratory arrest |
| 15+ | Cardiac arrest |

**Antidote:** Calcium gluconate 1 g (10 mL of 10% solution) IV over 3 minutes; have at bedside at all times

### Delivery Timing

| Condition | Recommended Delivery |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Gestational hypertension | ≥37 weeks |
| Preeclampsia without severe features | ≥37 weeks |
| Preeclampsia with severe features | ≥34 weeks (or earlier for maternal/fetal deterioration) |
| HELLP syndrome | ≥34 weeks; <34 weeks: steroids then delivery within 48h |
| Eclampsia | Stabilize first (Mg, BP control), then delivery regardless of GA |

### Aspirin Prophylaxis

- Dose: aspirin 81 mg/day
- Start: 12-16 weeks gestational age (before placentation complete)
- Stop: 36-37 weeks (bleeding risk with delivery)

**High-risk factors (1 factor sufficient):**
- Prior preeclampsia (especially early-onset or severe)
- Multifetal gestation
- Chronic kidney disease
- Autoimmune disease (SLE, antiphospholipid syndrome)
- Pre-existing diabetes (Type 1 or 2)
- Chronic hypertension

**Moderate-risk factors (2+ factors):**
- Nulliparity
- Obesity (BMI >30)
- Family history of preeclampsia
- Age ≥35
- Personal history of adverse pregnancy outcome
- Interpregnancy interval >10 years
- Low socioeconomic status / Black race (structural risk factor)

## Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH)

### Definition
- Primary PPH: blood loss >500 mL (vaginal delivery) or >1000 mL (cesarean delivery) within 24 hours of delivery; OR any blood loss causing hemodynamic instability
- Secondary PPH: significant bleeding 24 hours to 12 weeks postpartum

### Etiology — The 4 T's

| Cause | Frequency | Examples |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Tone (uterine atony) | ~80% | Overdistended uterus (macrosomia, multiples, polyhydramnios), prolonged labor, augmented labor, infection |
| Trauma | ~10% | Cervical/vaginal lacerations, uterine rupture, uterine inversion, episiotomy extensions |
| Tissue | ~5-10% | Retained placenta, retained placental fragments, placenta accreta spectrum |
| Thrombin | ~1% | Pre-existing coagulopathy, DIC (often from another T), von Willebrand disease, thrombocytopenia |

### Uterotonic Agents

| Agent | Dose | Mechanism | Contraindications |
|-------|------|-----------|------------------|
| Oxytocin (first-line) | 10-40 units in 500-1000 mL NS at 100-150 mL/hr; OR 10 units IM; NEVER undiluted IV bolus | Uterine smooth muscle contraction | Undiluted IV bolus causes severe hypotension, cardiac collapse |
| Carboprost (Hemabate) | 250 mcg IM q15-90 min; max 8 doses (2 mg total) | Prostaglandin F2-alpha; potent uterotonic | Asthma (causes severe bronchospasm); caution in cardiac disease, hypertension |
| Misoprostol (Cytotec) | 600-1000 mcg PR, SL, or PO | Prostaglandin E1 analog | Fewer contraindications; available in low-resource settings; side effects: fever, shivering, diarrhea |
| Methylergonovine (Methergine) | 0.2 mg IM; can repeat q2-4h | Ergot alkaloid; sustained uterine contraction | Hypertension (causes vasoconstriction, dangerous BP elevation) |
| Carbetocin | 100 mcg IV or IM single dose | Long-acting oxytocin analog; approved in Canada/Europe | Not FDA-approved; superior to oxytocin in some studies |

**Tranexamic Acid:**
- Dose: 1 g IV over 10-20 minutes; repeat 1 g if bleeding continues after 30 min or restarts within 24h
- Timing: within 3 hours of delivery (WOMAN trial — reduces death from PPH by 31% when given within 3 hours)
- Mechanism: antifibrinolytic (lysine analog); inhibits plasminogen activation
- Give alongside uterotonics, not instead of

### Surgical and Interventional Management

**Step-by-step escalation:**
1. Bimanual uterine massage and compression
2. Uterotonic agents (oxytocin + second-line agent)
3. Uterine tamponade: intrauterine balloon (Bakri balloon — fill with 300-500 mL saline); uterine packing (gauze or commercial packing systems)
4. Uterine artery embolization (UAE): interventional radiology; best for hemodynamically stable patients; preserves uterus; fertility generally maintained
5. Uterine compression sutures: B-Lynch suture, Hayman suture — brace uterus, reduce blood flow
6. Uterine and internal iliac artery ligation: reduce pelvic blood flow
7. Hysterectomy: definitive; last resort for childbearing; life-saving when other measures fail

### Placenta Accreta Spectrum (PAS)

**Definitions:**
- Accreta: placental villi adhere to myometrium (no decidua basalis)
- Increta: placental villi invade into myometrium
- Percreta: placental villi penetrate through myometrium into serosa or adjacent organs (bladder, bowel)

**Risk Factors:**
- Prior cesarean delivery (primary risk factor)
- Anterior placenta previa with prior cesarean (greatest risk: 3+ prior cesareans + previa — up to 67%)
- Uterine surgery (myomectomy, D&C with scarring, endometrial ablation)
- Asherman syndrome
- Multiparity

**Diagnosis:**
- Ultrasound (screening): loss of clear zone between placenta and myometrium, placental lacunae ("Swiss cheese" appearance), bridging vessels, bladder wall interruption
- MRI: adjunct to ultrasound, better delineation of parametrial invasion or bladder involvement; not required if ultrasound is diagnostic
- Definitive diagnosis: pathology after delivery

**Management:**
- Multidisciplinary team: maternal-fetal medicine, gynecologic oncology, urology, interventional radiology, blood bank, neonatology
- Planned cesarean hysterectomy at 34-35 weeks (balance fetal maturity vs. emergency risk)
- Corticosteroids for fetal lung maturity at 34 weeks if delivery planned before 37 weeks
- Preoperative: type and crossmatch, cell saver, IR consult for possible prophylactic balloon occlusion of internal iliac arteries
- Leave placenta in situ during cesarean; perform hysterectomy without attempting placental removal
- ICU-level care postoperatively

## Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM)

### Screening

**Universal screening approach (ACOG):**
- 1-hour glucose challenge test (GCT): 50 g oral glucose load (non-fasting); measure plasma glucose at 1 hour
  - Threshold: ≥140 mg/dL (some use ≥130 mg/dL for increased sensitivity — more false positives)
  - If positive: proceed to 3-hour diagnostic test

**3-hour 100g oral glucose tolerance test (GTT) — diagnostic:**
- Fasting: ≥95 mg/dL (Carpenter-Coustan) or ≥105 mg/dL (NDDG)
- 1 hour: ≥180 mg/dL (CC) or ≥190 mg/dL (NDDG)
- 2 hours: ≥155 mg/dL (CC) or ≥165 mg/dL (NDDG)
- 3 hours: ≥140 mg/dL (CC) or ≥145 mg/dL (NDDG)
- GDM diagnosed with 2 or more values meeting or exceeding threshold

**One-step approach (IADPSG/WHO — used by some centers):**
- 2-hour 75g OGTT (fasting)
- GDM if fasting ≥92, 1h ≥180, OR 2h ≥153 (any one value)
- Detects more cases (lower threshold); more women diagnosed; maternal/perinatal benefit of treatment less clearly established

### Management

**Glycemic Targets:**
- Fasting: <95 mg/dL
- 1-hour postprandial: <140 mg/dL
- 2-hour postprandial: <120 mg/dL

**Treatment Steps:**
1. Medical nutrition therapy (MNT): carbohydrate-controlled diet (40-45% calories from complex carbs); small frequent meals; registered dietitian referral
2. Exercise: moderate-intensity 30 min/day most days (improves insulin sensitivity)
3. Pharmacotherapy if targets not met after 1-2 weeks of lifestyle changes:
   - Insulin (preferred): NPH insulin at bedtime for fasting hyperglycemia; short-acting insulin (regular, lispro, aspart) before meals for postprandial; insulin does not cross placenta
   - Metformin: oral; crosses placenta; ACOG accepts as alternative to insulin; MiG trial — non-inferior to insulin but higher rate of preterm birth (not significant); about 46% require supplemental insulin
   - Glyburide: once preferred oral agent; now less favored — crosses placenta, associated with neonatal hypoglycemia, macrosomia; not recommended by some guidelines

**Fetal Surveillance:**
- Kick counts from 28 weeks
- NST twice weekly from 32-34 weeks (earlier if poorly controlled or on insulin)
- BPP or modified BPP as needed
- Growth ultrasound every 4 weeks from 28-36 weeks (macrosomia detection)

**Delivery Timing:**
- Well-controlled on diet alone: deliver by 40-41 weeks
- Controlled on medication: deliver by 39-40 weeks
- Poorly controlled: individualize, often 37-39 weeks

**Postpartum:**
- GDM resolves in most women after delivery; recheck 2-hour 75g OGTT at 4-12 weeks postpartum
- Lifetime risk of T2DM: 50-70%; annually screen with fasting glucose or HbA1c
- Lifestyle modification and metformin reduce progression to T2DM (DPP trial)

## Preterm Labor and Birth

### Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Preterm labor | Regular contractions + cervical change before 37 0/7 weeks |
| Preterm birth | Delivery before 37 0/7 weeks |
| Late preterm | 34 0/7 - 36 6/7 weeks |
| Very preterm | <32 weeks |
| Extremely preterm | <28 weeks |
| Periviable | 22 0/7 - 25 6/7 weeks (institution-dependent viability threshold) |

### Risk Factors

- Prior preterm birth (greatest risk; recurrence risk 15-50% depending on gestational age of prior birth)
- Short cervical length (<25 mm before 24 weeks on transvaginal US)
- Multiple gestation
- Cervical insufficiency (history of mid-trimester painless cervical dilation)
- Uterine anomalies (bicornuate, septate uterus)
- Infection (bacterial vaginosis, chorioamnionitis, UTI)
- Polyhydramnios
- Placenta previa or abruption
- PPROM (preterm premature rupture of membranes)
- Smoking, substance use, low BMI, nutritional deficiency
- Black race (significant disparity — likely multifactorial including structural racism)

### Diagnosis

- Clinical: regular painful contractions (≥4 in 20 min or ≥8 in 60 min) + documented cervical change OR initial cervical dilation ≥2 cm or effacement ≥80%
- Fetal fibronectin (fFN): glycoprotein normally found at choriodecidual junction; negative value (<50 ng/mL) has high negative predictive value (>95%) for delivery within 7-14 days; low positive predictive value; most useful to RULE OUT preterm birth

### Antenatal Corticosteroids

**Indications:** threatened or likely preterm delivery at 24 0/7 - 33 6/7 weeks (also consider 34 0/7 - 36 6/7 weeks — late preterm steroids, ALPS trial)

**Regimen:**
- Betamethasone 12 mg IM q24h x2 doses (preferred)
- Dexamethasone 6 mg IM q12h x4 doses (alternative)

**Benefits (reduce):**
- Respiratory distress syndrome (RDS) — primary benefit
- Intraventricular hemorrhage (IVH)
- Necrotizing enterocolitis (NEC)
- Neonatal mortality

**Rescue course:** single rescue course if first course given >14 days ago and delivery still anticipated before 34 weeks

### Tocolytics

Tocolytics delay delivery 48 hours (to allow steroids to work) and allow maternal transport; no tocolytic has been shown to significantly improve long-term neonatal outcomes

| Agent | Mechanism | Dose | Side Effects | Contraindications |
|-------|----------|------|-------------|------------------|
| Indomethacin | COX inhibitor (prostaglandin synthesis inhibitor) | 50-100 mg PR or PO load, then 25-50 mg PO q6h | Renal: oligohydramnios; Cardiac: premature ductus arteriosus closure | Use <32 weeks; limit to 48-72 h; >32 weeks contraindicated |
| Nifedipine | Calcium channel blocker | 30 mg PO load, then 10-20 mg q4-6h | Maternal hypotension, headache, flushing | Relative CI with Mg, cardiac disease |
| Magnesium sulfate | NMDA receptor antagonist; mechanism unclear | 4-6 g IV load, then 1-3 g/hr | Maternal flush, diplopia, nausea; respiratory depression at toxic levels | Renal failure, myasthenia gravis |
| Terbutaline | Beta-2 agonist | 0.25 mg SQ q20 min x3 (acute only) | Maternal tachycardia, palpitations, hypotension, pulmonary edema; fetal tachycardia | NOT for maintenance (FDA black box warning); cardiac disease |

### Magnesium for Neuroprotection

- Indication: anticipated delivery <32 weeks
- Protocol: 4-6 g IV over 20-30 min then 1-2 g/hr until delivery or 12 hours
- Evidence: MagNET, ACTOMgSO4, BEAM trials — reduces cerebral palsy risk by ~30%

### Cervical Cerclage

- Indications:
  - History-indicated: prior second-trimester loss due to cervical insufficiency
  - Ultrasound-indicated: short cervix (<25 mm) at 16-24 weeks in women with prior preterm birth
  - Physical exam-indicated (rescue cerclage): dilated cervix with no contractions (emergency)
- Procedure: McDonald (purse-string suture) or Shirodkar (submucosal, more permanent)
- Removal: at 36-37 weeks or when labor begins
- Contraindications: active labor, chorioamnionitis, active bleeding, ROM

### Progesterone

- 17-alpha hydroxyprogesterone caproate (17-OHPC, Makena): 250 mg IM weekly from 16-24 weeks through 36 weeks; for women with prior singleton preterm birth; FDA approval subsequently withdrawn (2023) due to PROLONG trial showing no benefit; decisions now shared between patient and provider
- Vaginal progesterone: 200 mg suppository or 90 mg gel nightly from 16-24 weeks; for women with short cervix (<25 mm) without prior preterm birth; supported by FIGO guidelines

## Labor and Delivery

### Stages of Labor

| Stage | Phase | Definition | Typical Duration |
|-------|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| First stage — Latent | — | Onset of regular contractions to 6 cm dilation | Up to 20 h nullipara; up to 14 h multipara |
| First stage — Active | — | 6 cm to complete dilation (10 cm) | ~0.5-0.7 cm/h nullipara; ~1-1.2 cm/h multipara |
| Second stage | — | Complete dilation to delivery of fetus | Up to 3h nullipara (2h without epidural); 2h multipara (1h without) |
| Third stage | — | Delivery of placenta | 0-30 min |

**Active management of third stage (AMTSL):** oxytocin 10 units IM or IV infusion immediately after delivery; controlled cord traction; uterine massage — reduces PPH

### Fetal Heart Rate Monitoring

**Category I (Normal):** Baseline 110-160 bpm, moderate variability (6-25 bpm), accelerations present or absent, no late or variable decelerations, no significant decelerations

**Category II (Indeterminate):** All FHR patterns not classified as Category I or III; requires evaluation, continued surveillance, possible intervention

**Category III (Abnormal):** Requires prompt evaluation and delivery if not resolved:
- Sinusoidal pattern
- Absent variability with recurrent late decelerations
- Absent variability with recurrent variable decelerations
- Absent variability with bradycardia

**Intrauterine Resuscitation Maneuvers:**
- Change maternal position (left lateral decubitus)
- IV fluid bolus (500-1000 mL LR)
- Oxygen supplementation (8-10 L/min via face mask — temporary)
- Tocolysis (terbutaline 0.25 mg SQ) for tachysystole
- Amnioinfusion for repetitive variable decelerations (cord compression)
- Expedite delivery if unresponsive

### Pain Management in Labor

| Method | Description | Pros/Cons |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Neuraxial (epidural) | Local anesthetic + opioid via epidural catheter | Most effective; continuous titration; allows conversion to surgical anesthesia; risk: hypotension, motor block, PDPH, fever |
| Combined spinal-epidural (CSE) | Single intrathecal dose + epidural catheter placed | Rapid onset; good for advanced labor or fast progressors |
| Spinal (intrathecal) | Single-dose spinal block | Used for cesarean; not for labor (limited duration) |
| IV opioids | Fentanyl, remifentanil PCA, morphine, nalbuphine | Less effective than epidural; neonatal respiratory depression; limited to patients unable/refusing neuraxial |
| Nitrous oxide | 50% N2O/50% O2 (Entonox) | Self-administered; mild analgesia; anti-anxiety; safe for fetus; less effective than epidural |

### Operative Vaginal Delivery

**Vacuum-assisted (ventouse):**
- Indications: prolonged second stage, fetal distress, maternal exhaustion, cardiac/neurologic condition precluding pushing
- Prerequisites: ≥34 weeks, vertex presentation, engaged head, known fetal position, ruptured membranes, empty bladder, adequate analgesia
- Cup placed at flexion point (posterior fontanelle); traction with contractions; max 3 pulls or 3 pop-offs

**Forceps:**
- Outlet, low, mid forceps based on fetal station
- Requires full dilation, ruptured membranes, vertex or face (mentum anterior) presentation
- Classical forceps: Simpson, Tucker-McLean; rotational: Kielland

**Contraindications to both:** face or brow presentation (forceps exception), fetal coagulation disorder (hemophilia), osteogenesis imperfecta, suspected macrosomia at term (relative)

## Cesarean Delivery

### Indications

**Absolute:**
- Prior classical uterine incision or T/J incision
- Complete placenta previa at term
- Active primary HSV with ROM or labor (relative — per guidelines)
- Umbilical cord prolapse
- Uterine rupture
- Certain fetal malpresentations (transverse lie)

**Common:**
- Arrest of labor (failure to progress)
- Nonreassuring fetal status unresponsive to intrauterine resuscitation
- Prior cesarean (VBAC candidate assessment required)
- Placenta previa/accreta
- Multiple gestation (certain presentations)
- Macrosomia >5000 g (non-diabetic) or >4500 g (diabetic)

### Prophylaxis

- Antibiotics: cefazolin 2 g IV (3 g if >120 kg) 30-60 minutes before skin incision; azithromycin 500 mg IV added to cefazolin for unscheduled cesareans (MATRIX trial — reduces endomyometritis)
- VTE prophylaxis: SCDs (sequential compression devices) placed before incision; LMWH postoperatively for high-risk patients (obesity, prior DVT, malignancy)

### Uterine Incision Types

| Type | Description | VBAC Candidacy |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| Low transverse (LTCS) | Most common; lower uterine segment; horizontal | Yes — TOLAC eligible |
| Low vertical | Vertical in lower segment; used for preterm, malpresentation | Usually yes — depends on extent |
| Classical | Vertical in upper uterine body | No — contraindication to TOLAC |
| T or J extension | Inadvertent extension of low transverse | Discuss with MFM |

### VBAC (Vaginal Birth After Cesarean)

**TOLAC (Trial of Labor After Cesarean) Success Factors:**
- Prior low transverse uterine incision
- Adequate pelvis
- No prior uterine rupture
- No classical or T/J incision
- Facility capable of emergency cesarean

**Success Rates:** overall 60-80%; prior vaginal birth (including prior VBAC) significantly increases success

**Uterine Rupture Risk:** ~0.5-0.9% with TOLAC after one prior LTCS; ~1.8-2% with ≥2 prior cesareans

**Signs of Uterine Rupture:** sudden loss of fetal station, fetal bradycardia, loss of contraction pattern, maternal abdominal pain/shoulder tip pain, vaginal bleeding, maternal hemodynamic instability — obstetric emergency, proceed to immediate cesarean

## Postpartum Care

### Normal Postpartum Changes

- Lochia rubra: bright red discharge, days 1-3
- Lochia serosa: pink-brown, days 4-10
- Lochia alba: white/yellow, up to 6 weeks
- Uterine involution: fundus at umbilicus day 1, decreases by 1 cm/day; at pubic symphysis by day 10-14
- Postpartum blues: tearfulness, mood lability days 3-5; resolves by 2 weeks; reassure and support

### Postpartum Depression

**Screening:** Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) at 4-6 weeks and 3-4 months; score ≥10 warrants further evaluation

**Prevalence:** 10-15% of postpartum women; onset within first 4 weeks (DSM-5 specifier) but can occur up to 12 months

**Risk Factors:** prior depression, anxiety, stressful life events, lack of social support, difficult infant temperament, thyroid dysfunction

**Treatment:**
- Mild-moderate: psychotherapy (CBT, IPT); support groups; exercise
- Moderate-severe: SSRIs (sertraline and paroxetine preferred in breastfeeding — low levels in breast milk); SNRIs; refer to psychiatry
- Brexanolone (Zulresso): IV neurosteroid (GABA modulator); FDA-approved for postpartum depression; 60-hour infusion in certified healthcare setting
- Zuranolone (Zurzuvae): oral neurosteroid; FDA-approved 2023; 14-day course; faster onset than SSRIs

**Postpartum Psychosis:** rare (1-2/1000); onset within 2 weeks; hallucinations, delusions, disorganized behavior, thoughts of harm to infant — psychiatric emergency; hospitalize; antipsychotics, mood stabilizers; lithium for bipolar

### Breastfeeding

**Benefits:**
- Infant: reduced otitis media, respiratory infections, GI infections, SIDS, obesity, diabetes, leukemia
- Maternal: faster uterine involution, reduced breast and ovarian cancer risk, possible weight loss benefit

**Lactation Physiology:**
- Prolactin: stimulated by nipple suckling; drives milk production (galactopoiesis)
- Oxytocin: letdown reflex; milk ejection
- Colostrum: first 2-5 days; high immunoglobulins (IgA), protein, low fat
- Transitional milk: days 5-14
- Mature milk: after 2 weeks

**Common Lactation Problems:**

| Problem | Cause | Treatment |
|---------|------|-----------|
| Engorgement | Milk coming in; inadequate emptying | Frequent feeding q2-3h; warm compress before feeding; cold compress after; NSAIDs for pain |
| Mastitis | Breast infection (S. aureus most common) | Dicloxacillin 500 mg QID x10-14d or cephalexin; continue breastfeeding; warm compress; complete drainage |
| Breast abscess | Untreated mastitis | I&D or ultrasound-guided aspiration + antibiotics; may continue breastfeeding from unaffected side |
| Nipple pain/trauma | Poor latch | Lactation consultant; assess latch; lanolin cream |
| Low milk supply | Many causes | Increase feeding frequency; ensure adequate emptying; rule out thyroid, pituitary disease; domperidone (not FDA-approved) |

**Medications in Breastfeeding:**
- LactMed database (NIH): reference for drug safety
- Generally compatible: acetaminophen, NSAIDs (ibuprofen preferred), most antibiotics, most antihistamines, metformin, levothyroxine, sertraline, paroxetine
- Avoid: tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones (cartilage — avoid in infants), chloramphenicol, radioactive iodine, methotrexate, cytotoxic agents, ergotamine, bromocriptine, cocaine, cannabis, alcohol in excess

### Contraception Postpartum

- Immediate postpartum LARC: IUD (Mirena, Paragard) or implant (Nexplanon) placed before hospital discharge (immediate postpartum insertion within 10 min of placental delivery — higher expulsion rate than interval but high uptake)
- Progestin-only pill (POP): safe to start at 6 weeks postpartum (some start earlier); does not affect milk supply
- Combined hormonal (estrogen + progestin): wait until ≥6 weeks postpartum if breastfeeding (estrogen reduces milk supply); ≥3 weeks if not breastfeeding (thrombosis risk)
- Barrier methods: condoms (resume immediately); diaphragm/cervical cap (refit at 6-week visit)
- Lactational amenorrhea method (LAM): >99% effective if exclusively breastfeeding + amenorrheic + infant <6 months — three conditions must all be met

## PCOS (Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome)

### Diagnostic Criteria

**Rotterdam Criteria (2003) — 2 of 3 required:**
1. Oligo- or anovulation (cycle length >35 days or <8 cycles/year, or amenorrhea)
2. Clinical or biochemical hyperandrogenism
   - Clinical: hirsutism (modified Ferriman-Gallwey score ≥8), acne, androgenic alopecia
   - Biochemical: elevated total testosterone, free testosterone, or DHEA-S
3. Polycystic ovarian morphology on ultrasound: ≥12 follicles 2-9 mm in either ovary OR ovarian volume >10 mL (in absence of dominant follicle, corpus luteum, or cyst)

**Required exclusion of:**
- Thyroid dysfunction (hypothyroidism can cause anovulation and menstrual irregularity)
- Hyperprolactinemia
- Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH) — 17-hydroxyprogesterone (17-OHP) elevated; 21-hydroxylase deficiency most common
- Cushing syndrome
- Androgen-secreting tumor

### Pathophysiology

- Insulin resistance (present in 50-70% regardless of BMI) → compensatory hyperinsulinemia
- Hyperinsulinemia → increases ovarian androgen production + decreases SHBG (increases free androgens)
- Increased LH pulsatility (GnRH pulsatility abnormal) → elevated LH, relatively suppressed FSH → LH:FSH ratio >2:1 (not diagnostic requirement)
- Androgen excess → inhibits follicle development → multiple small antral follicles arrested → anovulation

### Laboratory Evaluation

| Test | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| Total and free testosterone | Confirm hyperandrogenism; markedly elevated suggests tumor |
| DHEA-S | Adrenal androgen excess; elevated in adrenal source |
| 17-hydroxyprogesterone (fasting, early follicular) | Exclude non-classic CAH; if >200 ng/dL, perform ACTH stimulation test |
| TSH | Thyroid disease |
| Prolactin | Hyperprolactinemia |
| Fasting glucose and fasting insulin | Insulin resistance; HOMA-IR calculation |
| HbA1c | Diabetes screening |
| 2-hour 75g OGTT | Preferred for impaired glucose tolerance detection in PCOS |
| Fasting lipid panel | Dyslipidemia (elevated LDL, triglycerides; low HDL) |
| LH, FSH | LH:FSH ratio >2:1 supportive but not required |

### Management by Clinical Goal

**Menstrual Regulation and Endometrial Protection:**
- Combined oral contraceptive pills (OCP): first-line; suppress LH (reduces androgens), regulate menses, provide progestogen to protect endometrium from hyperplasia/carcinoma (unopposed estrogen from chronic anovulation is carcinogenic)
- Cyclic progestogen: medroxyprogesterone acetate 10 mg PO daily x10-14 days every 1-3 months (withdrawal bleed); use if OCP contraindicated
- Levonorgestrel IUD: endometrial protection; some androgen activity
- Risk without treatment: 3-fold increased risk of endometrial cancer with chronic anovulation

**Hyperandrogenism (Hirsutism, Acne, Alopecia):**
- OCP: reduces LH-driven androgen production; increases SHBG; reduces free testosterone
- Spironolactone: 50-200 mg/day; aldosterone antagonist with anti-androgen activity (blocks androgen receptor, inhibits 5-alpha reductase); monitor potassium (hyperkalemia risk); teratogen (feminization of male fetus) — use with reliable contraception (usually combined OCP)
- Finasteride: 5-alpha reductase inhibitor; 2.5-5 mg/day; teratogen — do not use in pregnancy
- Flutamide: androgen receptor blocker; hepatotoxicity limits use
- Eflornithine (Vaniqa): topical 13.9% cream; ornithine decarboxylase inhibitor; slows facial hair growth; adjunct to electrolysis or laser

**Insulin Resistance and Metabolic Features:**
- Lifestyle modification: primary intervention; 5-10% weight loss restores ovulation in many women; 150 min/week moderate-intensity aerobic exercise
- Metformin: 500-2000 mg/day (start 500 mg with meals, titrate); improves insulin sensitivity, reduces hepatic glucose production, reduces androgen levels, often restores ovulation; GI side effects (nausea, diarrhea) — take with food; first-line for metabolic features; consider in all women with PCOS for T2DM prevention
- GLP-1 receptor agonists: emerging evidence for PCOS (semaglutide, liraglutide); significant weight loss; improves androgen levels and menstrual regularity; not yet first-line but increasingly used

**Infertility and Ovulation Induction:**
- First-line: letrozole (aromatase inhibitor) 2.5-7.5 mg PO days 3-7 of cycle; superior to clomiphene for live birth rates in PCOS (NICHD Cooperative Reproductive Medicine Network, Legro 2014); reduces multiple gestation risk compared to clomiphene; mechanism: reduces estrogen → removes negative feedback → FSH surge
- Clomiphene citrate: SERM; 50-150 mg days 3-7; has anti-estrogenic endometrial effects (thin endometrium); ovulation rate ~80%, conception rate ~40%
- Metformin: adjunct to letrozole or clomiphene; especially useful when metformin alone restores ovulation
- Gonadotropins (FSH injections): for clomiphene/letrozole failure; high multiple pregnancy risk; requires ultrasound monitoring; low-dose step-up protocol in PCOS
- IVF: for failed ovulation induction, severe disease, or tubal/male factor; caution: OHSS risk (ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome) — use antagonist protocols, trigger with GnRH agonist instead of hCG, freeze-all strategy

**Long-Term Health Management:**
- Annual: blood pressure, fasting glucose or HbA1c, fasting lipids
- Endometrial cancer screening: biopsy if ≥3 years of amenorrhea/oligomenorrhea without progestogen protection; investigate all postmenopausal bleeding
- Cardiovascular risk: higher rates of hypertension, dyslipidemia, metabolic syndrome, possible increased CVD risk

## Menopause

### Definitions and Staging

| Stage | Definition |
|-------|-----------|
| Perimenopause | Variable cycle length (>7 days difference), vasomotor symptoms, rising FSH; spans 4-8 years |
| Menopause | 12 consecutive months of amenorrhea after final menstrual period; average age 51 (range 40-58) |
| Postmenopause | All years following menopause |
| Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI) | FSH >40 IU/L on 2 occasions ≥4 weeks apart, aged <40 years |
| Surgical menopause | Bilateral oophorectomy regardless of age; abrupt estrogen deficiency |

**Causes of POI:** idiopathic (most common); autoimmune (thyroid, adrenal, associated autoimmune panel); chromosomal (Turner syndrome 45,X, fragile X premutation); iatrogenic (chemotherapy, radiation, surgery); infections (mumps oophoritis)

### Symptoms of Menopause

**Vasomotor Symptoms (VMS):**
- Hot flashes: sudden intense heat (face, neck, chest) followed by sweating; last 1-5 minutes; occur up to 20x/day; disrupt sleep (night sweats)
- Prevalence: up to 75-80% of women in perimenopause/early menopause; persist >7 years in most women
- Mechanism: narrowing of thermoneutral zone; norepinephrine and serotonin dysregulation in hypothalamus; NKB (neurokinin B) from KNDy neurons in arcuate nucleus triggers GnRH pulses and thermoregulatory response

**Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause (GSM):**
- Formerly "vulvovaginal atrophy"
- Symptoms: vaginal dryness, burning, irritation; dyspareunia (vulvodynia, introital pain, deep dyspareunia); decreased lubrication; recurrent UTI; urinary urgency, dysuria, frequency
- Physical findings: pale, smooth vaginal walls; loss of rugae; decreased elasticity; pH >5.0; decreased vaginal secretions
- Progressive condition — worsens without treatment; does NOT self-resolve like VMS

**Other Menopause Symptoms:**
- Sleep disturbances (secondary to VMS or independent)
- Mood changes, anxiety, cognitive "brain fog"
- Joint and muscle aches (musculoskeletal syndrome of menopause)
- Hair thinning, skin changes
- Weight redistribution to abdominal region (metabolic shift)
- Decreased libido

### Menopausal Hormone Therapy (MHT/HRT)

**Indications:**
- Bothersome vasomotor symptoms
- GSM (local therapy preferred)
- Prevention of osteoporosis (when other agents not appropriate)
- POI (until average age of natural menopause — 51)
- Surgical menopause under age 51

**Systemic MHT Options:**

| Type | Route | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| Estrogen + progestogen | Oral, transdermal (patch, gel, spray) + oral/IUD progestogen | Required for women with intact uterus; prevents endometrial hyperplasia/cancer |
| Estrogen-only | Oral, transdermal | Only for women post-hysterectomy |
| Estrogen/bazedoxifene (Duavee) | Oral (TSEC — tissue-selective estrogen complex) | Alternative to estrogen + progestogen; bazedoxifene is SERM protecting endometrium; no progestogen needed |

**Progestogen Options:**
- Medroxyprogesterone acetate (MPA, Provera): synthetic; cyclic (10-14 days/month) or continuous; used in WHI — associated with slightly increased breast cancer risk and CV events in older women
- Micronized progesterone (Prometrium): bioidentical; 100-200 mg/day cyclic or continuous; better cardiovascular and breast cancer safety profile than MPA; preferred by many clinicians

**Routes of Estrogen and Clinical Differences:**
- Transdermal (patch, gel, spray): avoids first-pass hepatic metabolism; lower clotting factor stimulation; preferred for women with VTE risk, migraines, gallbladder disease, hypertriglyceridemia, obesity
- Oral: first-pass effect; increases SHBG, clotting factors (higher VTE risk relative to transdermal); increases triglycerides

**WHI Trial Context:**
- Original 2002 results: alarming (increased breast cancer, CVD, stroke, VTE in combined hormone arm) — caused major drop in HRT prescribing
- Subsequent analysis ("timing hypothesis" / "healthy woman" hypothesis): adverse effects were primarily in older women (>60 or >10 years postmenopause); women aged 50-59 or within 10 years of menopause had favorable cardiovascular risk-benefit profile; do not extrapolate WHI results to younger menopausal women
- Breast cancer: slightly increased risk with combined estrogen + MPA; not seen with estrogen alone; micronized progesterone likely has more favorable breast risk profile

**Contraindications to Systemic MHT:**

| Absolute | Relative |
|----------|---------|
| Unexplained vaginal bleeding | Hypertriglyceridemia (use transdermal) |
| Known or suspected breast cancer | Gallbladder disease |
| Other estrogen-sensitive malignancy (endometrial, rare ovarian) | Active liver disease |
| Prior VTE (estrogen-only) — relative for transdermal | Poorly controlled hypertension |
| Active liver disease | Recent MI/stroke (avoid oral; individualize transdermal) |
| Pregnancy | — |

**Local Vaginal Estrogen for GSM:**
- Products: conjugated estrogen cream (Premarin), estradiol cream, estradiol ring (Estring), estradiol vaginal tablet (Vagifem), estradiol vaginal softgel (Imvexxy), DHEA suppository (Intrarosa — converts to estrogen + androgen locally), ospemifene (oral SERM — FDA-approved for dyspareunia)
- Systemic absorption minimal with low-dose local products; no progestogen required
- Safe in breast cancer survivors per ACOG (discuss with oncologist; supported by SGO guidance for women with low-risk breast cancer); preferred over systemic for women with breast cancer history

**Non-Hormonal Management of VMS:**

| Agent | Dose | Evidence |
|-------|------|---------|
| Paroxetine 7.5 mg (Brisdelle) | 7.5 mg/day | Only FDA-approved non-hormonal; reduces hot flash frequency ~35-65% |
| Venlafaxine | 37.5-75 mg/day | Effective; good option for women with depression/anxiety |
| Escitalopram/citalopram | 10-20 mg/day | Effective; well tolerated |
| Desvenlafaxine | 100 mg/day | Approved for MDD; evidence for VMS |
| Gabapentin | 300 mg TID (or nightly for sleep disruption) | Effective; sedation; useful for sleep-related VMS |
| Clonidine | 0.1-0.3 mg/day | Alpha-2 agonist; modest benefit; hypotension limits use |
| Fezolinetant (Veozah) | 45 mg/day | NK3 receptor antagonist; FDA-approved 2023; first non-hormonal targeting VMS mechanism; liver monitoring required |
| CBT-based interventions | Menopause Thoughts program | Reduces hot flash bother and frequency |

## Endometriosis

### Pathophysiology and Theories
- Retrograde menstruation (Sampson's theory): most widely accepted; endometrial cells reflux through fallopian tubes during menses and implant on peritoneal surfaces
- Coelomic metaplasia: peritoneal mesothelial cells undergo metaplastic transformation to endometrial tissue
- Lymphovascular spread: explains distant sites (diaphragm, lung, rare cases)
- Immune dysregulation: impaired immune clearance of ectopic endometrial cells; altered NK cell and macrophage function
- Genetic predisposition: first-degree relatives have 6-fold increased risk

### Symptoms — The Classic "3 D's" Plus More
- Dysmenorrhea: cyclical pelvic pain before/during menses; often progressive worsening over years
- Dyspareunia: deep pelvic pain with intercourse; caused by endometriosis in cul-de-sac, uterosacral ligaments, rectovaginal septum
- Dyschezia: painful defecation (rectosigmoid involvement)
- Dysuria: painful urination (bladder involvement — uncommon)
- Infertility: 30-50% of infertile women have endometriosis; mechanism multifactorial (pelvic adhesions, tubal damage, impaired oocyte quality, impaired implantation, altered peritoneal environment)
- Chronic pelvic pain: non-cyclical; central sensitization contributes

### Diagnosis

**Clinical:**
- History: characteristic cyclical pelvic pain, dyspareunia, dysmenorrhea that is progressive
- Physical examination: fixed retroverted uterus (adhesions); uterosacral ligament nodularity; adnexal mass (endometrioma); tenderness with uterine motion; rectal examination for rectovaginal endometriosis

**Imaging:**
- Transvaginal ultrasound: gold standard for endometrioma detection; endometrioma appears as "ground glass" echogenicity (homogeneous low-level echoes) with no blood flow on Doppler; sensitivity/specificity >90% for endometrioma; poor sensitivity for superficial peritoneal disease
- MRI: superior for deep infiltrating endometriosis (DIE) — rectovaginal septum, bladder, ureteral, parametrial involvement; T1 bright signal in endometrioma (blood products); "shading sign" on T2 characteristic

**Laparoscopy (Gold Standard):**
- Direct visualization of lesions: blue-black powder burn lesions (old blood), clear or red "flame" lesions (active), white fibrotic lesions
- Biopsy confirmation (recommended but not required for surgical treatment)
- Staging per American Society for Reproductive Medicine (ASRM) I-IV based on location, extent, depth of infiltration, presence of adhesions

| ASRM Stage | Description |
|-----------|-----------|
| Stage I (Minimal) | Few superficial implants; score 1-5 |
| Stage II (Mild) | More implants, some deeper; score 6-15 |
| Stage III (Moderate) | Many deep implants, small endometriomas, some filmy adhesions; score 16-40 |
| Stage IV (Severe) | Large endometriomas, dense adhesions, often with DIE; score >40 |

Note: ASRM staging correlates poorly with pain symptoms; does not predict fertility outcomes reliably

**Serum CA-125:** elevated in endometriosis but low specificity (also elevated in malignancy, PID, fibroids, menstruation); not recommended for diagnosis or monitoring

### Medical Management

**NSAIDs:**
- First-line for dysmenorrhea; start 1-2 days before expected menses and continue through heaviest days
- Ibuprofen 400-800 mg TID-QID; naproxen sodium 550 mg BID
- Reduce prostaglandin synthesis; decrease uterine contractions

**Combined Hormonal Contraceptives (CHC):**
- Oral, patch, or vaginal ring; suppress ovulation and reduce endometrial proliferation
- Continuous or extended cycling (no placebo week) more effective for dysmenorrhea than cyclic
- First-line long-term management; evidence for pain reduction; does not cure disease but suppresses progression

**Progestins:**
- Norethindrone acetate: 5 mg PO daily; decidualizes and atrophies endometrial implants; highly effective for pain; add-back therapy approach avoids hypoestrogenic bone loss
- Medroxyprogesterone acetate (DMPA, Depo-Provera): 150 mg IM q3 months; induces amenorrhea; effective for pain; bone density loss with long-term use — DEXA monitoring after 2 years
- Levonorgestrel IUD (Mirena): local progestin delivery; reduces dysmenorrhea; good option for long-term management; systemic effects minimal
- Dienogest: European progestin with high selectivity; not FDA-approved in U.S. but available internationally

**GnRH Agonists:**
- Leuprolide acetate (Lupron): 3.75 mg IM monthly or 11.25 mg IM q3 months
- Nafarelin nasal spray; goserelin implant
- Mechanism: downregulate pituitary GnRH receptors → suppress LH/FSH → hypogonadotropic state → estrogen deficiency → amenorrhea; endometriosis implants atrophy
- Side effects: menopausal (hot flashes, vaginal dryness, sleep disturbance); bone loss (2-3% trabecular bone/6 months — reversible with add-back)
- Add-back therapy: norethindrone 5 mg PO daily (or norethindrone 5 mg + conjugated estrogen 0.625 mg); maintains pain control while preventing bone loss; allow use beyond 6 months
- Maximum 6 months without add-back; with add-back can use 12 months or longer

**GnRH Antagonists:**
- Elagolix (Orilissa): oral; competitive GnRH antagonist; immediate onset (no initial flare); dose-dependent suppression
  - 150 mg daily (partial suppression): up to 24 months; minimal bone loss; suitable for mild-moderate pain
  - 200 mg BID (full suppression): up to 6 months; significant bone loss; severe VMS; use add-back therapy (norethindrone 0.5 mg + E2 1 mg)
- Relugolix/estradiol/norethindrone (Myfembree): combination GnRH antagonist + add-back in single pill; FDA-approved for endometriosis and uterine fibroids

**Aromatase Inhibitors:**
- Letrozole or anastrozole + progestin or OCP
- Endometriosis lesions express aromatase and locally produce estrogen; aromatase inhibition reduces local and systemic estrogen
- Reserved for refractory cases; significant bone loss risk; off-label in premenopausal women

### Surgical Management

**Laparoscopic Excision vs. Ablation:**
- Excision (preferred): complete resection of implants; allows histologic confirmation; lower recurrence rate than ablation; superior pain outcomes for deep infiltrating endometriosis
- Ablation (fulguration, vaporization): destroys surface of lesion without excision; appropriate for superficial peritoneal disease; faster; higher recurrence

**Endometrioma:**
- Laparoscopic cystectomy (stripping technique): peel cyst wall from ovarian cortex; reduces recurrence vs. drainage; some ovarian reserve loss (stripping can remove follicles adjacent to cyst)
- Simple drainage alone: high recurrence rate (80-100%); not recommended as definitive treatment
- Endometrioma >4 cm: surgical management before IVF preferred to improve access to follicles and reduce infection risk during oocyte retrieval

**Definitive Surgery:**
- Total hysterectomy ± bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy (BSO)
- For completed childbearing and refractory severe disease
- BSO: removes estrogen source; most effective at reducing recurrence; surgical menopause in premenopausal women
- HRT after surgical menopause: generally recommended for quality of life; oral, transdermal, or local; risk of recurrence with estrogen alone low but possible (add progestogen if residual endometriosis)

**Surgical for Infertility:**
- Stage I-II: laparoscopic surgery (excision/ablation of lesions) improves spontaneous conception rates (NNT approximately 12)
- Stage III-IV: benefit of surgery on fertility less clear; IVF often recommended especially if failed expectant or surgical management, advanced age, or tubal/male factor
- Endometrioma: cystectomy may improve oocyte access but reduces ovarian reserve — individualize; IVF with or without prior surgery both acceptable

## Uterine Fibroids (Leiomyomas)

### Epidemiology and Types
- Most common pelvic tumor in women; prevalence 70-80% by age 50 (higher in Black women — earlier onset, larger fibroids, more severe symptoms)
- Types by location: submucosal (within endometrial cavity — most symptomatic; distorts cavity), intramural (within myometrium — most common overall), subserosal (on outer surface), pedunculated (on stalk), cervical

### Symptoms
- Abnormal uterine bleeding: heavy menstrual bleeding (menorrhagia), intermenstrual spotting; submucosal fibroids most symptomatic for bleeding
- Pelvic pressure/pain: bulk symptoms; urinary frequency/urgency (anterior fibroids compress bladder), constipation (posterior fibroids), pelvic heaviness
- Infertility: submucosal fibroids reduce implantation rates; intramural (controversial); subserosal do not affect fertility
- Pregnancy complications: increased risk of preterm labor, placenta previa (if distorting cavity), fetal malpresentation, need for cesarean, postpartum hemorrhage

### Management

**Medical:**
- Hormonal IUD (Mirena): reduces heavy bleeding; does not shrink fibroids; good long-term option
- GnRH agonists (leuprolide): 50% size reduction; preoperative to reduce fibroid size and correct anemia; use limited to 6 months; rebound growth after stopping
- GnRH antagonist + add-back (relugolix combination/Myfembree): oral; FDA-approved; once daily; reduces heavy bleeding and fibroid volume; long-term use possible with add-back
- Tranexamic acid: 1.3 g TID x5 days during menses; reduces bleeding volume ~50%
- NSAIDs: modest bleeding reduction; treat dysmenorrhea
- Oral contraceptives / progestins: limited evidence for fibroid shrinkage; help with bleeding control
- Iron supplementation: treat iron deficiency anemia from chronic bleeding

**Surgical:**
- Hysteroscopic myomectomy: submucosal fibroids (FIGO Type 0, 1, 2); outpatient; preserves uterus
- Laparoscopic or robotic myomectomy: intramural and subserosal; preserves fertility; recovery faster than open
- Open (abdominal) myomectomy: large fibroids, multiple fibroids, poor laparoscopic access; recovery longer
- Hysterectomy: definitive; only cure; fibroid recurrence impossible; for completed childbearing

**Minimally Invasive Non-Surgical:**
- Uterine fibroid embolization (UFE): IR procedure; polyvinyl alcohol particles via femoral artery; infarcts fibroid vascular supply; 70-90% improvement in symptoms; fibroid volume reduction 40-60%; preserves uterus; NOT recommended for women desiring future fertility (uterine artery blood supply to ovaries may be affected; endometrial ischemia; placentation risks)
- MRI-guided focused ultrasound (MRgFUS): non-invasive; thermal ablation; outpatient; limited availability; good for single accessible fibroid; volume reduction 20-30%; recurrence possible
- Radiofrequency ablation (Acessa): laparoscopic; ultrasound-guided needle; thermal ablation of fibroids; preserves uterus

## Ovarian Cysts and Masses

### Functional Cysts (Benign, Usually Self-Limiting)
- Follicular cyst: follicle >2.5 cm that fails to ovulate; typically resolves in 6-8 weeks
- Corpus luteum cyst: after ovulation; can hemorrhage (hemoperitoneum if large — sudden pain); resolves spontaneously
- Theca-lutein cysts: bilateral, multiple; associated with elevated beta-hCG (molar pregnancy, multiple gestation, ovarian hyperstimulation); resolve when hCG normalized

### Benign Ovarian Masses
- Mature cystic teratoma (dermoid): most common benign ovarian tumor; contain ectodermal derivatives (hair, fat, teeth, skin); US: hyperechoic with shadowing (dermoid plug); risk of torsion; remove if >5-6 cm or symptomatic
- Endometrioma: "chocolate cyst"; homogeneous low-level echoes; see Endometriosis section
- Serous cystadenoma: most common benign epithelial tumor; thin-walled, unilocular, serous fluid
- Mucinous cystadenoma: multilocular; can grow very large; mucinous fluid
- Fibroma: solid; can cause Meigs syndrome (fibroma + ascites + pleural effusion — right-sided)

### Risk Stratification (Premenopausal vs. Postmenopausal)

**IOTA (International Ovarian Tumor Analysis) Simple Rules:**

- Benign features (B-features): unilocular, solid components <7 mm, bilateral/multilocular with smooth walls <100 mm, no blood flow, acoustic shadows
- Malignant features (M-features): irregular solid, >4 papillary projections, irregular multilocular >100 mm, very strong blood flow (Color Score 4), ascites

**ACOG Referral Guidelines for Adnexal Mass:**

| Premenopausal | Postmenopausal |
|--------------|---------------|
| CA-125 markedly elevated or M-features | Any solid or semisolid component |
| Ascites | CA-125 elevation |
| Concern for cancer on imaging | Ascites |

**CA-125 Limitations:**
- Elevated in endometriosis, fibroids, PID, peritonitis, liver disease, early pregnancy — low specificity for cancer in premenopausal women
- Better specificity in postmenopausal women
- Use ROMA (Risk of Ovarian Malignancy Algorithm) combining CA-125 + HE4 + menopausal status for improved risk stratification

### Ovarian Torsion
- Surgical emergency; adnexa twists on its vascular pedicle; venous and arterial occlusion → ischemia → necrosis
- Risk factors: ovarian cyst or mass >5 cm, dermoid, high-risk pregnancy (hyperstimulation), history of torsion
- Presentation: sudden severe unilateral pelvic pain, often with nausea/vomiting; may have adnexal mass; often no fever unless necrosis
- Ultrasound: enlarged ovary with absent (or rarely present) blood flow on Doppler; whirlpool sign of twisted pedicle; normal Doppler does NOT rule out torsion (25-30% have flow present)
- Treatment: emergent laparoscopy with detorsion (untwisting); ovarian cystectomy or oophorectomy depending on tissue viability; oophoropexy (fixation) of contralateral ovary in recurrent cases; attempt ovarian conservation even if appears necrotic (recovery possible)

## Cervical Cancer Screening

### Current Guidelines (USPSTF 2018 / ACOG / ASCCP)

| Age Group | Recommended Screening | Interval |
|-----------|----------------------|---------|
| <21 years | No screening | — |
| 21-29 years | Cytology (Pap) alone | Every 3 years |
| 30-65 years | Preferred: Pap + hrHPV co-test | Every 5 years |
| 30-65 years | Acceptable: Pap alone | Every 3 years |
| 30-65 years | Acceptable: hrHPV alone (primary HPV) | Every 5 years |
| >65 years | Discontinue if adequate prior negative screening | — |

**Adequate prior negative screening:** 3 consecutive negative Pap tests OR 2 consecutive negative co-tests within 10 years before stopping, with most recent test within 5 years

**Do NOT screen:** post-hysterectomy (with removal of cervix) for benign indications and no prior CIN2+ history

### ASCCP 2019 Risk-Based Management

- Management based on estimated immediate CIN3+ risk rather than specific result alone
- Immediate CIN3+ risk ≥4%: colposcopy
- Immediate CIN3+ risk 0.1-4%: 1-year return
- Immediate CIN3+ risk <0.1%: 5-year return
- Risk incorporates current results + prior history

**Colposcopy Indications (High-Risk Cytology/HPV):**
- HSIL (high-grade squamous intraepithelial lesion)
- ASC-H (atypical squamous cells, cannot exclude HSIL)
- AGC (atypical glandular cells) — any category
- AIS (adenocarcinoma in situ)
- HPV 16 or HPV 18 positive (regardless of cytology)
- Persistent LSIL ≥2 years
- Persistent HPV positive without cytologic abnormality ≥2-3 years

### HPV Vaccination

**9-valent HPV vaccine (Gardasil 9):** HPV types 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58; covers ~90% of cervical cancers and ~90% of genital warts

**Schedule:**
- Ages 11-12 (routine): 2-dose series (0, 6-12 months); if series before age 15, 2 doses adequate
- Ages 13-26 (catch-up): 3-dose series if not previously vaccinated
- Ages 27-45: shared clinical decision-making (less benefit as higher likelihood of prior HPV exposure; still beneficial if not previously vaccinated)

## Breast Health

### Mammography Screening Recommendations

| Organization | Age to Start | Interval | Age to Stop |
|-------------|------------|---------|------------|
| USPSTF 2024 | 40 | Every 2 years | 74 (individualize 75+) |
| ACS (average risk) | 40-44 (choice); 45 mandatory | Annual 45-54; biennial option 55+ | Continue while healthy |
| ACOG | 40 | Annual or biennial | Individualize |

**High-Risk Screening (BRCA1/2 or lifetime risk ≥20%):**
- Annual mammography + annual breast MRI starting at age 30 (or 10 years before youngest affected family member)
- Consider bilateral risk-reducing mastectomy discussion

### BRCA1 and BRCA2

| Feature | BRCA1 | BRCA2 |
|---------|-------|-------|
| Lifetime breast cancer risk | 50-72% | 45-69% |
| Lifetime ovarian cancer risk | 44-46% | 11-17% |
| Associated cancers | Triple-negative breast cancer, ovarian | Pancreatic, prostate, male breast |
| Recommended prophylactic oophorectomy age | 35-40 years | 40-45 years (or after childbearing) |

**Risk Reduction Options for BRCA Carriers:**
- Enhanced surveillance (MRI + mammography)
- Chemoprevention: tamoxifen (20 mg/day x5 years), raloxifene (postmenopausal only), aromatase inhibitors (postmenopausal — anastrozole, exemestane); reduce breast cancer incidence by 40-50% in high-risk women
- Bilateral risk-reducing mastectomy: reduces breast cancer risk by >90%; does not eliminate risk (residual breast tissue at skin margin)
- Risk-reducing bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy (RRSO): reduces ovarian cancer risk by ~80%; also reduces breast cancer risk in premenopausal BRCA carriers by ~50% (through surgical menopause); timing: BRCA1 age 35-40, BRCA2 age 40-45 (ovarian cancer risk lower and later in BRCA2)

### Common Benign Breast Conditions

**Fibrocystic changes:**
- Most common benign breast condition; bilateral, diffuse, lumpy/ropey texture; cyclical tenderness worse premenstrually
- No increased cancer risk without atypia on biopsy
- Management: reassure; supportive bra; evening primrose oil; reduce caffeine (limited evidence); NSAIDs; danazol for severe pain (androgenic side effects)

**Fibroadenoma:**
- Most common benign breast mass in women <30; firm, mobile, well-circumscribed, rubbery
- Ultrasound: homogeneous, oval, well-circumscribed hypoechoic mass
- Management: core biopsy to confirm; small (<2 cm), stable fibroadenomas can be observed; excision for enlarging or symptomatic

**Breast cysts:**
- Common in premenopausal women; fluctuate with cycle
- Ultrasound: simple (anechoic, smooth walls, posterior enhancement) vs. complicated vs. complex
- Simple cysts: no aspiration needed; reassure
- Complicated: aspiration if symptomatic
- Complex (internal echoes, thick walls, solid component): biopsy

### LARC — Long-Acting Reversible Contraception

**Hormonal IUDs:**
- Mirena (52 mg LNG): approved 8 years; reduces bleeding by 90%; treats heavy menstrual bleeding; amenorrhea in 20% at 1 year; off-label for endometriosis, fibroids
- Kyleena (19.5 mg LNG): approved 5 years; lighter periods; lower systemic effects; smaller frame
- Liletta (52 mg LNG): approved 8 years; similar to Mirena
- Skyla (13.5 mg LNG): approved 3 years; smallest frame; designed for nulliparous women

**Copper IUD (Paragard):**
- Approved 10 years (used up to 12 years off-label)
- No hormones; spermicidal copper ions
- Most effective emergency contraception if placed within 5 days of unprotected intercourse
- Heavier, more painful periods in first 3-6 months (usually improves)
- Ideal for women avoiding hormones or breastfeeding

**Implant (Nexplanon):**
- 3-year etonogestrel subdermal implant (68 mg); inserted in upper inner arm
- Most effective reversible method (0.05% failure rate)
- Side effects: irregular bleeding (most common reason for removal); amenorrhea in some; headache; acne
- Insert/remove under local anesthesia; immediate effectiveness

### Emergency Contraception (EC)

| Method | Mechanism | Window | Effectiveness |
|--------|----------|--------|--------------|
| Levonorgestrel (Plan B One-Step) | Delays/inhibits ovulation | Within 72h (up to 120h) | 75-89% (decreases with time) |

| Copper IUD | Prevents fertilization and implantation | Within 120h (5 days) | >99%; also provides ongoing contraception |

- LNG EC less effective with obesity (BMI >26 kg/m2); ulipristal acetate preferred; copper IUD most effective regardless of BMI

### Contraindications to Estrogen-Containing Contraceptives (WHO MEC Category 3-4)

- Age ≥35 + smoking ≥15 cigarettes/day
- Hypertension with SBP ≥160 or DBP ≥100
- History of VTE or current VTE
- History of stroke or ischemic heart disease
- Complicated migraine (with aura)
- Active liver disease
- Breastfeeding <6 weeks postpartum
- Antiphospholipid antibodies

## Sexually Transmitted Infections

### Chlamydia and Gonorrhea

**Screening:** USPSTF recommends annual screening for all sexually active women ≤24 years and older women at increased risk (new/multiple partners, inconsistent condom use, prior STI)

**Chlamydia Treatment (IDSA/CDC 2021):**
- Uncomplicated urogenital/rectal: doxycycline 100 mg PO BID x7 days (preferred over azithromycin 1g x1 dose — superior efficacy especially for rectal chlamydia)
- Pregnancy: azithromycin 1g PO single dose; amoxicillin 500 mg TID x7 days; avoid doxycycline

**Gonorrhea Treatment:**
- Uncomplicated urogenital/rectal/pharyngeal: ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose (≥150 kg: 1g IM)
- Penicillin/cephalosporin allergy: test-of-cure at 1-2 weeks; consult ID; limited options (gentamicin 240 mg IM + azithromycin 2g PO)
- Disseminated gonococcal infection (DGI): ceftriaxone 1g IV/IM q24h x7 days
- Dual therapy no longer recommended (azithromycin resistance concerns; CDC 2021 removed azithromycin from uncomplicated gonorrhea unless coexisting chlamydia)

### Syphilis

**Stages and Treatment:**

| Stage | Definition | Treatment |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Primary | Painless chancre, regional lymphadenopathy; RPR may be negative | Benzathine penicillin G 2.4 MU IM single dose |
| Secondary | Diffuse maculopapular rash (palms and soles), condyloma lata, mucous patches, alopecia | Benzathine penicillin G 2.4 MU IM single dose |
| Latent — early (<1 year) | Asymptomatic, positive serology, infection <1 year | Benzathine penicillin G 2.4 MU IM single dose |
| Latent — late or unknown duration | Asymptomatic, positive serology, >1 year or unknown | Benzathine penicillin G 2.4 MU IM weekly x3 doses |
| Tertiary/neurosyphilis | Gummas, cardiovascular, neurologic; CSF abnormalities | Aqueous penicillin G 18-24 MU/day IV x10-14 days |
| Pregnancy | Any stage | Penicillin G (same stage-based dosing); desensitize if allergic — no penicillin alternatives in pregnancy |

**Interpretation:**
- Nontreponemal tests (RPR, VDRL): quantitative; use for screening and monitoring treatment response (4-fold decrease = adequate treatment); can be false positive (lupus, pregnancy, infection)
- Treponemal tests (FTA-ABS, TPPA, syphilis IgG): qualitative; confirm positive RPR; remain positive for life even after treatment

### Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV)

**Primary HSV (first episode):**
- Acyclovir 400 mg TID x7-10 days; or valacyclovir 1g BID x7-10 days; or famciclovir 250 mg TID x7-10 days

**Recurrent HSV (episodic therapy):**
- Valacyclovir 500 mg BID x3 days; or acyclovir 800 mg TID x2 days; initiate at prodrome

**Suppressive therapy:**
- Valacyclovir 500-1000 mg daily; acyclovir 400 mg BID; reduces recurrences by 70-80%; reduces transmission risk by 50%

**HSV in Pregnancy:**
- Primary first episode in third trimester: greatest risk of neonatal HSV; consider IV acyclovir for primary infection; cesarean delivery
- Suppressive acyclovir/valacyclovir from 36 weeks: reduces recurrence at delivery, reduces need for cesarean
- Cesarean delivery: active genital lesions (primary or recurrent) at onset of labor; prodrome without visible lesions is controversial (individualize)
- Neonatal HSV: high mortality/morbidity; IV acyclovir 20 mg/kg q8h; empiric treatment if exposure risk

### Bacterial Vaginosis (BV)

**Diagnosis:**
- Amsel criteria (3 of 4): thin gray/white homogeneous discharge; pH >4.5; positive whiff test (amine odor with KOH); clue cells on wet prep (>20% of epithelial cells)
- Nugent score on Gram stain (gold standard research criterion): score 7-10 = BV
- NAAT (Aptima BV): FDA-approved; highest sensitivity/specificity

**Treatment:**
- Metronidazole 500 mg PO BID x7 days (first-line)
- Metronidazole 0.75% vaginal gel 5g intravaginally daily x5 days
- Clindamycin 2% cream 5g intravaginally at bedtime x7 days
- Tinidazole 2g PO daily x2 days or 1g PO daily x5 days
- Secnidazole 2g PO single dose (Solosec)

**In Pregnancy:**
- Treat symptomatic women; treatment reduces symptoms but may not reduce preterm birth risk
- Oral metronidazole preferred (vaginal clindamycin associated with adverse outcomes in some studies)
- Avoid alcohol during treatment and 24 hours after metronidazole; 72 hours after tinidazole

### Trichomonas vaginalis

**Symptoms:** profuse yellow-green frothy malodorous discharge, vulvar pruritus, dyspareunia, "strawberry cervix" (colpitis macularis)

**Diagnosis:** wet prep (flagellated motile trophozoites — low sensitivity 50-70%); NAAT (most sensitive); Affirm VPIII (DNA probe)

**Treatment:**
- Women: metronidazole 500 mg PO BID x7 days (preferred over single dose — higher cure rate, especially HIV-positive)
- Men: metronidazole 2g PO single dose
- Pregnancy: metronidazole 500 mg BID x7 days; treatment reduces symptoms; data conflicting on preterm birth reduction
- Treat partners; test of cure at 3 months (high reinfection rate)

## Urinary Incontinence and Pelvic Floor Disorders

### Types of Urinary Incontinence

| Type | Mechanism | Symptoms | Treatment |
|------|----------|---------|----------|
| Stress incontinence (SUI) | Urethral hypermobility or intrinsic sphincter deficiency; failure of urethral closure mechanism with increased intraabdominal pressure | Leakage with cough, sneeze, laugh, exercise | Pelvic floor exercises (Kegel), pessary, midurethral sling |
| Urgency incontinence (UUI) | Detrusor overactivity; involuntary bladder contractions | Sudden strong urge to void with leakage; frequency, nocturia | Behavioral, bladder training, anticholinergics/beta-3 agonists |
| Mixed incontinence | Both SUI and UUI | Both types of symptoms | Address both components |
| Overflow incontinence | Incomplete bladder emptying; detrusor underactivity or outlet obstruction | Frequent small volume leakage; sensation of incomplete emptying | Treat underlying cause; catheterization |

### Evaluation
- History: type, frequency, severity (pad use), impact on quality of life; fluid intake; medications (diuretics, alpha-blockers, ACEi cough)
- Physical exam: pelvic exam; stress test (Valsalva or cough with full bladder); assess prolapse; Q-tip test (urethral hypermobility if >30-degree deflection)
- Urinalysis and culture: rule out UTI, hematuria
- Postvoid residual: ultrasound or catheter; PVR >150-200 mL suggests incomplete emptying
- Urodynamics: indicated before surgical treatment; complex cases; if diagnosis unclear

### Conservative Treatment
- **Pelvic floor muscle training (Kegel exercises):** most effective for SUI; 3 sets of 10 repetitions 3x/day; 12-week minimum; better compliance with physical therapy guidance
- **Bladder training:** urgency incontinence; timed voiding schedule with gradual lengthening of intervals; urge suppression techniques
- **Fluid management:** optimize (not restrict) fluid intake; reduce caffeine and alcohol; evening fluid restriction for nocturia
- **Weight loss:** each unit decrease in BMI reduces incontinence episodes
- **Pessaries:** intravaginal devices; stress: ring or Hodge pessary with support knob; prolapse: ring with support, donut, Gellhorn; clean and reinsert every 3 months (if self-managed weekly)

### Pharmacologic Treatment (Overactive Bladder/UUI)

| Drug Class | Agent | Dose | Notes |
|-----------|-------|------|-------|
| Anticholinergics | Oxybutynin | 5 mg TID (IR); 5-30 mg daily (ER) | Most common; dry mouth, constipation, cognitive effects (concern in elderly — AVOID) |
| Anticholinergics | Tolterodine | 1-2 mg BID (IR); 2-4 mg daily (ER) | Similar efficacy to oxybutynin; potentially fewer CNS effects |
| Anticholinergics | Solifenacin | 5-10 mg daily | Once daily; good efficacy; dry mouth |
| Anticholinergics | Darifenacin | 7.5-15 mg daily | M3 selective; less cognitive effect |
| Beta-3 agonist | Mirabegron | 25-50 mg daily | Relaxes detrusor; no anticholinergic SE; may increase BP; first-line in elderly |
| Beta-3 agonist | Vibegron | 75 mg daily | No CYP interactions; does not increase BP |

**Third-line for refractory OAB:**
- Percutaneous tibial nerve stimulation (PTNS): 12-week course of weekly 30-minute sessions
- Sacral neuromodulation (InterStim): implantable device; refractory UUI, urinary retention, fecal incontinence
- OnabotulinumtoxinA (Botox) 100 units intradetrusor injection: effective for 6-12 months; urinary retention risk (may need intermittent self-catheterization)

### Pelvic Organ Prolapse (POP)

**Definitions (POP-Q staging):**
- Stage 0: no prolapse
- Stage I: most distal portion >1 cm above hymen
- Stage II: within 1 cm above or below hymen
- Stage III: >1 cm beyond hymen
- Stage IV: complete eversion/procidentia

**Types:**
- Cystocele: anterior vaginal wall prolapse (bladder)
- Rectocele: posterior vaginal wall prolapse (rectum)
- Enterocele: small bowel herniation (posterior fornix/vault)
- Uterine prolapse: uterus descends toward or beyond introitus
- Vaginal vault prolapse: apex descends after hysterectomy

**Treatment:**
- Observation: asymptomatic Stage I-II; reassure; pelvic floor exercises
- Pessary: all stages; first-line non-surgical; most effective for anterior defects; types include ring, Gellhorn, donut, cube
- Surgery: symptomatic Stage III-IV or failed conservative; vaginal (colporrhaphy, native tissue repair, sacrospinous ligament fixation) vs. laparoscopic/robotic sacrocolpopexy (synthetic mesh to sacral promontory — gold standard for vault prolapse); mesh complications (erosion, exposure, dyspareunia) — FDA removed pelvic mesh from market (vaginal route) 2019; abdominal route still approved

## Vulvovaginal Conditions

### Vulvodynia
- Chronic vulvar pain >3 months without identifiable cause
- Localized (vestibulodynia/vulvar vestibulitis) vs. generalized; provoked (touch-evoked, insertion provoked) vs. unprovoked
- Diagnosis of exclusion (rule out candida, dermatoses, neuralgia, malignancy)
- Treatment: multidisciplinary (pain, physical therapy, psychology); topical lidocaine 5%; topical estrogen (especially postmenopausal or low estrogen states); tricyclics (amitriptyline 10-75 mg nightly); gabapentin; pelvic floor physical therapy (primary for localized provoked); vestibulectomy for refractory localized provoked vulvodynia

### Vulvar Dermatoses

**Lichen Sclerosus:**
- Chronic inflammatory condition; postmenopausal women most common (also children)
- Symptoms: severe pruritus, dyspareunia, dysuria; white parchment-like skin; figure-of-eight pattern (vulva and perianal); architectural changes (labial fusion, clitoral phimosis, introital stenosis)
- Biopsy recommended to confirm diagnosis and exclude malignancy
- Treatment: high-potency topical corticosteroid (clobetasol 0.05% daily x3 months then taper); maintenance therapy ongoing; 4% lifetime risk of squamous cell carcinoma — surveillance required

**Lichen Simplex Chronicus:**
- Thickened, lichenified vulvar skin from chronic scratching; itching-scratching cycle
- Treatment: break itch-scratch cycle; high-potency topical steroid; address underlying irritants/allergens

**Lichen Planus:**
- Erosive form: painful erosions, erythema, whitish lacy reticulation; can cause vaginal adhesions and scarring; associated with other autoimmune conditions
- Treatment: high-potency topical steroids; vaginal dilators to prevent adhesions; systemic immunosuppression for severe cases

### Vulvovaginal Candidiasis (VVC)

**Uncomplicated:**
- Azole therapy (any): fluconazole 150 mg PO single dose; topical: miconazole, clotrimazole, terconazole (various schedules)

**Complicated (severe, recurrent ≥4/year, non-albicans, immunocompromised):**
- Severe: fluconazole 150 mg PO q72h x2 doses; or topical azole x7-14 days
- Recurrent VVC: induction with fluconazole 150 mg q72h x3 doses, then maintenance fluconazole 150 mg weekly x6 months
- C. glabrata/non-albicans: boric acid 600 mg vaginal suppository nightly x14 days; nystatin vaginal suppository; consult ID

## Fertility and Assisted Reproduction

### Infertility Evaluation

**Definition:** failure to conceive after 12 months of regular unprotected intercourse (or 6 months if woman ≥35 years)

**Initial Evaluation:**

| Component | Tests |
|-----------|------|
| Ovarian reserve | AMH (anti-Mullerian hormone); FSH + estradiol day 3; antral follicle count (AFC) on transvaginal US |
| Ovulation | Basal body temperature charting; LH surge (OPK); mid-luteal progesterone ≥3 ng/mL confirms ovulation |
| Uterine cavity | Saline infusion sonogram (SIS/sonohysterogram) or hysterosalpingogram (HSG) — intrauterine pathology (polyps, fibroids, adhesions) |
| Tubal patency | HSG: dye injected through cervix; X-ray shows tubal fill and spill; also evaluates uterine contour |
| Male factor | Semen analysis (WHO 2021): volume ≥1.5 mL, total motility ≥42%, morphology ≥4% normal (Kruger strict), concentration ≥16 million/mL, total motile count ≥12 million |
| Hormonal | TSH, prolactin, FSH, LH, estradiol, AMH |

### Causes of Infertility

| Cause | Frequency |
|-------|---------|
| Male factor (alone or combined) | ~40% |
| Ovulatory dysfunction | ~25% |
| Tubal factor | ~15% |
| Unexplained | ~10-20% |
| Uterine/cervical | ~5-10% |
| Endometriosis | May overlap with multiple categories |

### Assisted Reproductive Technology (ART)

**Intrauterine Insemination (IUI):**
- Processed semen deposited into uterine cavity via catheter at time of ovulation
- Success rate: 10-20% per cycle (higher with ovarian stimulation)
- Indications: unexplained infertility, mild male factor (total motile count >5-10 million), cervical factor, donor sperm

**In Vitro Fertilization (IVF):**
- Controlled ovarian hyperstimulation → oocyte retrieval → fertilization in lab → embryo transfer
- Protocols: GnRH antagonist (most common — flexible, shorter duration, lower OHSS risk); GnRH agonist long-lupron protocol (more predictable, standard)
- Success rates (CDC ART data): live birth per cycle approximately 40-50% age <35; 30-40% age 35-37; 20-30% age 38-40; <10-15% age >42 (own eggs)
- Preimplantation genetic testing for aneuploidy (PGT-A): biopsy trophectoderm cells from blastocyst; identify euploid embryos; increases implantation rate per transfer; does not clearly increase cumulative live birth rates in all patients
- Ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS): follicle rupture releases VEGF → vascular permeability → ascites, pleural effusion, hemoconcentration; risk increased in PCOS, high AFC, high estradiol; prevention: GnRH antagonist protocol with agonist trigger; cabergoline; freeze-all (no fresh transfer)

## Ectopic Pregnancy

### Risk Factors
- Prior ectopic pregnancy (greatest risk — 10-25% recurrence)
- Prior tubal surgery or ligation
- Prior salpingitis/PID
- Endometriosis
- Prior abdominal or pelvic surgery
- Assisted reproductive technology (IVF)
- Current IUD (reduces absolute risk but shifts distribution toward ectopic if failure occurs)
- Progestin-only contraceptive failure

### Diagnosis
- Symptoms: amenorrhea + pelvic pain + vaginal bleeding (classic triad); range from asymptomatic to hemorrhagic shock
- Quantitative beta-hCG: in IUP, rises minimum 53-66% in 48 hours (doubling time 48-72h); slower rise or plateau suggests ectopic or failing IUP; discriminatory zone: IUP should be visible on TVUS at hCG ≥1500-3000 IU/L; if no IUP visible above discriminatory zone — concern for ectopic
- Transvaginal ultrasound: absence of IUP + adnexal mass/ring sign + free fluid in cul-de-sac; heterotopic pregnancy (rare but increased with ART)
- Serial hCG: most useful diagnostic tool in conjunction with ultrasound

### Management

**Medical (Methotrexate):**
- Indications: hemodynamically stable; ectopic sac ≤3.5 cm (some use 4 cm); no fetal cardiac activity; beta-hCG <5000 IU/L (relative; some treat higher with counseling); no contraindications to methotrexate
- Contraindications: breastfeeding, immunodeficiency, blood dyscrasias, liver disease (AST/ALT >2x ULN), renal disease (Cr >1.0), active pulmonary disease, peptic ulcer disease
- Single-dose protocol: MTX 50 mg/m2 IM; check hCG day 4 and day 7; expect decline >15% from day 4 to day 7; if <15% decline, repeat dose; if <15% after second dose, surgical management
- Two-dose protocol: MTX 50 mg/m2 IM day 1 and day 4; monitor day 7
- Counsel: avoid folic acid, sun exposure, NSAIDs, alcohol during treatment; contraception for at least 3 months after (teratogenicity); follow hCG to zero weekly
- Success rate: ~85-90% for appropriate candidates

**Surgical:**
- Laparoscopy preferred (faster recovery, less adhesions)
- Salpingostomy: linear incision over ectopic; remove products; leave tube; higher recurrence vs. salpingectomy
- Salpingectomy: remove tube; preferred for large ectopics, failed salpingostomy, severely damaged tube; does not significantly reduce fertility outcomes (contralateral tube takes over)
- Emergent surgery for: hemodynamic instability, rupture, hemoperitoneum, failed medical management

## Miscarriage and Pregnancy Loss

### Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Threatened abortion | Vaginal bleeding in first trimester with viable IUP (fetal cardiac activity present); cervix closed |
| Inevitable abortion | Cervix dilated or effaced, products of conception partially through os; miscarriage imminent |
| Incomplete abortion | Partial expulsion of products of conception; cervix open |
| Complete abortion | Complete expulsion of products of conception; cervix closing; uterus empty |
| Missed abortion | Fetal demise without expulsion; uterus retains products |
| Anembryonic pregnancy (blighted ovum) | Gestational sac without identifiable embryo or fetal pole |
| Recurrent pregnancy loss (RPL) | ≥2 consecutive or ≥3 total pregnancy losses before 20 weeks |

### Prognosis by Ultrasound Findings
- Cardiac activity visible: risk of subsequent loss ~5-10%; prior history increases risk
- Subchorionic hemorrhage: most resolve without consequence; large hematoma (>1/3 gestational sac volume) increases loss risk
- Crown-rump length without cardiac activity: CRL ≥7 mm without cardiac activity = failed pregnancy

### Management Options (Failed or Desired Termination)

| Option | Description |
|--------|-----------|
| Expectant management | Await spontaneous passage; 80-90% complete within 2 weeks; appropriate for stable, motivated patient |
| Medical management | Misoprostol 800 mcg vaginally (or buccal/SL); onset 6-8h; 80-85% successful within 24-48h; bleeding and cramping expected |
| Surgical (MVA or D&C) | Manual vacuum aspiration (early GA, office setting) or electric vacuum aspiration/surgical D&C; most complete; immediate; preferred for heavy bleeding, hemodynamic instability, or failed medical management |

**All management options equally effective and safe — patient preference drives selection**

**Rh Prophylaxis:** RhoGAM 300 mcg IM for all Rh-negative women with pregnancy loss ≥8 weeks; 50 mcg for <8 weeks (mini-RhoGAM); prevents alloimmunization

### Recurrent Pregnancy Loss (RPL) Evaluation

| Cause | Prevalence in RPL | Evaluation |
|-------|------------------|-----------|
| Chromosomal/embryonic aneuploidy | 50-60% of individual losses; lower in RPL | Karyotype products of conception; parental karyotype |
| Uterine anomalies | 15-20% | SIS or hysteroscopy; 3D ultrasound |
| Antiphospholipid syndrome | 5-15% | Lupus anticoagulant, anticardiolipin IgG/IgM, anti-beta2 glycoprotein IgG/IgM (2 positive tests ≥12 weeks apart) |
| Thrombophilia | Controversial | Factor V Leiden, prothrombin mutation, MTHFR (low yield in RPL workup per most guidelines) |
| Thyroid dysfunction | 10-15% | TSH; thyroid antibodies (anti-TPO) |
| Hormonal | 10-20% | Prolactin, fasting glucose, HbA1c, PCOS evaluation |
| Unexplained | 50%+ after workup | Empiric progesterone in luteal phase (some evidence); supportive care; 65-70% will have successful subsequent pregnancy |

## Abnormal Uterine Bleeding (AUB)

### PALM-COEIN Classification System

**Structural (PALM):**
- P — Polyp (endometrial or cervical)
- A — Adenomyosis (endometrial glands within myometrium)
- L — Leiomyoma (fibroids — submucosal most clinically significant for bleeding)
- M — Malignancy and hyperplasia

**Non-Structural (COEIN):**
- C — Coagulopathy (von Willebrand disease in 13% of women with heavy menstrual bleeding; platelet disorders)
- O — Ovulatory dysfunction (PCOS, thyroid, hyperprolactinemia, perimenopause)
- E — Endometrial (local hemostatic defects)
- I — Iatrogenic (medications: anticoagulants, antipsychotics increasing prolactin, IUD)
- N — Not yet classified

### Evaluation of AUB

| Test | Indication |
|------|-----------|
| Beta-hCG | All reproductive-age women — rule out pregnancy |
| CBC | Assess severity of blood loss; thrombocytopenia |
| Coagulation studies (PT/PTT, von Willebrand panel) | Heavy menstrual bleeding since menarche, family history, prior surgical bleeding |
| TSH | Thyroid dysfunction |
| Prolactin | Amenorrhea, irregular cycles |
| Pelvic ultrasound | Structural causes (fibroids, polyps, adenomyosis) |
| Endometrial biopsy (EMB) | Age ≥45 with AUB; any age with risk factors for endometrial cancer (obesity, PCOS, unopposed estrogen, tamoxifen, Lynch syndrome, failed medical management) |
| Hysteroscopy | Gold standard for endometrial cavity evaluation; polyp and fibroid resection |
| SIS (sonohysterogram) | Better visualization of intracavitary lesions than standard ultrasound |

### Medical Management

**Acute Uterine Bleeding (Emergent Heavy Bleeding):**
- IV conjugated estrogen 25 mg IV q4-6h x24h: rapid endometrial hemostasis; follow with combination OCP
- Tranexamic acid 1.3 g PO TID x5 days: reduce fibrinolysis; adjunct
- High-dose progestins (norethindrone 5-10 mg TID or medroxyprogesterone 20-40 mg TID): stabilize endometrium; taper over weeks

**Chronic Heavy Menstrual Bleeding:**
- LNG-IUD: most effective medical treatment; 90% reduction in bleeding; first-line for most women
- Tranexamic acid 1.3 g PO TID during days of heavy flow (max 5 days/cycle)
- NSAIDs: 30-50% reduction; use at onset of flow through heaviest days
- Combined OCP: 40-50% reduction; regulate cycles
- Progestin-only (norethindrone 5 mg TID days 5-26 of cycle): cyclic; or continuous for amenorrhea
- GnRH agonist: for fibroids or preoperative; temporary; bone loss limits long-term use

## Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

### Diagnosis
- Clinical diagnosis (low threshold — treat empirically to prevent long-term sequelae)
- Minimum criteria: uterine tenderness OR adnexal tenderness OR cervical motion tenderness in sexually active woman with no other explanation
- Additional criteria (increase specificity): oral temperature >38.3°C; mucopurulent cervical discharge; WBC on wet prep; elevated CRP/ESR; laboratory confirmation of GC or chlamydia

### CDC 2021 Treatment

**Outpatient (mild-moderate PID):**
- Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose PLUS doxycycline 100 mg PO BID x14 days PLUS metronidazole 500 mg PO BID x14 days
- Alternative: cefoxitin 2g IM + probenecid 1g PO single dose PLUS doxycycline 100 mg PO BID x14 days ± metronidazole

**Inpatient Indications:**
- Surgical emergency cannot be excluded (appendicitis, ruptured ectopic)
- Tubo-ovarian abscess (TOA)
- No clinical improvement on oral therapy after 72 hours
- Pregnancy
- Inability to tolerate oral medications
- Severe illness (high fever, nausea/vomiting)

**Inpatient IV Regimens:**
- Cefoxitin 2g IV q6h PLUS doxycycline 100 mg PO/IV q12h; transition to oral when improved (24h afebrile, clinical improvement)
- Clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h PLUS gentamicin 2 mg/kg loading dose then 1.5 mg/kg q8h (or 3-5 mg/kg daily dosing)

**Tubo-Ovarian Abscess:**
- Hospital admission; IV antibiotics (above regimens); 70% respond to antibiotics alone
- Surgical drainage: interventional radiology (CT-guided or transvaginal) for abscess >8 cm or failure to improve; laparoscopic or open drainage for rupture or failed IR drainage

### Long-Term Sequelae of PID (PEACH Trial and cohort data)
- Infertility: 8-13% after one episode of PID; 19-36% after 3+ episodes
- Ectopic pregnancy: 7-10x increased risk
- Chronic pelvic pain: 18% develop chronic pain
- Recurrent PID: 20-25% will have recurrent episode

## Osteoporosis

### Definition
- Bone mineral density (BMD) T-score ≤ -2.5 SD below young adult mean (WHO definition)
- Osteopenia: T-score -1.0 to -2.5
- Osteoporosis in premenopausal women and men: use Z-score (age-matched); Z-score ≤ -2.0 = "below expected range for age"

### Screening
- USPSTF 2018: BMD screening (DXA) for all women ≥65 years; screen younger women if 10-year fracture risk ≥ that of 65-year-old white woman (FRAX score)
- FRAX tool: calculates 10-year probability of major osteoporotic fracture and hip fracture; uses clinical risk factors with or without BMD
- Pharmacotherapy threshold (NOF guidelines): T-score ≤ -2.5; or T-score -1.0 to -2.5 with 10-year FRAX major osteoporotic fracture probability ≥20% or hip fracture probability ≥3%

### Risk Factors
- Female sex, advanced age
- Menopause (accelerated bone loss 2-3% per year in first 5-10 years postmenopause)
- POI or surgical menopause
- Low body weight/BMI <18.5
- Caucasian or Asian ethnicity
- Family history of osteoporosis or fragility fracture
- Prior fragility fracture
- Glucocorticoid use (≥5 mg prednisone equivalent daily x>3 months)
- Smoking, alcohol (>3 drinks/day)
- Low calcium and vitamin D intake
- Physical inactivity, immobility
- Secondary causes: celiac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, rheumatoid arthritis, hyperparathyroidism, hyperthyroidism, eating disorders, chronic kidney disease, malabsorption

### Prevention and Non-Pharmacologic Management
- Calcium: 1000 mg/day ages 19-50; 1200 mg/day women ≥51 and postmenopausal; dietary preferred (bioavailability, no constipation/kidney stone controversy with supplements)
- Vitamin D: 600-800 IU/day; many experts recommend 1000-2000 IU/day postmenopausal; supplement if serum 25-OH vitamin D <30 ng/mL
- Weight-bearing and resistance exercise: slows bone loss, improves balance/prevents falls
- Fall prevention: home safety assessment, strength and balance training, discontinue or reduce sedating medications, vision correction
- Smoking cessation; limit alcohol

### Pharmacologic Treatment

| Drug | Class | Dose | Notes |
|------|-------|------|-------|
| Alendronate | Bisphosphonate | 70 mg PO weekly or 10 mg daily | First-line; reduce fracture risk 50% at spine, 40% at hip; take on empty stomach, remain upright 30 min; GI side effects; atypical femur fractures and ONJ with long-term use |
| Risedronate | Bisphosphonate | 35 mg PO weekly or 150 mg monthly | Similar to alendronate; may have less GI side effects |
| Ibandronate | Bisphosphonate | 150 mg PO monthly or 3 mg IV q3 months | No proven hip fracture reduction in RCTs; spine only |
| Zoledronic acid | IV bisphosphonate | 5 mg IV annually | HORIZON trial: 70% spine, 41% hip fracture reduction; convenient once-yearly IV |
| Denosumab | RANKL inhibitor (biologic) | 60 mg SQ q6 months | Reduces RANK ligand — inhibits osteoclast differentiation; must not miss/delay doses (rebound bone loss and vertebral fractures); first-line in renal insufficiency (no dose adjustment) |
| Raloxifene | SERM | 60 mg PO daily | Reduces spine fractures; no proven hip fracture reduction; reduces breast cancer risk; INCREASES VTE risk; worsens hot flashes — avoid in symptomatic menopause |
| Teriparatide | PTH analogue (anabolic) | 20 mcg SQ daily | Builds new bone; indicated for severe osteoporosis, multiple fractures, or treatment failure; max 2 years; osteosarcoma risk (black box — do not use if prior radiation therapy); requires self-injection |
| Abaloparatide | PTHrP analogue (anabolic) | 80 mcg SQ daily | Similar to teriparatide; fewer hypercalcemia episodes; max 2 years |
| Romosozumab | Sclerostin inhibitor (anabolic/antiresorptive) | 210 mg SQ monthly x12 months | Dual mechanism; superior to alendronate for fracture reduction (ARCH trial); increased CV events in women with prior stroke or MI — contraindicated |

**Drug Holidays:**
- Bisphosphonates accumulate in bone — drug holiday after 3-5 years if stable BMD and no prior fractures
- Reassess annually; restart if BMD declines significantly or fracture occurs
- Do NOT take drug holiday with denosumab (severe rebound)

**Treatment Duration:**
- Bisphosphonates: 3-5 years oral, then holiday assessment; 3 years IV, then reassess
- Denosumab: indefinite or transition to bisphosphonate to prevent rebound
- Anabolics (teriparatide/abaloparatide): max 2 years lifetime; follow with antiresorptive
- Romosozumab: 12 months; follow with antiresorptive
