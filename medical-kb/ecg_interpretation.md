## Systematic ECG Interpretation Approach

A consistent, stepwise approach prevents missed findings. Perform each step in order before reaching a final interpretation.

### The Eight-Step Framework

1. **Rate** — Calculate ventricular rate using the 300 / large-box method or 1500 / small-box method
2. **Rhythm** — Regular vs. irregular; identify P waves; assess relationship between P and QRS
3. **Axis** — Determine electrical axis from leads I and aVF
4. **Intervals** — Measure PR, QRS, QT/QTc
5. **P wave morphology** — Shape, axis, consistency
6. **QRS morphology** — Width, notching, bundle branch patterns, hypertrophy criteria
7. **ST and T wave changes** — Elevation, depression, inversions, hyperacute T waves
8. **U waves** — Prominent, inverted, or fused with T wave

### Critical "Do Not Miss" Diagnoses

- STEMI (including posterior, right ventricular, and equivalents)
- Wide complex tachycardia (treat as VT until proven otherwise)
- Complete heart block with slow escape
- Torsades de pointes
- Hyperkalemia with wide QRS
- Wolff-Parkinson-White with atrial fibrillation
- Brugada pattern Type 1

## Rate Calculation Methods

### 300 / Large Box Method

Used for regular rhythms. Count the number of large boxes between two consecutive R waves and divide 300 by that number.

| Large boxes between R-R | Approximate heart rate |
|-------------------------|----------------------|
| 1 | 300 bpm |
| 2 | 150 bpm |
| 3 | 100 bpm |
| 4 | 75 bpm |
| 5 | 60 bpm |
| 6 | 50 bpm |
| 7 | 43 bpm |
| 8 | 37 bpm |
| 10 | 30 bpm |

Mnemonic: 300 — 150 — 100 — 75 — 60 — 50

### 1500 / Small Box Method

More precise. Count the number of small boxes (1 mm each, 0.04 sec each) between two R waves and divide 1500 by that number.

- Example: 20 small boxes → 1500 / 20 = 75 bpm

### Irregular Rhythms

Count the number of QRS complexes in a 6-second strip (30 large boxes) and multiply by 10. Alternatively, count in a 10-second strip and multiply by 6.

### Rate Classification

| Rate | Classification |
|------|---------------|
| < 60 bpm | Bradycardia |
| 60-100 bpm | Normal |
| 101-150 bpm | Tachycardia (moderate) |
| 151-250 bpm | Tachycardia (rapid) — suspect SVT, atrial flutter |
| > 250 bpm | Extreme — suspect VT, pre-excitation AF |

## Rhythm Analysis

### Identifying P Waves

- Location: Typically precede QRS by 120-200 ms in normal sinus rhythm
- Morphology: Upright in leads I, II, aVF; inverted in aVR
- Consistency: All P waves should look identical in sinus rhythm
- Presence of P wave confirms atrial activity; absence suggests AF, flutter, or junctional rhythm

### Regularity Assessment

- Regular: R-R intervals vary by less than 10%
- Regularly irregular: Predictable pattern of irregularity (e.g., Wenckebach)
- Irregularly irregular: Completely unpredictable R-R intervals (e.g., atrial fibrillation)

### P-to-QRS Relationship

| Relationship | Interpretation |
|--------------|---------------|
| 1:1, PR constant | Normal sinus rhythm or atrial tachycardia |
| Progressive PR lengthening, then dropped QRS | Mobitz I (Wenckebach) |
| Fixed PR, sudden dropped QRS | Mobitz II |
| P and QRS completely unrelated | Third-degree (complete) AV block or AV dissociation with VT |
| More QRS than P waves | Junctional or ventricular escape with retrograde block |
| More P than QRS | Any degree of AV block |

## Axis Determination

### Normal Axis

- Normal: -30 degrees to +90 degrees
- Standard reference: Hexaxial reference system using limb leads

### Rapid Two-Lead Method Using Leads I and aVF

| Lead I | Lead aVF | Axis |
|--------|----------|------|
| Positive | Positive | Normal (0 to +90) |
| Positive | Negative | Left axis deviation (LAD) |
| Negative | Positive | Right axis deviation (RAD) |
| Negative | Negative | Extreme ("northwest") axis |

### Left Axis Deviation (LAD)

- Definition: Axis more negative than -30 degrees
- ECG: Lead I positive, aVF negative, lead II negative confirms LAD beyond -30
- Causes:
  - Left anterior fascicular block (most common)
  - Left bundle branch block
  - Inferior MI (loss of inferior forces)
  - LV pacing
  - Hyperkalemia
  - Wolff-Parkinson-White (right-sided pathway)
  - Congenital: primum ASD, tricuspid atresia

### Right Axis Deviation (RAD)

- Definition: Axis more positive than +90 degrees
- ECG: Lead I negative, aVF positive
- Causes:
  - Right ventricular hypertrophy
  - Left posterior fascicular block (diagnosis of exclusion)
  - Pulmonary embolism (acute RV strain)
  - Pulmonary hypertension
  - Lateral MI (loss of lateral forces)
  - Normal variant in children and thin adults
  - Dextrocardia
  - Wolff-Parkinson-White (left-sided pathway)
  - COPD (vertical heart)

### Extreme (Northwest) Axis

- Definition: Both leads I and aVF negative; axis between -90 and +/-180
- Causes: Ventricular tachycardia, severe RVH, artificial pacing, hyperkalemia
- Clinical significance: High suspicion for ventricular origin of rhythm

## Intervals

### PR Interval

- Normal: 120-200 ms (3-5 small boxes)
- Measurement: From onset of P wave to onset of QRS

| PR Value | Interpretation |
|----------|---------------|
| < 120 ms | Pre-excitation (WPW), accelerated AV conduction, junctional rhythm with retrograde P |
| 120-200 ms | Normal |
| > 200 ms | First-degree AV block |
| Progressively lengthening | Mobitz I (Wenckebach) second-degree AV block |

### QRS Duration

- Normal: Less than 120 ms (less than 3 small boxes)
- Measurement: From onset to offset of QRS complex in the widest lead

| QRS Duration | Interpretation |
|--------------|---------------|
| < 120 ms | Normal or incomplete BBB (100-119 ms) |
| 120-159 ms | Complete BBB, pacing |
| > 160 ms | Ventricular tachycardia more likely; severe hyperkalemia; severe toxicity |

### QT Interval and QTc

- QT interval: From onset of QRS to end of T wave
- Measured in lead II or V5 (most reproducible)
- QT varies with heart rate — must correct to QTc

**Bazett Formula:** QTc = QT / sqrt(RR interval in seconds)

- Normal QTc: Less than 440 ms in men; less than 460 ms in women
- Borderline prolonged: 440-500 ms
- High torsades risk: QTc greater than 500 ms
- Short QT: QTc less than 340 ms

**Limitations of Bazett Formula:**
- Overcorrects at fast heart rates (falsely high QTc at HR > 100)
- Undercorrects at slow heart rates
- Fridericia formula (QT / RR^0.33) is more accurate at extremes of rate

### J-Point

- The junction between the end of the QRS and the start of the ST segment
- Normally at the isoelectric line
- J-point elevation may be normal (early repolarization) or pathological (STEMI, Brugada)

## P Wave Morphology

### Normal P Wave

- Upright in leads I, II, and aVF
- Inverted in aVR
- Biphasic in V1 (initial positive, terminal negative deflection)
- Duration less than 120 ms, amplitude less than 2.5 mm

### Abnormal P Wave Findings

| Finding | Morphology | Interpretation |
|---------|-----------|---------------|
| P pulmonale | Tall peaked P > 2.5 mm in II | Right atrial enlargement (COPD, pulmonary HTN) |
| P mitrale | Broad notched P > 120 ms, M-shaped in II | Left atrial enlargement (mitral stenosis, LVH) |
| Inverted P in II | Negative deflection | Ectopic atrial pacemaker or retrograde conduction |
| P absent | No P waves | AF, AFL, junctional rhythm, hyperkalemia |
| Varying P morphology | 3+ different P morphologies | Multifocal atrial tachycardia (MAT) |
| P buried in QRS | P not visible | AVNRT, junctional rhythm |
| P after QRS (RP < PR) | P follows QRS closely | AVNRT (slow-fast) or junctional rhythm |

### Sinus vs Non-Sinus P Waves

- Sinus P: Upright in I, II, aVF; inverted in aVR — confirms SA node origin
- Inverted in II: Ectopic low atrial or junctional with retrograde atrial activation
- Biphasic in II: Transition zone atrial pacemaker
- Negative in I: Possible dextrocardia or lead reversal (check other leads)

## QRS Morphology

### Nomenclature of QRS Deflections

- Q wave: First negative deflection before any positive deflection
- R wave: First positive deflection
- S wave: Negative deflection after an R wave
- R' (R prime): Second positive deflection
- Small letters (q, r, s): Small amplitude; capitals (Q, R, S): large amplitude
- QS pattern: Single negative deflection with no positive component

### Septal Q Waves (Normal)

- Small narrow Q waves (< 40 ms, < 25% R wave height) in I, aVL, V5-V6
- Represent normal septal depolarization from left to right
- Absence of septal Q waves in V5-V6 = LBBB or LVH pattern

### Pathological Q Waves

- Width greater than 40 ms OR depth greater than 25% of R wave height in same lead
- OR any Q in V1-V3 (always pathological)
- Represent transmural myocardial necrosis (infarction)
- Can persist indefinitely after MI (permanent scar)
- New Q waves in the acute setting with symptoms = STEMI equivalent until proven otherwise

### R Wave Progression

- Normal: R waves increase in height from V1 to V5-V6
- Poor R wave progression (PRWP): R < 3 mm through V3; causes include anterior MI, LVH, LBBB, RVH, COPD
- Reverse R wave progression: Decreasing R height across precordium (ischemia, cardiomyopathy)
- Transition zone: Lead where R = S (normally V3-V4)

## ST Segment and T Wave Analysis

### Normal ST Segment

- Isoelectric or within 1 mm of baseline in most leads
- Slight elevation (up to 2 mm in V1-V3, up to 1 mm in limb leads) may be normal early repolarization
- ST segment should be horizontal or have gentle upward slope toward T wave

### ST Elevation: Categories

| Pattern | Description | Interpretation |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| Convex ("domed") | Curved upward, peaked apex | STEMI until proven otherwise |
| Concave ("saddle-shaped") | Scooped, concave upward | Pericarditis, early repolarization |
| Coved (Brugada type 1) | Descending ST with negative T | Brugada pattern — high risk |
| J-point elevation with normal shape | Early repolarization | Often benign; high J-wave amplitude with horizontal slope increases SCD risk |

### ST Depression: Categories

| Pattern | Description | Significance |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Horizontal | Flat, stays at same level | Ischemia — highest specificity |
| Downsloping | Descends away from QRS | Ischemia — high specificity |
| Upsloping | Ascends toward T wave | Less specific; may be normal at fast rates |
| "Scooped" | Digitalis effect pattern | Digoxin effect (not toxicity alone) |

### T Wave Findings

| Finding | Description | Interpretation |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| Hyperacute T | Tall, broad, symmetric, peaked | Early STEMI (precedes ST elevation) |
| Inverted T | Negative deflection | Ischemia, LVH, BBB (if discordant), RVSD, CNS event |
| Deep symmetric inversion | Deeply inverted, symmetric | Wellens syndrome (critical LAD); massive PE; Takotsubo |
| Flattened T | Reduced amplitude | Ischemia, electrolyte abnormality, normal variant |
| Biphasic T | Initially positive, then negative | Wellens Type A (V2-V3); also ischemia in other leads |
| Peaked narrow T | Narrow spike appearance | Hyperkalemia |

## U Waves

### Normal U Wave

- Small deflection after T wave, same polarity as T wave
- Most prominent in V2-V3 at slow heart rates
- Amplitude normally less than T wave amplitude in same lead
- Represents late ventricular repolarization (possibly Purkinje fiber repolarization or papillary muscle repolarization)

### Abnormal U Waves

| Finding | Interpretation |
|---------|---------------|
| Prominent U > T amplitude in same lead | Hypokalemia (most common cause), bradycardia, antiarrhythmic drugs |
| Inverted U wave | Ischemia (particularly LAD territory), LVH, hypertension |
| TU fusion | Hypokalemia — effectively prolongs QT interval |
| U wave in V1 | Abnormal; suggests hypokalemia or drug effect |

## Sinus Rhythms

### Normal Sinus Rhythm (NSR)

- Rate: 60-100 bpm
- P wave: Upright in I, II, aVF; inverted in aVR
- P-QRS relationship: Every P followed by QRS; PR 120-200 ms
- QRS: Normal morphology unless aberrancy

### Sinus Bradycardia

- Rate: Less than 60 bpm with sinus P waves
- Causes: Athletes (normal), vasovagal, hypothyroidism, hypothermia, sick sinus syndrome, inferior MI, medications (beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin), increased ICP
- Treatment: Usually observation; atropine 0.5 mg IV if symptomatic; temporary pacing if refractory

### Sinus Tachycardia

- Rate: Greater than 100 bpm with sinus P waves; usually less than 150 bpm
- P wave morphology identical to NSR
- Causes: Pain, anxiety, fever, hypovolemia, anemia, hyperthyroidism, PE, heart failure, drugs (stimulants, anticholinergics)
- Treatment: Address underlying cause; never treat sinus tachycardia with antiarrhythmics (compensatory mechanism)

### Sinus Arrhythmia

- Rate variation with respiration — increases with inspiration, decreases with expiration
- RR intervals vary by more than 10% but P morphology is identical
- Normal finding, especially in young patients and athletes
- Non-respiratory sinus arrhythmia: variation unrelated to breathing; less common

### Sick Sinus Syndrome (SSS)

- Also called tachy-brady syndrome or sinus node dysfunction
- Features: Inappropriate sinus bradycardia, sinus pauses, sinus arrest, sinoatrial block, alternating bradycardia and atrial tachyarrhythmias (tachy-brady)
- Sinus pause: RR interval > 2x normal RR (at least 2 seconds)
- Sinus arrest greater than 3 seconds = pacemaker indication
- Diagnosis: Holter monitor, event recorder, electrophysiology study
- Treatment: Permanent pacemaker; rate control for tachycardia component (but AV nodal blockers may worsen bradycardia — dual-chamber pacing preferred)

### Sinoatrial (SA) Exit Block

- Type I: Progressive shortening of PP interval then dropped P wave (analogous to Wenckebach)
- Type II: Sudden absence of P wave; PP interval of the pause is a multiple of the normal PP interval
- Causes: Digoxin toxicity, sick sinus syndrome, inferior MI, increased vagal tone

## Supraventricular Tachycardias

### General SVT Approach

- Definition: Tachycardia originating at or above the bundle of His; narrow complex (QRS < 120 ms) unless aberrant conduction
- Rate: Typically 150-250 bpm for most reentrant SVTs
- First-line intervention: Vagal maneuvers (Valsalva, carotid sinus massage, diving reflex)
- Pharmacologic: Adenosine 6 mg rapid IV push with saline flush; if no response, 12 mg x2
- Adenosine effects: Transiently blocks AV node — terminates AVNRT and AVRT; reveals flutter waves in atrial flutter; does NOT terminate atrial tachycardia but may slow rate

### AVNRT (AV Nodal Reentrant Tachycardia)

- Most common SVT in adults
- Mechanism: Reentrant circuit within or near the AV node using fast and slow pathways
- Typical (slow-fast): P wave buried in QRS or just after QRS; pseudo-r' in V1 and pseudo-S in inferior leads
- Rate: 150-250 bpm; regular
- ECG: Regular narrow complex tachycardia; RP < PR (P closer to preceding QRS than next QRS)
- Treatment: Vagal maneuvers → adenosine → calcium channel blockers (verapamil, diltiazem) or beta-blockers → cardioversion; long-term: ablation of slow pathway (>95% cure rate)

### AVRT (AV Reentrant Tachycardia) — WPW Related

- Mechanism: Reentrant circuit using accessory pathway and AV node
- Orthodromic AVRT: Conduction down AV node, retrograde up accessory pathway — narrow QRS (unless bundle branch block on ipsilateral side)
- Antidromic AVRT: Conduction down accessory pathway, retrograde up AV node — wide QRS (pre-excited)
- Rate: 150-250 bpm; regular
- ECG during tachycardia: RP relationship varies; retrograde P waves often visible in ST segment
- Treatment: Adenosine terminates orthodromic AVRT; cardioversion for unstable; ablation definitive

### Atrial Tachycardia (AT)

- Mechanism: Automatic, reentrant, or triggered activity from atrial focus outside SA node
- ECG: P waves visible before each QRS; P morphology different from sinus P; isoelectric baseline between P waves (distinguishes from flutter)
- Rate: Atrial rate 100-250 bpm; AV block can occur (2:1, 3:1)
- RP > PR (P closer to preceding QRS in the preceding cycle than the next QRS) in many forms
- Treatment: AV nodal blockers to control rate; antiarrhythmics; ablation; address underlying cause (structural heart disease, channelopathy)

### Atrial Flutter

- Mechanism: Macroreentrant circuit in right atrium around tricuspid valve annulus (typical/isthmus-dependent)
- Rate: Atrial rate 300 bpm (range 250-350); most common ventricular response = 150 bpm with 2:1 block
- ECG: Sawtooth flutter waves — negative in II, III, aVF (typical counterclockwise); positive in aVL, V1
- Key rule: Any tachycardia with ventricular rate near 150 bpm = "flutter until proven otherwise"
- AV conduction ratios: 2:1 (most common), 3:1, 4:1, or variable
- No isoelectric baseline between flutter waves (continuous undulation)
- Adenosine: Slows ventricular rate transiently, reveals flutter waves clearly
- Treatment: Rate control (AV nodal blockers); cardioversion; ablation of cavotricuspid isthmus (highly effective, >90% cure)
- Anticoagulation: Same risk as AF; CHA2DS2-VASc score applies

### Atrial Fibrillation (AF)

- Mechanism: Chaotic rapid firing from multiple pulmonary vein and atrial foci
- ECG: Irregularly irregular R-R intervals; absent discrete P waves; fibrillatory baseline (fine or coarse)
- Atrial rate: 350-600 chaotic impulses/min; ventricular rate depends on AV node conduction
- Ventricular rate: Uncontrolled 100-180 bpm; controlled < 100 bpm
- Classification:
  - Paroxysmal: Self-terminating within 7 days
  - Persistent: Lasts > 7 days; requires intervention to terminate
  - Long-standing persistent: Continuous > 12 months
  - Permanent: Accepted ongoing AF; rate control strategy
- Treatment: Rate control vs rhythm control (EAST-AFNET trial: early rhythm control reduces cardiovascular events); anticoagulation per CHA2DS2-VASc; cardioversion; ablation (pulmonary vein isolation)
- CHA2DS2-VASc scoring: CHF, HTN, Age ≥75 (x2), Diabetes, Stroke/TIA (x2), Vascular disease, Age 65-74, Sex (female) — score ≥2 in men, ≥3 in women → anticoagulation

### Multifocal Atrial Tachycardia (MAT)

- Mechanism: Multiple ectopic atrial foci firing rapidly
- ECG: Rate > 100 bpm; at least 3 distinct P wave morphologies in same lead; irregular R-R intervals (may mimic AF)
- Key distinguishing feature from AF: Distinct P waves with isoelectric baseline present
- Causes: COPD (most common), hypoxia, hypomagnesemia, theophylline toxicity, sepsis, metabolic disorders
- Treatment: Address underlying cause (especially hypoxia and COPD); magnesium IV; verapamil or diltiazem for rate control; NOT cardioversion (will not maintain sinus rhythm)

## Wide Complex Tachycardias

### Initial Assessment

- Wide complex tachycardia = QRS > 120 ms at rate > 100 bpm
- Differential: VT, SVT with aberrancy (BBB), SVT with accessory pathway (antidromic AVRT or AF with pre-excitation)
- Default approach: Treat as VT if uncertain — treating VT as SVT with AV nodal blockers can be fatal
- If hemodynamically unstable: Immediate synchronized cardioversion regardless of mechanism

### Brugada Algorithm for VT vs SVT with Aberrancy

Perform steps sequentially; if any step is positive, diagnose VT:

1. Is there an RS complex in ANY precordial lead?
   - No RS complex in any V lead = VT (positive predictive value ~100%)
2. RS interval > 100 ms in any precordial lead?
   - Yes = VT
3. AV dissociation present?
   - P waves marching independently of QRS = VT (diagnostic)
4. Morphology criteria met in V1 and V6?
   - LBBB pattern: R > 30 ms in V1, notched S in V1, QRS onset to S nadir > 70 ms in V1, Q in V6 = VT
   - RBBB pattern: Monophasic R or Rr' in V1, R/S < 1 in V6 = VT
5. If none of the above = SVT with aberrancy

### Diagnostic Clues Favoring VT

- AV dissociation: Most specific sign — P waves and QRS completely unrelated
- Fusion beats: QRS morphology between native QRS and VT QRS (premature ventricular activation merges with sinus beat)
- Capture beats: Normal narrow QRS within wide complex tachycardia (sinus impulse briefly captures ventricle)
- Extreme axis (northwest axis)
- QRS concordance: All precordial leads positive (positive concordance) or all negative (negative concordance)
- QRS > 160 ms (longer duration favors VT)
- Similar QRS morphology to known PVCs on baseline ECG

### Ventricular Tachycardia (VT) Morphologies

**LBBB Pattern VT (negative in V1):**
- Suggests origin in right ventricle or septal right-sided origin
- Differential includes ARVC (arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy), RV outflow tract VT (benign in structurally normal heart — inferior axis), septal VT

**RBBB Pattern VT (positive in V1):**
- Suggests origin in left ventricle
- Seen with LV aneurysm, prior MI, dilated cardiomyopathy
- Broader RSR' morphology with slurred components vs clean RSR' of true RBBB

### Torsades de Pointes

- Polymorphic VT with characteristic twisting of QRS axis around isoelectric line
- Always associated with prolonged QT interval (acquired or congenital)
- ECG: Gradual increase then decrease in QRS amplitude, rotating polarity, rate 200-250 bpm
- Triggers: Drugs (antiarrhythmics, antibiotics, antipsychotics — see drug section), hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, bradycardia, congenital LQTS
- Treatment:
  - IV magnesium sulfate 2 g over 1-2 minutes (first-line, even with normal magnesium level)
  - Correct hypokalemia (target K > 4.5 mEq/L) and hypomagnesemia
  - Isoproterenol IV infusion (shortens QT by increasing heart rate)
  - Temporary overdrive pacing (atrial or ventricular) — shortens QT
  - Lidocaine (does not prolong QT)
  - Stop all QT-prolonging drugs
  - Do NOT use amiodarone (prolongs QT further)
  - Do NOT use other antiarrhythmics that prolong QT (procainamide, sotalol)

### Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)

- Chaotic, disorganized ventricular depolarization — no identifiable P, QRS, or T waves
- No cardiac output — cardiac arrest
- Treatment: Immediate unsynchronized defibrillation (200 J biphasic); CPR; epinephrine; amiodarone if shock-refractory

## Bradyarrhythmias and AV Blocks

### First-Degree AV Block

- PR interval > 200 ms; every P wave followed by QRS
- No dropped beats; 1:1 conduction maintained
- Usually benign; no treatment required
- Causes: Increased vagal tone, inferior MI, AV nodal disease, medications (digoxin, beta-blockers, CCBs)
- Can contribute to symptoms if PR very prolonged (> 300 ms) — loss of AV synchrony

### Second-Degree AV Block Type I (Mobitz I / Wenckebach)

- Progressive PR prolongation until one P wave is not conducted (dropped QRS)
- RR interval shortens progressively before the dropped beat
- Grouped beating pattern on rhythm strip
- Location: Usually AV node (supranodal)
- Causes: Inferior MI, increased vagal tone, medications, athletic training, myocarditis
- Generally benign; usually does not progress to complete block
- Treatment: Observation unless symptomatic; atropine if symptomatic; pacemaker rarely needed

### Second-Degree AV Block Type II (Mobitz II)

- Fixed PR interval (normal or prolonged); sudden dropped QRS without preceding PR change
- Location: Below AV node — His bundle or bundle branches (infranodal)
- High risk of progression to complete (third-degree) block — unpredictable
- Causes: Anterior MI (extensive His-Purkinje disease), infiltrative disease, fibrosis, cardiac surgery
- Wide QRS in conducted beats suggests bilateral bundle branch disease
- Treatment: Permanent pacemaker indicated even if asymptomatic

### 2:1 AV Block

- Every other P wave blocked; cannot distinguish Mobitz I from II from a single strip
- Narrow QRS suggests Mobitz I (AV nodal block); wide QRS suggests Mobitz II (infranodal)
- Clinical context and additional rhythm strips help differentiate
- Atropine test: If block improves with atropine (more P waves conduct) = likely AV nodal (Mobitz I); if worsens or no change = likely infranodal (Mobitz II)

### High-Degree AV Block

- 3:1 or greater conduction ratio (only every third or more P wave is conducted)
- Not complete block (some P waves do conduct)
- Often requires pacemaker

### Third-Degree (Complete) AV Block

- No relationship between P waves and QRS complexes — complete AV dissociation
- P rate faster than QRS rate (key distinguishing feature from VT where QRS rate is faster)
- Escape rhythm:
  - Junctional escape (AV node or His bundle): Rate 40-60 bpm; narrow QRS; more reliable
  - Ventricular escape: Rate 20-40 bpm; wide QRS; unreliable; higher risk of asystole
- Causes: Inferior MI (often transient, AV nodal), anterior MI (often permanent, infranodal), Lyme disease, sarcoidosis, calcified aortic stenosis, infiltrative disease, digoxin toxicity, congenital complete heart block (maternal anti-SSA/Ro antibodies)
- Treatment: Transcutaneous pacing if unstable; transvenous temporary pacing; permanent pacemaker (virtually always indicated)

### Pacemaker Indications (Summary)

| Indication | Class |
|------------|-------|
| Symptomatic sick sinus syndrome | I |
| Mobitz II second-degree AV block | I |
| Third-degree (complete) AV block | I |
| Symptomatic bradycardia any cause | I |
| Asymptomatic complete block with low escape rate or wide QRS | I |
| Post-MI high-degree or complete AV block not expected to resolve | I |
| Bifascicular block with syncope (infranodal disease suspected) | IIa |
| Symptomatic Mobitz I | IIa |
| Sinus node dysfunction, documented HR < 40 bpm with symptoms | I |
| Sinus pause > 3 seconds | IIa |

## Bundle Branch Blocks

### RBBB (Right Bundle Branch Block)

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- QRS duration ≥ 120 ms
- RSR' ("rabbit ears" or "M-shaped" pattern) in V1 and V2
- Broad, slurred S wave in leads I, aVL, V5, V6

**Pathophysiology:**
- Right bundle branch fails to conduct
- RV depolarizes late, from left to right via slow cell-to-cell conduction
- Terminal broad R' in V1 = late RV activation

**Causes:**
- Normal variant (isolated RBBB without structural disease — common)
- Right ventricular pressure overload: pulmonary embolism, pulmonary hypertension
- Ischemia (RCA or LAD depending on septal supply)
- Cardiomyopathy
- Myocarditis
- Post-cardiac surgery (right ventriculotomy)
- Brugada syndrome (RBBB morphology pattern, not true conduction block)

**Ischemia Assessment in RBBB:**
- ST and T wave changes in RBBB can still be interpreted for ischemia
- The primary ST changes are in leads where RBBB does not cause ST abnormality (I, II, aVF, V4-V6)
- ST elevation in V1-V3 in RBBB = possible anterior ischemia overlaid on RBBB

**New RBBB with Clinical Symptoms:**
- New RBBB in setting of acute PE: suggests RV strain
- New RBBB with chest pain: evaluate carefully; may represent acute ischemia
- S1Q3T3 with RBBB = acute cor pulmonale pattern in PE

### LBBB (Left Bundle Branch Block)

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- QRS duration ≥ 120 ms
- Broad, notched (or monophasic) R wave in leads I, aVL, V5, V6 — no septal Q wave
- Deep S wave (or QS pattern) in V1, V2, V3
- ST and T waves discordant (opposite direction) from main QRS vector — this is NORMAL in LBBB

**Pathophysiology:**
- Left bundle branch fails to conduct
- LV depolarizes late, from right to left via slow cell-to-cell conduction
- Loss of normal septal Q waves in lateral leads

**Causes:**
- More serious than RBBB — always associated with structural disease
- CAD, hypertensive cardiomyopathy, dilated cardiomyopathy, valvular disease, myocarditis

**Critical Rule:** New LBBB in the setting of chest pain or hemodynamic instability = STEMI equivalent — activate cath lab.

**Sgarbossa Criteria for Ischemia in LBBB:**

| Criterion | Points | Sensitivity | Specificity |
|-----------|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Concordant ST elevation ≥1 mm (same direction as QRS) | 5 | Low | Very high |
| Concordant ST depression ≥1 mm in V1-V3 | 3 | Moderate | High |
| Discordant ST elevation ≥5 mm (opposite QRS, excessive) | 2 | Moderate | Moderate |

- Score ≥ 3 = high likelihood of acute MI
- Modified Sgarbossa: Uses ratio ST elevation / S wave depth ≥ 0.25 (more sensitive)
- Discordant ST changes are EXPECTED and NORMAL in LBBB; only excessive discordance (≥ 5 mm absolute or ≥ 0.25 ratio) is pathological

## Fascicular Blocks

### Left Anterior Fascicular Block (LAFB)

- Criteria: Left axis deviation (-45 to -90 degrees); qR pattern in I and aVL; rS pattern in II, III, aVF; QRS duration < 120 ms (unless combined with RBBB)
- Mechanism: Block of anterior-superior fascicle of the left bundle; inferior wall activates first (initial inferior vector), then superior-lateral wall activated late (terminal leftward-superior vector)
- Most common fascicular block
- Causes: CAD, cardiomyopathy, hypertensive disease, aging (fibrosis), Chagas disease

### Left Posterior Fascicular Block (LPFB)

- Criteria: Right axis deviation (> +90 degrees); rS pattern in I and aVL; qR pattern in II, III, aVF; QRS duration < 120 ms
- Diagnosis of exclusion — must rule out RVH, pulmonary disease, lateral MI, normal variant RAD
- Less common than LAFB (posterior fascicle has dual blood supply — LAD and RCA)
- Associated with more severe conduction system disease when present

### Bifascicular Block

- RBBB + LAFB: Most common bifascicular block; right axis on ECG with RBBB + left axis
  - Wait — RBBB + LAFB produces left axis deviation despite two blocked fascicles
  - ECG: Wide QRS with RBBB morphology + left axis deviation (-45 to -90 degrees)
- RBBB + LPFB: RBBB morphology + right axis deviation (after excluding other causes of RAD)
- Significance: Two of three fascicles blocked; third fascicle (or AV node) maintains conduction
- Risk of progression to complete heart block

### Trifascicular Block

- Bifascicular block + first-degree AV block (prolonged PR suggests intermittent disease in the remaining fascicle)
- Or bifascicular block + intermittent complete heart block
- High risk of complete AV block and syncope
- Consideration for prophylactic pacemaker if symptomatic

## Ischemia and Infarction Patterns

### Time Course of ECG Changes in STEMI

| Time | ECG Change |
|------|-----------|
| Minutes (hyperacute) | Tall, broad, peaked T waves (hyperacute T waves) |
| 0-6 hours | ST elevation (convex, domed pattern) |
| 6-24 hours | Q waves develop; T wave inversion begins |
| 24-72 hours | ST elevation begins to resolve; deep T inversions |
| Days to weeks | Q waves persist; T waves may normalize |
| Weeks to months | Persistent Q waves (permanent scar); ST returns to baseline |

### Reciprocal ST Depression

- ST depression in leads electrically opposite to area of ST elevation
- Confirms ischemic etiology (not pericarditis — pericarditis lacks true reciprocal changes)
- Example: Inferior STEMI (elevation in II, III, aVF) with reciprocal depression in I, aVL
- Reciprocal changes increase specificity for STEMI diagnosis

## STEMI Localization

### STEMI Territory Localization Table

| Territory | Leads with ST Elevation | Reciprocal Depression | Culprit Artery |
|-----------|------------------------|----------------------|----------------|
| Anterior | V1-V4 | II, III, aVF (sometimes) | LAD (mid to distal) |
| Anteroseptal | V1-V3 | None typically | LAD (proximal to diagonal) |
| Anterolateral (extensive) | V1-V6, I, aVL | II, III, aVF | LAD (proximal, before first diagonal and first septal) |
| Lateral | I, aVL, V5-V6 | II, III, aVF | LCx or diagonal branch of LAD |
| High lateral | I, aVL | II, III, aVF | First diagonal or obtuse marginal |
| Inferior | II, III, aVF | I, aVL | RCA (85%), LCx (15%) |
| Posterior | ST depression V1-V3, tall R V1-V2, upright T V1-V3 | None (V1-V3 changes ARE the equivalent) | RCA or LCx |
| Right ventricular | ST elevation V4R (right-sided lead) | None | Proximal RCA (before RV marginals) |

### Posterior STEMI

- No direct posterior leads on standard ECG
- Mirror image sign in V1-V3: ST depression + tall broad R wave + upright T wave = reciprocal image of posterior ST elevation, Q wave, and T inversion
- ST depression in V1-V3 that is horizontal or downsloping with tall R waves = posterior STEMI until proven otherwise
- Confirm with posterior leads V7-V9 (placed on left back): ST elevation ≥ 0.5 mm in V7-V9
- Management: Same as STEMI — urgent cath lab activation
- Important: Posterior STEMI is often missed; any anterior ST depression that seems "out of proportion" should prompt posterior lead placement

### Right Ventricular Infarction

- Occurs in 30-50% of inferior STEMIs (proximal RCA occlusion before RV marginal branches)
- Clinical triad: Hypotension + clear lungs + elevated JVP in setting of inferior MI
- ECG: Obtain right-sided leads in all inferior STEMI — ST elevation ≥ 1 mm in V4R is most sensitive
- Right-sided leads (V3R, V4R) diagnostic; ST elevation in V4R most specific
- Management:
  - IV fluid resuscitation — RV is preload dependent
  - Avoid nitrates (cause preload reduction — can precipitate catastrophic hypotension)
  - Avoid diuretics
  - Avoid morphine (reduces preload)
  - Maintain sinus rhythm (RV depends on atrial kick — cardiovert AF immediately)
  - Urgent reperfusion (PCI preferred)

### LAD Occlusion Patterns — Proximal vs Distal

| Level of LAD Occlusion | ECG Pattern |
|------------------------|-------------|
| Proximal (before first septal and first diagonal) | V1-V6 + I + aVL elevation; loss of septal Q; LBBB possible |
| Mid (after first diagonal, before second diagonal) | V2-V5 elevation |
| Distal | V4-V5 elevation only |
| First diagonal branch | I, aVL, V5-V6 elevation (lateral pattern) |

## NSTEMI and Unstable Angina

### Diagnostic Criteria for NSTEMI on ECG

- ST depression (horizontal or downsloping ≥ 0.5-1 mm in ≥ 2 contiguous leads)
- T wave inversions (≥ 1 mm in ≥ 2 contiguous leads with prominent R wave)
- Transient ST elevation (resolves within 20 minutes)
- New or dynamic ECG changes with troponin elevation = NSTEMI
- Symptoms without ECG changes + elevated troponin = NSTEMI
- Normal ECG does NOT exclude NSTEMI

### TIMI and GRACE Risk Scoring

- TIMI Risk Score (0-7): Points for age ≥ 65, ≥ 3 CAD risk factors, prior coronary stenosis, ST deviation on ECG, ≥ 2 anginal events in 24h, prior aspirin use in 7 days, elevated serum cardiac markers
- GRACE Score: Incorporates age, HR, BP, creatinine, Killip class, cardiac arrest at presentation, ST deviation, elevated biomarkers — provides in-hospital and 6-month mortality
- High risk features warranting early invasive strategy (within 24 hours): Dynamic ECG changes, elevated troponin, hemodynamic instability, signs of heart failure, sustained VT/VF, EF < 40%, prior PCI/CABG, GRACE score > 140

## Special Ischemia Patterns

### Wellens Syndrome

- ECG finding during pain-free interval (not during active chest pain)
- Represents critical proximal LAD stenosis
- Type A (less common): Biphasic T waves in V2-V3 (initially upright, then inverted)
- Type B (more common): Deep symmetric T wave inversions in V2-V3 (sometimes V1-V4)
- Normal or mildly elevated cardiac biomarkers
- R waves preserved (no infarction yet)
- Critical warning: These patients are at very high risk for anterior wall MI
- Management: Urgent cardiac catheterization — do NOT perform stress testing (can precipitate MI); patients often feel well between episodes
- Do not mistake for NSTEMI being managed conservatively — these patients need the cath lab within 24-48 hours

### De Winter T Waves

- ECG pattern representing acute proximal LAD occlusion (STEMI equivalent)
- Not widely recognized as STEMI but requires same urgency
- Criteria:
  - Upsloping ST depression ≥ 1 mm at J-point in V1-V6
  - Tall, symmetrically peaked T waves in V1-V6
  - ST elevation in aVR (≥ 1 mm)
  - Absence of ST elevation in precordial leads
- Mechanism: Probably represents a form of complete proximal LAD occlusion with unusual ECG expression
- Prevalence: About 2% of all LAD occlusions
- Management: Activate cath lab immediately — same as STEMI

### Hyperacute T Waves

- First ECG change in STEMI — may precede ST elevation by minutes
- Morphology: Broad, tall, symmetric T waves; larger than normal; often taller than R wave in the affected leads
- Most visible in V2-V5 in anterior MI; in II, III, aVF in inferior MI
- Key distinguishing feature from hyperkalemia T waves: Hyperacute T waves are broad (wide base); hyperkalemia T waves are narrow and peaked ("tent-shaped")
- Management: Serial ECGs every 15-30 minutes; if developing into STEMI pattern, activate cath lab

### Aortic Dissection on ECG

- May mimic STEMI if dissection involves coronary ostia
- Stanford Type A (ascending aorta) can cause inferior STEMI if dissection extends to RCA
- Key: If STEMI pattern is present but mechanism unclear, consider dissection — especially with tearing back pain, BP differential, or wide mediastinum on CXR
- Thrombolytics are absolutely contraindicated in aortic dissection
- Obtain CT angiography if clinical suspicion exists before thrombolytics

### Takotsubo Cardiomyopathy (Stress Cardiomyopathy)

- ECG may mimic STEMI; ST elevation typically in anterior leads
- Evolves to deep T wave inversions across the precordium (sometimes leads I, II, III — very diffuse)
- QT prolongation prominent (risk of torsades)
- Distinguishing features: Disproportionate T wave inversion across multiple territories; diffuse changes not confined to one vascular territory; clinical context (emotional/physical stress, predominantly postmenopausal women)
- Diagnosis: Ventriculography showing apical ballooning; angiography with no obstructive CAD

## Electrolyte Effects on ECG

### Hyperkalemia

**Progressive ECG Changes with Rising Potassium:**

| Serum K+ Level | ECG Changes |
|----------------|-------------|
| 5.5-6.5 mEq/L | Peaked, narrow, symmetric T waves (tented T waves) |
| 6.5-7.0 mEq/L | PR interval prolongation; P wave flattening |
| 7.0-7.5 mEq/L | P wave disappears; widening QRS |
| 7.5-8.0 mEq/L | Sinusoidal ("sine wave") pattern — QRS merges with T |
| > 8.0 mEq/L | VF or asystole |

**Treatment Ladder:**
1. Calcium gluconate 1-2 g IV (membrane stabilization — does NOT lower K+; immediate action within minutes)
2. Insulin 10 units + dextrose 50% 50 mL IV (shifts K+ into cells; onset 15-30 min; duration 2-4 h)
3. Sodium bicarbonate 50-100 mEq IV (shifts K+ intracellularly; most effective in metabolic acidosis)
4. Albuterol nebulization 10-20 mg (shifts K+ intracellularly; onset 30 min)
5. Sodium polystyrene sulfonate (Kayexalate) or patiromer (eliminates K+; slow onset hours)
6. Furosemide IV (increases renal K+ excretion if functioning kidneys)
7. Dialysis (definitive removal; urgent in severe cases with cardiac manifestations)

### Hypokalemia

**ECG Changes:**
- Flattening of T waves
- Prominent U waves (most visible in V2-V3)
- U wave amplitude exceeds T wave amplitude in same lead = significant hypokalemia
- TU fusion — appears as prolonged QT (actually QTU)
- ST depression
- Progressive QT prolongation — risk of torsades de pointes

**Thresholds:**
- K+ < 3.5 mEq/L: ECG changes begin
- K+ < 3.0 mEq/L: Significant risk
- K+ < 2.5 mEq/L: Life-threatening arrhythmia risk

### Hypercalcemia

- Shortened QT interval (most reliable sign)
- Shortened ST segment — QRS goes directly into T wave
- Bradycardia, PR prolongation in severe cases
- Osborn J waves (also seen in hypothermia)

### Hypocalcemia

- Prolonged QT interval (prolonged ST segment)
- Normal T wave duration
- Risk of torsades
- Causes: Hypoparathyroidism, pancreatitis, vitamin D deficiency, citrate toxicity from massive transfusion

### Hypomagnesemia

- Widened QRS, prolonged QT
- U waves, T wave flattening
- Potentiates hypokalemia-induced arrhythmias
- Treatment: IV magnesium sulfate for torsades even with normal serum Mg level

## Drug Effects on ECG

### Digoxin Effect vs Toxicity

**Digoxin Effect (Therapeutic, Not Toxic):**
- Scooped ("reverse tick" or "hockey stick") ST depression — concave down in leads with upright QRS
- Shortened QT interval
- PR prolongation
- T wave flattening or inversion
- These changes confirm therapeutic digoxin; do NOT diagnose toxicity

**Digoxin Toxicity (Almost Any Arrhythmia):**
- PAT with block (paroxysmal atrial tachycardia with 2:1 AV block) — most classic toxicity pattern
- Bidirectional VT — pathognomonic for digoxin toxicity (alternating QRS axis beat-to-beat)
- Frequent PVCs (bigeminy, trigeminy)
- Accelerated junctional rhythms
- Atrial flutter or AF with complete AV block
- VF in severe toxicity
- Treatment: Digoxin-specific antibody fragments (Digibind / DigiFab); avoid calcium in hyperkalemia from digoxin toxicity (theoretical risk)

### QT-Prolonging Drugs

**Antiarrhythmics (Class Ia, Ic, III):**
- Quinidine (Class Ia) — prolongs QT; risk increases with hypokalemia; quinidine syncope = torsades
- Procainamide (Class Ia) — prolongs QT; also causes lupus-like syndrome
- Disopyramide (Class Ia) — prolongs QT; anticholinergic effects
- Sotalol (Class III) — QT prolongation; beta-blocking + K+ channel blocking effects; dose-related risk
- Amiodarone (Class III) — prolongs QT; paradoxically low torsades risk despite significant QT prolongation (heterogeneous effect)
- Dofetilide, ibutilide — significant QT prolongation; require initiation in monitored setting

**Antibiotics:**
- Azithromycin — moderate QT prolongation; cardiac risk in high-risk patients
- Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, moxifloxacin) — QT prolongation; moxifloxacin highest risk
- Chloroquine / hydroxychloroquine — QT prolongation; avoid in baseline prolonged QT
- Pentamidine — significant QT prolongation

**Antipsychotics:**
- Haloperidol — QT prolongation especially with IV use; dose-dependent
- Ziprasidone — moderate QT prolongation
- Thioridazine — significant QT prolongation; multiple other antipsychotics
- Droperidol — black box warning for QT prolongation

**Antidepressants:**
- Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs): QRS widening (sodium channel block) + QT prolongation; TCA overdose = wide QRS, rightward axis, sinus tachycardia; sodium bicarbonate treatment
- Citalopram, escitalopram — dose-dependent QT prolongation; maximum doses exist for this reason

**Other Notable QT Prolongers:**
- Methadone — significant QT prolongation; common in opioid treatment programs
- Ondansetron (Zofran) — QT prolongation; IV doses > 32 mg contraindicated
- Domperidone — QT prolongation; not available in US
- Tacrolimus — QT prolongation; organ transplant patients
- Arsenic trioxide — QT prolongation in cancer treatment

**Combined Risk Assessment:**
- Two QT-prolonging drugs together dramatically increases risk
- Hypokalemia + hypomagnesemia + QT-prolonging drug = very high torsades risk
- Obtain ECG when starting QT-prolonging drugs; repeat at steady state

### Sodium Channel Blockers (Class Ia/Ic, TCAs)

- Widen QRS due to slowed phase 0 depolarization
- Cause right axis deviation of terminal QRS forces (terminal 40 ms rightward deviation)
- Lead I: Deep S wave; aVR: R wave > 3 mm or R/S > 0.7
- In TCA overdose: QRS > 100 ms predicts seizures; QRS > 160 ms predicts ventricular arrhythmias
- Treatment: Sodium bicarbonate (alkalinization + sodium loading)

## Pericarditis

### ECG Stages of Acute Pericarditis

| Stage | Timing | ECG Features |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| Stage I | Hours to days (acute) | Diffuse ST elevation (concave/saddle-shaped); PR depression; Spodick's sign |
| Stage II | Days to week | ST normalizes; T waves flatten |
| Stage III | Weeks | Diffuse T wave inversions |
| Stage IV | Weeks to months | ECG normalizes |

### Diagnostic ECG Features

- Diffuse ST elevation (concave / saddle-shaped — concave up): Present in I, II, III, aVF, V2-V6 (virtually all leads)
- ST depression in aVR and V1 (opposite to all other leads)
- PR depression: Inflammation of atrial myocardium — most specific ECG finding for pericarditis; visible in leads II, V5-V6; PR elevation in aVR
- Spodick's sign: Downsloping TP segment in lead II — specific for pericarditis
- Sinus tachycardia: Common, especially with fever

### Differentiating Pericarditis from STEMI

| Feature | Pericarditis | STEMI |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| ST shape | Concave (saddle-shaped) | Convex (domed) |
| Distribution | Diffuse, almost all leads | Regional (coronary territory) |
| Reciprocal changes | Absent (except aVR/V1) | Present |
| PR depression | Present | Absent |
| Q waves | Not present | Develop over hours |
| Lead aVR | ST depression | ST elevation (LMCA/proximal LAD occlusion) |

### Treatment of Pericarditis

- First-line: Aspirin 750-1000 mg every 8 hours x 2 weeks (with proton pump inhibitor) PLUS colchicine 0.5 mg twice daily x 3 months
- Alternative to aspirin: Ibuprofen 600 mg every 8 hours x 2 weeks + colchicine
- Corticosteroids: Reserved for refractory cases, recurrent episodes, specific causes (connective tissue disease, uremic pericarditis); avoid as first-line (associated with higher recurrence)
- Avoid NSAIDs in post-MI pericarditis (Dressler's syndrome) — may impair myocardial healing; use colchicine and low-dose aspirin

## Pre-excitation and Wolff-Parkinson-White Syndrome

### WPW ECG Criteria

- Short PR interval (< 120 ms) — accessory pathway conducts faster than AV node
- Delta wave — slurred initial upstroke of QRS (pre-excitation of ventricular myocardium)
- Wide QRS (> 120 ms) — fusion of pre-excited and normally conducted depolarization
- Secondary ST and T wave changes — discordant with QRS direction (normal consequence of abnormal depolarization)

### WPW Pathway Localization

| Delta wave polarity | Likely pathway location |
|--------------------|------------------------|
| Positive delta in II, III, aVF; negative in V1 | Posteroseptal or right posterior |
| Negative delta in II, III, aVF; positive V1 | Anteroseptal |
| Negative delta in I, aVL; positive inferior leads | Left lateral |
| Positive delta all precordial leads | Right free wall |

### WPW Tachyarrhythmias

**Orthodromic AVRT:**
- Narrow complex regular tachycardia (most common tachycardia in WPW)
- Antegrade conduction via AV node; retrograde via accessory pathway
- Retrograde P waves visible in ST segment
- Treatment: Vagal maneuvers → adenosine → AV nodal agents → cardioversion

**Antidromic AVRT:**
- Wide complex regular tachycardia — antegrade via accessory pathway
- Fully pre-excited QRS (maximally wide, delta-wave morphology throughout)
- Rare; high risk of degeneration to VF
- Treatment: Procainamide IV or cardioversion; avoid AV nodal blockers

**AF with Pre-excitation (Most Dangerous):**
- Irregular wide complex rapid tachycardia — irregular rhythm distinguishes from VT
- Ventricular rate can be extremely rapid (> 200-300 bpm) if accessory pathway has short refractory period
- Rates > 250 bpm in WPW-AF = high risk of VF and sudden cardiac death
- ECG: Grossly irregular wide complex tachycardia with varying QRS morphology (fusion of pre-excited and normally conducted beats)

**Critical Contraindications in WPW with AF:**
- AV nodal blocking agents are CONTRAINDICATED: Adenosine, digoxin, verapamil, diltiazem, beta-blockers
- Mechanism of harm: Block AV node → all impulses forced down accessory pathway → extreme ventricular rate → VF
- Safe treatment: Procainamide IV (slows accessory pathway conduction), ibutilide, cardioversion
- Stable: IV procainamide 15-17 mg/kg over 30-60 min
- Unstable: Immediate synchronized cardioversion

**Long-Term Management:**
- Ablation of accessory pathway: First-line definitive treatment; > 95% cure rate
- Risk stratification: Asymptomatic WPW — electrophysiology study to assess pathway refractory period; short refractory period = high risk of rapid AF conduction → SCD

## Inherited Arrhythmia Syndromes

### Brugada Syndrome

**Type 1 Pattern (Diagnostic):**
- Coved ST elevation ≥ 2 mm in ≥ 1 right precordial lead (V1-V3) — typical high V1-V2 placement (2nd intercostal space)
- ST elevation with descending portion and negative T wave (not biphasic)
- "Coved" morphology = elevated ST slopes downward into negative T wave
- May be spontaneous or unmasked by sodium channel blockers, fever, vagal tone, alcohol, cocaine

**Type 2 Pattern (Not Diagnostic Alone):**
- Saddleback ST elevation with positive or biphasic T wave in V1-V3
- Must convert to Type 1 with sodium channel blocker challenge (ajmaline, flecainide, procainamide) to be diagnostic

**Clinical Associations:**
- Predominantly males (8:1 ratio)
- Asian descent (particularly Southeast Asian)
- Sudden unexplained nocturnal death syndrome (SUNDS)
- VF during sleep or rest; vagal predominance
- Fever is a common trigger — antipyretics essential in Brugada patients

**Management:**
- ICD: Indicated for prior cardiac arrest, sustained VT, or spontaneous Type 1 pattern with symptoms (syncope)
- Quinidine: Class I antiarrhythmic; reduces VF episodes; option for asymptomatic patients with inducible VF or ICD storms
- Sodium channel blockers CONTRAINDICATED: Flecainide, propafenone, procainamide worsen pattern and risk

### Long QT Syndrome (LQTS)

**Congenital LQTS Subtypes:**

| Type | Gene | Channel | Trigger | Key Features |
|------|------|---------|---------|--------------|
| LQTS1 | KCNQ1 | IKs (slow K+) | Exercise, swimming | T waves broad-based; most common; best beta-blocker response |
| LQTS2 | KCNQ2 | IKr (rapid K+) | Auditory stimuli, startle, sleep | T waves notched or low amplitude; avoid sudden loud sounds |
| LQTS3 | SCN5A | INa (late Na+) | Sleep, rest, bradycardia | Distinctive long ST segment; late T wave; mexiletine may help |

**Diagnostic Criteria (Schwartz Score):**
- QTc > 480 ms: 3 points; 460-479: 2 points; 450-459 (in males): 1 point
- Torsades: 2 points; T wave alternans: 1 point; notched T waves in 3 leads: 1 point
- Bradycardia: 0.5 points; syncope with stress: 2 points; without stress: 1 point
- Congenital deafness: 0.5 points; family history: 1-2 points
- Score > 3.5: High probability LQTS

**Risk Stratification:**
- QTc ≥ 500 ms: High risk of cardiac events
- Male sex, younger age at presentation, prior cardiac arrest = higher risk
- Event rates vary by genotype

**Treatment:**
- All types: Beta-blockers (first-line — most effective in LQTS1); avoid QT-prolonging drugs; maintain normal electrolytes; treat fever aggressively
- LQTS2: Avoid sudden auditory triggers; keep K+ ≥ 4.5 mEq/L
- LQTS3: Mexiletine (late INa blocker) may shorten QT and reduce events; ICD often recommended
- ICD: Prior cardiac arrest; symptomatic on medical therapy; very high risk features
- Left cardiac sympathetic denervation (LCSD): Surgical option for recurrent events despite ICD or as adjunct

### Short QT Syndrome

- QTc < 340 ms consistently
- Tall peaked T waves; J-point may be elevated
- Risk of sudden cardiac death and AF
- Autosomal dominant; gain-of-function mutations in K+ channels or loss-of-function in Ca2+ channels
- Treatment: ICD; quinidine may prolong QT

### Catecholaminergic Polymorphic VT (CPVT)

- ECG at rest: Normal QTc, normal baseline
- During exercise or emotional stress: Bidirectional VT or polymorphic VT
- Mechanism: RYR2 or CASQ2 mutations causing abnormal ryanodine receptor calcium release
- Treatment: Beta-blockers; flecainide (additional benefit); ICD; LCSD

### Early Repolarization Syndrome

- J-point elevation ≥ 1 mm in ≥ 2 inferior or lateral leads; often notching or slurring at J-point
- Common finding (prevalence 1-5% of general population) — most cases benign
- Associated with increased SCD risk when: J-wave amplitude ≥ 2 mm, horizontal/downsloping ST, inferior leads involvement, history of VF
- Differentiation from STEMI: Stable over time, no symptoms, no reciprocal changes, no evolution

## Pacemaker ECG Basics

### Pacing Spikes and Morphology

- Pacing spike: Vertical deflection (stimulus artifact) on ECG
- Atrial pacing: Spike before P wave; atria capture with normal-appearing P morphology
- Ventricular pacing: Spike before QRS; wide complex QRS with LBBB morphology (standard RV apical pacing)
- Biventricular pacing (CRT): Spike before QRS; may produce narrower QRS or different morphology than RV pacing alone

### Pacemaker Modes (NBG Code)

| Position | Meaning | Common Values |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| 1st letter | Chamber paced | A (atrial), V (ventricular), D (dual) |
| 2nd letter | Chamber sensed | A, V, D, O (none) |
| 3rd letter | Response to sensing | I (inhibit), T (trigger), D (dual), O (none) |

- VVI: Ventricular paced, ventricular sensed, inhibited by native beat — single chamber demand pacing
- DDD: Dual chamber paced and sensed, tracks atrial rate and paces ventricle with AV delay — most physiologic
- AAI: Atrial pacing only — used in sinus node dysfunction with intact AV conduction

### Pacemaker Malfunction

| Type | Description | ECG Finding |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| Failure to pace | No pacing spike when expected | Pause without spike; bradycardia |
| Failure to capture | Spike without resulting complex | Pacing spike without P wave or QRS |
| Failure to sense | Inappropriate pacing despite native rhythm | Spike falls within or after native QRS (competitive pacing) |
| Pacemaker-mediated tachycardia | Endless loop tachycardia in DDD pacemakers | Regular tachycardia at upper rate limit |
| Rate-related failure | Pacing at wrong rate | Rate different from programmed settings |

### Pacemaker-Induced Changes

- LBBB morphology of paced QRS: Normal for transvenous RV apical lead (current flows from apex rightward and superiorly)
- ST depression/T wave inversion in paced beats: Normal discordant repolarization — do not interpret as ischemia
- Ischemia in paced rhythm: Apply modified Sgarbossa criteria (same as LBBB)
- Pseudo-right bundle branch block in epicardial LV pacing: Less common pattern

## Miscellaneous Patterns

### Pulmonary Embolism

- ECG often nonspecific; sinus tachycardia most common finding
- S1Q3T3 pattern: Deep S wave in lead I, Q wave in lead III, T wave inversion in lead III — specific but not sensitive
- New RBBB — RV strain pattern
- RV strain: T wave inversions in V1-V4 and/or right precordial leads
- Right axis deviation
- P pulmonale (right atrial enlargement)
- AF or flutter (acute)
- All findings reflect acute RV pressure overload
- Sensitivity of ECG for PE: Low; normal ECG does not exclude PE

### Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH)

**Voltage Criteria (most commonly used):**
- Sokolow-Lyon: S in V1 + R in V5 or V6 ≥ 35 mm
- Cornell: R in aVL + S in V3 > 28 mm (men) or > 20 mm (women)
- Limb lead: R in aVL > 11 mm

**Associated Findings (increase specificity):**
- LVH strain pattern: ST depression and T wave inversion in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5-V6)
- Left axis deviation
- Left atrial enlargement (P mitrale)
- Prolonged QRS duration

**Note:** Voltage alone has low sensitivity (~50%) but high specificity when combined with repolarization changes.

### Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (RVH)

- Right axis deviation (> +90 degrees)
- Tall R wave in V1 (R > S in V1; R > 7 mm in V1)
- qR pattern in V1
- Deep S waves in V5-V6
- RV strain pattern: T wave inversions V1-V4
- Causes: Pulmonary hypertension, COPD, pulmonary stenosis, mitral stenosis, congenital shunts

### Hypothermia

- Osborn (J) waves: Characteristic positive deflection at J-point (junction of QRS and ST segment), most prominent in V3-V4, II; amplitude increases as temperature falls
- Present below core temperature 30-32 degrees C
- Bradycardia, PR prolongation, wide QRS, QT prolongation
- Risk of VF below 28-30 degrees C
- Atrial and ventricular arrhythmias possible
- Defibrillation may be ineffective until temperature is raised

### Dextrocardia

- Global lead reversal: P, QRS, T all inverted in lead I
- Progressive decrease in R wave across V1-V6 (reverse R wave progression)
- Lead aVR: Positive P, QRS, T (looks like "normal" lead I)
- Distinguish from lead reversal: In lead reversal, precordial leads are normal (chest leads placed correctly)
- Management: Reverse all limb leads and place precordial leads on right chest for standard ECG

### CNS Events and ECG

- Large cerebral events (subarachnoid hemorrhage, stroke, head trauma) can cause dramatic ECG changes
- Deep T wave inversions across multiple leads (may mimic anterior ischemia)
- QT prolongation (risk of torsades)
- U waves
- "Neurogenic T waves" — deep, wide, symmetric T inversions
- ST changes, Q waves (rare)
- Mechanism: Sympathetic storm, catecholamine surge, direct hypothalamic effects on cardiac electrophysiology
- Key point: ECG changes from CNS event alone should not trigger urgent coronary catheterization without clinical correlation

## Quick Reference Tables

### Summary: Narrow vs Wide Complex Tachycardia Management

| Rhythm | Stable Management | Unstable Management |
|--------|------------------|---------------------|
| SVT (AVNRT/AVRT) | Vagal → adenosine → CCB/BB | Synchronized cardioversion |
| Atrial flutter | Rate control; cardioversion; ablation | Synchronized cardioversion |
| Atrial fibrillation | Rate control; anticoagulation; rhythm control | Synchronized cardioversion |
| VT (monomorphic) | Amiodarone IV; lidocaine; cardioversion | Synchronized cardioversion |
| VT (polymorphic/torsades) | Magnesium; stop QT drugs; pacing | Defibrillation (unsynchronized if no R wave) |
| VF | CPR + defibrillation | CPR + defibrillation |
| WPW with AF | Procainamide IV or cardioversion | Synchronized cardioversion |

### Key Numeric Thresholds

| Parameter | Normal | Abnormal |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| PR interval | 120-200 ms | < 120 ms (pre-excitation); > 200 ms (1st degree block) |
| QRS duration | < 120 ms | ≥ 120 ms (complete BBB); > 160 ms (VT likely) |
| QTc (men) | < 440 ms | > 500 ms (high torsades risk) |
| QTc (women) | < 460 ms | > 500 ms (high torsades risk) |
| ST elevation | < 1 mm (limb); < 2 mm (V1-V3) | ≥ 1 mm (limb); ≥ 2 mm (V1-V3) for STEMI criteria |
| ST depression | 0 mm | ≥ 0.5 mm (ischemia threshold) |
| Sinus pause | < 2 sec | > 3 sec (pacemaker indication) |
| Delta wave | Absent | Present = pre-excitation |
| P wave amplitude | < 2.5 mm | ≥ 2.5 mm in II = RAE |
| P wave duration | < 120 ms | ≥ 120 ms = LAE |

### Drugs Absolutely Contraindicated in Specific Arrhythmias

| Drug Class | Contraindicated In | Risk |
|------------|-------------------|------|
| AV nodal blockers (adenosine, CCB, digoxin, BB) | WPW with AF | Ventricular fibrillation |
| QT-prolonging antiarrhythmics (amiodarone) | Torsades de pointes | Worsening torsades |
| Sodium channel blockers (flecainide, procainamide) | Brugada syndrome | VF, worsening pattern |
| Sodium channel blockers | Structural heart disease/ischemia | Pro-arrhythmia, increased mortality |
| Nitrates | RV infarction; severe aortic stenosis | Catastrophic hypotension |
| Beta-blockers | Decompensated HF (acute) | Cardiogenic shock |
| Thrombolytics | Suspected aortic dissection | Death from hemorrhage |

### ECG Patterns That Are STEMI Equivalents (Require Cath Lab Activation)

| Pattern | Description | Culprit |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Classic STEMI criteria | ST elevation in ≥ 2 contiguous leads | Varies |
| New LBBB with chest pain | ST changes in LBBB meeting Sgarbossa | Usually LAD |
| Posterior STEMI | ST depression + tall R in V1-V3 | RCA or LCx |
| De Winter T waves | Upsloping ST depression + tall T + aVR elevation | Proximal LAD |
| Wellens syndrome (with pain) | Biphasic or deep T V2-V3 | Proximal LAD |
| Hyperacute T waves with symptoms | Broad tall T before ST elevation | Acute onset |
