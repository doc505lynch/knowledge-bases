## 1. Core Concepts and Philosophy

### The Golden Hour

The "Golden Hour" was coined by Dr. R. Adams Cowley at the University of Maryland Shock Trauma Center in the 1970s. Cowley observed that trauma patients who reached definitive surgical care within 60 minutes of injury had significantly improved survival compared to those who did not.

**Evidence review:**
- The term is conceptually valid but not rigidly time-bound — it represents a window of opportunity, not a fixed deadline.
- More recent evidence (Lerner and Moscati, 2001) challenges the strict 60-minute threshold; outcomes depend on injury type, mechanism, and intervention capability.
- For penetrating truncal trauma (especially gunshot wounds to the torso), the "platinum 10 minutes" — time at scene — may be more predictive than total transport time.
- Blunt multisystem trauma may tolerate slightly longer prehospital times if life-threatening hemorrhage is not present.
- The core principle remains valid: prolonged time to hemorrhage control and surgical intervention worsens outcomes.

### Platinum 10 Minutes

The "Platinum 10 Minutes" is the target maximum scene time for critically injured trauma patients.

**Rationale:**
- Scene time is non-productive time for definitive hemorrhage control.
- Only life-saving interventions should be performed on scene.
- Airway, breathing threats, and critical hemorrhage may require brief on-scene treatment.
- Everything else (IV access, splinting, detailed assessment) is performed en route.

**On-scene priorities:**
- Hemorrhage control (tourniquet application, wound packing)
- Airway positioning or adjunct if obstructed
- Decompression of tension pneumothorax if immediately life-threatening
- Spinal motion restriction if indicated — do not delay transport

**Load and Go criteria:**
- Penetrating trauma to head, neck, chest, or abdomen
- Altered mental status from presumed hemorrhagic shock
- Uncontrolled external hemorrhage
- Mechanism suggesting high-energy injury with physiologic compromise

### Mechanism of Injury (MOI)

Understanding how energy was transferred to the patient guides anticipation of injuries even before examination findings are apparent.

#### Blunt vs. Penetrating Trauma

| Feature | Blunt Trauma | Penetrating Trauma |
|---|---|---|
| Energy transfer | Diffuse, distributed over surface area | Concentrated along wound tract |
| Cavitation | Temporary cavity at impact | Permanent + temporary cavity (bullets) |
| Predictability | Less predictable injury pattern | More predictable by trajectory |
| Common causes | MVC, falls, assaults, blast | GSW, stab wounds, impalement |
| Occult injuries | More common | Less common but deep tract possible |

#### High-Energy vs. Low-Energy Mechanisms

**High-energy indicators (blunt):**
- Motor vehicle crash (MVC) at >40 mph
- Rollover or ejection from vehicle
- Death of another occupant in same vehicle
- Fall >20 feet (or 3x patient height)
- Motorcycle crash at >20 mph without protective gear
- Pedestrian or cyclist struck by vehicle

**High-energy indicators (penetrating):**
- High-velocity rifle rounds (muzzle velocity >2000 fps)
- Close-range shotgun wounds
- Multiple stab wounds

**Low-energy mechanisms:**
- Stab wounds with non-serrated blade
- Low-caliber handgun at distance
- Falls from standing height in healthy adults

### Kinematics

Kinematics is the study of motion and energy transfer as it relates to trauma.

**Key principles:**

- **Newton's first law:** An object in motion stays in motion. In deceleration injuries (MVC), the body continues moving after the vehicle stops — energy must be absorbed somewhere.
- **Kinetic energy:** KE = 1/2 mv^2. Velocity is the dominant variable. Doubling mass doubles KE; doubling velocity quadruples KE. This explains the lethality of high-velocity projectiles.
- **Energy transfer:** Tissue damage is proportional to energy transferred per unit volume. Dense, inelastic tissue (liver, spleen) absorbs more energy and sustains greater injury than elastic tissue (lung).

#### Deceleration Injuries

- **Down-and-under pathway (MVC):** Pelvis slides under lap belt → lumbar spine compression/fracture; chest hits steering column → cardiac contusion, aortic shear injury.
- **Up-and-over pathway:** Head and thorax contact windshield or A-pillar; neck hyperextension or flexion injury; thoracic aorta shear at ligamentum arteriosum.
- **Aortic injury:** The descending aorta is fixed at the ligamentum arteriosum; the arch and heart are mobile. Sudden deceleration creates a shear force at this junction — traumatic aortic injury.

#### Blast Injuries

Blast injury is classified into four categories:

| Category | Mechanism | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | Blast overpressure wave (barotrauma) | Pulmonary blast injury, tympanic membrane rupture, bowel rupture |
| Secondary | Fragmentation (shrapnel) | Penetrating wounds, eye injuries |
| Tertiary | Body displacement by blast wind | Blunt trauma, fractures, TBI |
| Quaternary | All other blast-related injuries | Burns, crush, inhalation, toxic exposure |

**Clinical pearls — blast:**
- Pulmonary blast injury presents with dyspnea, hemoptysis, and hypoxia; may not be immediately apparent.
- Air embolism is a complication of pulmonary blast injury — avoid positive pressure ventilation if possible (increases risk).
- TBI is the signature injury of modern blast exposure; may occur even without obvious external head trauma.
- Tympanic membrane rupture: do not use as sole indicator of significant blast exposure — may be absent despite serious primary blast injury.

### Scene Safety and Size-Up

Scene safety precedes all patient contact.

**Standard size-up sequence:**
1. Scene safety — look for ongoing hazards (fire, traffic, downed power lines, hostile subjects, structural instability, hazmat)
2. Standard precautions — gloves at minimum; mask/eye protection for suspected airborne or blood splash risk
3. Number of patients — single vs. multiple casualty incident (MCI)
4. Mechanism of injury — gather visual information before touching patient
5. Need for additional resources — ALS, fire/rescue, helicopter, additional ambulances, hazmat team

**MCI triage (START system):**
- **Immediate (Red):** Breathing present only after repositioning; RR >30 or <10; no radial pulse; altered mental status
- **Delayed (Yellow):** Ambulatory or can follow commands; injuries present but not immediately life-threatening
- **Minor (Green):** "Walking wounded"; minor injuries
- **Expectant (Black):** Apneic after repositioning; injuries not survivable with available resources

## 2. Primary Survey — MARCH / XABCDE Framework

### Historical Context

The traditional ABC (Airway, Breathing, Circulation) approach was developed for cardiac arrest and medical emergencies. In trauma, uncontrolled hemorrhage kills faster than airway obstruction in most penetrating and many blunt trauma scenarios. The military experience in Iraq and Afghanistan — where 90% of preventable combat deaths were from hemorrhage — drove the shift to hemorrhage-first assessment.

**MARCH framework (Military/TCCC):**
- M — Massive hemorrhage
- A — Airway
- R — Respiration (Breathing)
- C — Circulation
- H — Hypothermia prevention

**XABCDE framework (Civilian PHTLS 9th/10th edition):**
- X — Exsanguinating hemorrhage (critical external bleeding)
- A — Airway
- B — Breathing
- C — Circulation
- D — Disability
- E — Expose/Environment

Both frameworks prioritize hemorrhage control before airway in the trauma patient.

## 3. Massive Hemorrhage Control (M / X)

### Overview

Hemorrhage is the leading cause of preventable death in trauma — responsible for approximately 30-40% of trauma fatalities. The majority of these deaths occur within the first hour. Control of external hemorrhage is the single highest-priority intervention in trauma.

### Tourniquet Application

#### Commercial Tourniquets

| Device | Type | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| CAT (Combat Application Tourniquet) | Windlass | Single-handed application; proven in combat; TCCC standard |
| SOFTT-W (Special Operations Forces Tactical Tourniquet-Wide) | Windlass | Wide band (1.5"); effective on larger limbs; requires two hands for self-application |
| RATS (Rapid Application Tourniquet System) | Elastic | Fast application; less evidence base than CAT |
| SAM-XT | Windlass | Auto-locking buckle; TCCC approved |

#### Application Technique

1. Apply 2-3 inches proximal to the wound edge (above the wound, toward the torso).
2. Thread the strap through the buckle or self-adhesive closure; pull tight.
3. Turn the windlass rod until bleeding stops — do not stop at pain; tighten until hemorrhage is controlled.
4. Secure the windlass with the locking clip or hook-and-loop strap.
5. Note the time of application in marker directly on the tourniquet or on the patient's skin.
6. Do NOT cover the tourniquet — it must remain visible.
7. Do NOT remove a tourniquet in the prehospital setting once applied for life-threatening hemorrhage.

**Tourniquet time considerations:**
- Limb viability is generally preserved for up to 2 hours of tourniquet application.
- Beyond 2 hours, risk of permanent neurovascular injury increases substantially.
- Some evidence supports safe application up to 4-6 hours in extreme circumstances; however, limb salvage declines significantly after 2 hours.
- Notify receiving facility of application time.

**Contraindications / limitations:**
- Tourniquets cannot be applied to junctional wounds (axilla, groin, neck).
- Not effective for partial amputations with intact bone if bone routes blood flow distally.
- Improperly sized or loose tourniquets may increase hemorrhage by obstructing venous outflow without stopping arterial flow (venous tourniquet effect).

### Wound Packing

Used for wounds not amenable to tourniquet application (junctional, truncal) or as an adjunct to tourniquet application.

**Technique:**
1. Open the wound and visually identify the bleeding source if possible.
2. Pack hemostatic gauze (preferred) tightly into the wound — do not leave air pockets.
3. Use all of the available gauze; push it firmly to the base of the wound with a finger or fist.
4. Apply firm, sustained direct pressure for a minimum of 3 minutes (hemostatic gauze) or 5 minutes (plain gauze).
5. Maintain pressure and do not prematurely check the wound.
6. Secure with a pressure bandage.

**Hemostatic agents:**

| Agent | Active Component | Mechanism | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Combat Gauze (QuikClot) | Kaolin | Activates the clotting cascade (contact activation) | TCCC first-line; no exothermic reaction with current formulation |
| Celox Gauze | Chitosan | Ionic interaction; binds red blood cells independent of coagulation | Effective in coagulopathic patients; does not require intact clotting cascade |
| HemCon Bandage | Chitosan | Same as Celox | Bandage format |
| XStat | Compressed cellulose sponges with chitosan | Rapidly expands to fill wound cavity | For deep narrow wounds (gunshot channels); radiopaque markers; must be removed surgically |

### Junctional Hemorrhage

Junctional wounds — axilla, groin, pelvis, neck — cannot be controlled with standard extremity tourniquets.

**Devices:**

- **JETT (Junctional Emergency Treatment Tool):** Rigid disc that applies direct pressure to the femoral triangle; approved for inguinal wounds.
- **SAM-JT (SAM Junctional Tourniquet):** Inflatable pneumatic bladder device; targets the inguinal region and may be used for pelvic hemorrhage control.
- **AAJT (Abdominal Aortic Junctional Tourniquet):** Applies pressure at the level of the distal aorta; for bilateral lower extremity or pelvic hemorrhage.

**Neck wounds:**
- No commercial junctional tourniquet approved for neck.
- Wound packing with direct pressure is the prehospital standard.
- Avoid circumferential neck compression — risk of bilateral carotid occlusion and venous engorgement.
- Foley catheter balloon tamponade has been used in penetrating neck injuries in hospital settings.

### Stop the Bleed Training Principles

The "Stop the Bleed" program (B-Con) was developed after the Sandy Hook shooting to train laypersons in basic hemorrhage control.

**Core principles (A-B-C):**
- **Alert:** Call 911.
- **Bleeding:** Find the bleeding source.
- **Compress:** Apply direct pressure or tourniquet.

**Bystander tourniquet application:**
- Use a commercial tourniquet if available.
- If not: improvise with belt, necktie, or strip of cloth — 2 inches wide minimum; windlass with stick or pen.
- Improvised tourniquets are less reliable but may be life-saving.

## 4. Airway Management (A)

### Initial Airway Assessment

**Signs of airway compromise:**
- Stridor (partial obstruction)
- Gurgling (fluid in airway — suction immediately)
- Snoring (soft tissue obstruction)
- Absent breath sounds
- Cyanosis
- Accessory muscle use
- Agitation or decreased consciousness

### Basic Airway Maneuvers

**Head-tilt/chin-lift:**
- Most effective for unconscious patients without suspected C-spine injury.
- Contraindicated with any possibility of cervical spine injury.

**Jaw-thrust:**
- Preferred technique with suspected C-spine injury.
- Displaces the mandible anteriorly, lifting the tongue off the posterior pharynx.
- Technique: Place fingers behind the angles of the mandible; thrust forward.
- Can be performed while maintaining in-line cervical stabilization.

**Recovery position:**
- For unconscious patients with no suspected spinal injury and who are breathing adequately.
- Left lateral decubitus with upper airway dependent to allow drainage.

### Airway Adjuncts

#### Oropharyngeal Airway (OPA)

- Holds the tongue off the posterior pharynx in unconscious patients.
- **Sizing:** Measure from the corner of the mouth to the earlobe or the center of the mouth to the angle of the jaw.
- **Insertion:** In adults — insert upside down (concave facing upward), rotate 180 degrees as you advance. Alternative (especially in children): use tongue blade, insert curved side down without rotation.
- **Contraindications:** Conscious or semi-conscious patients — triggers gag reflex and vomiting.

#### Nasopharyngeal Airway (NPA)

- Better tolerated in semi-conscious patients than OPA.
- **Sizing:** Measure from the nostril to the earlobe or tragus of ear.
- **Insertion:** Lubricate with water-soluble gel; insert along the floor of the nasal cavity (perpendicular to the face, not angled upward); advance until flange rests at the naris.
- **Contraindications:** Suspected basilar skull fracture (Battle's sign, raccoon eyes, hemotympanum, CSF rhinorrhea/otorrhea) — risk of intracranial placement, though rare. Many systems still allow NPA with suspected basilar skull fracture based on risk-benefit analysis; follow local protocol.
- Right nostril preferred (most NPA curves favor right-sided insertion).

#### Suction

- Yankauer (rigid tonsil-tip) suction: preferred for oropharynx; effective for blood, vomit, secretions.
- Flexible suction catheter: for suctioning through ETT or NPA.
- Limit suction to 15 seconds to avoid hypoxia.
- Suction before ventilation in obstructed airways.

### Advanced Airway Management

#### Endotracheal Intubation (ETI)

Considered the gold standard for definitive airway management.

**Indications in trauma:**
- GCS <8 (inability to protect airway)
- Respiratory failure (hypoxia or hypoventilation not corrected by supplemental O2)
- Anticipated airway deterioration (expanding neck hematoma, inhalation burns, angioedema)
- Facilitation of mechanical ventilation

**Rapid Sequence Intubation (RSI):**

RSI is the preferred technique in trauma to minimize aspiration risk and facilitate laryngoscopy.

**RSI sequence (7 Ps):**
1. **Preparation** — equipment check (ETT, laryngoscope, suction, bag-mask, confirmation device), IV access, drugs drawn up, monitoring
2. **Preoxygenation** — 100% O2 via non-rebreather mask for 3-5 minutes; BVM if apneic
3. **Pretreatment** — atropine (pediatrics, bradycardia risk), lidocaine (historically for ICP — not well supported), fentanyl (optional for sympatholytic in TBI)
4. **Paralysis and induction** — simultaneous administration of induction agent + neuromuscular blocker
5. **Position** — sniffing position (unless C-spine precautions); BURP maneuver if needed
6. **Placement** — direct or video laryngoscopy; confirm with waveform capnography + auscultation
7. **Post-intubation management** — sedation, analgesia, secure tube, ongoing monitoring

**RSI drugs in trauma:**

| Drug | Dose | Route | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ketamine | 1-2 mg/kg IV or 4-6 mg/kg IM | IV preferred | First-line induction in trauma; maintains BP and HR; bronchodilator; analgesic; dissociative. Historically avoided in TBI (concerns re: ICP elevation) — now considered safe and may be beneficial. |
| Etomidate | 0.3 mg/kg IV | IV | Hemodynamically neutral; single dose adrenal suppression (controversial clinical significance in trauma) |
| Succinylcholine | 1.5 mg/kg IV | IV | Depolarizing NMB; rapid onset (45-60 sec) and offset (10-12 min); contraindicated >24h after major burns, crush injury, denervating injuries (hyperkalemia risk) |
| Rocuronium | 1.2 mg/kg IV | IV | Non-depolarizing NMB; onset 60-90 sec at high dose; duration 45-60 min; reversible with sugammadex 16 mg/kg |
| Midazolam | 0.1 mg/kg IV | IV | Adjunct for amnesia/sedation; use cautiously — hypotension in hypovolemia |

**Post-intubation management:**
- Confirm ETT placement: primary — waveform capnography; secondary — auscultation (bilateral, equal), chest rise, O2 saturation improvement.
- Secure ETT: commercial holder or tape.
- Set ventilator: tidal volume 6-8 mL/kg ideal body weight; rate 10-12/min; titrate O2 to SpO2 94-98%.
- Administer sedation and analgesia: propofol infusion or fentanyl + midazolam drip.
- Avoid hypocapnia unless herniation present.

#### Supraglottic Airways

Used when intubation is not possible or fails.

| Device | Type | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| King LT airway | Supraglottic | Blind insertion; dual cuffs (esophageal + pharyngeal); allows ventilation without laryngoscopy |
| LMA (Laryngeal Mask Airway) | Supraglottic | Classic; does not protect against aspiration but allows ventilation |
| i-gel | Supraglottic | No inflatable cuff; thermoplastic material conforms to anatomy |
| Combitube | Dual-lumen | Blind insertion; esophageal or tracheal placement both permit ventilation; largely replaced by King LT |

**Supraglottic airways do not protect against aspiration** — they are temporizing devices.

#### Surgical Cricothyrotomy

Indicated when airway cannot be secured by conventional means — "cannot intubate, cannot oxygenate" (CICO) scenario.

**Anatomy:**
- Cricothyroid membrane: lies between thyroid cartilage (superior) and cricoid cartilage (inferior).
- Located in the midline of the anterior neck, inferior to the thyroid notch.
- Average dimensions in adults: 22-30mm wide, 9-10mm tall.
- Overlying structures: skin, subcutaneous tissue, cricothyroid muscles. No major vessels in the midline.

**Scalpel-finger-tube technique (open/surgical):**
1. Identify the cricothyroid membrane by palpation.
2. Stabilize the larynx with non-dominant hand.
3. Make a transverse stab incision through the membrane — horizontal, 1-2 cm.
4. Hook index finger into the incision to confirm entry into the airway.
5. Insert a cuffed ETT (6.0-7.0) or cuffed tracheostomy tube (size 6) through the incision, directed caudally.
6. Inflate cuff; ventilate; confirm with waveform capnography.

**Melker cricothyrotomy kit (Seldinger technique):**
1. Stab incision through cricothyroid membrane.
2. Insert needle + syringe; aspirate air to confirm position.
3. Advance guidewire through needle.
4. Remove needle; advance dilator + airway catheter over wire.
5. Remove wire and dilator; inflate cuff; ventilate.

**Pediatric note:** Needle cricothyrotomy (14g angiocath + 3mL syringe barrel + 7.0 ETT connector) is preferred under age 8-10 — the pediatric cricothyroid membrane is too small for surgical tube placement. This provides oxygenation but not adequate ventilation; CO2 rises rapidly — bridge only.

## 5. Breathing and Ventilation (B / R)

### Tension Pneumothorax

**Definition:** Air accumulates in the pleural space and cannot escape; progressive increase in intrathoracic pressure compresses the mediastinum and contralateral structures, impairs venous return, and causes hemodynamic collapse.

**Pathophysiology:** One-way valve effect allows air in but not out. Progressive tension shifts mediastinum away from affected side, compresses the contralateral lung and superior/inferior vena cava, reducing preload and cardiac output.

**Clinical signs:**

| Sign | Notes |
|---|---|
| Hypoxia / respiratory distress | Early, often the first sign |
| Decreased or absent breath sounds on affected side | Examine carefully; may be subtle in noisy environments |
| Tracheal deviation AWAY from affected side | Late finding; difficult to appreciate in prehospital setting |
| Hypotension | Occurs with significant mediastinal shift and impaired venous return |
| Jugular venous distension (JVD) | May be absent with concomitant hypovolemia |
| Tachycardia progressing to bradycardia | Bradycardia is a preterminal sign |

**Clinical pearls:**
- In the prehospital setting, do not wait for all classic signs — tension pneumothorax is a clinical diagnosis.
- Positive pressure ventilation (PPV) dramatically accelerates tension pneumothorax in a patient with pneumothorax — any deterioration after intubation must prompt immediate consideration.
- SpO2 will be low or falling; ETCO2 may show decreased waveform with hemodynamic compromise.

**Needle thoracostomy (decompression):**

*Second intercostal space (ICS), midclavicular line (2nd ICS MCL):*
- Classic landmark; go over the superior border of the 3rd rib to avoid neurovascular bundle.
- Use a 14g angiocatheter or dedicated needle (3.25 inch / 8cm needle now recommended for obese patients and those with body armor).
- Chest wall thickness in some patients exceeds standard 3.25cm catheters — failure rate reported 10-40% at this landmark in civilian patients.
- If no rush of air with needle: failure to decompress — reassess or use alternate site.

*Fourth or fifth ICS, anterior axillary line (4th-5th ICS AAL):*
- TCCC and many EMS protocols now prefer this site.
- Thinner chest wall than MCL; higher success rates in obese and muscular patients.
- Go over the superior border of the 5th or 6th rib.
- Insert needle/finger perpendicular to the chest wall.

**Finger thoracostomy:**
- Used when needle decompression fails or is not reliable (tension PTX in intubated patient).
- Make a 2-3cm incision over the 4th-5th ICS, anterior axillary line.
- Blunt dissect through intercostal muscles with Kelly clamp.
- Sweep finger into pleural space — confirm air rush, absence of solid tissue.
- Convert to tube thoracostomy when resources permit.

**Tube thoracostomy (chest tube):**
- Site: 4th-5th ICS, anterior axillary line (same as finger thoracostomy).
- Tube size: 28-32 Fr for hemothorax or hemopneumothorax; 20-24 Fr for pneumothorax alone.
- Technique: incision, blunt dissection, finger sweep, insert tube directed posteriorly and superiorly, connect to water seal or flutter valve.
- Confirm with breath sounds, CXR, improvement in hemodynamics and oxygenation.

### Open Pneumothorax (Sucking Chest Wound)

**Definition:** A defect in the chest wall that communicates with the pleural space, allowing atmospheric air to enter with each breath.

**Pathophysiology:** If the wound is >2/3 the diameter of the trachea, air preferentially enters the chest through the wound rather than the trachea — effective ventilation is impaired.

**Treatment:**
1. Apply a vented chest seal (Asherman seal, Bolin seal, or HyFin seal) to the wound.
2. Vented seals allow air to exit on expiration but prevent entry on inspiration, reducing tension pneumothorax risk.
3. Monitor for development of tension pneumothorax — if develops, lift one edge of the seal to release pressure.
4. Non-vented occlusive dressings (petroleum gauze) risk converting open PTX to tension PTX — if used, tape only 3 sides.
5. Tube thoracostomy should be placed at a remote site (not through the wound).

### Massive Hemothorax

**Definition:** Accumulation of >1500 mL of blood in the pleural space (or >200 mL/hr output after chest tube placement).

**Causes:** Laceration of intercostal vessels, internal mammary artery, lung parenchyma, hilar structures, or great vessels.

**Signs:** Decreased breath sounds on affected side, dullness to percussion (vs. hyperresonance in PTX), hemodynamic instability.

**Treatment:**
1. Tube thoracostomy: large-bore (32-36 Fr); allows drainage and re-expansion.
2. Large-bore IV access; aggressive volume resuscitation with blood products.
3. Massive hemothorax with ongoing hemorrhage is a surgical emergency.
4. Autotransfusion of collected blood may be performed.
5. Indications for thoracotomy: >1500 mL initial drainage, ongoing output >200 mL/hr x3 hours, hemodynamic instability.

### Flail Chest

**Definition:** Three or more consecutive ribs fractured in two or more places, creating a free-floating segment that moves paradoxically (inward with inspiration, outward with expiration).

**Significance:** The underlying pulmonary contusion — not the mechanical paradox — is responsible for most of the morbidity.

**Management:**
- Supplemental O2; consider early intubation with positive pressure ventilation.
- Adequate analgesia — essential to allow deep breathing and prevent atelectasis.
- Intercostal nerve blocks or epidural analgesia in hospital setting.
- Avoid excessive IV fluids — worsens pulmonary contusion and edema.

### Rib Fractures

- Each rib fracture increases mortality approximately 19% in elderly patients.
- Two or more rib fractures in elderly patients warrant hospital admission and aggressive pain management.
- Pain management: IV opioids, NSAIDs (caution with platelet effects), regional anesthesia (serratus anterior plane block, intercostal blocks, paravertebral blocks, epidural).
- Incentive spirometry: reduces atelectasis and pneumonia.
- Splinting the chest when coughing.

## 6. Circulation and Shock (C)

### Shock Recognition

Shock is defined as inadequate tissue perfusion to meet metabolic demands — specifically, inadequate oxygen delivery relative to oxygen consumption.

**Early clinical signs of hemorrhagic shock:**
- Tachycardia (HR >100) — earliest sign; may be absent with beta blockers, athlete's resting bradycardia
- Skin pallor, cool extremities, diaphoresis
- Prolonged capillary refill (>2 seconds)
- Decreased pulse pressure (narrowing before frank hypotension)
- Anxiety, restlessness

**Late signs:**
- Hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg) — a late finding; significant volume loss has already occurred
- Altered mental status (confusion, agitation, somnolence)
- Absent radial pulse suggests SBP <80 mmHg
- Mottling

**Shock index:** HR / SBP. Normal = 0.5-0.7. SI >1.0 suggests significant hemorrhage and poor outcomes.

### IV Access

**Peripheral IV:**
- Two large-bore IVs (14-16 gauge) in the antecubital fossa or forearm.
- If peripheral access fails after 2 attempts or 90 seconds in a critically ill patient, proceed to IO or central access.

**Intraosseous (IO) access:**
- Provides rapid reliable vascular access when peripheral IV cannot be established.
- Onset of action for medications equivalent to IV route.
- Can infuse all IV medications including blood products, vasopressors, and RSI drugs.

| Device | Site | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| EZ-IO (powered drill) | Proximal tibia, proximal humerus, distal tibia | Most common EMS/ED device; 15-second placement |
| FAST1 | Manubrium (sternal) | Manual insertion; higher flow rates; caution if sternal fracture suspected |
| BIG (Bone Injection Gun) | Proximal tibia, proximal humerus | Spring-loaded; pediatric sizes available |

**Proximal tibial IO site:**
- 2 cm below tibial tuberosity, medial flat surface.
- Insert perpendicular to the bone.
- Confirm placement: no extravasation, stable position, can aspirate marrow (optional — does not always aspirate), fluids flow with gravity or pressure.

**Humeral IO site:**
- Greater tubercle of the humerus, approximately 1 cm above the surgical neck.
- Preferred when proximal tibia is not accessible (pelvic/lower extremity injury, bilateral lower extremity injuries).
- Higher flow rates than tibial IO.

### Fluid Resuscitation

#### Permissive Hypotension

For penetrating trauma and blunt trauma without TBI, target SBP 80-90 mmHg prehospital — do NOT normalize blood pressure before surgical hemorrhage control.

**Rationale:**
- Aggressive crystalloid resuscitation dilutes clotting factors (dilutional coagulopathy).
- Raising blood pressure before surgical control increases hydrostatic pressure on clots — "popping the clot."
- Permissive hypotension preserves perfusion to vital organs at a level that does not worsen hemorrhage.

**Exception — TBI:**
- With concomitant TBI, permissive hypotension is harmful — hypotension causes secondary brain injury.
- Target SBP ≥90 mmHg (ideally ≥110 mmHg per Brain Trauma Foundation guidelines) when TBI is present.

#### Crystalloid Restriction

- Isotonic crystalloid (0.9% NS or LR) causes dilutional coagulopathy, hypothermia, and edema when given in large volumes.
- Limit prehospital crystalloid to 1 liter maximum in adults with hemorrhagic shock.
- Prefer blood products when available.
- Lactated Ringer's is preferred over normal saline for large volumes — normal saline causes hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis.

#### Damage Control Resuscitation

A strategy originating in military trauma, now standard in civilian trauma centers.

**Core components:**
1. Permissive hypotension (target SBP 80-90 if no TBI)
2. Early use of blood products (pRBCs and FFP) instead of crystalloid
3. 1:1:1 ratio resuscitation (pRBCs:FFP:Platelets) — see Massive Transfusion Protocol below
4. Early use of tranexamic acid (TXA)
5. Damage control surgery — abbreviated surgery for hemorrhage control, abdominal packing, temporary closure; definitive repair deferred until physiology corrected

### Tranexamic Acid (TXA)

**Mechanism:** Antifibrinolytic — inhibits plasminogen activation and prevents fibrin clot breakdown. Does not create clots; preserves existing clots.

**Indication:** Suspected significant hemorrhage in trauma.

**Dosing:**
- 1 g IV over 10 minutes within 3 hours of injury
- Then 1 g IV over 8 hours

**CRASH-2 Trial (Lancet 2010):**
- 20,211 trauma patients, 274 hospitals, 40 countries
- All-cause mortality reduced: RR 0.91 (95% CI 0.85-0.97)
- Hemorrhagic death reduced: RR 0.85 (95% CI 0.76-0.96)
- Greatest benefit when given within 1 hour of injury (RR 0.68)
- Benefit reduced but still present between 1-3 hours
- Harm signal (increased mortality) if given after 3 hours — do NOT give TXA beyond 3 hours

**Military Application of TXA (MATTERs study):**
- US military study demonstrated mortality benefit in combat casualties with massive transfusion.
- TXA + MTP: 14% mortality vs 28% without TXA.

**Pediatric dosing:** 15-30 mg/kg IV (max 1g) — evidence emerging from CRASH-3 and pediatric subgroups.

**Contraindications:** Thromboembolic disease, disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) — use with caution.

### Pericardial Tamponade

**Mechanism:** Blood accumulates in the pericardial sac (rigid structure), compresses the right ventricle, impairs venous return and cardiac output.

**Beck's triad:**
1. Hypotension
2. Muffled (distant) heart sounds
3. Jugular venous distension (JVD)

Beck's triad is present in only 40-50% of cases — do not rule out tamponade because all three signs are absent.

**Additional signs:**
- Pulsus paradoxus: >10 mmHg drop in SBP with inspiration (increased right heart filling during inspiration further compresses left heart)
- Electrical alternans on ECG (alternating QRS morphology)
- Echocardiography (FAST exam — subxiphoid view): most sensitive prehospital/ED diagnostic tool

**Treatment:**
- Pericardiocentesis: subxiphoid approach, 16-18g spinal needle directed toward left shoulder at 45 degrees; aspirate 20-30 mL relieves tamponade temporarily.
- Definitive: surgical drainage (thoracotomy or pericardial window).
- Emergency resuscitative thoracotomy: indicated for penetrating cardiac trauma with witnessed arrest or arrest within 15 minutes of arrival.

## 7. Disability — Neurological Assessment (D)

### Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)

| Category | Score | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Eye Opening | 4 | Spontaneous |
| | 3 | To voice/command |
| | 2 | To pain |
| | 1 | No response |
| Verbal | 5 | Oriented, conversational |
| | 4 | Confused |
| | 3 | Inappropriate words |
| | 2 | Incomprehensible sounds |
| | 1 | No response |
| Motor | 6 | Obeys commands |
| | 5 | Localizes pain |
| | 4 | Withdraws from pain |
| | 3 | Abnormal flexion (decorticate) |
| | 2 | Abnormal extension (decerebrate) |
| | 1 | No response |

**Score interpretation:**
- 14-15: Mild
- 9-13: Moderate
- 3-8: Severe (GCS <8 = "Gate is closed" — consider intubation)
- Minimum score: 3 (not 0)

**Limitations:**
- Verbal component unreliable in intubated patients (reported as "1T" or "1intubated").
- Eye opening component unreliable with periorbital edema or ocular injury.
- GCS is a single point-in-time assessment — trends over time are more informative than single values.
- GCS should be assessed before any sedating or paralytic medications.

### AVPU Scale

A rapid, simpler assessment:
- **A** — Alert
- **V** — Responds to Voice
- **P** — Responds to Pain
- **U** — Unresponsive

AVPU correlates roughly with GCS: A = GCS 14-15; V = GCS 10-13; P = GCS 6-9; U = GCS 3-5.
AVPU = P approximately corresponds to GCS <8.

### Pupillary Assessment

- **PERLA:** Pupils Equal, Round, and Reactive to Light — normal finding.
- **Unilateral fixed and dilated pupil (blown pupil):** Uncal herniation — compression of CN III by the uncus of the temporal lobe due to expanding mass lesion (epidural or subdural hematoma). Ipsilateral to the mass lesion in most cases.
- **Bilateral fixed and dilated pupils:** Bilateral herniation, bilateral CN III compression, severe hypoxia/ischemia, or high-dose anticholinergic/sympathomimetic medication.
- **Pinpoint pupils:** Opioid toxicity, pontine hemorrhage.
- **Anisocoria:** Unequal pupils; may be physiological (up to 20% of population) or pathological.

### Blood Glucose

Hypoglycemia mimics or worsens TBI — always check blood glucose in altered mental status.
- BGL <60 mg/dL: administer dextrose (D50W 25g IV or D10W 250 mL IV).
- Hyperglycemia: also worsens TBI outcomes — avoid dextrose-containing fluids in TBI unless hypoglycemic.

## 8. Exposure and Environmental Control (E)

### Full Exposure Assessment

- Remove all clothing with trauma shears.
- Inspect the entire body: anterior, posterior (log roll), axillae, perineum, scalp.
- Do not overlook areas hidden by clothing, body habitus, or position.

**Log roll technique:**
- Minimum 3-4 rescuers for a spinal-precaution patient.
- One rescuer maintains in-line cervical stabilization throughout.
- Patient rolled 90 degrees; examine posterior surface; place on backboard if using; roll back.

### Hypothermia Prevention

Hypothermia is one component of the "Triad of Death" in trauma, alongside coagulopathy and acidosis. Each component potentiates the others — a self-reinforcing death spiral.

**Triad of Death:**

| Component | Mechanism in Trauma | Effect on Coagulation |
|---|---|---|
| Hypothermia | Heat loss, cold fluids, exposed body | Enzymatic dysfunction of clotting cascade; platelet dysfunction |
| Acidosis | Tissue hypoperfusion, anaerobic metabolism, lactic acid accumulation | Inhibits thrombin generation, impairs fibrinogen |
| Coagulopathy | Dilution of clotting factors, factor consumption, fibrinolysis | Directly impairs hemostasis |

**Prevention measures:**
- Remove wet clothing immediately.
- Warm blankets or reflective "space blankets."
- Warmed IV fluids (when available).
- Heated transport environment.
- Warm humidified O2 if intubated.
- HPMK (Hypothermia Prevention and Management Kit) — military; includes heat-reflective components.

**Temperature thresholds:**
- Mild hypothermia: 32-35°C
- Moderate hypothermia: 28-32°C
- Severe hypothermia: <28°C — high risk of refractory ventricular fibrillation

## 9. Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

### Classification by Severity

| Category | GCS | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Mild TBI | 13-15 | May include concussion; normal CT possible |
| Moderate TBI | 9-12 | Abnormal CT likely; significant risk of deterioration |
| Severe TBI | 3-8 | Life-threatening; intubation indicated; ICU management required |

### Primary vs. Secondary Injury

**Primary injury:** Mechanical damage occurring at the moment of impact — shearing, tearing, contusion of brain tissue. This injury is irreversible and cannot be treated.

**Secondary injury:** Subsequent biochemical and physiological processes that worsen the initial damage — this is the target of treatment.

**Causes of secondary TBI:**

| Cause | Target Value | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Hypoxia | SaO2 >94%, PaO2 >60 mmHg | Brain is exquisitely sensitive to hypoxia; each hypoxic episode doubles mortality |
| Hypotension | SBP ≥90 mmHg | Even a single hypotensive episode (SBP <90) doubles mortality in severe TBI |
| Hyperthermia | Normothermia (36-37.5°C) | Increases metabolic demand; worsens edema |
| Hypoglycemia | BGL 140-180 mg/dL | Deprive neurons of glucose |
| Hyperglycemia | Avoid BGL >180 | Worsens neuroinflammation |
| Hypocarbia (aggressive hyperventilation) | PaCO2 35-40 mmHg | Cerebral vasoconstriction reduces CBF |
| Hypercarbia | PaCO2 <45 mmHg | Vasodilation increases ICP |
| Coagulopathy | Normal coagulation parameters | Expands intracranial hematomas |
| Anemia | Hgb ≥7-10 g/dL | Reduces O2 delivery |

### Intracranial Pressure and Herniation

**Normal ICP:** 5-15 mmHg
**Elevated ICP (intracranial hypertension):** >20 mmHg
**Cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP):** MAP - ICP. Target CPP 50-70 mmHg.

**Monroe-Kellie doctrine:** The intracranial vault has fixed volume — brain, blood, and CSF. An increase in one component requires a corresponding decrease in another. Beyond compensatory capacity, ICP rises sharply.

**Herniation syndromes:**

| Type | Description | Signs |
|---|---|---|
| Uncal (transtentorial) | Temporal lobe uncus compresses CN III and brainstem | Ipsilateral fixed dilated pupil, contralateral hemiparesis, declining GCS |
| Central | Bilateral downward displacement of brainstem | Bilateral pinpoint then dilated pupils, decorticate then decerebrate posturing |
| Tonsillar | Cerebellar tonsils herniate through foramen magnum | Cushing triad, respiratory arrest |

**Cushing's triad (reflex):** Hypertension (widened pulse pressure) + Bradycardia + Irregular respirations. Late sign of severe intracranial hypertension and impending herniation.

### Prehospital TBI Management

1. Ensure airway — intubate if GCS <8 or unable to protect airway
2. Maintain SpO2 >94% — 100% O2 preferred
3. Maintain SBP ≥90 mmHg — IV fluids, vasopressors if necessary
4. Avoid fever — active cooling if needed
5. Head of bed 30 degrees if not in spinal precautions
6. Avoid hypotonic fluids (free water lowers serum osmolality, worsens cerebral edema)
7. Do NOT hyperventilate unless herniation signs present

### Hyperosmolar Therapy

Used for signs of herniation (blown pupil, decerebrate posturing, Cushing's triad) as a temporizing bridge to surgery.

| Agent | Dose | Mechanism | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mannitol 20% | 0.25-1 g/kg IV | Osmotic diuresis; reduces ICP within 20-30 minutes; duration 2-6 hours | Requires intact blood-brain barrier; monitor for hypotension and rebound ICP |
| Hypertonic saline 3% | 2-4 mL/kg IV | Osmotic; draws water from brain | More hemodynamically stable than mannitol; preferred if hypotensive |
| Hypertonic saline 23.4% | 30 mL IV over 10-20 min | Rapid, potent ICP reduction | Central line preferred; most potent rapid HTS formulation |

### Hyperventilation

**Normal ventilation target:** PaCO2 35-40 mmHg (respiratory rate 10-12/min in adults).

**Prophylactic hyperventilation:** Contraindicated — causes cerebral vasoconstriction, reduces CBF, worsens ischemia.

**Therapeutic hyperventilation for herniation:** PaCO2 30-35 mmHg — only as a short-term bridge when clinical herniation is present. Should not be maintained for >30-60 minutes.

### ICP Monitoring

**Indications (BTF Guidelines):**
- GCS 3-8 after resuscitation AND abnormal CT scan
- GCS 3-8 with normal CT if ≥2 of: age >40, unilateral/bilateral posturing, SBP <90 mmHg

**Methods:**
- Ventriculostomy (external ventricular drain, EVD): gold standard; allows both ICP monitoring and CSF drainage for ICP reduction
- Intraparenchymal bolt (Camino, Codman): easier to place; cannot drain CSF

### Specific TBI Types

**Epidural hematoma (EDH):**
- Blood between skull and dura, usually from middle meningeal artery laceration.
- Classic presentation: initial loss of consciousness → lucid interval → rapid deterioration.
- Lens-shaped (biconvex) hyperdensity on CT.
- Surgical emergency — outcomes excellent with early evacuation.

**Subdural hematoma (SDH):**
- Blood between dura and arachnoid, from bridging vein tearing.
- Crescent-shaped hyperdensity on CT.
- Acute SDH: high mortality; associated with significant parenchymal injury.
- Chronic SDH: elderly, anticoagulated patients; may present weeks after minor trauma.

**Subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH):**
- Blood in subarachnoid space; traumatic SAH from cortical vessel disruption.
- Thunderclap headache (spontaneous) vs. head trauma (traumatic).
- Risk of vasospasm after spontaneous SAH.

**Diffuse Axonal Injury (DAI):**
- Widespread disruption of axonal integrity from rotational/shear forces.
- CT may appear normal initially; MRI (especially SWI/GRE sequences) more sensitive.
- Correlates with loss of consciousness out of proportion to CT findings.
- Associated with poor functional outcomes in severe cases.
- Grading: Grade I (lobar white matter), Grade II (corpus callosum), Grade III (brainstem).

### Seizure Prophylaxis

- Phenytoin or levetiracetam (Keppra) x7 days after severe TBI.
- Reduces early post-traumatic seizures but does not prevent late epilepsy.
- Levetiracetam preferred — fewer drug interactions, no need for levels.

## 10. Spinal Cord Injury

### Mechanisms of Injury

| Mechanism | Associated Injuries |
|---|---|
| Hyperflexion | Anterior wedge fracture; posterior ligament disruption; C5-C6 most common level |
| Hyperextension | Posterior element fractures; anterior ligament tear; common in elderly (spondylosis); central cord syndrome |
| Axial compression | Burst fracture; Jefferson fracture (C1); thoracolumbar burst |
| Rotation | Fracture-dislocation; facet joint disruption |
| Distraction | Chance fracture (lap belt); spinal cord distraction |
| Penetrating | Stab, gunshot — direct cord injury |

### Spinal Motion Restriction (SMR)

Modern approach has shifted from universal spinal immobilization to selective spinal motion restriction based on clinical criteria.

**NEXUS Criteria for low-risk clearance (no imaging required if ALL five present):**
1. No posterior midline cervical tenderness
2. Normal level of alertness (GCS 15; oriented to person, place, time, event)
3. No evidence of intoxication
4. No focal neurological deficit
5. No painful distracting injury

If ALL five NEXUS criteria are met, C-spine clearance can be considered in the field without imaging.

**Canadian C-Spine Rule (CCR):**
For alert, stable patients after trauma:
- High-risk factors (image required): age ≥65, dangerous mechanism (fall >3 feet or 5 steps, axial load to head, high-speed MVC, bicycle/ATV collision), paresthesias in extremities
- Low-risk factors (safe to assess range of motion): simple rear-end MVC, sitting in ED, ambulatory post-injury, delayed neck pain onset, no midline tenderness
- Assess rotation: if patient can rotate neck 45 degrees left and right — no imaging needed

**SMR technique:**
- Cervical collar: rigid collar (Philadelphia, Aspen, Miami-J) reduces movement but does not eliminate it.
- Do not use long backboards for extended transport — pressure injuries, discomfort, may be harmful.
- Current PHTLS guidance: SMR is indicated for unconscious trauma patients, neurological deficit, midline tenderness, or high-risk mechanism with inability to assess.
- Patients with penetrating trauma do NOT routinely require spinal immobilization — multiple studies show no benefit; delays in airway management are harmful.

### Neurogenic Shock

Caused by disruption of the descending sympathetic pathways in the spinal cord — loss of vasoconstrictor and chronotropic sympathetic tone.

**Distinguishing neurogenic from hemorrhagic shock:**

| Feature | Neurogenic Shock | Hemorrhagic Shock |
|---|---|---|
| Heart rate | Bradycardia (paradoxical) | Tachycardia |
| Blood pressure | Hypotension | Hypotension |
| Skin | Warm, flushed (vasodilated) | Cool, pale, clammy |
| Level of injury | Typically T6 or above | Any |
| Mechanism | Loss of sympathetic tone | Volume depletion |
| Response to fluids | Partial response | Responds but may need vasopressors |

**Management:**
- IV fluids: cautious volume replacement (do not over-resuscitate — risk of pulmonary edema)
- Vasopressors: first-line for persistent hypotension despite adequate volume
  - Norepinephrine: alpha + beta1 effects; addresses both hypotension and bradycardia; preferred
  - Phenylephrine: pure alpha agonist; avoids reflex bradycardia; caution if already bradycardic
  - Dopamine: historically used; variable receptor effects; less predictable
- Atropine: for symptomatic bradycardia (HR <40, hemodynamically significant)
- Target MAP ≥85 mmHg for spinal cord perfusion pressure (ACS ATLS guideline)

### ASIA Classification

The American Spinal Injury Association (ASIA) Impairment Scale classifies severity of spinal cord injury:

| Grade | Description |
|---|---|
| A — Complete | No motor or sensory function preserved in sacral segments S4-S5 |
| B — Incomplete sensory | Sensory but not motor function preserved below neurological level, including S4-S5 |
| C — Incomplete motor | Motor function preserved below neurological level; >50% of key muscles grade <3 |
| D — Incomplete motor | Motor function preserved below neurological level; ≥50% of key muscles grade ≥3 |
| E — Normal | Normal motor and sensory function |

### Incomplete Spinal Cord Syndromes

| Syndrome | Description | Common Cause |
|---|---|---|
| Central cord syndrome | Greater weakness in arms than legs; sacral sparing; bladder dysfunction | Hyperextension in elderly with spondylosis |
| Anterior cord syndrome | Loss of motor function and pain/temperature bilaterally; preserved proprioception/vibration | Anterior spinal artery injury, flexion injury |
| Brown-Sequard syndrome | Ipsilateral motor loss + proprioception loss; contralateral pain/temperature loss | Penetrating injury (hemisection) |
| Posterior cord syndrome | Loss of proprioception and vibration; motor preserved | Rare; posterior column injury |

### Methylprednisolone — No Longer Recommended

The National Acute Spinal Cord Injury Studies (NASCIS II and III) suggested modest neurological improvement with high-dose methylprednisolone given within 8 hours of injury. However:
- Subsequent analysis revealed significant methodological flaws.
- Associated with increased rates of pneumonia, sepsis, wound infection, and GI bleeding.
- Multiple spine societies, ACS-COT, AANS, and CNS do NOT recommend methylprednisolone for acute SCI.
- Current PHTLS: methylprednisolone is not part of standard SCI management.

## 11. Hemorrhagic Shock — Classification and Management

### Classes of Hemorrhage

| Class | Blood Loss (adult 70 kg) | % Blood Volume | HR | Systolic BP | Pulse Pressure | RR | Urine Output (mL/hr) | Mental Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | <750 mL | <15% | <100 | Normal | Normal or increased | 14-20 | >30 | Normal, slightly anxious |
| II | 750-1500 mL | 15-30% | 100-120 | Normal | Decreased | 20-30 | 20-30 | Anxious, mildly confused |
| III | 1500-2000 mL | 30-40% | 120-140 | Decreased | Decreased | 30-40 | 5-20 | Confused, lethargic |
| IV | >2000 mL | >40% | >140 | Very low | Very low | >35 | <5 | Confused to unconscious |

**Notes:**
- Tachycardia may be absent in athletes, elderly, patients on beta-blockers.
- Children compensate remarkably well and may maintain normal BP until 25-30% volume loss.
- Elderly patients have reduced physiological reserve; Class II may present more like Class III.

### Massive Transfusion Protocol (MTP)

Activated for:
- >10 units pRBCs in 24 hours
- >4 units pRBCs in 1 hour with ongoing hemorrhage
- Shock Index >1.0 with penetrating mechanism or suspected significant hemorrhage

**1:1:1 ratio:**
- 1 unit pRBCs : 1 unit FFP : 1 unit apheresis platelets (or 1 pooled 6-pack)
- Approximates whole blood transfusion
- Reduces total crystalloid administration
- Reduces dilutional coagulopathy

**Additional components:**
- Fibrinogen (cryoprecipitate): 10 units cryoprecipitate or 3-4g fibrinogen concentrate when fibrinogen <1.5 g/L
- Calcium: each unit of blood product contains citrate (anticoagulant) which chelates ionized calcium; administer 1g calcium gluconate IV per 4 units of blood product; target iCa >1.1 mmol/L
- TXA: as above
- Warm all blood products — cold blood exacerbates hypothermia

**Monitoring during MTP:**
- TEG (thromboelastography) or ROTEM (rotational thromboelastometry): point-of-care coagulation testing to guide targeted component therapy
- PT/INR, aPTT, fibrinogen, platelet count, CBC every 30-60 minutes during active MTP

## 12. Abdominal Trauma

### Mechanisms

**Blunt:** MVC (most common), falls, direct blows, seat belt injuries, handlebar injuries.

**Penetrating:** Gunshot wounds, stab wounds. Trajectory determines which organs are at risk.

**Solid organ injury:** Liver (most commonly injured in blunt trauma), spleen (most commonly injured in blunt trauma requiring surgery), kidneys.

**Hollow viscus injury:** Stomach, small intestine, colon — rupture causes peritonitis; may not be apparent immediately.

### FAST Exam

**Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma (FAST):**
A bedside ultrasound exam designed to identify free fluid (hemoperitoneum, pericardial effusion) rapidly.

**Four standard views:**

| View | Target Area | Positive Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Right upper quadrant (RUQ) / Morrison's pouch | Between liver and right kidney | Free fluid (anechoic stripe) |
| Left upper quadrant (LUQ) / Perisplenic | Between spleen and left kidney / splenorenal space | Free fluid |
| Subxiphoid / Pericardial | Pericardium, 4-chamber cardiac view | Pericardial effusion / tamponade |
| Suprapubic / Pelvic | Pouch of Douglas (female), rectovesical pouch (male) | Free fluid |

**Extended FAST (eFAST):**
Adds bilateral thoracic views to detect pneumothorax:
- Loss of lung sliding (M-mode "stratosphere sign" vs. normal "seashore sign")
- Absent lung point

**Limitations of FAST:**
- Operator-dependent.
- Cannot identify solid organ laceration grade without CT.
- Obesity, subcutaneous emphysema, bowel gas reduce image quality.
- FAST does NOT reliably detect bowel injury or retroperitoneal hemorrhage.
- A negative FAST does not exclude intraabdominal injury.

### Solid Organ Injury Grading (AAST)

**Splenic injury:**

| Grade | Description |
|---|---|
| I | Subcapsular hematoma <10% surface area; laceration <1 cm |
| II | Subcapsular hematoma 10-50%; laceration 1-3 cm not involving trabecular vessels |
| III | Subcapsular hematoma >50% or expanding; laceration >3 cm or involving trabecular vessels |
| IV | Laceration involving segmental or hilar vessels with >25% devascularization |
| V | Shattered spleen; hilar vascular injury with devascularization |

**Non-operative management (NOM) criteria (spleen/liver):**
- Hemodynamically stable (responds to initial resuscitation)
- No peritoneal signs
- No evidence of other injuries requiring laparotomy
- CT-documented injury grade
- Adequate monitoring and surgical backup available

### Retroperitoneal Hematoma

- Zone I (central): major vascular injury (aorta, IVC, mesenteric vessels) — surgical exploration required.
- Zone II (flank/lateral): renal injury — non-operative management if stable; explore if expanding.
- Zone III (pelvic): pelvic fracture hemorrhage — typically managed non-operatively with pelvic binder and angioembolization; do NOT explore unless penetrating mechanism.

### Pelvic Fractures

High-energy pelvic fractures are associated with massive hemorrhage (up to 3-4 liters into retroperitoneal space).

**Pelvic binder application:**
- Apply at the level of the greater trochanters (NOT the iliac crests).
- Reduces pelvic volume; tamponades venous hemorrhage.
- Devices: SAM Pelvic Sling, T-POD, improvised sheet wrapped and tied.
- Do not remove until hemorrhage is controlled.

**Definitive management:** Angiography with embolization (arterial), external fixation or internal fixation, pre-peritoneal pelvic packing.

## 13. Thoracic Trauma

### Aortic Injury

Traumatic aortic injury (TAI) occurs at the isthmus — just distal to the left subclavian artery at the ligamentum arteriosum — in 90% of cases.

**Mechanism:** Sudden deceleration (high-speed MVC, fall, blast) causes shear force between the mobile arch/heart and the fixed descending aorta.

**80-85% die at scene.** Of those who survive to hospital, injuries range from intimal tear to complete transection.

**Radiographic signs:**
- Widened mediastinum (>8 cm or >0.25 of chest width)
- Loss of aortic knob
- Left apical pleural cap
- Depression of left mainstem bronchus
- Deviation of nasogastric tube to the right
- Left hemothorax

**CT aortography:** Definitive diagnosis.

**Management:** Permissive hypotension (target SBP 100-120 to reduce aortic shear force), urgent TEVAR (thoracic endovascular aortic repair) or open surgical repair.

### Tracheobronchial Injury

- Usually within 2 cm of the carina.
- Presents with massive air leak after chest tube placement, persistent pneumothorax, subcutaneous emphysema.
- Bronchoscopy diagnostic; surgical repair definitive.

### Esophageal Injury

- Rare; often from penetrating trauma.
- Presents with mediastinitis (fever, chest pain, subcutaneous emphysema) hours to days later.
- High mortality — urgent surgical repair.

### Traumatic Diaphragmatic Rupture

- Typically blunt; left side more common (right side protected by liver).
- Bowel herniation into chest may not be apparent initially.
- Presents with dyspnea, absent breath sounds, bowel sounds in chest.
- Nasogastric tube in chest on CXR is diagnostic for left-sided rupture.

## 14. Burns

### Burn Assessment

#### Total Body Surface Area (TBSA) — Rule of Nines (Adults)

| Body Region | TBSA |
|---|---|
| Head and neck | 9% |
| Each upper extremity (arm) | 9% (18% total) |
| Anterior torso | 18% |
| Posterior torso | 18% |
| Each lower extremity (leg) | 18% (36% total) |
| Perineum / genitalia | 1% |
| Total | 100% |

**Palmar method:** Patient's palm (including fingers) = approximately 1% TBSA. Useful for irregular burns.

#### Lund-Browder Chart

More accurate in children — adjusts for the proportionally larger head and smaller legs in pediatric patients. Used for precise TBSA calculation in pediatric burns.

#### Burn Depth Classification

| Depth | Appearance | Sensation | Healing | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Superficial (first degree) | Red, dry, no blisters | Painful | 3-5 days without scarring | Sunburn; not included in TBSA for fluid calculations |
| Superficial partial thickness (second degree) | Moist, red, blistering | Very painful | 7-14 days, minimal scarring | Intact pain receptors |
| Deep partial thickness (second degree) | Pale, waxy, less moist | Reduced pain | 14-21+ days; scarring; may require grafting | Damage to deep dermis |
| Full thickness (third degree) | Leathery, white/brown/black, dry | Painless | No spontaneous healing; requires grafting | All dermis destroyed |
| Fourth degree | Bone, muscle, tendon exposed | Painless | Amputation often required | Involves structures deep to skin |

#### Criteria for Burn Center Referral (ABA Guidelines)

- Partial thickness burns >10% TBSA
- Any full-thickness burn
- Burns involving face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, major joints
- Circumferential burns of extremity or chest
- Electrical burns (including lightning)
- Chemical burns
- Inhalation injury
- Burns in patients with significant comorbidities
- Burns with concomitant trauma where burn poses greatest risk
- Pediatric burns without pediatric expertise at local facility

### Burn Fluid Resuscitation

#### Parkland Formula

**Formula:** 4 mL × kg body weight × % TBSA burned = total volume in first 24 hours (lactated Ringer's only).

**Timing:**
- First half (50%): given in the first 8 hours from time of burn (not time of arrival).
- Second half (50%): given over the next 16 hours.

**Urine output targets:**
- Adults: 0.5 mL/kg/hr (approximately 30-50 mL/hr)
- Children: 1 mL/kg/hr
- Electrical burns: 1-2 mL/kg/hr (to flush myoglobin)

**Example:** 70 kg adult with 30% TBSA burn:
- Total 24h volume: 4 × 70 × 30 = 8,400 mL LR
- First 8h: 4,200 mL
- Next 16h: 4,200 mL

#### Modified Brooke Formula

**Formula:** 2 mL × kg × % TBSA burned over first 24h (LR).

Some centers prefer modified Brooke — associated with less fluid overload ("fluid creep").

#### Colloid Resuscitation

- After 24 hours, add 5% albumin (0.3-1 mL/kg/%TBSA) to maintain oncotic pressure.
- Colloid in the first 24 hours is not routinely recommended — capillary leak allows albumin to extravasate.

### Airway Management in Burns

**Signs of inhalation injury (any one indicates high risk):**
- Singed nasal hairs or eyebrows
- Carbonaceous (black, sooty) sputum
- Hoarseness or change in voice
- Stridor
- Facial and oral burns
- History of enclosed space exposure

**Principle:** Edema from inhalation injury and burn resuscitation progresses over hours — early elective intubation is far safer than emergency intubation of a swollen, distorted airway.

**Early intubation indications:**
- Stridor or significant hoarseness
- Burns to lips/oropharynx
- Large burns (>40% TBSA) with expected massive resuscitation
- Impending transport to burn center where reassessment may be delayed

#### Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

**CO binds hemoglobin with 250x the affinity of oxygen** — forms carboxyhemoglobin (COHb), which does not carry oxygen. SpO2 pulse oximetry is falsely normal — only co-oximetry (ABG/venous COHb level) accurately measures.

**Symptoms:**

| COHb Level | Symptoms |
|---|---|
| <10% | Mild headache |
| 10-20% | Moderate headache, dizziness, nausea |
| 20-40% | Severe headache, altered mental status, tachycardia |
| 40-60% | Coma, seizures, cardiovascular collapse |
| >60% | Death |

**Treatment:**
- 100% O2 via non-rebreather mask: reduces COHb half-life from ~5 hours (room air) to ~60-90 minutes.
- Hyperbaric oxygen (HBO): reduces half-life to ~20 minutes. Indicated for COHb >25%, neurological symptoms (confusion, syncope, seizure), cardiac involvement, pregnancy (any level).

#### Cyanide Poisoning

Released from burning synthetic materials (plastics, polyurethane foam, carpets, upholstery). Frequently co-exists with CO poisoning in structure fires.

**Signs:** Hemodynamic instability, severe lactic acidosis, altered mental status disproportionate to COHb level.

**Treatment:**
- Hydroxocobalamin (Cyanokit): 5g IV over 15 minutes — binds cyanide to form cyanocobalamin (vitamin B12), excreted renally. First-line prehospital/ED treatment.
- Alternative: sodium thiosulfate ± sodium nitrite (older regimen; nitrites worsen CO toxicity — avoid in combined CO/CN poisoning).

### Circumferential Burns and Escharotomy

- Full-thickness circumferential burns of extremities create a rigid eschar that compresses underlying soft tissue, causing compartment syndrome.
- Circumferential chest burns restrict chest wall expansion — respiratory failure.
- Escharotomy: medial and lateral incisions through the eschar (painless in full-thickness burns) to release compression. Performed in burn center.

### Electrical Burns

- Entrance and exit wounds often underestimate extent of deep tissue injury — electricity travels through deep tissues along the path of least resistance (blood vessels, nerves, muscles).
- Cardiac dysrhythmias: monitor ECG for at least 24 hours for any ECG abnormality, loss of consciousness, or ventricular arrhythmia at scene.
- Rhabdomyolysis: aggressive IV hydration, alkalinize urine.
- High-voltage (>1000V): thoracic and abdominal injury possible from arc flash and blast effect.

## 15. Pediatric Trauma

### Anatomical and Physiological Differences

| Feature | Clinical Implication |
|---|---|
| Larger head-to-body ratio | Greater risk of head injury; inertial TBI without skull fracture; Waddell triad |
| Flexible, compliant chest wall | Rib fractures require significant force — their absence does not exclude significant intrathoracic injury; pulmonary contusion may exist without rib fractures |
| Liver and spleen proportionally larger and less protected by ribs | More vulnerable to abdominal injury; solid organ injuries are the most common abdominal injury |
| Malleable, incomplete ossified bones | Greenstick fractures; bones absorb less energy, transmitting more to underlying structures |
| Thin abdominal wall with less protective fat | Visceral organs more exposed |
| Smaller circulating volume | 70-80 mL/kg total blood volume; small absolute blood loss can be physiologically significant |
| Strong compensatory reserve | Children maintain normal BP despite >25% volume loss — hypotension is a late, preterminal sign in children |
| Higher baseline HR and RR | Normal adult HR ranges do not apply to children |

### Vital Signs by Age

| Age | Heart Rate | Systolic BP | Respiratory Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Newborn | 120-160 | 60-90 | 40-60 |
| Infant (1-12 mo) | 100-160 | 70-100 | 30-40 |
| Toddler (1-3 yr) | 90-150 | 80-110 | 20-30 |
| School age (6-12 yr) | 70-120 | 80-120 | 15-25 |
| Adolescent (>12 yr) | 60-100 | 90-130 | 12-20 |

**Minimum systolic BP by age rule:** 70 + (2 × age in years) for children 1-10 years.

### Broselow Tape

Color-coded length-based device for estimating weight and providing drug doses, equipment sizes, and vital sign parameters based on the child's length. Reduces dosing errors significantly.

### Vascular Access

- Peripheral IV: smaller veins; more difficult under stress; 2 attempts or 90 seconds — proceed to IO.
- IO access: tibial tuberosity (first choice); distal femur (alternative); humeral head (toddlers and infants).
- Umbilical vein catheterization: neonates — accessible for 7-10 days of life.

### Fluid Resuscitation

- Initial bolus: 20 mL/kg LR or NS for hemorrhagic shock.
- Reassess after each bolus.
- After 3 boluses (60 mL/kg) without response — consider blood transfusion.
- Permissive hypotension has not been validated in children — maintain age-appropriate SBP.

### Waddell Triad

Classic pediatric pedestrian-struck injury pattern:
1. **Femur fracture** — vehicle bumper strikes the child's femur.
2. **Intra-abdominal injury** — bumper or body strikes the abdomen.
3. **Contralateral head injury** — child falls and strikes head on the opposite side.

All three injuries should be actively sought when any one is identified in a child struck by a vehicle.

### Child Abuse (Non-Accidental Trauma)

**Indicators of abuse:**
- Mechanism inconsistent with injury pattern or developmental stage.
- Bruising in unusual locations (ears, neck, buttocks, genitalia) — non-ambulatory children rarely bruise.
- Multiple injuries at different stages of healing.
- Delayed presentation.
- Patterned bruising (belt, cord, hand, cigarette burns).
- Spiral fractures in pre-ambulatory children.
- Intracranial hemorrhage without witnessed trauma (shaken baby syndrome / abusive head trauma).

**Reporting:** Mandatory reporting in all US states for suspected child abuse.

## 16. Tactical Combat Casualty Care (TCCC)

### Overview

TCCC was developed by COL Ronald Bellamy MD and Frank Butler MD in the early 1990s based on analysis of preventable combat deaths. The finding: 90% of preventable deaths resulted from hemorrhage; 60% of those could have been prevented with basic interventions.

**Three phases of care:**

### Phase 1: Care Under Fire (CUF)

Environment: Active threat; provider and casualty are under direct fire.

**Priorities:**
- Return fire / take cover.
- Direct casualty to move to cover if able.
- Apply tourniquet to life-threatening extremity hemorrhage — self-aid or buddy aid.
- Minimize scene time — no assessment beyond hemorrhage control.
- Keep casualty from doing anything that draws more fire.
- Airway management is deferred unless it can be accomplished safely.

### Phase 2: Tactical Field Care (TFC)

Environment: No longer under direct threat; relatively safe position.

**MARCH assessment:**
- M: Reassess tourniquet, address additional hemorrhage, wound packing.
- A: Airway management — NPA, positioning; intubation if indicated and resources allow.
- R: Assess for tension PTX; needle decompression (4th-5th ICS AAL preferred by TCCC); occlusive dressing for open chest wound.
- C: IV/IO access; TXA if within 3 hours and massive hemorrhage suspected; fluid resuscitation (Hextend or blood products if available); minimal crystalloid.
- H: Hypothermia prevention — HPMK, Blizzard rescue blanket, dry clothing.

**Additional TFC interventions:**
- Eye injuries: hard shield, no pressure.
- Burns: burn dressings; stop the burning process.
- Splint fractures.
- Reassess mental status; document findings on TCCC card.

### Phase 3: Tactical Evacuation Care (TACEVAC)

Environment: Casualty evacuation (ground CASEVAC or air MEDEVAC).

**Additional capabilities:**
- Advanced airway management (if available).
- Mechanical ventilation.
- Blood products (fresh whole blood, type O negative pRBCs, lyophilized plasma).
- Monitoring: SpO2, ETCO2, BP.
- Reassess all interventions; prevent tourniquet time from exceeding 2 hours if possible without risking hemorrhage return.
- Pain management: ketamine 0.1-0.5 mg/kg IV for moderate to severe pain (preserves airway reflexes); oral transmucosal fentanyl citrate (OTFC) 800mcg for moderate pain.

### TCCC Casualty Card (DD Form 1380)

Documents:
- Time of tourniquet application
- Medications administered with time and route
- Interventions performed
- Vital signs
- Mechanism of injury
- Allergies

### Hemorrhage Control Devices in TCCC

| Device | Indication | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| CAT or SOFTT-W | Extremity hemorrhage | TCCC primary tourniquet devices |
| Combat Gauze | Wound packing | Kaolin-impregnated; TCCC first-line hemostatic gauze |
| XStat | Deep narrow wound channels | 30 compressed cellulose sponges; each expands 3-4x; radiopaque |
| SAM-JT or JETT | Junctional (groin) hemorrhage | Pneumatic compression of femoral vessels |
| AAJT | Bilateral lower extremity or pelvic hemorrhage | Circumferential abdominal aortic compression |
| Chest seals (HyFin Vent, Bolin) | Open pneumothorax | Vented preferred; bilateral application in unconscious casualties |

## 17. Crush Syndrome and Compartment Syndrome

### Crush Syndrome

**Definition:** Systemic manifestation of rhabdomyolysis following prolonged muscle compression.

**Pathophysiology:**
1. Prolonged compression → muscle cell ischemia.
2. On release: reperfusion injury — oxygen free radicals, inflammatory mediators released.
3. Myocyte contents (myoglobin, potassium, phosphate, uric acid, creatine kinase) released into circulation.
4. Myoglobin precipitates in renal tubules → acute tubular necrosis → acute kidney injury.
5. Potassium release → hyperkalemia → cardiac arrest.
6. Fluid shifts into injured muscle → hypovolemia, compartment syndrome.

**Management:**

| Intervention | Details |
|---|---|
| IV fluids before extrication | 1-1.5 L/hr normal saline (NOT lactated Ringer's — contains K+); flush myoglobin |
| Target urine output | 200-300 mL/hr to clear myoglobin |
| Urinary alkalinization (controversial) | Sodium bicarbonate 1-1.5 mEq/kg in 1L D5W at 200-250 mL/hr: alkalinizes urine, reduces myoglobin tubular precipitation; controversial — may worsen hypocalcemia |
| Hyperkalemia treatment | Calcium gluconate 1-2g IV (membrane stabilization); sodium bicarbonate (shifts K+ intracellularly); insulin/dextrose; kayexalate; dialysis if refractory |
| Monitoring | ECG for peaked T-waves, widened QRS, sine wave pattern (severe hyperkalemia); K+ levels |
| Cardiac arrest on extrication | ACLS; aggressive hyperkalemia management; consider ECMO for refractory cardiac arrest |

**Avoid lactated Ringer's:** LR contains 4 mEq/L of potassium — in the setting of crush syndrome with pre-existing hyperkalemia, this may be harmful.

### Compartment Syndrome

**Definition:** Pressure within a closed fascial compartment rises to levels that impair perfusion of the tissues within.

**Pathophysiology:** Edema or bleeding within a closed compartment → increased pressure → capillary bed collapse → ischemia → myonecrosis → further edema (vicious cycle).

**Causes:** Fractures (tibial fractures most common), crush injuries, burns, reperfusion injury, tight casts/bandages, prolonged tourniquet use.

**Classic signs (5 Ps):**
1. **Pain** — disproportionate to injury; especially with passive stretch of muscles in the compartment (most sensitive sign)
2. **Pressure** — firm, tense compartment on palpation
3. **Paresthesias** — numbness and tingling in distribution of nerves traversing compartment
4. **Paresis** — weakness of muscles in compartment
5. **Pulselessness** — absent distal pulses (late, ominous sign)

**Compartment pressure measurement:**
- Normal compartment pressure: 0-8 mmHg.
- Fasciotomy indicated when:
  - Absolute pressure >30 mmHg, OR
  - Delta pressure (diastolic BP - compartment pressure) <30 mmHg (preferred threshold).

**Fasciotomy:**
- Surgical release of the fascial envelope of affected compartments.
- Leg: 4-compartment fasciotomy via two incisions (anteromedial, posterolateral).
- Forearm: volar and dorsal fasciotomy.
- Must be performed before irreversible myonecrosis (typically within 6-8 hours of onset).

## 18. Drowning and Environmental Emergencies

### Drowning

**Revised terminology (World Congress on Drowning 2002):**
- "Drowning": process of respiratory impairment from submersion/immersion in liquid. May survive (non-fatal drowning) or die (fatal drowning). "Near drowning," "wet/dry drowning," "secondary drowning" are no longer recommended terms.

**Pathophysiology:**
- Hypoxemia is the primary insult — regardless of salt vs. fresh water.
- Aspiration of small amounts of water impairs surfactant function and causes non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema.
- Laryngospasm may initially prevent water entry — "dry drowning" (occurs in 10-15%).
- Hypothermia in cold-water submersion — may be protective.

**Cold water submersion:**
- Diving reflex (mammalian): cold water stimulates trigeminal nerve receptors → bradycardia + peripheral vasoconstriction → blood shunted to core organs (heart, brain). May prolong survival.
- Hypothermia (core temp <30°C) reduces cerebral metabolic rate — explains prolonged survival reports in cold-water submersion, particularly in children.

**"Not dead until warm and dead" principle:**
- Full resuscitation efforts should continue until core body temperature is ≥35°C.
- ETCO2 monitoring: persistent ETCO2 >10 mmHg suggests viable cardiac output.
- ECMO: extracorporeal life support (ECMO/ECPR) should be considered for hypothermic cardiac arrest unresponsive to standard ACLS.

**Prehospital management:**
- Remove from water — minimize unnecessary movement if spinal injury suspected (diving accident, unknown mechanism).
- Begin CPR immediately if apneic; airway is the priority (most drowning patients die from hypoxemia, not arrhythmia).
- AED when available; hypothermic hearts are often resistant to defibrillation until warmed.
- 100% O2; intubate for respiratory failure.

### Lightning Strike

- Direct strike, step potential (current travels along ground), side flash, contact injury, blunt barotrauma.
- Cardiac and respiratory arrest: resuscitation protocols apply; unlike other cardiac arrests, survival is possible even after prolonged CPR.
- Reverse triage in MCI lightning: attend to those who are unresponsive first (those who are awake will likely survive).
- Lichtenberg figures (ferning pattern on skin): pathognomonic of lightning strike.

## 19. Hanging and Strangulation

### Judicial Hanging

High-velocity judicial hanging (long-drop method) is designed to fracture the C2-C3 junction and cause immediate spinal cord transection.

**Hangman's fracture (traumatic spondylolisthesis of C2):**
- Bilateral fractures through the pedicles of C2 with variable anterior displacement of C2 on C3.
- Also occurs in hyperextension/distraction mechanisms (diving, MVC).
- Paradoxically, the fracture causes cord decompression in many cases — neurological deficit is variable.
- Management: rigid cervical immobilization; neurosurgical consultation; often managed with halo or surgical fixation.

### Non-Judicial Hanging and Strangulation

Ligature strangulation, manual strangulation, and partial suspension (incomplete hanging).

**Mechanisms of injury:**
- Venous obstruction: jugular vein compression → cerebral venous hypertension → cerebral edema, petechial hemorrhages.
- Arterial compression: carotid artery compression → cerebral ischemia.
- Carotid sinus stimulation: reflex bradycardia, cardiac arrest.
- Cervical spine injury: less common than judicial hanging; primarily concern with falls during partial suspension.

**"Delayed stroke" after strangulation:**
- Blunt carotid or vertebral artery injury from ligature or manual pressure.
- Initial presentation may be neurologically normal.
- Intimal dissection → thrombosis → embolic or flow-limiting ischemic stroke hours to days later.
- CTA or MRA of cervical vessels should be performed in all strangulation victims presenting to ED.

**Petechial hemorrhages:**
- Scleral, conjunctival, facial — from increased venous pressure.
- Not specific to strangulation; present in any significant Valsalva.

**Prehospital management:**
- Airway — edema from venous obstruction may cause progressive stridor; early intubation.
- C-spine motion restriction if mechanism suggests potential injury (fall, drop).
- Treat any associated injuries.

## 20. Transport Decisions and Trauma Systems

### Load and Go vs. Stay and Play

**Load and go (scoop and run):**
- Philosophy: rapid transport with minimal scene interventions for time-critical injuries.
- Appropriate for: penetrating truncal trauma, TBI with deterioration, uncontrolled internal hemorrhage, tension PTX requiring decompression only.
- Perform only immediately life-saving interventions on scene.

**Stay and play:**
- Stabilize on scene before transport.
- Appropriate for: medical cardiac arrest, diabetic emergency, isolated extremity injury, burns requiring aggressive airway intervention.
- Not appropriate for surgical emergencies.

**PHTLS principle:** Trauma is a surgical disease; the operating room is definitive care. Scene time is the delay to the OR.

### Trauma Center Criteria

**CDC/ACS Field Triage Decision Scheme (2021):**

Step 1 — Physiological:
- GCS <14 (or <15 for TBI concern)
- SBP <90 mmHg
- RR <10 or >29 (or need for ventilatory support)

Step 2 — Anatomic:
- Penetrating injury to head, neck, torso, extremity proximal to elbow/knee
- Open or depressed skull fracture
- Chest wall instability or deformity (flail chest)
- Two or more long bone fractures
- Crushed, degloved, mangled, or pulseless extremity
- Amputation proximal to wrist or ankle
- Pelvic fracture
- Open fracture of tibia/fibula with neurological or vascular compromise
- Spinal cord injury
- TBI with loss of consciousness >5 minutes
- Paralysis

Step 3 — Mechanism:
- Falls >20 feet (adults) or >10 feet (children)
- High-speed MVC (>40 mph, significant intrusion, ejection, death in same vehicle)
- Auto vs. pedestrian/cyclist at >20 mph
- Motorcycle crash >20 mph
- All penetrating trauma to head/neck/torso

Step 4 — Special considerations:
- Age >55 or <5 years
- SBP <110 mmHg in patients >65 years
- Anticoagulation / bleeding disorders
- Burns (see criteria above)
- Dialysis-dependent patients with signs of significant injury
- Pregnancy >20 weeks with significant mechanism
- EMS provider judgment

### Trauma Center Levels

| Level | Capabilities |
|---|---|
| Level I | Comprehensive trauma care 24/7; residency training; research; treats all injuries |
| Level II | Full trauma capabilities; may not have all subspecialties 24/7; no research requirement |
| Level III | Prompt assessment, resuscitation, stabilization; transfers complex cases |
| Level IV | Basic trauma capabilities; rural settings; stabilizes and transfers |

### Air Medical Transport

**Advantages:**
- Shorter transport time over long distances
- Advanced care capability in flight
- Access to geographically remote patients

**Indications for air transport:**
- Ground transport >30-45 minutes to trauma center
- Entrapment requiring prolonged extrication with critical injuries
- Multiple casualty incidents exceeding ground resources
- Inaccessible terrain
- Weather and landing zone permitting

**Physiological effects of flight:**
- Altitude: cabin altitude 2000-8000 feet in pressurized aircraft — expanded gas volumes; tension PTX will worsen; air splints will expand.
- Vibration: may dislodge clots; difficult to auscultate.
- Noise: preclude standard auscultation; waveform capnography mandatory.
- Temperature: generally controlled in aircraft but may be an issue in open aircraft (helicopter).

**Contraindications to air transport:**
- Decompression illness (unless going to HBO chamber — then fly at or below sea level pressure)
- Relative: unstabilized tension PTX

### Secondary Survey

After the primary survey and life-threatening interventions are addressed, the secondary survey is a head-to-toe structured examination to identify all injuries.

**Head:** Scalp lacerations, skull fractures (palpation), Battle's sign, raccoon eyes, hemotympanum, CSF otorrhea/rhinorrhea, facial fractures.

**Neck:** Tracheal deviation, subcutaneous emphysema, JVD, carotid tenderness/bruit, zones of penetrating injury, laryngeal crepitus.

**Chest:** Paradoxical movement (flail), breath sounds, heart sounds, subcutaneous emphysema.

**Abdomen:** Tenderness, guarding, rigidity, distension, seat belt sign (bruising along lap belt — increases risk of hollow viscus injury and lumbar Chance fracture).

**Pelvis:** Palpate pubic symphysis and iliac wings once (repeat manipulation increases hemorrhage from unstable fracture).

**Extremities:** Deformity, angulation, distal pulses, neurovascular status, open wounds.

**Neurological:** GCS, sensation, motor strength in all extremities, rectal tone, bulbocavernosus reflex (spinal cord assessment).
