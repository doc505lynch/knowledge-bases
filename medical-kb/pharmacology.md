# 1. Pharmacokinetics

## Absorption

- **Bioavailability (F):** fraction of administered dose reaching systemic circulation unchanged
- **Routes and typical bioavailability:**
  - IV: 100% (by definition)
  - IM: 75–100%
  - Subcutaneous: 75–100%
  - Oral: highly variable (5–100%)
  - Sublingual: bypasses first-pass; high F (e.g., nitroglycerin ~100%)
  - Rectal: partial first-pass avoidance
  - Transdermal: slow, sustained; bypasses first-pass
  - Inhaled: rapid onset, variable systemic absorption

- **Factors reducing oral absorption:**
  - Food (some drugs), gastric pH, gut motility, P-glycoprotein efflux
  - Chelation: fluoroquinolones + divalent cations (Ca2+, Mg2+, Al3+, Fe2+)
  - Bile acid binding resins bind co-administered drugs

## First-Pass Effect (Hepatic First-Pass Metabolism)

- Drug absorbed from GI tract enters portal circulation before reaching systemic circulation
- Extensive first-pass drugs have low oral bioavailability
- Examples of high first-pass drugs:
  - Morphine (F ~25%)
  - Lidocaine (F ~35%, given IV only for arrhythmia)
  - Nitroglycerin (F <1% oral — use SL or transdermal)
  - Propranolol (F ~25%, variable)
  - Verapamil (F ~20%)
  - Testosterone (F ~10%)
  - Naloxone (F ~2% — given parenterally)

## Volume of Distribution (Vd)

- **Definition:** Vd = Dose / Cp (initial plasma concentration)
- Represents apparent volume drug would occupy if uniformly distributed at plasma concentration
- Does NOT correspond to real anatomical volume

| Vd Range | Interpretation | Example Drugs |
|----------|---------------|---------------|
| Low (<1 L/kg) | Confined to plasma/ECF; large, highly protein-bound | Warfarin (0.1 L/kg), heparin |
| Moderate (0.3–0.7 L/kg) | Some tissue distribution | Many antibiotics |
| High (>1 L/kg) | Extensive tissue binding; lipophilic | Chloroquine (~300 L/kg), amiodarone (~60 L/kg), digoxin (~7 L/kg) |

- **Clinical relevance:**
  - High Vd → dialysis ineffective for overdose removal (e.g., tricyclics, digoxin)
  - Low Vd → amenable to dialysis (e.g., lithium, salicylates, methanol)

## Protein Binding

- Most drugs bind albumin (acidic drugs) or alpha-1 acid glycoprotein (basic drugs)
- Only **free (unbound)** drug is pharmacologically active and available for metabolism/excretion
- **Conditions reducing albumin** (increase free drug):
  - Cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, malnutrition, pregnancy
- **Highly protein-bound drugs (>90%):**
  - Warfarin (99%), phenytoin (90%), valproate (90%), NSAIDs (>95%), ceftriaxone (85–95%)
- **Displacement interactions:**
  - Sulfonamides displace bilirubin → kernicterus in neonates
  - Aspirin displaces warfarin → increased anticoagulation

## CYP450 Enzyme System

### Overview
- Located primarily in hepatocytes (ER membrane) and GI enterocytes
- Phase I metabolism: oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis
- Key isoforms: CYP3A4 (most abundant, ~50% of drug metabolism), CYP2D6, CYP2C9, CYP2C19, CYP1A2

### CYP3A4

| Role | Drugs |
|------|-------|
| Substrates | Statins (simvastatin, atorvastatin), benzodiazepines (midazolam, alprazolam, triazolam), CCBs (amlodipine, diltiazem, verapamil), tacrolimus, cyclosporine, amiodarone, carbamazepine, testosterone, estrogens, fentanyl, methadone, erythromycin, clarithromycin, HIV PIs |
| Inducers (increase metabolism → lower drug levels) | Rifampin (most potent), carbamazepine, phenytoin, phenobarbital, St. John's Wort, efavirenz, nevirapine, modafinil |
| Inhibitors (decrease metabolism → raise drug levels) | Azole antifungals (ketoconazole > itraconazole > fluconazole), macrolides (erythromycin, clarithromycin — NOT azithromycin), HIV PIs (ritonavir, most potent), grapefruit juice, amiodarone, verapamil, diltiazem, cimetidine |

### CYP2D6

| Role | Drugs |
|------|-------|
| Substrates | Codeine (prodrug → morphine), tramadol, tamoxifen, TCAs (amitriptyline, nortriptyline), haloperidol, risperidone, metoprolol, carvedilol, propafenone, dextromethorphan |
| Inducers | Few clinically significant; rifampin (minor) |
| Inhibitors | Fluoxetine, paroxetine (potent), bupropion, duloxetine, amiodarone, quinidine (most potent), terbinafine |

- **Genetic polymorphism:** Poor metabolizers (7–10% Caucasians) — codeine overdose risk; ultra-rapid metabolizers — codeine ineffective for pain
- Poor metabolizers + paroxetine = codeine toxicity even at standard doses

### CYP2C9

| Role | Drugs |
|------|-------|
| Substrates | Warfarin (S-warfarin, active isomer), phenytoin, NSAIDs (ibuprofen, celecoxib, naproxen), losartan, sulfonylureas (glipizide, glyburide), THC |
| Inducers | Rifampin, carbamazepine, phenobarbital |
| Inhibitors | Fluconazole (potent), amiodarone, metronidazole, miconazole, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole |

- **Warfarin interaction:** Fluconazole + warfarin = massive INR elevation; metronidazole + warfarin = INR increase

### CYP2C19

| Role | Drugs |
|------|-------|
| Substrates | Proton pump inhibitors (omeprazole, pantoprazole, lansoprazole), clopidogrel (prodrug activation), diazepam, phenytoin, voriconazole, some SSRIs (citalopram, escitalopram) |
| Inducers | Rifampin, carbamazepine |
| Inhibitors | Omeprazole, esomeprazole (self-inhibiting), fluoxetine, fluvoxamine, cimetidine |

- **Clinical:** Poor CYP2C19 metabolizers (~2–15% depending on ancestry) → clopidogrel reduced activation → higher MACE risk
- PPIs reduce clopidogrel efficacy (controversial; avoid omeprazole + clopidogrel when possible; pantoprazole preferred)

### CYP1A2

| Role | Drugs |
|------|-------|
| Substrates | Theophylline, caffeine, clozapine, olanzapine, haloperidol, warfarin (R-warfarin, less active), tacrine, melatonin, duloxetine |
| Inducers | Cigarette smoke (PAHs), rifampin, carbamazepine, phenobarbital, cruciferous vegetables, omeprazole (minor) |
| Inhibitors | Fluvoxamine (most potent), ciprofloxacin, enoxacin, amiodarone, cimetidine, oral contraceptives |

- **Clinical:** Smokers need higher clozapine/olanzapine doses; smoking cessation → increased drug levels → toxicity

## Phase I vs Phase II Metabolism

| Phase | Reactions | Result | Enzymes |
|-------|-----------|--------|---------|
| Phase I | Oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis | Adds/exposes functional group; may activate prodrug or create toxic metabolite | CYP450, MAO, alcohol dehydrogenase |
| Phase II | Conjugation (glucuronidation, sulfation, acetylation, methylation, glutathione conjugation) | Usually inactivates drug; increases water solubility for renal/biliary excretion | UGTs, SULTs, NATs, GSTs |

- **Acetylation (NAT2):** Isoniazid, hydralazine, procainamide, sulfonamides
  - Fast acetylators: lower drug levels, less toxicity for most
  - Slow acetylators: higher risk drug-induced lupus (hydralazine, procainamide), peripheral neuropathy (INH — give pyridoxine)
- **Glucuronidation (UGT):** Morphine → morphine-6-glucuronide (active, accumulates in renal failure), bilirubin, lorazepam, oxazepam, temazepam
  - LOT drugs (Lorazepam, Oxazepam, Temazepam) safe in liver disease — only undergo glucuronidation

## First-Order vs Zero-Order Kinetics

| Feature | First-Order | Zero-Order |
|---------|------------|------------|
| Rate | Proportional to drug concentration | Constant regardless of concentration |
| Half-life | Constant | Not constant — increases as dose increases |
| Drug examples | Most drugs | Phenytoin (at therapeutic doses), ethanol, aspirin (high dose), warfarin (partially) |
| Elimination curve | Exponential decay | Linear decay |
| Clinical implication | Predictable dosing | Small dose increase → disproportionate rise in level |

- **Phenytoin:** Michaelis-Menten (saturable) kinetics — zero-order at therapeutic levels
  - At levels of 10–20 mg/L, enzymes near saturation → small dose increase → large level increase → toxicity
  - Dose adjustments require very small increments (25–30 mg changes)

## Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM) Targets

| Drug | Target Range | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------|
| Vancomycin | AUC/MIC 400–600 mg·h/L | AUC-guided dosing preferred over trough-only; target MIC ≤1 mcg/mL for MRSA |
| Aminoglycosides (gentamicin/tobramycin) — traditional | Peak 5–10 mcg/mL; trough <2 mcg/mL | Trough >2 = nephrotoxicity risk |
| Aminoglycosides — extended interval (once daily) | Random level at 6–14h post-dose; Hartford nomogram | Not for endocarditis, pregnancy, burn patients |
| Digoxin | 0.5–0.9 ng/mL (HF); 0.8–2 ng/mL (afib rate control) | Sample at least 6h post-dose; toxicity >2 ng/mL |
| Lithium | 0.6–1.2 mEq/L (maintenance); 0.8–1.0 acute mania | Trough (12h post-dose); toxicity >1.5 mEq/L |
| Phenytoin (total) | 10–20 mcg/mL | Correct for albumin; free level preferred in hypoalbuminemia |
| Phenytoin (free) | 1–2 mcg/mL | Target when albumin low or uremia present |
| Valproate | 50–100 mcg/mL (epilepsy); up to 125 for mania | Trough level |
| Carbamazepine | 4–12 mcg/mL | Trough level; auto-induces own metabolism |
| Tacrolimus | 5–15 ng/mL (trough, whole blood) | Target varies by organ/time post-transplant |
| Cyclosporine | 100–400 ng/mL (trough) | Varies by organ and protocol |
| Theophylline | 5–15 mcg/mL | >20 = toxicity (seizures, arrhythmias) |
| Amikacin | Peak 20–35 mcg/mL; trough <5 mcg/mL | Traditional dosing |
| Sirolimus | 5–15 ng/mL | Whole blood trough |

### Phenytoin Correction for Hypoalbuminemia
- Corrected phenytoin = measured total phenytoin / [(0.2 × albumin) + 0.1]
- In renal failure (CrCl <10): divide by (0.1 × albumin) + 0.1

# 2. Pharmacodynamics

## Receptor Types

| Receptor Type | Mechanism | Onset | Examples |
|--------------|-----------|-------|---------|
| Ligand-gated ion channels (ionotropic) | Ion channel opens directly | Milliseconds | Nicotinic ACh (NMJ, ganglia), GABA-A, NMDA, AMPA |
| G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) | Second messenger cascade | Seconds–minutes | Beta-adrenergic (cAMP), muscarinic M1/M3 (IP3/DAG), opioid, alpha-2 (inhibit cAMP), dopamine |
| Receptor tyrosine kinases | Phosphorylation cascade | Minutes–hours | Insulin receptor, growth factors (EGF, PDGF) |
| Nuclear/intracellular receptors | Gene transcription | Hours–days | Glucocorticoids, thyroid hormone, sex steroids, vitamin D, retinoic acid |
| Enzyme-linked receptors (non-RTK) | JAK-STAT pathway | Minutes–hours | Cytokine receptors, erythropoietin, GH |

### Key GPCR Subtypes

| Receptor | G protein | Effect |
|---------|----------|--------|
| Beta-1, Beta-2, D1, H2, V2 | Gs → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP | Increase cAMP |
| M2, Alpha-2, D2, opioid | Gi → inhibit adenylyl cyclase | Decrease cAMP |
| M1, M3, Alpha-1, H1, V1 | Gq → PLC → IP3 + DAG | Increase Ca2+, PKC |

## Agonist, Antagonist, Partial Agonist

| Term | Definition | Clinical Example |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| Full agonist | Binds and produces maximal response (efficacy = 1) | Morphine at mu-opioid receptor |
| Partial agonist | Binds but produces submaximal response; acts as antagonist in presence of full agonist | Buprenorphine (mu-opioid), buspirone (5-HT1A), pindolol (beta) |
| Antagonist (competitive) | Binds reversibly; shifts dose-response right; overcome by increasing agonist dose | Naloxone, beta-blockers, atropine |
| Antagonist (non-competitive) | Binds irreversibly or allosterically; reduces maximal response; cannot be overcome | Phenoxybenzamine (alpha), aspirin (COX), organophosphates (AChE) |
| Inverse agonist | Binds receptor and reduces baseline activity below constitutive activity | Some antihistamines at H1 |

## Therapeutic Index

- **Therapeutic Index (TI) = TD50 / ED50** (or LD50 / ED50 in animals)
- High TI = safer drug (large window between therapeutic and toxic doses)
- Low TI drugs require TDM:
  - Digoxin, lithium, warfarin, phenytoin, valproate, carbamazepine, aminoglycosides, vancomycin, tacrolimus, cyclosporine, theophylline, methotrexate

## Dose-Response Concepts

- **Potency:** dose required for 50% effect (EC50); lower EC50 = more potent
- **Efficacy:** maximal effect achievable; independent of potency
- **Tolerance:** diminishing response with repeated dosing (e.g., opioids, nitrates)
- **Tachyphylaxis:** rapid tolerance (e.g., ephedrine, indirect sympathomimetics)
- **Desensitization:** receptor uncoupling from G protein with agonist overexposure
- **Down-regulation:** decreased receptor number with chronic agonist exposure (e.g., beta-receptors with chronic beta-agonist use)
- **Up-regulation:** increased receptor number with chronic antagonist exposure (e.g., beta-receptors with chronic beta-blocker → rebound tachycardia on abrupt discontinuation)

# 3. Cardiovascular Pharmacology

## ACE Inhibitors (ACEi)

**Mechanism:** Block conversion of angiotensin I → angiotensin II; reduce aldosterone; increase bradykinin (causes cough and angioedema)

**Drugs:** Lisinopril, enalapril, ramipril, captopril, benazepril, fosinopril, quinapril

**Doses (lisinopril example):** 2.5–5 mg/day starting; titrate to 10–40 mg/day

**Indications:** HTN, HFrEF (reduces mortality), post-MI, diabetic nephropathy, CKD proteinuria

**Contraindications:**
- Pregnancy (all trimesters) — fetotoxic, oligohydramnios, renal dysgenesis
- Bilateral renal artery stenosis (or unilateral in solitary kidney)
- Prior ACEi-induced angioedema (absolute)
- Concurrent use with aliskiren in DM or CKD
- Do NOT combine with ARB + ACEi + aldosterone antagonist ("triple RAAS block" — high hyperkalemia/AKI risk)

**Side Effects:**
- Dry cough (10–15%; bradykinin) — switch to ARB
- Angioedema (0.1–0.7%; more common in Black patients) — potentially fatal; never rechallenge
- Hyperkalemia
- First-dose hypotension (especially if volume-depleted)
- AKI (if bilateral RAS or volume-depleted) — creatinine rise up to 30% acceptable

## Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs)

**Mechanism:** Block AT1 receptor; do NOT increase bradykinin → no cough/less angioedema

**Drugs:** Losartan, valsartan, candesartan, irbesartan, olmesartan, telmisartan, azilsartan

**Indications:** Same as ACEi; preferred if ACEi cough; HFrEF (valsartan, candesartan mortality benefit)

**Contraindications:** Same as ACEi (pregnancy, bilateral RAS)

**Note:** Losartan + valsartan/sacubitril (Entresto) used in HFrEF — ARNi class

## Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)

### Dihydropyridines (DHP)
- **Drugs:** Amlodipine, nifedipine, felodipine, nicardipine, clevidipine (IV), nimodipine
- **Mechanism:** Block L-type Ca2+ channels in vascular smooth muscle > cardiac tissue
- **Effect:** Vasodilation (peripheral and coronary), minimal negative chronotropy/inotropy
- **Indications:** HTN, angina (stable/Prinzmetal), Raynaud's
- **Amlodipine dose:** 2.5–10 mg/day
- **SE:** Peripheral edema (ankle), reflex tachycardia (especially nifedipine), flushing, headache
- **Nimodipine:** Only CCB for subarachnoid hemorrhage (prevents cerebral vasospasm — given orally, crosses BBB)

### Non-Dihydropyridines (Non-DHP)
- **Drugs:** Diltiazem, verapamil
- **Mechanism:** Block L-type Ca2+ in cardiac tissue (SA/AV node) > smooth muscle
- **Effect:** Negative chronotropy, dromotropy (slow AV conduction), mild negative inotropy
- **Indications:** HTN, rate control in afib/flutter, SVT, angina; verapamil for cluster headache prophylaxis
- **Contraindications:** Systolic HF (negative inotropy), heart block (2nd/3rd degree), sick sinus syndrome, concurrent beta-blocker (high-degree block risk), WPW with afib
- **SE:** Constipation (verapamil — most common), bradycardia, AV block, peripheral edema
- **Overdose:** Hyperinsulinemia-euglycemia therapy (high-dose insulin + dextrose), calcium gluconate, vasopressors, ECMO/pacing

## Thiazide Diuretics

**Drugs:** Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ), chlorthalidone (longer acting, preferred), metolazone, indapamide

**Mechanism:** Block Na-Cl cotransporter in distal convoluted tubule → Na and water excretion

**Indications:** HTN (first-line), edema, hypercalciuria (kidney stones — increase Ca reabsorption), nephrogenic DI (paradoxical)

**Doses:** HCTZ 12.5–25 mg/day; chlorthalidone 12.5–25 mg/day

**Contraindications:** Severe renal failure (GFR <30, except metolazone), allergy to sulfonamides (relative)

**Side Effects:**
- Hypokalemia (most common electrolyte SE)
- Hyponatremia (SIADH-like, especially elderly women)
- Hyperuricemia (gout exacerbation)
- Hyperglycemia (impair insulin release)
- Hypercalcemia (retain calcium)
- Hypomagnesemia
- Dyslipidemia (mild, transient)
- Photosensitivity

## Beta-Blockers

**Mechanism:** Competitive antagonist at beta-adrenergic receptors

| Selectivity | Drugs | Notes |
|------------|-------|-------|
| Beta-1 selective (cardioselective) | Metoprolol, atenolol, bisoprolol, esmolol (IV, ultra-short), nebivolol | Safer in asthma/COPD but selectivity lost at high doses |
| Non-selective (B1+B2) | Propranolol, carvedilol (alpha+beta), labetalol (alpha+beta), nadolol, timolol | B2 block → bronchoconstriction, mask hypoglycemia symptoms |
| Intrinsic sympathomimetic activity (ISA) | Pindolol, acebutolol | Partial agonist; less resting bradycardia; less useful in post-MI |

**Effects:** Decrease HR, contractility, BP, renin release; prolong AV conduction (rate control)

**Indications:**
- HTN, HFrEF (carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol — mortality benefit)
- Post-MI (reduce mortality), stable angina
- Rate control in afib/flutter
- Hyperthyroidism (propranolol — blocks peripheral T4→T3 conversion)
- Portal hypertension/varices prophylaxis (propranolol, nadolol)
- Migraine prophylaxis (propranolol, timolol, metoprolol)
- Pheochromocytoma (only AFTER alpha blockade to avoid hypertensive crisis)
- Essential tremor (propranolol)
- Performance anxiety (propranolol — low dose PRN)

**Contraindications:**
- Decompensated HF (acute exacerbation — can initiate once stabilized)
- Significant bradycardia or high-degree heart block
- Severe reactive airway disease (asthma) — use with caution in COPD
- Pheochromocytoma WITHOUT prior alpha-blockade

**Side Effects:**
- Fatigue, bradycardia, cold extremities
- Bronchoconstriction (non-selective)
- Mask hypoglycemia symptoms (tachycardia, tremor) — sweating preserved
- Impotence, depression
- Dyslipidemia (non-selective; increase TG, decrease HDL)
- Rebound hypertension/angina if abruptly stopped — taper over 1–2 weeks
- Hyperkalemia (suppress renin → less aldosterone)

## Alpha-Blockers

### Alpha-1 Selective
- **Drugs:** Prazosin, doxazosin, terazosin, tamsulosin (uro-selective alpha-1A)
- **Mechanism:** Block alpha-1 receptors on vascular smooth muscle → vasodilation
- **Indications:** HTN (prazosin, doxazosin, terazosin), BPH (all — reduce urethral tone)
- **SE:** First-dose orthostatic hypotension (take first dose at bedtime), reflex tachycardia, nasal congestion, intraoperative floppy iris syndrome (tamsulosin — warn ophthalmologists before cataract surgery)

### Alpha-2 Agonists (Central Acting)
See Clonidine section below.

### Phenoxybenzamine (Non-selective, Irreversible Alpha Blocker)
- **Use:** Pheochromocytoma preoperative preparation (given first, then beta-blocker)
- **Mechanism:** Irreversible alpha-1/alpha-2 blockade

## Clonidine

**Mechanism:** Alpha-2 agonist in brainstem (locus coeruleus) → reduces sympathetic outflow → decreases HR, BP; also imidazoline receptor agonist

**Indications:** HTN (second-line), HTN urgency (oral load), opioid withdrawal symptoms, ADHD (children), Tourette's, menopausal hot flashes, spasticity

**Doses:** 0.1–0.3 mg BID (oral); transdermal patch weekly

**SE:** Dry mouth, sedation, constipation, bradycardia; rebound hypertension on abrupt discontinuation (more common at higher doses/long-term use — taper)

**Overdose:** Mimics opioid OD (bradycardia, hypotension, miosis, sedation); treat with supportive care; naloxone partially effective (blocks imidazoline effect)

## Hydralazine

**Mechanism:** Direct arteriolar vasodilator (unclear mechanism — possibly K+ channel activation or NO-mediated)

**Indications:** HTN in pregnancy (drug of choice with labetalol), HFrEF (with nitrates — A-HeFT trial in Black patients), hypertensive urgency/emergency (IV)

**Doses:** Oral 10–75 mg QID; IV 10–20 mg q4–6h for hypertensive emergency in pregnancy

**SE:** Reflex tachycardia (give with beta-blocker), fluid retention (give with diuretic), headache, flushing
- **Drug-induced lupus** (slow acetylators at higher doses >200 mg/day): arthralgias, rash, +ANA (anti-histone antibodies); resolves on discontinuation

## Nitroprusside

**Mechanism:** Releases NO → both arterial and venous dilation (balanced preload and afterload reduction); immediate onset, seconds duration

**Indications:** Hypertensive emergency (especially with acute HF, aortic dissection — though use is now less preferred due to toxicity risk), flash pulmonary edema

**Administration:** IV infusion only; light-sensitive (wrap in foil); requires arterial line monitoring

**SE:**
- Cyanide toxicity (from nitric oxide metabolism → cyanide → thiocyanate)
  - Risk with high doses (>3 mcg/kg/min), prolonged use (>72h), renal failure
  - Symptoms: lactic acidosis, altered mental status, seizures
  - Treatment: sodium thiosulfate (converts cyanide → thiocyanate), hydroxocobalamin (preferred)
- Thiocyanate toxicity (accumulates in renal failure): tinnitus, blurred vision, psychosis
- Reflex tachycardia, hypotension

**Preferred alternatives:** Nicardipine IV, clevidipine IV, labetalol IV for most hypertensive emergencies

## Antiarrhythmics — Vaughan-Williams Classification

### Class I — Sodium Channel Blockers

| Subclass | Drugs | Key Features | Indications |
|---------|-------|-------------|-------------|
| IA — moderate Na block, K block | Quinidine, procainamide, disopyramide | Slow phase 0; prolong QRS AND QT; proarrhythmic | Afib conversion, VT (procainamide); quinidine for Brugada |
| IB — weak Na block (fast off) | Lidocaine, mexiletine, tocainide | Preferentially affect ischemic tissue; shorten APD; minimal QRS effect | Lidocaine IV for VT/VF (now second-line); mexiletine oral for VT, long QT syndrome type 3 |
| IC — strong Na block (slow off) | Flecainide, propafenone | Markedly slow conduction; widen QRS; minimal QT change | Afib/flutter in structurally normal hearts; WPW; AVOID in structural heart disease (CAST trial: increased mortality) |

**Key points:**
- Quinidine: cinchonism (tinnitus, headache), diarrhea, thrombocytopenia, QT prolongation → torsades
- Procainamide: drug-induced lupus (anti-histone), agranulocytosis, active metabolite NAPA (renally cleared)
- Disopyramide: strong anticholinergic SE; negative inotropy — AVOID in HF; useful in vagally-mediated afib and hypertrophic obstructive cardiomyopathy

### Class II — Beta-Blockers
- Propranolol, metoprolol, esmolol (IV, t1/2 = 9 minutes)
- Decrease SA node automaticity, slow AV conduction, decrease cAMP-mediated triggered activity
- Indications: SVT, rate control in afib/flutter, VT/VF storm in ischemia, LQTS (propranolol)

### Class III — Potassium Channel Blockers (Prolong Repolarization)

| Drug | Key Features |
|------|-------------|
| Amiodarone | Multi-class (I, II, III, IV); most effective antiarrhythmic; numerous toxicities (see below); very long t1/2 (40–55 days) |
| Dronedarone | Amiodarone analog WITHOUT iodine; less toxicity; AVOID in permanent afib, HFrEF (increases mortality — ANDROMEDA trial); less effective than amiodarone |
| Sotalol | Class III + non-selective beta-blocker; prolongs QT → torsades risk (especially with hypokalemia/bradycardia); used for afib/VT; renal dose adjustment required |
| Dofetilide | Pure IKr blocker; QT prolongation → torsades; hospital initiation required; multiple drug interactions (avoid verapamil, cimetidine, trimethoprim) |
| Ibutilide | IV only; afib/flutter cardioversion; QT prolongation; watch for torsades |

### Class IV — Calcium Channel Blockers (Non-DHP)
- Verapamil, diltiazem
- Slow AV nodal conduction; rate control in afib/flutter; terminate AV nodal reentrant SVT (adenosine preferred first-line)
- CONTRAINDICATED in WPW + afib (block AV node → force conduction down accessory pathway → VF)

### Other Antiarrhythmics

**Adenosine:**
- Activates A1 receptors → hyperpolarize AV node via IKAdo → transient AV block
- Drug of choice for paroxysmal SVT (AVNRT, AVRT)
- Dose: 6 mg IV rapid push → 12 mg if no response; 12 mg if central line used first
- Half-life: <10 seconds; give with rapid flush
- SE: Flushing, chest tightness, transient asystole/heart block (brief), bronchospasm
- Drug interactions: Theophylline is competitive antagonist (use higher dose); dipyridamole potentiates (use lower dose); carbamazepine — risk of prolonged block

**Digoxin:** See dedicated section below.

**Magnesium:**
- Drug of choice for torsades de pointes (2 g IV over 2–15 minutes)
- Also used for refractory VF, digoxin toxicity arrhythmias, eclampsia

## Amiodarone — Comprehensive Toxicity Profile

**Pharmacokinetics:**
- t1/2 = 40–55 days (range 26–107 days)
- Vd = ~60 L/kg (massive tissue distribution)
- CYP3A4 substrate AND inhibitor; CYP2C9 inhibitor (warfarin interaction — reduce warfarin dose ~30–50%)
- Loading dose often required (IV or oral)
- IV: 150 mg over 10 min then 1 mg/min x6h then 0.5 mg/min; oral load 400–800 mg/day x 1–3 months then maintenance 100–400 mg/day

| System | Toxicity | Monitoring | Management |
|--------|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Thyroid | Hypothyroidism (most common) OR hyperthyroidism (iodine load + direct toxicity); contains ~37% iodine by weight | TFTs (TSH + free T4) at baseline and every 3–6 months | Hypothyroid: levothyroxine; Hyperthyroid: thionamides ± steroids; consider drug discontinuation |
| Pulmonary | Pulmonary toxicity (pneumonitis → fibrosis); 1–17% incidence; can be fatal | CXR + PFTs (DLCO) at baseline and annually; CT chest if symptoms | Discontinue amiodarone; corticosteroids |
| Hepatic | Hepatotoxicity (phospholipidosis → cirrhosis); elevated LFTs | LFTs baseline and q6 months | Discontinue if significant elevation |
| Corneal | Corneal microdeposits (virtually universal with long-term use); usually asymptomatic; rarely impair vision; blue-grey halo around lights | Annual slit-lamp exam | Usually reversible on discontinuation; not indication to stop if asymptomatic |
| Neurologic | Tremor, ataxia, peripheral neuropathy, sleep disturbance | Clinical assessment | Dose reduction or discontinuation |
| Dermatologic | Photosensitivity (use sunscreen); blue-grey discoloration of sun-exposed skin (lipofuscinosis) with long-term use | Clinical | Sunscreen; discoloration may be irreversible |
| Cardiovascular | Bradycardia, AV block, QT prolongation, proarrhythmia (rare compared to other class III agents) | ECG monitoring | Reduce dose; pacemaker if significant block |
| Drug interactions | Inhibits CYP2C9 (warfarin — increase INR), CYP2D6 (digoxin — increase digoxin level), CYP3A4 | INR, digoxin levels | Reduce warfarin 30–50%; reduce digoxin 50% |

**Contraindications:** Severe sinus node dysfunction, 2nd/3rd degree heart block (without pacemaker), cardiogenic shock, iodine hypersensitivity (relative)

## Digoxin

**Mechanism:**
1. **Positive inotropy:** Inhibits Na/K-ATPase → increases intracellular Na → reduces Ca efflux via Na-Ca exchanger → increases intracellular Ca → stronger contraction
2. **Negative chronotropy/dromotropy:** Vagomimetic effect → slows SA node, prolongs AV conduction (rate control)

**Indications:**
- HFrEF: modest symptom benefit; does NOT reduce mortality; reduces hospitalizations
- Atrial fibrillation: rate control (especially at rest; less effective during exercise — use beta-blocker or CCB for activity)

**Dosing:** 0.125–0.25 mg/day oral; renal dose adjustment essential (80% renally eliminated)
- Loading dose for afib: 0.25 mg IV q6h x3 (total ~0.75–1 mg)

**Therapeutic range:** 0.5–0.9 ng/mL (HF); drawn ≥6h post-dose

**Toxicity (> 2 ng/mL, but toxicity can occur within therapeutic range):**
- GI: nausea, vomiting, anorexia, diarrhea (earliest symptoms)
- Visual: xanthopsia (yellow-green tint), blurred vision, halos — classic
- Neuro: fatigue, confusion, delirium (especially elderly)
- Cardiac: virtually any arrhythmia; classic = PVCs, junctional tachycardia, PAT with block, bidirectional VT (pathognomonic), AV block
- ECG changes (not toxicity, just therapeutic effect): "scooping" of ST segment, shortened QT, T-wave changes

**Toxicity precipitants:**
- Hypokalemia (most important — K+ and digoxin compete for same Na/K-ATPase binding site)
- Hypomagnesemia, hypercalcemia
- Hypothyroidism (reduces renal clearance)
- Renal failure (accumulation)
- Drug interactions: amiodarone, verapamil, quinidine, spironolactone all increase digoxin levels

**Reversal — Digoxin Immune Fab (Digibind/DigiFab):**
- Indications: life-threatening arrhythmia, hyperkalemia (K >5.5 — marker of severe toxicity), hemodynamic instability, ingestion >10 mg (adult) or >4 mg (child)
- Dose: 1 vial (40 mg) binds ~0.5 mg digoxin; empiric 10–20 vials for life-threatening toxicity
- After administration: total serum digoxin level rises markedly (measures bound + free) — do NOT repeat level to guide therapy; monitor clinical response

## Anticoagulants

### Warfarin

**Mechanism:** Inhibits vitamin K epoxide reductase (VKOR) → prevents recycling of vitamin K → depletes factors II, VII, IX, X (and proteins C and S)
- Factor VII (t1/2 = 6h) depletes first → early INR rise does NOT indicate full anticoagulation
- Full effect requires ~5 days (depletion of factor II, t1/2 = 60h)

**Indications:** Afib, DVT/PE, mechanical heart valves (only anticoagulant for mechanical valves), antiphospholipid syndrome

**Monitoring:** INR; target 2–3 for most indications; 2.5–3.5 for mechanical aortic/mitral valve

**Drug interactions:** Extremely numerous (see dedicated table)

**Reversal:**

| Urgency | Agent | Onset | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|-------|
| Non-urgent | Vitamin K oral | 24–48h | Hold warfarin + oral vit K; adequate for asymptomatic INR elevation |
| Semi-urgent | Vitamin K IV | 6–12h | IV > oral for faster effect; anaphylaxis risk (give slowly) |
| Urgent (active bleeding) | 4-factor PCC (Kcentra) | Minutes | Replaces II, VII, IX, X + proteins C, S; preferred over FFP |
| Urgent | Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP) | Minutes | Replaces all clotting factors; large volume (2–4 units); time to thaw |
| Life-threatening | PCC + Vitamin K IV | Minutes + hours | Combination prevents INR rebound after PCC wears off |

### Unfractionated Heparin (UFH)

**Mechanism:** Binds antithrombin III → enhances its inhibition of thrombin (IIa) and factor Xa; requires AT-III for activity

**Indications:** DVT/PE treatment/prophylaxis, ACS, PE, bridging, during procedures, CRRT anticoagulation

**Monitoring:** aPTT (therapeutic = 60–100 seconds, ~2–2.5x normal); anti-Xa levels for obese patients

**Administration:** IV infusion with weight-based protocol; SQ for prophylaxis (5000 units BID/TID)

**Reversal:** Protamine sulfate (1 mg per 100 units heparin; max 50 mg; risk of hypotension/anaphylaxis — slow IV push)
- Caution: protamine allergy in patients with prior protamine exposure (NPH insulin, prior cardiac surgery)

**Side Effects:**
- **Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT):**
  - Type I (non-immune): transient, mild thrombocytopenia in first 2 days; not clinically significant
  - Type II (immune, true HIT): days 5–14 (or earlier with prior heparin exposure); platelet drop >50% or <100k; IgG antibodies against heparin-PF4 complex; paradoxical THROMBOSIS (HITT)
  - 4Ts score: Thrombocytopenia severity, Timing, Thrombosis, oTher cause
  - Management: STOP all heparin (including flushes, LMWH); start non-heparin anticoagulant (argatroban, bivalirudin, fondaparinux); do NOT transfuse platelets

### Low Molecular Weight Heparin (LMWH)

**Drugs:** Enoxaparin, dalteparin, tinzaparin

**Mechanism:** Predominantly inhibits factor Xa (>IIa) via AT-III; more predictable pharmacokinetics

**Monitoring:** Generally not required; monitor anti-Xa levels in: pregnancy, obese (>150 kg), renal failure, children, underweight

**Therapeutic doses:** Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg SQ q12h (or 1.5 mg/kg q24h for VTE treatment)

**Renal adjustment:** Reduce dose or switch to UFH if CrCl <30 mL/min

**Reversal:** Protamine partially reverses (~60% anti-Xa activity); no complete reversal agent

**Advantages over UFH:** Predictable kinetics, no monitoring needed, lower HIT risk, SQ dosing

### Fondaparinux

**Mechanism:** Synthetic pentasaccharide; selectively inhibits factor Xa via AT-III; NO anti-IIa activity

**Indications:** DVT/PE treatment/prophylaxis, HIT (alternative), ACS

**Renal excretion:** Contraindicated if CrCl <30 mL/min

**NO reversal agent** (protamine does not work; andexanet alfa may provide partial reversal)

**Does NOT cause HIT** (does not bind PF4)

### Direct Thrombin Inhibitors (DTIs)

| Drug | Route | Indications | Reversal |
|------|-------|-------------|---------|
| Argatroban | IV | HIT, PCI in HIT, liver metabolism (use in renal failure) | Discontinue (t1/2 = 45 min); elevates INR (confounds warfarin monitoring) |
| Bivalirudin | IV | PCI, HIT, CRRT | Discontinue (t1/2 = 25 min) |
| Dabigatran | Oral | Afib (non-valvular), VTE treatment/prophylaxis | Idarucizumab (Praxbind — specific reversal agent); dialyzable |

**Dabigatran:**
- Prodrug; not metabolized by CYP450; renally eliminated (80%)
- Avoid if CrCl <15–30 mL/min
- P-glycoprotein interactions (rifampin, dronedarone reduce levels)
- Idarucizumab: humanized antibody fragment; binds dabigatran; reverses within minutes

### Direct Oral Factor Xa Inhibitors

| Drug | Dose (afib/VTE) | Reversal | Notes |
|------|----------------|---------|-------|
| Rivaroxaban | 20 mg OD with dinner (afib); 15 mg BID x21d then 20 mg OD (VTE) | Andexanet alfa; PCC (4-factor) off-label | CYP3A4/P-gp substrate |
| Apixaban | 5 mg BID (afib); 10 mg BID x7d then 5 mg BID (VTE) | Andexanet alfa | Preferred in renal failure; partially hepatic |
| Edoxaban | 60 mg OD (after 5–10d parenteral) | Andexanet alfa | Not approved for afib if CrCl >95 |
| Betrixaban | 80 mg OD | Limited data | Extended VTE prophylaxis |

**Andexanet alfa (Andexxa):** Recombinant modified factor Xa decoy protein; binds and sequesters Xa inhibitors; very expensive; approved for rivaroxaban and apixaban

## Antiplatelets

### Aspirin

**Mechanism:** Irreversibly acetylates COX-1 (and COX-2) → prevents thromboxane A2 (TXA2) synthesis in platelets → reduces platelet aggregation; also inhibits prostacyclin (PGI2) in endothelium at higher doses

**Antiplatelet dose:** 81 mg daily (full antiplatelet effect with minimal GI SE)

**Antiinflammatory/analgesic:** 325–1000 mg q4–6h

**Antiplatelet effect lasts:** 7–10 days (platelet lifespan; platelets cannot regenerate COX)

**Indications:** ACS, post-PCI (DAPT), secondary stroke/TIA prevention, primary prevention (limited role — only high CV risk without high bleeding risk)

**Reversal:** Platelet transfusion (1 unit raises platelet count ~10k; provides non-aspirin-inhibited platelets); DDAVP (desmopressin) may help

**SE:** GI bleeding (especially with NSAIDs, anticoagulants), gastric ulcers, Reye's syndrome (children with viral illness — avoid aspirin <18 years), tinnitus/salicylism at high doses, bronchospasm in aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease

### Clopidogrel

**Mechanism:** Prodrug → activated by CYP2C19 → active metabolite irreversibly blocks P2Y12 ADP receptor on platelets → reduces ADP-mediated aggregation

**Indications:** ACS, post-PCI (DAPT with aspirin), PAD, secondary stroke prevention (with or without aspirin)

**Onset:** 2–8 hours; antiplatelet effect lasts 7–10 days after last dose

**CYP2C19 interaction:**
- Poor metabolizers (~2–14% depending on ancestry): reduced activation → reduced platelet inhibition → higher MACE risk
- PPIs (especially omeprazole) reduce efficacy via CYP2C19 competition — use pantoprazole if PPI needed
- Prasugrel or ticagrelor preferred in poor metabolizers or acute STEMI

**Reversal:** Platelet transfusion; DDAVP

### Ticagrelor

**Mechanism:** Direct-acting (NOT a prodrug) reversible P2Y12 antagonist; does not require CYP2C19 activation

**Indications:** ACS (preferred over clopidogrel in PLATO trial for reduction of CV death/MI/stroke); post-PCI

**Advantages:** More consistent antiplatelet effect, faster onset/offset, does not depend on CYP2C19

**SE:** Dyspnea (without bronchospasm — 14%; mechanism unclear; usually resolves); bradycardia (ventricular pauses); bleeding; AVOID in severe hepatic impairment; contraindicated with strong CYP3A4 inhibitors/inducers

**Reversal:** Hold drug; platelet transfusion less effective (drug still circulating and can rebind transfused platelets)

### Prasugrel

**Mechanism:** Prodrug → activated by CYP3A4/CYP2B6 (and others) → irreversible P2Y12 block; more complete and consistent activation than clopidogrel

**Indications:** ACS with PCI (TRITON-TIMI trial); NOT for medical management of NSTEMI without PCI

**Contraindications:**
- Prior stroke or TIA (net harm — increased intracranial hemorrhage)
- Age ≥75 years (generally — net clinical harm in elderly)
- Weight <60 kg (use reduced dose 5 mg)
- Active bleeding

**Reversal:** Platelet transfusion (irreversible binding)

### GP IIb/IIIa Inhibitors

**Drugs:** Abciximab (ReoPro), eptifibatide (Integrilin), tirofiban (Aggrastat)

**Mechanism:** Block fibrinogen binding to GP IIb/IIIa receptor on platelets → prevent platelet-platelet aggregation (final common pathway)

**Use:** IV; high-risk PCI, NSTEMI (adjunct); largely replaced by potent P2Y12 inhibitors

**Reversal:** Abciximab: platelet transfusion effective (antibody redistributes to transfused platelets); eptifibatide/tirofiban: short t1/2 (~2h), discontinue → platelet function recovers in 4h

# 4. Antimicrobials

## Beta-Lactams

**Mechanism:** Bind penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs) → inhibit cell wall (peptidoglycan) synthesis → cell lysis
- Bactericidal; time-dependent killing (efficacy depends on time drug level exceeds MIC, not peak)
- Resistance mechanisms: beta-lactamases (enzymatic hydrolysis), altered PBPs (MRSA — mecA gene encodes PBP2a)

### Penicillins by Generation

| Generation | Drugs | Coverage | Notes |
|-----------|-------|----------|-------|
| Natural penicillins | Penicillin G (IV), Penicillin V (oral) | Streptococcus, syphilis, Neisseria meningitidis, enterococci (ampicillin preferred) | Penicillin G benzathine for syphilis/strep pharyngitis prophylaxis |
| Aminopenicillins | Ampicillin, amoxicillin | Broader gram-neg (H. influenzae, E. coli, Listeria); enterococci, Listeria | Amox/clav (Augmentin), amp/sulbactam add beta-lactamase inhibitor |
| Penicillinase-resistant (anti-staph) | Nafcillin, oxacillin, dicloxacillin | MSSA (NOT MRSA) | Preferred for serious MSSA bacteremia (nafcillin/oxacillin superior to vancomycin for MSSA) |
| Antipseudomonal | Piperacillin/tazobactam (pip/tazo), ticarcillin/clav | Broad gram-neg including Pseudomonas; anaerobes | Pip/tazo: workhorse for hospital-acquired infections |

### Cephalosporins by Generation

### Carbapenems

| Drug | Coverage | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| Imipenem/cilastatin | Broadest: gram-pos, gram-neg (incl. Pseudomonas), anaerobes; NOT MRSA, not Stenotrophomonas | Cilastatin prevents renal tubular metabolism; seizure risk (especially with renal failure) |
| Meropenem | Similar to imipenem; slightly better Pseudomonas, less seizure risk | Preferred for CNS infections |
| Ertapenem | Broad gram-neg including ESBL; NO Pseudomonas, NO Acinetobacter, NO MRSA | Once daily dosing; used for outpatient OPAT; good for ESBL without Pseudomonas concern |
| Doripenem | Similar to meropenem | Less commonly used |

**Carbapenem resistance:** KPC (Klebsiella pneumoniae carbapenemase), NDM (New Delhi metallo-beta-lactamase), OXA-48
- KPC: susceptible to ceftazidime/avibactam, meropenem/vaborbactam
- NDM: ceftazidime/avibactam + aztreonam combination; aztreonam/avibactam

### Monobactams
- **Aztreonam:** Only gram-negative aerobes (including Pseudomonas); no gram-positive, no anaerobes
- Safe in penicillin allergy (minimal cross-reactivity EXCEPT with ceftazidime — share identical R1 side chain)
- Used in cystic fibrosis, febrile neutropenia

### Beta-Lactam Allergy
- True penicillin IgE-mediated allergy: 10% report penicillin allergy; <1% confirmed on testing
- Cross-reactivity: penicillins-cephalosporins: <2% (mostly due to shared R1 side chains, not beta-lactam ring); carbapenems-penicillins: <1%
- Confirmed penicillin anaphylaxis: use aztreonam, carbapenems (minimal cross-reactivity) or desensitize

## Aminoglycosides

**Drugs:** Gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin, streptomycin, neomycin (topical/oral only)

**Mechanism:** Irreversibly bind 30S ribosomal subunit → misreading of mRNA → mistranslation → abnormal proteins → cell membrane disruption; concentration-dependent killing

**Coverage:** Gram-negative aerobes (including Pseudomonas); streptomycin for TB/plague/brucellosis; used synergistically with cell wall agents for enterococcal/streptococcal endocarditis

**Monitoring:**
- Traditional dosing: peak (5–10 mcg/mL gentamicin) and trough (<2 mcg/mL gentamicin) drawn around 3rd dose
- Extended-interval (once-daily): Hartford nomogram using 6-14h level post-dose; do NOT use for endocarditis, CrCl <40, pregnancy, neonates, burns

**Toxicities:**
- **Nephrotoxicity:** Proximal tubular damage; usually non-oliguric AKI; reversible; risk with prolonged use, high troughs, concurrent nephrotoxins (amphotericin B, vancomycin, NSAIDs, contrast)
- **Ototoxicity:** Vestibular (dizziness, ataxia — gentamicin/tobramycin) or cochlear (hearing loss — amikacin); often irreversible; related to sustained high levels
- **Neuromuscular blockade:** Rare; risk with concurrent neuromuscular blocking agents; calcium gluconate as antidote

**Penetration:** Poor CNS penetration; poor anaerobic activity (require O2 for uptake); poor lung penetration (alkaline environment may help)

## Macrolides

**Drugs:** Azithromycin (Z-pack), clarithromycin, erythromycin

**Mechanism:** Bind 50S ribosomal subunit (23S rRNA) → inhibit translocation → bacteriostatic (bactericidal at high concentrations)

**Coverage:** Gram-positive cocci, atypical organisms (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Legionella), H. influenzae (azithromycin/clarithromycin), MAC (clarithromycin/azithromycin), H. pylori (clarithromycin)

**Clinical uses:**
- Azithromycin: CAP (atypicals), STIs (Chlamydia), MAC prophylaxis/treatment, pharyngitis (penicillin allergy), COPD exacerbation
- Clarithromycin: H. pylori regimens, MAC, CAP
- Erythromycin: Gastric motility agent (motilin agonist), pertussis, Legionella, Campylobacter

**Drug interactions:**
- Erythromycin and clarithromycin: potent CYP3A4 inhibitors → increase levels of statins, warfarin, tacrolimus, cyclosporine, etc.
- Azithromycin: NOT a significant CYP3A4 inhibitor; fewer drug interactions
- All macrolides: prolong QT interval → avoid with other QT-prolonging agents

**SE:** GI (nausea, cramping — highest with erythromycin, least with azithromycin), hepatotoxicity (cholestatic jaundice — erythromycin estolate), QT prolongation, hearing loss (high-dose azithromycin in elderly)

**Resistance:** Methylation of 23S rRNA (erm genes); efflux pumps (mef genes); significant in Streptococcus pneumoniae and Streptococcus pyogenes

## Tetracyclines

**Drugs:** Tetracycline, doxycycline, minocycline, tigecycline (glycylcycline class)

**Mechanism:** Bind 30S ribosomal subunit → inhibit aminoacyl-tRNA binding → bacteriostatic

**Coverage:** Broad: gram-positives, gram-negatives, atypicals (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Rickettsia, Ehrlichia, Anaplasma), spirochetes (Lyme, syphilis), Brucella, Vibrio, Tularemia; tigecycline extends to MRSA, VRE, ESBL, Acinetobacter

**Clinical uses:**
- Doxycycline: Lyme disease (drug of choice most stages), RMSF (drug of choice — even children), Chlamydia, PID (with metronidazole), CAP (atypicals), STIs, MRSA skin infections
- Minocycline: Nocardia, acne, MRSA
- Tigecycline: MDR gram-negatives, MSSA/MRSA, anaerobes; NOT for bacteremia (subtherapeutic serum levels); NOT for Pseudomonas

**Contraindications:**
- Pregnancy (Category D — teeth discoloration, inhibit bone growth)
- Children <8 years (except RMSF where benefit outweighs risk)

**SE:** GI (nausea/vomiting with doxycycline — take with food), photosensitivity, esophageal ulceration (take with full glass of water, remain upright), tooth discoloration/enamel hypoplasia (children < 8 years), hepatotoxicity (high dose IV), Fanconi syndrome (expired tetracycline), intracranial hypertension (pseudotumor cerebri)

**Absorption:** Chelation with divalent cations (Ca2+, Mg2+, Al3+, Fe2+) → take on empty stomach (not doxycycline), separate from antacids by 2h; dairy products reduce absorption (tetracycline > doxycycline)

## Vancomycin

**Mechanism:** Binds D-Ala-D-Ala terminus of peptidoglycan precursors → inhibits transglycosylation → cell wall synthesis disruption; bactericidal against gram-positives; does NOT enter gram-negative outer membrane

**Coverage:** MRSA, coagulase-negative staphylococci, Enterococcus (NOT VRE), Streptococcus, Clostridioides difficile (oral only for CDI)

**Dosing:**
- IV: 15–20 mg/kg q8–12h (based on actual body weight)
- AUC-guided dosing target: AUC/MIC 400–600 mg·h/L (ASHP/IDSA/SIDP 2020 guidelines); replaces trough-only monitoring
- First AUC measurement: after 3rd or 4th dose (steady state)

**Red Man Syndrome:**
- NOT an allergic reaction (not IgE-mediated)
- Anaphylactoid reaction: direct mast cell degranulation → flushing, erythema, pruritus over face/neck/chest
- Occurs during or shortly after infusion; infusion rate-dependent
- Management: slow infusion rate (infuse over ≥60 min; some recommend 90–120 min for higher doses); premedicate with diphenhydramine; hydroxyzine; slow infusion
- Distinguishfrom true allergy (urticaria, bronchospasm, hypotension = consider true allergy; skin blushing without other features = Red Man syndrome)

**Toxicities:**
- Nephrotoxicity (especially with aminoglycosides, NSAIDS — additive)
- Ototoxicity (primarily with concurrent ototoxins; high levels)
- Thrombocytopenia, neutropenia (rare)

**VRE (Vancomycin-Resistant Enterococcus):**
- vanA (high-level resistance to vancomycin AND teicoplanin) or vanB
- Treat with linezolid, daptomycin, ampicillin (if susceptible), quinupristin-dalfopristin (E. faecium only)

---

## Linezolid

**Mechanism:** Binds 23S rRNA of 50S subunit → inhibits formation of 70S initiation complex → protein synthesis inhibition; bacteriostatic against most organisms (bactericidal vs. Streptococcus)

**Coverage:** MRSA, VRSA, VRE, Streptococcus, penicillin-resistant Streptococcus pneumoniae; excellent oral bioavailability (~100%)

**Clinical uses:** MRSA pneumonia (preferred over vancomycin for lung penetration), VRE infections, skin/soft tissue MRSA

**Toxicities:**

| Toxicity | Details | Monitoring |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| Thrombocytopenia | Dose- and duration-dependent; usually reversible; check CBC weekly | Platelet count weekly; avoid if baseline low |
| Serotonin syndrome | MAO-A inhibitor → dangerous with SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs, tramadol, meperidine, triptans, tyramine-rich foods | Avoid concurrent serotonergic drugs; 2-week washout |
| Optic neuropathy | Rare; reversible if drug stopped; risk with >28 days use | Monitor visual acuity with long courses |
| Peripheral neuropathy | Usually with prolonged courses | Clinical monitoring |
| Lactic acidosis | Mitochondrial protein synthesis inhibition; rare; serious | Lactate if clinically concerning |
| Drug interactions (serotonin syndrome) | SSRIs (most common), MAOIs (absolutely contraindicated), meperidine, tramadol, fentanyl (partial MAOi), dextromethorphan | Review medication list always |

**Duration:** Typically 7–14 days; avoid >28 days without close monitoring

## Daptomycin

**Mechanism:** Cyclic lipopeptide; inserts into gram-positive cell membrane → depolarization → cell death; calcium-dependent

**Coverage:** MRSA (including VISA/VRSA), VRE, Streptococcus, gram-positive coverage only

**Critical limitation: INACTIVATED BY PULMONARY SURFACTANT — absolutely DO NOT use for pneumonia (lung infections)**

**Indications:** MRSA bacteremia/endocarditis (right-sided; left-sided: 6-week course at 8–10 mg/kg/day), VRE bacteremia, skin/soft tissue infections

**Dosing:** 4–6 mg/kg q24h (SSTIs); 6–10 mg/kg q24h (bacteremia/endocarditis)

**SE:**
- Myopathy/rhabdomyolysis (CPK elevation — monitor CPK weekly; hold statins during therapy; especially risk with statin co-administration)
- Eosinophilic pneumonia (rare)
- Peripheral neuropathy (rare)

---

## Fluoroquinolones

**Mechanism:** Inhibit DNA gyrase (gram-negatives — topoisomerase II) and topoisomerase IV (gram-positives) → DNA strand breakage → bactericidal; concentration-dependent killing

| Generation | Drugs | Extended Coverage | Clinical Use |
|-----------|-------|------------------|-------------|
| 1st | Nalidixic acid (obsolete) | UTIs only | Obsolete |
| 2nd | Ciprofloxacin, norfloxacin | Gram-negatives including Pseudomonas; limited gram-positive | UTIs, prostatitis, GI infections, anthrax, Pseudomonas |
| 3rd | Levofloxacin | Enhanced gram-positive (S. pneumoniae); gram-negatives; atypicals | CAP, UTI, pyelonephritis, anthrax; respiratory fluoroquinolone |
| 4th | Moxifloxacin, gemifloxacin | Best gram-positive; excellent anaerobes; NO Pseudomonas activity | CAP, acute bacterial sinusitis, ABECB; do NOT use for UTI (inadequate urine levels) |

**Drug interactions:**
- Chelation with antacids, sucralfate, iron, calcium, zinc — space 2h apart
- Prolong QT — avoid with other QT-prolonging agents
- CYP1A2 inhibition (ciprofloxacin, enoxacin) → increase theophylline/clozapine levels

**SE:**
- GI: nausea (most common)
- CNS: headache, dizziness, insomnia, confusion (elderly), seizures (rare, lower threshold)
- QT prolongation (especially moxifloxacin)
- Tendinopathy/tendon rupture (Achilles most common; risk factors: age >60, corticosteroids, renal failure)
- Peripheral neuropathy (irreversible in some cases) — FDA black box warning
- Aortic aneurysm/dissection risk — FDA black box warning; avoid in patients with aortic disease
- Dysglycemia (hypoglycemia with gatifloxacin — withdrawn; hypo/hyperglycemia with others)
- Phototoxicity (sparfloxacin, lomefloxacin most; others less)
- Avoid in growing children (theoretical cartilage damage — animal studies; risk/benefit in CF, anthrax)

## Antifungals — Full Comparison

### Azoles

**Mechanism:** Inhibit 14-alpha-demethylase (CYP51) → depletes ergosterol → disrupts fungal membrane; fungistatic against Candida, fungicidal against some molds

| Drug | Spectrum | Key Features |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Fluconazole | Candida spp (NOT C. krusei, often NOT C. glabrata/C. auris), Cryptococcus | IV/oral; excellent CSF penetration (Cryptococcal meningitis); drug of choice for mucocutaneous candidiasis, esophageal candidiasis, candidal UTI; CYP2C9 inhibitor (warfarin), CYP3A4 inhibitor |
| Itraconazole | Candida, Aspergillus, endemic fungi (Histo, Blasto, Coccidioides, Sporothrix), dermatophytes | Erratic oral absorption (requires acid); IV formulation; less CNS penetration; negative inotrope |
| Voriconazole | Candida (all spp), Aspergillus (drug of choice), Fusarium, Scedosporium | Excellent CNS penetration; drug of choice for invasive aspergillosis; visual disturbances (transient); hepatotoxicity; QT prolongation; photosensitivity; CYP2C19 substrate (genetic polymorphism affects levels); CYP3A4 inhibitor |
| Posaconazole | Candida, Aspergillus, Mucor/Rhizopus (zygomycetes), endemic fungi | Drug of choice prophylaxis in AML/HSCT; only azole with activity against mucormycosis; QT prolongation; suspension requires food/acidic beverage; tablet/IV preferred |

### Echinocandins

**Mechanism:** Inhibit beta-1,3-glucan synthase → disrupt fungal cell wall (no mammalian equivalent → very low toxicity); fungicidal against Candida; fungistatic against Aspergillus

| Drug | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Caspofungin | First; loading dose 70 mg then 50 mg/day; hepatic adjustment |
| Micafungin | No loading dose needed; fewer drug interactions; hepatic dose adjustment not needed |
| Anidulafungin | Once daily; minimally hepatically metabolized; no renal adjustment |

**Coverage:** Candida spp (including azole-resistant C. glabrata, C. krusei, C. auris); Aspergillus (adjunct); NOT Cryptococcus, NOT Mucor, NOT Fusarium, NOT endemic fungi

**Drug of choice:** Invasive candidiasis/candidemia (Step-down to fluconazole when stable and susceptible)

**SE:** Histamine-like infusion reactions; hepatotoxicity; rash; minimal overall toxicity

### Amphotericin B (AmB)

**Mechanism:** Binds ergosterol in fungal cell membrane → pores → leakage → fungicidal; also binds cholesterol in mammalian membranes → toxicity

| Formulation | Notes |
|-------------|-------|
| Amphotericin B deoxycholate (conventional) | Most nephrotoxic; infusion-related reactions; historical; use limited |
| Amphotericin B lipid complex (ABLC, Abelcet) | Reduced nephrotoxicity; still significant |
| Liposomal amphotericin B (L-AmB, AmBisome) | Least nephrotoxic; preferred formulation; expensive; drug of choice for CNS cryptococcal meningitis (induction), mucormycosis, visceral leishmaniasis |
| Amphotericin B colloidal dispersion (ABCD, Amphotec) | More infusion reactions; rarely used |

**Coverage:** Broadest spectrum: Candida, Aspergillus, Cryptococcus, Mucor/Rhizopus, endemic fungi, Fusarium; NOT azole-resistant mechanisms (different target)

**SE:**
- Nephrotoxicity: renal tubular acidosis, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, decreased GFR; prehydration with NS reduces nephrotoxicity; monitor K+, Mg2+, Cr
- Infusion reactions: fever, chills, rigors, hypotension (premedicate with acetaminophen, diphenhydramine, hydrocortisone; meperidine for rigors)
- Anemia (decreased EPO)
- Phlebitis

### Antifungal Summary Table

| Property | Azoles | Echinocandins | Amphotericin B |
|---------|--------|--------------|----------------|
| Target | Ergosterol synthesis (CYP51) | Cell wall (beta-glucan synthase) | Ergosterol membrane binding |
| Spectrum | Candida, Aspergillus (vori/isa/posa), endemic fungi, Mucor (posa/isa) | Candida (all spp), Aspergillus (static) | Broadest |
| Candida krusei | Resistant to fluconazole; susceptible to voriconazole, echinocandins | Yes | Yes |

### Flucytosine (5-FC)

**Mechanism:** Prodrug → converted by fungal cytosine deaminase → 5-fluorouracil → inhibits thymidylate synthase → DNA synthesis inhibition; fungistatic; penetrates CNS well

**Use:** ALWAYS in combination (resistance develops rapidly with monotherapy); AmB + 5-FC for cryptococcal meningitis induction

**SE:** Myelosuppression (leukopenia, thrombocytopenia), hepatotoxicity; renal dose adjustment required; monitor levels (target 40–60 mcg/mL trough)

# 5. Pain Pharmacology

## NSAIDs

**Mechanism:** Inhibit cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes → reduce prostaglandin synthesis → anti-inflammatory, analgesic, antipyretic, antiplatelet effects

| COX Isoform | Location | Function |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| COX-1 | Ubiquitous (constitutive) | Gastric cytoprotection (PGE2 → mucus/bicarbonate), platelet TXA2, renal prostaglandins |
| COX-2 | Inducible (inflammation); constitutive in kidney, brain, endothelium | Mediates inflammation, pain, fever; prostacyclin (PGI2) in endothelium (antiplatelet) |

**Non-selective NSAIDs:** Ibuprofen, naproxen, ketorolac, indomethacin, diclofenac, meloxicam (COX-2 preferential at low doses), piroxicam

**COX-2 selective (coxibs):** Celecoxib

### NSAID Side Effects

| System | Effect | Mechanism |
|--------|-------|----------|
| GI | Peptic ulcers, GI bleeding, perforation | COX-1 inhibition → loss of PGE2 → decreased mucus/bicarb → mucosal injury |
| Renal | AKI, sodium retention, hypertension, hyperkalemia, papillary necrosis (chronic) | COX-1/2 inhibition → reduce renal prostaglandins → afferent arteriolar constriction; most risk with volume depletion, HF, CKD |
| Cardiovascular | Increased MI/stroke risk (coxibs and non-selective); hypertension | COX-2 selective: reduce PGI2 without reducing TXA2 → prothrombotic; fluid retention |
| Platelet | Reversible platelet inhibition (except aspirin — irreversible) | COX-1 → TXA2 inhibition |
| Hypersensitivity | Aspirin/NSAID-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD): bronchospasm, nasal polyps, urticaria | Arachidonic acid shifted to leukotriene pathway |
| Hepatic | Transaminase elevation (diclofenac most); rare liver failure | Direct hepatotoxicity |

**GI protection:** Use PPI (omeprazole, pantoprazole) with NSAIDs if high GI risk; misoprostol (PGE1 analog — causes diarrhea) also reduces NSAID GI complications

**Contraindications:** CKD, volume depletion, HF (worsen), pregnancy (particularly 3rd trimester — premature closure of ductus arteriosus), aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease, active GI bleeding/ulcer

## Acetaminophen (Paracetamol)

**Mechanism:** Central COX inhibition; mechanism not fully understood; weak peripheral anti-inflammatory effect; does NOT affect platelets; no GI/renal prostaglandin inhibition

**Dose:** 325–1000 mg q4–6h; maximum 4 g/day (3 g/day in liver disease, elderly, regular alcohol use)

**NAPQI Toxicity:**
- Normal: acetaminophen → mainly glucuronidation/sulfation (~90%) → non-toxic conjugates; ~5–10% CYP2E1/3A4 → NAPQI → immediately detoxified by glutathione
- Overdose OR glutathione depletion: NAPQI accumulates → covalent binding to hepatocytes → centrilobular necrosis
- Risk factors for toxicity: fasting/malnutrition (depleted glutathione), chronic alcohol (induced CYP2E1 + depleted glutathione), CYP450 inducers (rifampin, carbamazepine), chronic therapeutic ingestion

**Toxicity Timeline:**
- Phase I (0–24h): nausea, vomiting, malaise
- Phase II (24–72h): apparent improvement; rising LFTs, RUQ pain
- Phase III (72–96h): hepatic necrosis, coagulopathy (elevated INR), renal failure, encephalopathy, peak LFTs
- Phase IV (4 days–2 weeks): recovery or liver failure/death

**Treatment:**
- **N-Acetylcysteine (NAC):** Drug of choice; replenishes glutathione; also a direct antioxidant
  - IV protocol: 150 mg/kg over 1h → 50 mg/kg over 4h → 100 mg/kg over 16h (21-hour protocol); extend if high ALT/INR still rising
  - Oral protocol: 140 mg/kg loading → 70 mg/kg q4h x 17 doses (72h)
  - Rumack-Matthew nomogram: use time of ingestion and serum acetaminophen level to determine need for treatment
  - Treat if level above treatment line (150 mcg/mL at 4h) on nomogram
- Activated charcoal if early (<2h post-ingestion, no vomiting, no altered mental status)
- Liver transplantation evaluation for fulminant hepatic failure (King's College Criteria)

## Opioid Analgesics

**Mechanism:** Bind opioid receptors (mu, kappa, delta) → inhibit adenylyl cyclase (Gi) → decrease cAMP → reduce neuronal excitability → analgesia, sedation, euphoria, respiratory depression

**Receptor effects:**
- Mu (MOR): analgesia, euphoria, respiratory depression, constipation, physical dependence, miosis, bradycardia
- Kappa (KOR): spinal analgesia, sedation, dysphoria, diuresis, miosis
- Delta (DOR): spinal/supraspinal analgesia, mood modulation

|------|----------|--------------|---------|-------|
| Morphine | 30 | 10 | 3–4h | Reference standard |
| Oxycodone | 20 | N/A (oral; 15 mg if IV available) | 3–4h | Short-acting; also ER formulation |
| Hydromorphone (Dilaudid) | 7.5 | 1.5 | 3–4h | Higher potency; use in renal failure (less active metabolite than morphine) |
| Hydrocodone | 30 | N/A | 4–6h | Oral only; schedule II |
| Codeine | 200 | 120 | 3–4h | Prodrug (CYP2D6 → morphine); avoid in ultra-rapid metabolizers and children; avoid in breastfeeding |

### Opioid Toxicity and Reversal

**Toxicity triad:** Miosis (pinpoint pupils), respiratory depression, altered consciousness (opioid toxidrome)
- Other: bradycardia, hypotension, decreased bowel sounds, hypothermia

**Naloxone (Narcan):**
- Competitive opioid receptor antagonist; reverses all opioid effects
- IV dose: 0.4–2 mg q2–3min; intranasal: 4 mg; IM: 0.4–2 mg
- Onset: 2–3 min IV; duration 30–90 min (SHORT — shorter than most opioids)
- Repeat dosing required; continuous infusion for sustained intoxication (especially methadone, fentanyl patches)
- Dose titrate to adequate respiration, NOT full alertness (avoid precipitating opioid withdrawal)
- Naltrexone: oral or IM long-acting; used for OUD maintenance and alcohol use disorder

### Opioid-Induced Constipation (OIC)

**Mechanism:** Mu-opioid receptors in enteric nervous system → decreased motility, increased anal sphincter tone, decreased secretions → constipation

**Prevention:** Start stimulant laxative (senna ± docusate) with every patient starting scheduled opioids; unlike other opioid effects, tolerance to constipation does NOT develop

**Treatment options:**
- Senna (stimulant laxative) — first-line
- Polyethylene glycol (osmotic)
- Bisacodyl
- Methylnaltrexone (Relistor): peripherally-acting mu-opioid receptor antagonist (PAMORA); does not cross BBB; treats OIC without reversing analgesia or precipitating withdrawal; SQ injection
- Naloxegol (Movantik): oral PAMORA; prevent by CYP3A4 inhibitors
- Lubiprostone: chloride channel activator; FDA-approved for OIC

### Neuropathic Pain Agents

| Drug Class | Drugs | Mechanism | Notes |
|-----------|-------|----------|-------|
| Gabapentinoids | Gabapentin, pregabalin | Alpha-2-delta subunit of voltage-gated Ca2+ channels → reduce excitatory neurotransmitter release | DPN, PHN, fibromyalgia, spinal cord injury pain; sedation, dizziness, peripheral edema; renal dose adjustment |
| TCAs | Amitriptyline, nortriptyline | NE/5-HT reuptake inhibition; Na channel block; adjuvant analgesia | DPN, PHN; nortriptyline better tolerated; anticholinergic SE |
| SNRIs | Duloxetine, venlafaxine | NE/5-HT reuptake inhibition | DPN (duloxetine FDA-approved), fibromyalgia, MSK pain |
| Topical lidocaine | 5% lidocaine patch | Na channel block; local analgesia | PHN; minimal systemic absorption |
| Capsaicin | Topical 0.025–0.1%, 8% patch | Depletes substance P from sensory neurons (TRPV1 activation) | PHN, DPN; burning sensation on application |
| Tramadol | See opioid table | Weak opioid + SNRI | NB: interactions and seizure risk |

## Diabetes Medications

### Insulin

| Type | Onset | Peak | Duration | Use |
|------|-------|------|---------|-----|
| Rapid-acting (lispro, aspart, glulisine) | 5–15 min | 1–2h | 3–5h | Mealtime bolus |
| Regular (short-acting) | 30–60 min | 2–4h | 5–8h | Mealtime; insulin drips (only regular used IV) |
| NPH (intermediate) | 2–4h | 4–10h | 10–18h | Twice-daily basal; variable absorption |
| Glargine (Lantus, Basaglar) | 2–4h | Peakless | 20–24h | Once-daily basal; do NOT mix |
| Detemir (Levemir) | 3–4h | Minimal peak | 18–22h | Once or twice daily |
| Degludec (Tresiba) | 1–9h | Peakless | >42h | Ultra-long basal; flexible dosing |
| Inhaled (Afrezza) | 12–15 min | 1h | 2.5–3h | Rapid-acting; contraindicated in lung disease |

**SE:** Hypoglycemia (most important), weight gain, lipodystrophy at injection site, insulin resistance (high doses), hypokalemia (insulin shifts K+ into cells)

### Metformin

**Mechanism:** Activates AMPK → reduces hepatic gluconeogenesis; improves insulin sensitivity; mild weight loss; does not cause hypoglycemia alone

**Indications:** First-line for T2DM; CV benefit (UKPDS); can use in CKD with dose adjustment (hold if eGFR <30; reduce dose if 30–45; OK if >45)

**SE:** GI (nausea, diarrhea — start low, take with food; usually resolves), lactic acidosis (rare; risk with eGFR <30, IV contrast, severe illness, liver failure, sepsis), vitamin B12 deficiency with long-term use

**Hold for:** IV iodinated contrast (hold if eGFR <60; resume 48h after if renal function stable), major surgery, acute illness

### GLP-1 Receptor Agonists

**Drugs:** Semaglutide (Ozempic SQ, Rybelsus oral, Wegovy for obesity), liraglutide (Victoza, Saxenda), dulaglutide (Trulicity), exenatide (Byetta, Bydureon), tirzepatide (Mounjaro — GLP-1/GIP dual agonist)

**Mechanism:** Stimulate GLP-1 receptor → glucose-dependent insulin release → reduce glucagon → delay gastric emptying → central appetite suppression

**Benefits:** CV mortality reduction (liraglutide LEADER, semaglutide SUSTAIN-6); significant weight loss; kidney protection (semaglutide FLOW); low hypoglycemia risk (glucose-dependent)

**SE:** GI (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea — most common, especially early; mitigate with slow dose titration), pancreatitis (rare), gastroparesis/delayed gastric emptying (caution before anesthesia), thyroid C-cell tumors (rodent data; contraindicated in personal/family history of MTC or MEN2), injection site reactions

### SGLT2 Inhibitors

**Drugs:** Empagliflozin (Jardiance), dapagliflozin (Farxiga), canagliflozin (Invokana), ertugliflozin (Steglatro)

**Mechanism:** Inhibit sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 in proximal tubule → glucosuria → glucose lowering; osmotic diuresis; natriuresis → reduce preload/BP; also direct cardiac/renal protective effects independent of glucose

**Benefits:**
- Empagliflozin EMPA-REG: CV death reduction, HF hospitalization, kidney protection
- Dapagliflozin DAPA-HF: HF mortality reduction (even in non-diabetics)
- Canagliflozin CREDENCE: renal protection in DKD

**SE:**
- Genital mycotic infections (most common — glucosuria provides substrate; women > men)
- UTI (modest increase)
- Euglycemic DKA (glucose may be normal/near-normal — measure ketones, not glucose alone; risk with fasting, surgery, illness, low carb diet) — hold 3–4 days before major surgery
- Hypotension/dehydration (with diuretics)
- Amputations (canagliflozin — FDA warning; less clear for others)
- Fournier's gangrene (rare; necrotizing fasciitis of perineum — FDA warning)
- Bone fractures (canagliflozin)
- Hyperkalemia (less aldosterone effect)

### DPP-4 Inhibitors (Gliptins)

**Drugs:** Sitagliptin (Januvia), saxagliptin (Onglyza), alogliptin (Nesina), linagliptin (Tradjenta)

**Mechanism:** Inhibit dipeptidyl peptidase-4 → prevent GLP-1 degradation → increase active GLP-1 → glucose-dependent insulin release; weight neutral; low hypoglycemia risk

**SE:** Nasopharyngitis, headache; pancreatitis (rare, controversial); saxagliptin — HF hospitalization (avoid in HF); joint pain (arthralgia); skin bullous pemphigoid (rare)

### Sulfonylureas

**Drugs:** Glipizide, glyburide (glibenclamide), glimepiride

**Mechanism:** Block ATP-sensitive K+ channels on pancreatic beta cells → depolarization → Ca2+ influx → insulin secretion (GLUCOSE INDEPENDENT — causes hypoglycemia)

**SE:** Hypoglycemia (most important; glyburide most risk — active metabolites; avoid in elderly, renal failure); weight gain; disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol (chlorpropamide most)

**Glyburide:** Avoid in elderly (Beers Criteria) and CKD (active metabolites accumulate → hypoglycemia)

### Thiazolidinediones (TZDs)

**Drugs:** Pioglitazone, rosiglitazone

**Mechanism:** Activate PPAR-gamma → improve insulin sensitivity in adipose and muscle

**SE:** Weight gain (fluid retention and fat redistribution), edema, HF exacerbation (CONTRAINDICATED in NYHA Class III/IV), bone fractures (especially in women), bladder cancer (pioglitazone — FDA warning, controversial), macular edema; rosiglitazone: CV risk concerns (restricted access)

### Alpha-Glucosidase Inhibitors

**Drugs:** Acarbose, miglitol

**Mechanism:** Inhibit intestinal alpha-glucosidase → delay carbohydrate absorption → blunt post-meal glucose rise

**SE:** GI (flatulence, diarrhea, bloating — very common; limits use); hepatotoxicity (rare, acarbose); if hypoglycemia occurs while on these agents, treat with GLUCOSE (dextrose/glucose tablets) — NOT sucrose/table sugar (requires alpha-glucosidase to split)

## Thyroid Pharmacology

### Hypothyroidism Treatment

**Levothyroxine (LT4):**
- Synthetic T4; first-line treatment
- Absorbed in small intestine; take on empty stomach 30–60 min before food
- t1/2 = 7 days; slowly adjust dose (recheck TSH in 6–8 weeks)
- Drug interactions: calcium, iron, antacids, PPIs, cholestyramine reduce absorption (space 4h apart); CYP3A4 inducers (rifampin, carbamazepine) increase metabolism → higher dose needed
- Initiate low dose in cardiac patients/elderly (risk angina, AF)
- Target TSH: 0.5–2.5 mIU/L (most patients); 1–4 mIU/L elderly; 0.1–2.5 mIU/L pregnant

**Liothyronine (LT3):** Short-acting T3; used in myxedema coma (IV); thyroid cancer preparation (shorter withhold before RAI); combination LT4/LT3 controversial for hypothyroid symptoms

**Myxedema Coma:** IV T3 or T4 (T3 preferred for rapid effect), hydrocortisone (adrenal insufficiency may coexist), supportive care

### Hyperthyroidism Treatment

**Thionamides:**

| Drug | Key Features |
|------|-------------|
| Methimazole (MMI) | First-line (except 1st trimester pregnancy); once daily dosing; fewer SE; inhibits thyroid peroxidase → blocks thyroid hormone synthesis AND iodine organification |
| Propylthiouracil (PTU) | Preferred 1st trimester (MMI teratogenic — choanal atresia, aplasia cutis); thyroid storm (blocks peripheral T4→T3 conversion in addition to synthesis); hepatotoxicity (FDA black box — liver failure; avoid long-term use) |

**Common SE:** Agranulocytosis (0.3–0.5%; check CBC if fever/sore throat — discontinue immediately; can be life-threatening), rash, arthralgias; hepatotoxicity (PTU > MMI); ANCA-positive vasculitis (PTU)

**Agranulocytosis:** Do NOT switch to the other thionamide (cross-reactivity); use RAI or surgery

**Radioactive Iodine (RAI/I-131):**
- Destroys thyroid tissue; preferred definitive therapy for Graves' disease
- Contraindicated in pregnancy, breastfeeding, severe ophthalmopathy (worsens eye disease — use antithyroid drugs instead)
- May worsen Graves' ophthalmopathy → give steroids prophylactically

**Potassium Iodide (SSKI/Lugol's):**
- Wolff-Chaikoff effect: large iodine load transiently inhibits thyroid hormone synthesis and release
- Use in thyroid storm, pre-thyroidectomy (reduces vascularity)
- Give 1 hour AFTER thionamide (otherwise iodine substrate increases thyroid hormone synthesis)
- Jod-Basedow phenomenon: iodine load in iodine-deficient/autonomous thyroid → hyperthyroidism

**Beta-blockers (propranolol):** Control symptoms (tachycardia, tremor, anxiety); blocks peripheral T4→T3 conversion; does not affect thyroid function

**Thyroid Storm Treatment:**
1. PTU (preferred over MMI for T4→T3 conversion block)
2. Lugol's iodine (1 hour after PTU)
3. Propranolol IV/oral (high dose)
4. Hydrocortisone (blocks T4→T3, treats relative adrenal insufficiency)
5. Supportive care (cooling, fluids, treat precipitant)

# 7. Psychiatry Pharmacology

## SSRIs (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors)

**Mechanism:** Block serotonin reuptake transporter (SERT) → increase synaptic 5-HT; onset of therapeutic effect 2–6 weeks (despite rapid receptor occupancy — likely due to adaptive receptor changes)

**Drugs:** Fluoxetine, sertraline, paroxetine, citalopram, escitalopram, fluvoxamine

**Indications:** Major depressive disorder (first-line), anxiety disorders (GAD, social anxiety, panic disorder), OCD, PTSD, premenstrual dysphoric disorder, bulimia (fluoxetine)

| Drug | Key Features |
|------|-------------|
| Fluoxetine | Longest t1/2 (1–6 days; active metabolite norfluoxetine t1/2 = 4–16 days); fewest withdrawal symptoms; potent CYP2D6 and CYP2C19 inhibitor; safe in pregnancy (most data); activating |
| Sertraline | Fewest drug interactions; preferred in cardiac patients, pregnancy, elderly; mild CYP2D6 inhibition |
| Paroxetine | Potent CYP2D6 inhibitor; most anticholinergic (most constipation, dry mouth); most sedating; teratogenic concerns (cardiac defects — avoid in pregnancy); most sexual SE; most discontinuation syndrome |
| Citalopram | QT prolongation (dose-dependent; max 40 mg/day, 20 mg/day in elderly/liver disease); few drug interactions |
| Escitalopram | S-enantiomer of citalopram; better tolerated; fewer drug interactions; QT prolongation (less than citalopram); preferred in elderly |
| Fluvoxamine | Primarily for OCD; potent CYP1A2 and CYP2C19 inhibitor; multiple drug interactions |

**SE:** Sexual dysfunction (most common cause of non-adherence; all SSRIs — anorgasmia, delayed ejaculation, decreased libido), GI (nausea early — give with food), insomnia/activation (fluoxetine), weight gain (paroxetine most), platelet inhibition (increase bleeding risk with NSAIDs/anticoagulants), hyponatremia (SIADH — elderly), QT prolongation (citalopram)

**Discontinuation syndrome:** Flu-like symptoms, dizziness, sensory disturbances ("brain zaps"), irritability; fluoxetine has least risk (long t1/2); taper gradually

## SNRIs (Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors)

**Drugs:** Venlafaxine, desvenlafaxine, duloxetine, levomilnacipran, milnacipran (fibromyalgia)

**Mechanism:** Block SERT + NET (norepinephrine transporter) → increase 5-HT and NE; NE effect more prominent at higher doses

**Duloxetine:** FDA-approved for MDD, GAD, fibromyalgia, DPN, musculoskeletal pain, stress urinary incontinence; CYP2D6 inhibitor; avoid in hepatic impairment

**Venlafaxine:** MDD, anxiety, panic, social anxiety, hot flashes; dose-dependent BP increase (check BP); discontinuation syndrome (taper); extended-release preferred (fewer GI SE)

**SE:** Similar to SSRIs + NE-related: increased BP, sweating, insomnia, urinary retention; fewer sexual SE than SSRIs (duloxetine)

## Serotonin Syndrome

**Mechanism:** Excess serotonergic activity — usually drug combination

**Precipitants:** SSRI/SNRI + MAOIs (most dangerous), SSRIs + linezolid (MAOi), SSRIs + tramadol, SSRIs + triptans, SSRIs + fentanyl, SSRIs + dextromethorphan, SSRI + St. John's Wort, SSRI + meperidine, SSRI + TCAs, SSRI + lithium

**Clinical Triad (Hunter Criteria):**
- **Mental status changes:** agitation, anxiety, confusion
- **Autonomic dysfunction:** tachycardia, hyperthermia, diaphoresis, hypertension, mydriasis
- **Neuromuscular abnormalities:** clonus (pathognomonic), hyperreflexia, tremor, myoclonus, ataxia

**Treatment:**
1. Discontinue offending agents
2. Benzodiazepines (cyproheptadine adjunct — 5-HT2A antagonist)
3. Cyproheptadine (serotonin antagonist) 12–32 mg/day
4. Supportive: cooling for hyperthermia, IV fluids
5. Severe: intubation, paralysis (non-depolarizing) for hyperthermia/rigidity; avoid succinylcholine (risk of hyperkalemia with muscle breakdown)
6. IV NTG or nitroprusside for severe hypertension

**Distinguish from NMS:** Serotonin syndrome = rapid onset (hours), hyperreflexia, clonus, mydriasis; NMS = gradual onset (days), hyporeflexia/rigidity (lead-pipe), miosis typically

## TCAs (Tricyclic Antidepressants)

**Drugs:** Amitriptyline, nortriptyline, imipramine, desipramine, clomipramine, doxepin

**Mechanism:** Block SERT + NET; also block muscarinic, H1, alpha-1 receptors → side effects

**Indications:** MDD (second-line), neuropathic pain, migraine prophylaxis, OCD (clomipramine), panic disorder, enuresis (imipramine), fibromyalgia

**SE (related to non-selective binding):**
- Anticholinergic: dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, blurred vision, confusion (especially elderly — Beers Criteria)
- Antihistamine (H1): sedation, weight gain
- Alpha-1 block: orthostatic hypotension
- Cardiac: QT prolongation, QRS widening → risk of VT (torsades), especially in overdose
- Lowered seizure threshold

**TCA Overdose (life-threatening):**
- Triad: QRS widening (>100–120 ms), anticholinergic toxidrome, altered consciousness/seizures
- Treatment: sodium bicarbonate (IV bolus 1–2 mEq/kg) → narrows QRS, reduces toxicity (increase pH → decrease drug binding to Na channels); intubation; avoid physostigmine (risk of seizures/asystole); benzodiazepines for seizures; avoid flumazenil if co-ingestion of benzos (may precipitate seizures)
- QRS >160 ms: very high risk of VT/VF

## MAOIs (Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors)

**Drugs:**
- Non-selective, irreversible: phenelzine, tranylcypromine
- MAO-B selective: selegiline (low dose for Parkinson's; high dose/patch for depression, becomes non-selective)
- Reversible MAO-A inhibitor: moclobemide (not in US)

**Mechanism:** Inhibit MAO → increase NE, 5-HT, dopamine; MAO-A primarily metabolizes 5-HT and NE; MAO-B primarily metabolizes dopamine

**Indications:** MDD (treatment-resistant, atypical features), panic disorder; selegiline for Parkinson's; rarely used due to interactions

**Drug Interactions (potentially fatal):**
- **Tyramine crisis (cheese reaction):** MAOIs prevent intestinal/hepatic MAO degradation of dietary tyramine → tyramine absorbed → displaces NE from vesicles → hypertensive crisis; foods: aged cheese, cured meats, wine, beer, sauerkraut, soy sauce, overripe fruit; wait 14 days after stopping MAOI before consuming
- **Serotonin syndrome:** Any serotonergic drug (SSRIs, SNRIs, meperidine, tramadol, dextromethorphan, TCAs, triptans); 14-day washout required between MAOI and SSRIs/SNRIs; 5 weeks for fluoxetine
- **Sympathomimetic crisis:** Stimulants, decongestants (pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine), cocaine
- **Hypoglycemia** enhancement with insulin/sulfonylureas

**SE:** Orthostatic hypotension, insomnia, weight gain, sexual dysfunction, liver toxicity (phenelzine)

## Lithium

**Mechanism:** Not fully understood; inhibits inositol monophosphatase → depletes inositol → reduces IP3 signaling; modulates glycogen synthase kinase-3 (GSK-3); neuroprotective; reduces neuronal excitability

**Indications:** Bipolar disorder (acute mania, maintenance — mood stabilizer, reduces suicide risk), augmentation of antidepressants in MDD, schizoaffective disorder

**Therapeutic range:** 0.6–1.2 mEq/L (maintenance); 0.8–1.0 mEq/L (acute mania); trough (12h post-dose); narrow therapeutic index

**Toxicity:**

| Level | Symptoms |
|-------|---------|
| 1.5–2.0 mEq/L (mild) | Tremor (fine), nausea, diarrhea, polyuria, fatigue |
| 2.0–2.5 mEq/L (moderate) | Coarse tremor, drowsiness, confusion, ataxia, slurred speech, muscle twitching |
| >2.5 mEq/L (severe) | Seizures, cardiac arrhythmias, hypotension, irreversible neurological damage, coma, death |

**Precipitants of toxicity:** Dehydration (NSAIDs reduce renal prostaglandins → reduce lithium excretion), thiazide diuretics (increase lithium reabsorption), ACEi/ARBs (increase lithium reabsorption), sodium depletion, fever/sweating, decreased renal function

**Chronic side effects:** Hypothyroidism (inhibits thyroid hormone synthesis — monitor TFTs annually), nephrogenic diabetes insipidus (polyuria, polydipsia — amiloride treatment), weight gain, acne, psoriasis exacerbation, tremor (treat with propranolol), cognitive effects, leukocytosis (benign)

**Dialysis:** Lithium is dialyzable (low protein binding, low Vd) — use hemodialysis for severe toxicity

## Antipsychotics

### First-Generation (Typical) Antipsychotics — FGAs

**Mechanism:** Primarily D2 receptor blockade; also muscarinic, histamine H1, alpha-1 antagonism

| Drug | Potency | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Chlorpromazine (CPZ) | Low | Prototype; most sedating, most anticholinergic, most hypotension; low EPS |
| Thioridazine | Low | QT prolongation; retinal pigmentation; restricted use |
| Haloperidol (Haldol) | High | Least sedating, least anticholinergic; most EPS; available IM/IV; decanoate form |
| Fluphenazine | High | Available as decanoate (long-acting IM) |
| Perphenazine | Mid | Intermediate |
| Thiothixene | High | High EPS |

**EPS (Extrapyramidal Symptoms):**

| Syndrome | Timing | Description | Treatment |
|---------|--------|-------------|---------|
| Acute dystonia | Hours–days | Painful involuntary muscle contractions (neck, eye, tongue); torticollis, oculogyric crisis | Diphenhydramine IV/IM; benztropine IM; dramatic relief |
| Akathisia | Days–weeks | Subjective restlessness, inability to sit still; often mistaken for anxiety/worsening psychosis | Dose reduction; propranolol (most effective); benzodiazepines; switch antipsychotic |
| Parkinsonism | Weeks–months | Bradykinesia, rigidity, pill-rolling tremor, shuffling gait | Dose reduction; benztropine; amantadine; switch to low-EPS agent |
| Tardive dyskinesia (TD) | Months–years | Late-onset rhythmic involuntary movements (mouth, tongue, extremities); often irreversible | Prevention; dose reduction or discontinue; switch to clozapine/quetiapine; VMAT2 inhibitors (valbenazine, deutetrabenazine) |

**Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS):**
- **Mechanism:** Dopamine receptor blockade → loss of dopaminergic inhibition of thermoregulation
- **Clinical features:** 4 classic features:
  - **Hyperthermia** (often >40°C)
  - **Rigidity** ("lead-pipe" — severe)
  - **Mental status changes** (altered consciousness, coma)
  - **Autonomic instability** (tachycardia, labile BP, diaphoresis, tachypnea)
  - Elevated CK (rhabdomyolysis), leukocytosis, elevated LFTs
- **Timing:** Days to weeks after starting/increasing antipsychotic (or abruptly stopping dopaminergic agents in PD patients)
- **Treatment:**
  1. **Discontinue offending agent immediately**
  2. **Dantrolene** (direct muscle relaxant — inhibits sarcoplasmic reticulum Ca2+ release; primary pharmacological treatment)
  3. **Bromocriptine/amantadine** (dopamine agonists — address mechanism)
  4. **Benzodiazepines** (symptom control)
  5. Aggressive supportive care: cooling, IV fluids, prevent rhabdomyolysis-induced AKI, ICU
  6. Do NOT use succinylcholine (hyperkalemia risk with muscle breakdown)

**Other FGA SE:** QT prolongation (especially thioridazine, pimozide), orthostatic hypotension (low-potency), sedation, anticholinergic effects, sexual dysfunction (hyperprolactinemia — galactorrhea, amenorrhea, gynecomastia), weight gain, photosensitivity, lenticular deposits (chlorpromazine)

### Second-Generation (Atypical) Antipsychotics — SGAs

**Mechanism:** D2 antagonism + 5-HT2A antagonism; lower EPS risk; variable metabolic profiles

| Drug | Key Features | Notable SE |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| Clozapine | Most effective for treatment-resistant schizophrenia; reduces suicidality; most sedating; no TD; D4 antagonism; 5-HT2A | Agranulocytosis (most critical SE); seizures (dose-dependent); metabolic syndrome; hypersalivation; myocarditis (first month); cardiomyopathy; orthostatic hypotension; CYP1A2 substrate (smoking effect); constipation |
| Olanzapine | Most metabolic SE; most weight gain; diabetes risk; sedating; low EPS | Metabolic syndrome (most prominent), weight gain, sedation |
| Quetiapine | Low EPS; most sedating of SGAs; used for bipolar depression and insomnia (low dose) | Sedation, metabolic SE, orthostatic hypotension; preferred with Parkinson's (low D2) |
| Risperidone | Dose-dependent EPS (higher risk above 6mg); most hyperprolactinemia of SGAs; available long-acting IM | EPS (dose-dependent), metabolic SE, hyperprolactinemia |
| Aripiprazole | Partial D2 agonist; minimal metabolic SE; minimal sedation; no QT prolongation; weight neutral | Akathisia, insomnia, nausea; may worsen psychosis if D2 partial agonism insufficient |
| Ziprasidone | Least metabolic effect; must take with food (reduces absorption); QT prolongation | QT prolongation; requires food with dose |
| Lurasidone | Minimal metabolic SE; no QT prolongation; effective for bipolar depression; take with food (≥350 cal) | Akathisia, sedation |
| Paliperidone | Active metabolite of risperidone; similar profile; long-acting IM available | EPS, hyperprolactinemia |
| Asenapine | SL formulation only; must dissolve under tongue; no food/drink for 10 min | Sedation, akathisia, oral hypoesthesia |
| Iloperidone | Dose titration required; QT prolongation; orthostatic hypotension | QT, orthostatic hypotension |
| Cariprazine | D3/D2 partial agonist; good for negative symptoms; bipolar depression | Akathisia, nausea |

### Clozapine — ANC Monitoring Protocol

**Agranulocytosis risk:** 0.5–2%; peak risk at 6–18 weeks; can be fatal; requires REMS (Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy) program (Clozapine REMS)

| ANC (cells/mm3) | Action |
|----------------|--------|
| ≥1500 (≥1000 in Benign Ethnic Neutropenia) | Continue clozapine; monitoring per schedule |
| 1000–1499 (or 500–999 in BEN) | Continue; increase monitoring frequency to 3x/week |
| 500–999 (or 250–499 in BEN) | Interrupt treatment; daily monitoring; hematology consult |
| <500 (<250 in BEN) | Discontinue immediately; hospitalize; DO NOT rechallenge |

**Monitoring schedule:**
- Weekly for first 6 months
- Every 2 weeks for months 6–12
- Monthly after 12 months

**Other monitoring:** Fasting glucose/lipids (metabolic), ECG (QT; myocarditis first month), troponin/CRP if cardiac symptoms, constipation assessment (risk of ileus/megacolon)

## Appendix: High-Yield Mnemonics and Clinical Pearls

### Drugs Requiring Monitoring
- **"VITAL" drugs (low TI, require TDM):** Vancomycin, digoxin, phenytoin, lithium, aminoglycosides, tacrolimus/cyclosporine
- Carbamazepine and valproate: also require TDM and CBC/LFTs (hematologic and hepatic toxicity)

### Common Exam Pitfalls
- Vancomycin AUC-guided (NOT trough-only) per 2020 ASHP/IDSA guidelines
- Daptomycin CANNOT treat pneumonia (surfactant inactivation)
- MRSA treatment: vancomycin or daptomycin for bacteremia; linezolid for pneumonia
- Amiodarone: always check TFTs, PFTs, LFTs, eye exam — multisystem toxicity
- Clozapine ANC monitoring is mandatory — know thresholds
- NMS treatment: dantrolene + bromocriptine (NOT cyproheptadine — that is for serotonin syndrome)
- Serotonin syndrome: hyperreflexia and clonus (vs. NMS: lead-pipe rigidity)
- Codeine and tramadol: avoid in CYP2D6 ultra-rapid metabolizers and children
- Fluoroquinolones: tendinopathy, peripheral neuropathy, aortic aneurysm — FDA black box warnings
- ACEi vs ARB: cough = bradykinin (ACEi only) → switch to ARB; angioedema = absolute contraindication to ACEi (also can occur with ARB but less common)
- Aspirin + COX-2 inhibitor: aspirin prevents PGI2 inhibition advantage of coxibs; combined use negates GI benefit of coxib
- Metformin: HOLD for IV contrast (if eGFR <60); lactic acidosis risk
- Phenytoin overdose/toxicity signs: NYSTAGMUS (lateral > upward), ATAXIA, DIPLOPIA — classic triad; with extreme toxicity: seizures (paradoxically)
